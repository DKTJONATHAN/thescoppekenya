## Legal Friction Over Saba Saba Demonstrations

Nairobi Regional Police Commander Issa Mohamud declared the planned Saba Saba protests illegal today, citing a failure by organizers to provide mandatory formal notification to law enforcement, a decision that ignites a complex debate over constitutional assembly rights.

The pronouncement follows a high-level security coordination meeting involving regional commanders from Nairobi, Kiambu, Machakos, and Kajiado. Authorities maintained that the Public Order Act remains the primary framework governing public gatherings, requiring organizers to inform police of their intent to demonstrate to allow for the coordination of security resources. This requirement is intended to balance the state’s duty to maintain public order with the rights of citizens to gather in public spaces.

However, the legal landscape in Kenya features a persistent tension between these administrative requirements and the protections granted under Article 37 of the Constitution. Article 37 explicitly guarantees the right of every person to assemble, demonstrate, and present petitions to public authorities. Historically, this constitutional right has been interpreted by the courts as needing no prior approval from police, often positioning the police notification requirement as an administrative formality rather than a prerequisite for the exercise of a fundamental right.

This legal gap creates a scenario where the state defines protests as unlawful due to procedural lapses, while organizers view such declarations as infringements on their core freedoms. Similar debates often emerge as the capital seeks to modernize its urban management, much like the recent rollout of the [Nairobi Air Quality Action Plan](https://zandani.co.ke/article/nairobi-launches-air-quality-action-plan-real-time-monitoring), where the city must balance infrastructure and policy with the daily realities of its inhabitants. The current clash highlights the absence of a unified legislative interpretation that harmonizes the older Public Order Act with the expansive 2010 Constitution.

### Conflicting Interpretations of Assembly Rights

Human rights groups and civil society organizations have publicly rejected the police designation, asserting that their intent to proceed remains unchanged. These groups argue that the notification requirement is frequently weaponized to stifle dissent rather than facilitate safe demonstrations. Legal analysts suggest that the judiciary has consistently leaned toward the interpretation that the right to assembly is not conditional on police authorization, though the practical execution of these protests often remains subject to aggressive state intervention.

Civil society advocates argue that demanding formal notice ignores the spontaneous nature of civic expression. They plan to march toward Parliament to present a formal petition detailing grievances related to police conduct and broader systemic human rights violations. By insisting on their constitutional prerogative, these groups are challenging the state to prove that a lack of administrative paperwork can truly override a supreme legal guarantee of expression.

### The Path Toward Judicial Clarification

The standoff forces a broader discussion on the limits of administrative regulation in a democratic state. As the public watches to see how security forces will respond on the ground, legal scholars indicate that the courts may soon need to provide a definitive ruling to resolve the ongoing discord regarding the Public Order Act. While entertainment events like the upcoming [boxing match between Arrow Bwoy and Shakib Cham Lutaaya](https://zandani.co.ke/article/arrow-bwoy-shakib-cham-lutaaya-boxing-match) follow clear licensing protocols, political assembly operates under a different set of expectations that remain dangerously ambiguous for all parties involved.

The refusal of activists to cancel the event suggests that the dispute is no longer just about the protests themselves, but about the precedent set for future civic engagement. Observers will be looking to see if the police will attempt to physically obstruct the marchers or if the state will allow the procession to proceed in acknowledgement of the constitutional protections that remain at the heart of the debate. Future judicial interventions will likely determine whether the notification requirement serves as a bridge for cooperation or a barrier to participation.
