## Vodacom Group Reports Strong Growth as Safaricom Buyout Hits Legal Wall

Vodacom Group has posted a 22.9 percent surge in headline earnings per share for the fiscal year ending March 31, 2026, yet the firm remains unable to finalize its US$2.1 billion acquisition of a further 20 percent stake in Safaricom due to an active court order.

### Capital Flows and The Billion Dollar Standoff

The fiscal year 2026 results from Vodacom reveal a company operating at peak efficiency in most of its footprint. Total EBITDA reached R62.6 billion, buoyed significantly by a 44.5 percent surge in EBITDA from Vodafone Egypt. With free cash flow standing at R21.8 billion and a dividend payout of 735 cents per share, the group is clearly in a position to deploy capital. However, the planned consolidation of its position in Kenya is currently frozen.

The deal, valued at US$2.1 billion, was intended to push Vodacom’s equity stake in Safaricom to 54.93 percent. For the Kenyan Treasury, this transaction represents a critical pillar in its ongoing fiscal roadmap, designed to monetize state-held assets to manage debt obligations. These debt pressures have already surfaced in other sectors, as [Ruto attributes higher fuel prices to infrastructure investment and middle-income status](https://zandani.co.ke/article/ruto-attributes-higher-fuel-prices-to-infrastructure-investment-and-), and the delay in the Vodacom payout leaves a hole in the government’s expected liquidity pool for the current budget cycle.

The market is currently pricing in this uncertainty. Safaricom shares have traded within a narrow range as institutional investors await clarity on when or if the government’s divestment will proceed. The legal challenge, which has triggered a conservatory order, effectively traps the capital flow that would have otherwise signaled a long-term commitment from the South African parent company to the Kenyan market.

### Market Context and Strategic Uncertainty

Vodacom CEO Shameel Joosub has successfully pivoted the company toward high-growth international markets, mitigating the financial headwinds seen in the South African segment. While the company has cleared major legacy issues, such as the November 2025 settlement of the Kenneth Makate matter, the Kenyan court action introduces a new, localized regulatory risk.

For Kenyan consumers, this standoff creates a period of corporate limbo. Safaricom remains the dominant player in M-Pesa and data provision, but the ownership structure is now the subject of intense scrutiny. The government’s intent to divest contrasts with the constitutional and legal hurdles raised by those seeking to block the transaction. This tug-of-war affects local equity liquidity, as the planned entry of a larger foreign stake often leads to broader institutional interest, which is currently stalled. Investors are increasingly wary that prolonged litigation could force the government to rethink its divestment strategy or seek alternative, less lucrative buyers if the current terms become untenable.

### Why it matters now

The 22.9 percent growth in headline earnings per share serves as a benchmark for the scale of Vodacom’s ambition. It demonstrates that the business is healthy and prepared for expansion, making the blockage in Nairobi the primary bottleneck to the group’s regional strategy. If the deal fails to materialize, the government must find alternative revenue streams to balance the fiscal year, potentially placing further strain on public spending or local borrowing requirements.

### What to watch next

The immediate focus for the market is the May 18, 2026, hearing. This court date is a critical trigger for both Safaricom’s share price and the government’s treasury planning. Investors should monitor whether the court upholds the conservatory order or provides a path for the US$2.1 billion transaction to move forward. The outcome will dictate the timeline for the next major shareholder filing from Safaricom and provide a clearer signal on the stability of the telecommunications ownership structure in Kenya.
