## Understanding the Finance Bill 2026 and Your Wallet

As Kenya debates the latest fiscal proposals, a major law firm has flagged serious concerns about the Finance Bill 2026. While the discussion sounds like a boardroom matter, these tax changes carry direct consequences for your daily spending and the cost of basic services.

### Will your mobile money and internet costs rise?

One of the most significant points raised in the critique concerns taxes on digital financial services. The government is looking for new ways to capture revenue from the growing fintech sector. However, when companies face higher administrative hurdles and new levies, those costs rarely stay within the company balance sheet. They are typically passed down to the end user. If the tax on digital services increases, Kenyans who rely on platforms like M-Pesa for daily transactions or pay for monthly internet bundles could see those service fees edge higher. The concern is that if the cost of moving money or staying connected becomes too expensive, it discourages digital adoption, which is the engine of the modern Kenyan economy.

### How does taxing undistributed profits affect company health?

The Finance Bill proposes new tax measures on undistributed profits. For a regular citizen, this might sound like a distant corporate issue, but it is actually about business survival. When companies retain profits, they use that money to expand, hire more staff, or weather economic downturns. If the government taxes these reserves too aggressively, companies lose their liquidity. This creates a ripple effect. A business that lacks the cash flow to grow will likely freeze hiring or reduce its service quality to maintain profitability. In an economy where job creation is already a major hurdle, reducing the ability of businesses to reinvest in themselves can slow down economic momentum for everyone.

### Why is the rental income tax a concern for tenants?

The proposal to increase taxes on rental income has sparked worry among property owners and tenants alike. When the government tightens the tax net on landlords, the immediate response from property owners is often to adjust rent prices upward to maintain their margins. For a Kenyan tenant already struggling with the rising cost of living, this is a direct threat. If the tax burden on landlords becomes too heavy, the cost of housing will inevitably climb, pushing affordable living further out of reach for urban dwellers. It is a cycle where policy meant to collect revenue ends up adding to the financial burden of the average household.

### Why it matters now

This is not just about tax law. It is about how the cost of living is structured. Just as motorists recently felt the sting of poor infrastructure on University Way, where lack of oversight led to dangerous road conditions, these tax measures represent a fiscal "trench" for ordinary Kenyans. If the government proceeds without considering how these costs trickle down to the person on the street, the result will be a tighter squeeze on disposable income. 

### What to watch next

The focus now shifts to the public participation phase of the legislative process. Citizens should watch how the Kenya Revenue Authority and Treasury respond to these institutional critiques. Lawmakers will soon decide whether to push forward with these aggressive measures or refine them to protect consumer affordability. While some of these adjustments seem minor on paper, their cumulative effect on your monthly budget is substantial.

When corporations face higher taxes, the consumer usually pays the final bill.
