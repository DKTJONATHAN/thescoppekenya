During a consultative meeting regarding the Social Health Authority rollout held this week, activist Gabriel Oguda was abruptly silenced by a senior government official. The incident occurred while Oguda attempted to present technical questions and public grievances regarding the new national health transition.

## The theater of stakeholder participation

### A script written in advance
The forum was intended to provide a platform for dialogue concerning the transition to the new health insurance framework. However, the atmosphere soured when the activist began highlighting specific inconsistencies in the proposed policy. Instead of addressing the substantive concerns regarding the operational capacity of the fund, the official intervened with a dismissive gesture. By stating, "Aah! Aah! With all due respect," the official effectively halted the critique, preventing further inquiry and demonstrating that there was no genuine space for dissenting expert opinion.

### The irony of forced inclusion
This confrontation highlights a recurring trend where public consultations function as mere rubber stamps for pre-determined government policies. Although officials frame these events as exercises in transparency, the reality suggests that criticism is tolerated only when it remains superficial. By silencing active participants, the state reveals that these forums are designed to maintain appearances rather than address the structural failures that threaten the livelihoods of ordinary citizens. The episode indicates that engagement is often restricted to a controlled environment where the outcome is settled before the discussion begins.

### When the stage becomes a cage
The public suppression of Oguda serves as a stark warning to those seeking a meaningful seat at the policymaking table. It exposes a political culture where officials prioritize the protection of their narratives over the practical concerns of those most affected by their decisions. While the government promotes its various modernization initiatives, the human element of public policy remains ignored. Genuine participation is being sacrificed for a performance where the script is written and controlled entirely by those in power, leaving little room for the public to influence the actual direction of the program.
