## Matatu Drivers Say Ksh10 Fuel Drop is a Ghost Benefit

Drivers and conductors across Kenya are threatening to park their vehicles again, claiming that the recent government-brokered fuel price reduction only benefits vehicle owners while leaving the actual workers to struggle with unmanageable daily targets and stagnant wages.

The mood at bus parks in Nairobi and Mombasa has shifted from cautious hope to open defiance as transport workers accuse the government of negotiating behind their backs. For the men and women who spend twelve hours a day on the road, the recent ten shilling drop in diesel prices feels more like a PR stunt than real relief. They argue that while the President and vehicle owners shook hands in Mombasa last week, the people who actually handle the cash and the steering wheels were left standing in the cold. There is a growing sense that the street-level economy is being ignored in favor of high-level political optics.

This fresh wave of anger follows a press briefing on Wednesday, May 27, where the Matatu Workers Union and the Long Distance Drivers and Conductors Association (LODCA) officially issued a strike notice. The workers are protesting their exclusion from the negotiations that saw diesel prices dip from Ksh 242.92 to Ksh 232.86 per litre. Although a previous nationwide strike was called off on May 22 after a State House meeting in Mombasa, the unions now say that agreement is hollow because it lacks worker representation. They point out that despite Nairobi Governor Johnson Sakaja promising inclusive talks back on May 19, the final deal ignored the economic welfare of the crew members who are still forced to hit daily targets as high as Ksh 8,000 before they can earn a cent for themselves.

### Voices from the Stage

Maurice Oduor, a vocal leader for the workers, says that the Mombasa negotiations were a private club for the bosses that ignored the very people who keep the industry moving. He believes that the Ksh 10 reduction is a drop in the ocean that will not change the daily reality for a driver who still has to pay a standing fee of Ksh 46 per trip regardless of the pump price. Oduor insists that unless the government brings the actual workers to the table to discuss wages and working conditions, any deal remains a paper-only solution that does not reflect the reality of the Kenyan road.

In the city center, the sentiment is even more blunt among those who feel the pinch at the pump every morning. One veteran driver on the Rongai route noted that when fuel prices go up, the vehicle owners immediately raise the daily targets, but when prices drop by a small margin, those targets never come down. He explained that a ten-shilling difference is ghost money because it is swallowed up by the vehicle owner's profit margin before the conductor even sees a coin. For him, the strike is not just about fuel but about a system that treats drivers as replaceable parts rather than partners in the economy. ### The Road Ahead

The threat of a nationwide work stoppage now looms over the transport sector, with unions warning that the suspension of the May 22 strike is officially over. They are demanding an immediate seat at the table with the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA) and the Ministry of Transport to discuss a pricing and wage structure that benefits the entire ecosystem. If the government does not respond to this notice with a concrete plan for inclusion, Kenyans can expect empty stages and grounded buses by the end of the week. The focus now shifts back to State House and City Hall to see if the promised inclusivity will finally materialize or if the country will grind to a halt.

The engines are running, but the patience of the Kenyan driver is finally idling on empty.
