## KTDA Hydropower Projects and the Billion-Shilling Drain

The Office of the Director of Public Prosecutions has formally demanded a status update from the Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission regarding stalled energy projects. At the center of this inquiry lies Ksh978 million in missing funds, directly sourced from the hard-earned monthly dividends of smallholder tea farmers.

### From Dividends to Stalled Infrastructure

For the tea farmers of Kericho and Bomet, the promise of the KTDA Power Company was framed as a path toward energy independence. The narrative was simple: by investing a portion of their bonus payments into hydropower, these cooperatives would lower their processing costs and increase long-term profitability. In reality, the vision of generating 2.6 megawatts of power has become a cautionary tale of financial exposure. 

The financial burden on individual farmers was not transparent. Instead of being presented as a voluntary investment, contributions were often treated as non-negotiable deductions from their monthly green leaf deliveries. This effectively converted the earnings of families living in rural highlands into a private investment vehicle for infrastructure projects that never reached operational status. While corporate entities maintained structural control over these investments, the financial risk remained squarely on the backs of the small-scale producers. If a family is struggling to pay school fees or manage home maintenance, as seen in wider economic shifts like those impacting the stability of [Nairobi’s rental market](https://zandani.co.ke/article/spotting-the-deal-breakers-essential-red-flags-to-watch-for-in-nairo), the loss of these dividends represents a significant hit to household liquidity.

### A Timeline of Abandoned Ambition

The trajectory of these energy projects follows a familiar pattern in the Kenyan public sector. What began as an ambitious development plan has devolved into a cycle of inquiries and finger-pointing.

*   2018-2019: The initial project phase saw aggressive mobilization of farmer funds, with KTDA Power Company leveraging tea bonuses to break ground on the Settet and Kipsonoi hydropower sites.
*   2021-2023: Construction at the Chemosit River site slowed to a crawl. Procurement irregularities emerged, and site audits began to reveal that the cost of equipment and labor did not align with the progress on the ground.
*   2024-2026: After formal complaints were filed by Senator Aaron Cheruiyot regarding the misappropriation of funds, the project sites have largely been abandoned. The current ODPP directive represents the latest attempt to force accountability for the nearly one billion shillings that vanished during the construction process.

### Why It Matters Now

The situation is a clear case of redirected capital. When large-scale organizations leverage the funds of smallholder farmers to enter complex energy markets, the farmers rarely have a seat at the board level to oversee how those billions are spent. As the cost of agricultural inputs continues to rise, the absence of these bonuses puts a tangible strain on local economies in the Rift Valley. This is not just a story about technical procurement failures or administrative friction between the EACC and the ODPP. It is a story about the diversion of rural wealth into a financial vacuum. Just as students and parents await clarity on [university placement](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kuccps-clarifies-university-placement-criteria-and-role-of-cut-off-p), these farmers are left waiting for a return on an investment that may never deliver.

### What to Watch Next

The EACC is now under pressure to provide a forensic audit of the Ksh978 million. The core issue remains whether the commission can trace the flow of cash from the farmers to the contractors and identify where the chain of oversight was broken. If the investigation stalls again, it will reinforce the sentiment that smallholder contributions are treated as a slush fund rather than a protected asset. Expect more demands for public disclosure regarding the specific procurement contracts signed during the peak of the construction phase.

Institutional memory in Kenya is short, but the bill for bad management is always paid by the public.
