By Celestine Nzioka The National Transport and Safety Authority (NTSA) will review its 21-year smart driving licence production agreement. Director General Nashon Kondiwa pledged this on Thursday, April 9, 2026, before the National Assembly's Public Debt and Privatisation Committee, amid mounting parliamentary concerns over the deal’s revenue-sharing model.

Parliamentary Scrutiny of PPP The deal, signed between NTSA and a private consortium led by PesaPrint, has attracted sharp criticism from Members of Parliament (MPs) for what they describe as an inequitable revenue-sharing model. Under the current framework, private partners are poised to receive approximately 77 percent of projected revenues, leaving the government with less than a quarter over the contract's duration. Committee chairperson Abdi Shurie, alongside other members, questioned the fairness and transparency of the arrangement, deeming it contrary to public interest. Shurie highlighted that projected revenues over 21 years stand at approximately Ksh900 billion against costs of Ksh300 billion, representing a 300 percent profit margin for the private entity. Hon. Daudi further expressed dismay, stating that "Seventy-seven per cent going to a private entity for 21 years makes no sense."

NTSA's Justification and Funding Constraints Director General Kondiwa defended the adoption of a Public-Private Partnership (PPP) model, attributing the shift to persistent funding constraints from the National Treasury. He acknowledged NTSA's disadvantaged position during negotiations, stating, "We are not even negotiating our own money; we are negotiating our services and the revenue is going elsewhere." Kondiwa explained that the previous fully government-funded system hampered NTSA's ability to meet demand, resulting in the issuance of only 2.7 million licences over nearly a decade, significantly below its five million target. This challenge underscores broader governmental financial pressures; for instance, Trade Cabinet Secretary Moses Kuria has previously warned of potential fuel supply shocks and rationing, illustrating the pervasive nature of resource allocation issues impacting public services across various sectors.

Consortium Details and Future Review The consortium behind the smart driving licence deal reportedly includes a prominent bank in the country, which acquired one of the oldest banks, leading MPs to question why a deal comparable to national identification production was entrusted to private entities. Kondiwa conceded that NTSA may have been at a disadvantage during negotiations and committed to re-engaging stakeholders to consider necessary adjustments to the agreement's architecture. The initial phase of the project, valued at an estimated Sh42 billion, is expected to be financed entirely through private debt and equity over the first two to three years. The PPP, which involves KCB Bank Kenya Limited and Pesa Print Limited, aims to modernize driver licensing, enhance traffic law enforcement, and improve road safety. The project also includes the deployment of 700 fixed speed cameras and 300 mobile units, linked to a National Command and Control Centre for real-time monitoring and automated fines. Motorists will pay Sh3,000 for the issuance, renewal, or replacement of each smart driving licence.
