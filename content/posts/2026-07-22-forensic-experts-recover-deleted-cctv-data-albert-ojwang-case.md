## Digital Forensics Expose Surveillance Gaps at Nairobi Central

Forensic investigators operating under the Independent Policing Oversight Authority (IPOA) have successfully recovered deleted surveillance footage from Nairobi Central Police Station, uncovering a 700GB discrepancy in the logs surrounding the death of Albert Ojwang. This recovery process exposes a significant gap between the data officially provided by the station and the actual storage capacity utilized by its security hardware.

The recovery centers on the inherent mechanics of digital storage. Surveillance systems typically maintain footage on physical drives until the device overwrites older files with new information. While the station initially reported that only 100GB of active, accessible footage existed, forensic analysis revealed the hardware held nearly 800GB of archival data. By deploying specialized software to bypass standard interface deletion protocols, the investigative team accessed files that had been purged from the system view but remained physically present on the disks.

This technical intervention shifts the Ojwang inquiry from testimony toward objective digital evidence. The 700GB gap suggests a deliberate effort to obscure station activity during the relevant timeframe. For the oversight authority, this discrepancy serves as a critical indicator of institutional obstruction. The investigation is no longer dependent on human accounts, but rather on the immutable data recorded by the storage hardware itself.

### The Mechanism of Data Persistence

The retrieval of this evidence hinges on the distinction between file deletion and data erasure. Removing a file through standard interface protocols typically deletes the index pointer that directs the operating system to the file location, but it leaves the underlying binary data untouched. Unless the station server performed a comprehensive disk wipe or allowed new data to overwrite those specific sectors, the evidence remained in a state of suspended animation.

Technical experts involved in the recovery confirmed that this persistent data provides a comprehensive timeline, which stands in direct contradiction to the fragmented logs originally turned over to the IPOA. This finding places immense pressure on station management to justify why seven-eighths of the recorded history was deleted while the raw data remained on the drives. Authorities are now scrutinizing the digital chain of custody to identify exactly who issued the deletion commands and at what specific time those instructions were executed.

### Implications for Institutional Transparency

This development marks a significant shift in Za Ndanis of civilian oversight within Kenya. The application of sophisticated forensic tools to law enforcement infrastructure represents an escalation in the methods used to hold public institutions accountable. The disparity between reported data and physical evidence acts as a digital fingerprint, revealing administrative decisions that were intended to remain concealed.

The findings have forced a direct confrontation between the oversight authority and the Nairobi station command. With the recovered files currently undergoing independent analysis, the claim that the footage was accidentally lost or unavailable has been effectively neutralized. The digital footprint left by staff now serves as a roadmap for the ongoing inquiry. 

As the investigation progresses, the focus is shifting toward the individual accountability of those who managed the deletion attempt. Legal teams are preparing to cross-reference the recovered metadata with official duty rosters to establish a precise timeline of personnel presence. The IPOA intends to present the restored footage as the centerpiece of their formal findings, a move likely to trigger secondary internal audits across other regional command centers. By utilizing forensic verification, the oversight body is establishing a precedent where surveillance integrity is no longer a matter of administrative discretion but a baseline requirement for public safety operations. This case serves as a warning that in a digital era, the destruction of evidence is increasingly difficult to achieve without leaving an enduring trail for investigators to follow.
