## New KCC and the Financial Fog Over Your Breakfast Table

Why should a KSh 953 million loss at a state dairy corporation matter to the person buying a packet of milk in Nairobi or the farmer milking cows in the Rift Valley? The answer lies in your monthly budget.

The state dairy corporation is facing a deep financial crisis that stretches far beyond boardrooms and government ministries. With millions in unpaid dues to farmers and a missing reserve fund, the company is struggling to keep the country's milk supply chain moving. For the average Kenyan, this instability is not just a headline; it is a signal that the price and availability of one of our most essential staples could be headed for a period of uncertainty.

### Where did the milk money go?

At the heart of the current trouble is the Auditor General's discovery of a missing KSh 3.17 billion. This money was meant to be a milk mop-up reserve, a vital buffer the government uses to buy excess milk during periods of high production. By holding this reserve, the state can keep prices steady for consumers and prevent farm gate prices from crashing when supply is high.

When that money goes missing from the books, the corporation loses its ability to intervene in the market. Without this cushion, we see the volatility that hits our pockets. If the corporation cannot effectively manage the supply chain, the immediate impact is felt at the retail shelf where prices often climb to compensate for operational inefficiencies. When the state lacks the liquidity to stabilize the market, the consumer is usually the one who pays the final cost.

### Why are farmers waiting for their pay?

The dairy sector runs on trust. When a farmer delivers milk to the New KCC, they expect timely payment to cover the costs of animal feed, veterinary services, and labor. Right now, there is an outstanding KSh 4.5 billion owed to these farmers. This is not just a statistic; it represents thousands of rural families who are currently unable to reinvest in their herds.

When farmers are not paid, they stop spending on high-quality feed. This leads to a decline in milk volume and, more importantly, a decline in quality. Over the last year, the corporation has struggled to meet its procurement targets of 120 million liters, managing to secure significantly less. As supply drops, the cost of processing what remains rises, placing additional upward pressure on the price of milk in your local duka.

### How does the privatization debate create a standstill?

There is a loud public debate about whether the government should privatize the entity. While ministers provide conflicting signals, the corporation remains in a state of paralysis. This wait and see attitude prevents long term investment in cooling plants and transport logistics.

When management is unsure if the entity will exist in its current form by next year, they rarely make the bold decisions needed to modernize operations. Instead, we see trade payables ballooning to nearly KSh 4 billion. This is a classic case of policy indecision leading to operational decay. Just as we saw when [landlords were reminded of their legal limits regarding tenant lockouts](https://zandani.co.ke/article/lawyer-danstan-omari-says-landlords-cannot-legally-lock-out-tenants-), business operations require clear rules and consistent management to remain fair to all parties. Without clarity on ownership, the infrastructure that keeps our dairy supply chain robust is slowly rusting.

### Why it matters now

The stability of the dairy sector is the backbone of food security for many working households. When the state corporation struggles to balance its books, it loses its leverage to shield the public from market shocks. If the liquidity crisis continues, the corporation will be unable to play its role as the primary buyer for smallholder farmers, potentially forcing many to exit the industry entirely.

### What to watch next

Keep a close eye on the government's next capital injection. If the state continues to pour in grants to cover operating losses rather than fixing the underlying accounting discrepancies, the price of processed dairy products will likely remain high. Additionally, watch for any concrete policy decisions regarding the future of the entity. A clear direction from the ministry is the only way to end the current atmosphere of uncertainty that is stifling farm gate production and unsettling the market.
