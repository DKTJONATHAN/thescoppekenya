## The Price of Relief: Why Kenyans are Wary of the Tariff Freeze

Kenyans are reacting to the Ministry of Energy and Petroleum’s decision to block the latest electricity tariff hike with a mix of immediate relief and lingering economic anxiety. While the cost of living remains a primary concern for households, many fear this reprieve is merely a temporary patch on a grid system struggling with aging infrastructure and mounting operational debts.

The government officially halted the retail electricity tariff review application submitted by Kenya Power today, effectively cancelling the planned adjustments that were set to impact consumer bills. Energy Cabinet Secretary Opiyo Wandayi confirmed the move, noting that the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority will no longer proceed with the public participation exercises that were intended to justify these rate increases. This intervention stops a process that would have seen power costs climb above the current levels, a relief for many who are already reeling from high monthly electricity expenditures.

### Voices from the Streets

For Juma, a trader operating a welding shop in Muthurwa Market, the news is a double-edged sword. He admits that any price hike would have forced him to cut production hours, yet he worries about the reliability of the supply. He says that if the power stays cheap but flickers or cuts out every other day, his business still loses money. He would prefer to pay a bit more if it meant the lights stayed on consistently so he could finish his evening orders without relying on expensive backup generators.

Economist Samuel Kiptoo notes that while the government’s intervention acts as a necessary shield against inflation, it does not erase the underlying fiscal reality of the energy sector. He points out that the current tariff structure often falls short of covering the maintenance costs required for a robust grid. By deferring these costs, the state is essentially kicking the can down the road, creating a scenario where infrastructure decay might lead to more frequent and prolonged blackouts. He warns that the lack of revenue growth for the utility could eventually compromise the quality of service, leaving consumers with stable rates but increasingly unstable power delivery.

In the informal settlements of Gikomba, small business owners who rely on electricity for refrigeration and lighting express a cautious optimism. One shopkeeper mentions that she has already adjusted her inventory to account for the current expensive rates. While she is glad she does not have to worry about a bill increase next month, she questions whether this is a genuine change in policy or just a tactic to keep public frustration levels down. She is more interested in seeing [M-Pesa transactions hide customer numbers by default](https://zandani.co.ke/article/m-pesa-transactions-hide-customer-numbers-by-default) than she is in promises of long-term energy sustainability, focusing instead on the daily struggle to keep her small store profitable amid rising operational costs.

### The Sustainability Trap

The government’s decision creates a clear divide between immediate consumer relief and the financial sustainability of the power grid. Investors and industry stakeholders are watching closely, as the move signals a potential shift toward tighter state control over pricing mechanisms. The concern is that if the utility is not allowed to adjust its rates to reflect its real costs, the quality of maintenance will decline, eventually requiring a massive bailout or a sudden, sharp price correction later.

While the current move protects consumers from an immediate rise in bills, it does nothing to address the structural deficits within the power sector. The challenge now lies in how the ministry plans to balance the political necessity of keeping energy affordable with the technical requirement of funding grid improvements. Without a clear plan to bridge this gap, many fear the current reprieve will only lead to more severe energy service failures in the future. As the country balances these competing interests, the public mood remains one of cautious relief held in check by the memory of previous promises that failed to deliver lasting stability.
