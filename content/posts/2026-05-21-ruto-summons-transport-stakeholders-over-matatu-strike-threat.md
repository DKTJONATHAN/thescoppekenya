## Why Is President Ruto Calling Matatu Operators to Mombasa?

The transport sector is at a standstill as the government faces off with matatu owners over fuel costs. President Ruto has summoned industry leaders to Mombasa today to bridge a widening gap between official diesel pricing and the operational reality of public transit.

If you have been wondering why your daily commute remains expensive despite government announcements about fuel price adjustments, the answer lies in a specific tug of war over a Ksh 46 margin. While the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority has implemented minor shifts, the industry argues these moves do not reflect the true cost of keeping a vehicle on the road.

### What is the specific math behind the Ksh 46 demand?

The tension currently rests on the price of diesel, which sits at Ksh 232.86 per litre following the latest regulatory updates. Operators are pushing for a reduction of Ksh 46 per litre, arguing that the government-backed decrease of Ksh 10.06 is insufficient to cover rising operational overheads. 

For the average matatu owner, this Ksh 46 gap is not just a rounding error. It represents the difference between a profitable day and a loss that threatens vehicle maintenance schedules and staff wages. The operators maintain that current fuel costs force them to keep passenger fares artificially high, creating a cycle where the commuter pays for the inefficiency of the fuel pricing model. The Mombasa negotiation table is being framed as the only place where these raw cost figures can be reconciled against the national budget realities.

### Why do transport stakeholders reject current price adjustments?

Industry leaders argue that the current pricing model ignores the reality of vehicle maintenance and rising costs of spare parts. While policymakers often look at global logistics, matatu operators are looking at the daily drain on their bank accounts. When an operator fills a tank, that Ksh 46 difference adds up to thousands of shillings in lost revenue every week. 

This is the primary reason the industry remains skeptical of the government's current offer. For them, until the fuel price accounts for the true cost of doing business, they remain unwilling to lower their own fare structures for passengers. This disconnect is why previous attempts at mediation failed, leading to the threat of a total nationwide strike that would paralyze movement across major cities and towns.

### Why it matters now

The situation is critical because the stability of Kenya's economy relies on the flow of goods and people. If the meeting in Mombasa fails to produce a compromise, a nationwide strike would disrupt everything from school attendance to the delivery of produce to urban markets. Recent history, such as the tragic events seen in the Ruiru motorcycle protests, highlights how quickly tensions in the transport sector can escalate into public disorder. The cost of fuel is directly linked to the cost of living, and every extra shilling spent on a matatu fare is a shilling taken away from a household's food or utility budget.

### What to watch next

The key indicator of success will be whether the government offers a direct subsidy or tax relief that brings the effective cost of diesel closer to the industry's target. If no middle ground is found, the one week pause on strike action will expire, potentially triggering a shutdown. Keep an eye on any announcements regarding specific tax concessions on petroleum products or changes to road maintenance levies, as these are the tools President Ruto is most likely to use to appease the transport unions without completely dismantling the existing fuel pricing framework.

The debate is not just about fuel prices, but about who bears the burden of the cost of living crisis in the transport sector.
