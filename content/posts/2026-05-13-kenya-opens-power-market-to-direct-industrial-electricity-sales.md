## Kenya Energy Reform and the End of the Power Monopoly

The Kenyan government has gazetted regulations allowing electricity producers to sell directly to industrial consumers. This historic shift ends the decades-long monopoly held by Kenya Power, introducing an open access system that fundamentally restructures how the nation's grid generates and distributes revenue.

### The Decoupling of Generation and Distribution

The Gazettement of the Energy (Electricity Market, Bulk Supply, and Open Access) Regulations on May 8, 2026, marks the end of the single-buyer model. For over half a century, Kenya Power acted as the mandatory middleman between generators and consumers. Under the new framework, the utility transitions into a common carrier. Independent power producers and state owned KenGen, which accounts for 59 percent of the country's supply, can now bypass the utility to sign direct contracts with large scale buyers.

Eligibility for this direct market is currently restricted to high volume users. Those on the distribution network must meet a minimum threshold of 1 MVA, while those on the transmission network require at least 10 MVA. These direct supply contracts, regulated by the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA), will have durations ranging from one to ten years. This move follows a global pattern of liberalizing energy markets to lower the cost of doing business for heavy industry.

### The Fiscal Threat of the Cross-Subsidization Gap

While the move is a victory for manufacturers, it exposes a significant vulnerability in the national grid's financial structure. Historically, Kenya Power has relied on a cross-subsidization model where a small number of industrial clients effectively fund the connection and maintenance costs for millions of residential homes. In the year ended June 2025, industrial and commercial consumption reached 7,313 gigawatt-hours. This segment accounts for 70 percent of Kenya Power's total sales revenue despite representing a fraction of the total customer base.

As these high value clients migrate to direct deals with producers to secure lower rates, the pool of funds used to insulate residential consumers from the true cost of electricity will shrink. This fiscal pressure comes at a time when the government is tightening its belt. Recently, [Treasury CS John Mbadi rules out new taxes as government targets revenue collection efficiency](https://zandani.co.ke/article/treasury-cs-john-mbadi-rules-out-new-taxes-as-government-targets-rev) to manage the national budget. Without state intervention or new taxes to cover the utility's operational costs, the burden of grid upkeep will likely shift toward the domestic consumer.

### Transitioning to a Wheeling Revenue Model

To mitigate the loss of retail margins, Kenya Power will now rely on "wheeling" fees. This is a toll-like system where third party producers pay to use the existing national grid infrastructure to move their power to their private clients. EPRA is tasked with setting these fees to ensure they are high enough to maintain the transmission lines but low enough to remain attractive for private trade. 

The transition mirrors the deregulation of the telecommunications sector decades ago. In those instances, the incumbent utility often struggled to maintain its legacy infrastructure while its most profitable customers fled to leaner, more efficient competitors. If the wheeling fees do not cover the shortfall left by the disappearing 70 percent industrial revenue share, a significant tariff hike for ordinary households becomes mathematically inevitable.

### Why it matters now
The deregulation allows manufacturers to negotiate cheaper, more reliable power, which could lower the cost of locally produced goods. However, it also creates an immediate revenue risk for the national utility. With the "anchor tenants" of the grid moving out, the financial stability of the entire energy value chain is now under scrutiny.

### What to watch next
The first major Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) between KenGen and Special Economic Zones will serve as the benchmark for future pricing. Analysts are also monitoring the upcoming EPRA tariff review cycles to see how quickly the loss of industrial margins translates into higher residential bills. The ability of Kenya Power to collect wheeling fees efficiently will be critical.

History shows that when the biggest payers leave a collective system, those remaining must pay more for the same service.
