## Finance Bill 2026 Kenya Targets Digital Economy for Tax Revenue

The Kenya Treasury and Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA) have introduced the Finance Bill 2026 Kenya, proposing extensive tax reforms for the nation’s rapidly expanding digital economy. These new rules, effective with the bill’s enactment, aim to integrate virtual assets, online platforms, and electronic transactions more firmly into the national tax framework, seeking to broaden the tax base.

### Virtual Asset Taxation Framework

The Finance Bill 2026 Kenya establishes a formal recognition and taxation framework for virtual assets, including cryptocurrencies and digital tokens. Virtual asset service providers, such as exchanges and trading platforms, will now face mandates to disclose comprehensive user and transaction data to tax authorities. This measure aligns with global efforts to regulate digital financial activities, similar to the broader financial scrutiny seen in cases like the recent police action against Nairobi Governor Johnson Sakaja for alleged contempt of Senate.

### Information Reporting and Penalties

Under the proposed legislation, virtual asset service providers must file annual information returns with KRA. These returns require details of reportable users and individuals with controlling interests, significantly altering how crypto activities are monitored. Furthermore, providers facilitating trading platforms or exchange transactions must furnish complete operational records. Non-compliant firms face severe penalties, including fines up to Ksh100,000 per false statement or three years imprisonment for submitting false information, and penalties up to Ksh1 million for failing to file returns. The Bill also enables Kenya to engage in international agreements for automatic information exchange on virtual assets, enhancing cross-border tracking and countering tax evasion.

### Expanded Definition of Royalties

The Treasury also seeks to expand the definition of royalties to encompass fees earned through digital platforms and payment systems. This means income from payment networks, card schemes, clearing systems, and digital processing platforms will now be classified as taxable royalty income. This broadened definition includes payment gateways, switching systems, and settlement platforms, effectively widening the tax base to include more technology-driven financial services and reflecting the evolving nature of global commerce, much like the changing dynamics in sports as Ipswich Town faced their Championship final day drama.

### Strengthening Electronic Tax Systems

Proposals within the Bill aim to fortify Kenya’s electronic tax system by allowing the KRA to generate pre-filled tax returns for individuals and businesses, simplifying the filing process. Taxpayers will also be required to fully comply with electronic invoicing, online filing, and digital payment systems. Non-compliance with these electronic tax requirements may result in significant penalties, depending on the violation's nature.

### Oversight of Digital Betting and Gambling

Additionally, the Finance Bill 2026 Kenya tightens oversight of the digital betting and gambling sector. It expands definitions to cover digital deposits and wallet-based betting systems, ensuring winnings from such platforms are explicitly subject to tax under the new regulatory framework.
