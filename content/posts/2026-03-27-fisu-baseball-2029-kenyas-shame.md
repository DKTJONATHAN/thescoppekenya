The International University Sports Federation (FISU) has finally stumbled into the 21st century, confirming baseball and softball for the North Carolina 2029 FISU World University Games. This isn't a grand slam; it's a long-overdue bunt that exposes the rotten core of university sports in Kenya. While FISU congratulates itself, we in Kenya must confront our own pathetic reality.

## FISU Baseball Softball 2029: A Global Game, a Local Farce

Let's be clear: FISU's decision to cement baseball and softball as official sports for the 2029 Games is hardly revolutionary. The World University Baseball Championship has existed since 2002, and softball since 2004, albeit with "bumpy ride with consistent participation". Baseball even popped up as an optional sport way back in the 90s. So, this isn't innovation; it's the bare minimum. FISU President Leonz Eder and WBSC President Riccardo Fraccari patting themselves on the back for "reinforcing collaboration" and a "major step forward" is frankly laughable. They're moving at a glacial pace while our own sports administrators remain entirely comatose. The North Carolina 2029 Games will bring together thousands of student-athletes from 150 countries, a spectacle that should shame our Kenyan universities into action, but likely won't.

### The Shame of Kenya University Baseball's Barren Fields

While the world gears up for North Carolina 2029, the state of university sports kenya baseball and softball is nothing short of a national disgrace. Our universities, largely obsessed with a handful of 'traditional' sports, have actively neglected the potential of these diamond games. For decades, ambitious student-athletes have struggled with non-existent facilities and zero institutional support. This isn't just a missed opportunity; it's a cruel betrayal of young talent. The tragic fact that as recently as 2022, Kenya boasted only one regulation baseball field, a six-hour drive from the capital, speaks volumes about the utter incompetence of our sports ministry and university chancellors. They trumpet academic excellence but consistently fail to foster holistic development through diverse sports. Their myopia is criminal.

### JKUAT: A Lone, Blazing Beacon in the Darkness

Amidst this wasteland, one institution shines with a rare, defiant brilliance: Jomo Kenyatta University of Agriculture and Technology (JKUAT). They are not merely participating; they are pioneering. JKUAT hosts one of the nation's most active baseball and softball programs, with the majority of our national teams comprising their very own students. This isn't accidental; it's the result of foresight and genuine commitment. JKUAT has even set aside a staggering 10 acres for a "Nairobi Field of Dreams" complex, a visionary project poised to become an East African epicenter for the sport. This magnificent effort, supported by partners like IfWeBuildIt.org and the Nairobi American Buffaloes, is a direct rebuke to every other university administration content to sit on their hands. When JKUAT hosted the Nairobi Baseball Community Championship Festival in September 2025, it wasn't just a game; it was a thunderous declaration that proper investment yields tangible results. Titus Mutwiri, President of the Baseball Federation of Kenya, rightly lauded JKUAT's "instrumental role". This is precisely the kind of bold, proactive leadership that should be mandatory, not exceptional, for kenya university baseball fisu aspirations. Read more about this initiative in the original piece: University Baseball & Softball: A Proper Home Run for FISU!

### KUSA and Federations: A Whisper, Not a Roar

The Kenya University Sports Association (KUSA) and our national federations, the Kenya Softball Federation (SFK) and Baseball Federation of Kenya, are undoubtedly doing their best with scraps. The SFK, established in 1996, runs national leagues, and universities like Kenyatta University, Masinde Muliro University of Science and Technology (MMUST), Multimedia University, and Dedan Kimathi University of Technology are participating in their 2025/26 season. MMUST's women's softball team even clinched second place in the Kenya Softball Federation National series in November 2024, qualifying for KUSA Nationals playoffs. These are commendable individual efforts. Softball has been "well-received as a main sport in the Kenya University games calendar", and Baseball5 is being promoted as an accessible entry point. But let’s be honest, these are mere whispers against a backdrop of national apathy. The federations, led by individuals like Titus Mutwiri and Francis Njeru Karugu, are constantly fighting uphill battles. They need to turn these whispers into a deafening roar, demanding proper resources and strategic national support for kenyan university softball teams and their baseball counterparts. Their collaboration on Baseball5 is smart, but it's a stopgap, not a solution.

### FISU Opportunities Kenyan Students Deserve, But Don't Get

The tragic irony is that while FISU champions global university sports, the vast majority of fisu opportunities kenyan students could seize remain locked behind institutional neglect. Kenya is already preparing for the 2026 FISU World University Championship Cross Country, proving we *can* engage. Yet, our student-athletes capable of excelling in baseball and softball are left to rot because our educational and sports bodies refuse to invest. Imagine the international exposure, the scholarships, the sheer pride of seeing Kenyan university teams competing on the world stage at the North Carolina 2029 Games. This is not some abstract dream; it’s a tangible benefit stolen from our youth by the inertia and short-sightedness of those in power.

The time for polite requests is over. The government, through its toothless Sports Ministry, must immediately develop a comprehensive national strategy for university sports diversification. University administrations must be forced, by hook or by crook, to invest in infrastructure, coaching, and talent identification for sports beyond the usual suspects. JKUAT has shown the way; now the rest must follow, or face public shaming for their continued dereliction of duty. We demand action, not just more empty promises.
