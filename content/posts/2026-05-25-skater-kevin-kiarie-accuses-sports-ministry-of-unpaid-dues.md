## Roller skating star Kevin Kiarie disputes state funding pledges

Professional roller skater Kevin Kiarie has publicly challenged the Ministry of Sports over a significant shortfall in promised financial support for his international competitions, citing a documented deficit of KSh 1.91 million that remains unsettled by government officials today.

The dispute centres on a pledged total package of KSh 2.78 million, intended to cover the operational costs of international representation. Documentation provided by Kiarie indicates that the athlete has only received KSh 870,000 to date. This leaves the skater struggling to reconcile the disparity between the initial government commitment and the actual funds disbursed. The shortfall exposes a recurring systemic failure where non-traditional sports disciplines are left in a precarious financial position due to vague administrative protocols regarding state-sponsored travel and competition expenses.

### The systemic funding bottleneck

The conflict highlights the fragility of the current sports funding framework for minority disciplines in Kenya. Unlike football or athletics, which often benefit from established budgetary lines and consistent sponsorship models, roller skating operates in a policy gray area. The existing guidelines for disbursement lack a robust mechanism to track pledged support against actual expenditure. Consequently, athletes like Kiarie are forced to front their own costs while waiting for reimbursements that are frequently delayed or partially stalled by bureaucratic hurdles.

The Ministry of Sports maintains no public portal for athletes to track the status of their approved grants or request mediation when pledges are not met. This absence of a formal dispute resolution channel leaves competitors with little recourse beyond public litigation or social media appeals. The KSh 1.91 million deficit is not merely a debt to an individual but a signifier of how the current fiscal structure prioritizes visibility over the actual development of diverse sports talent. As stakeholders examine the state of youth engagement, the focus on administrative transparency becomes as critical as [KNEC opening online registration for Grade 10 assessment centres](https://zandani.co.ke/article/knec-opens-grade-10-assessment-centre-registration), where policy clarity remains the backbone of institutional success.

### Administrative silence and the accountability gap

Government representatives have remained silent regarding the discrepancies in Kiarie’s account. No official statement has been released to explain the missing funds or to provide a timeline for the settlement of the outstanding balance. This silence is common when ministry departments face scrutiny over fund allocation. While the Ministry often champions the success of Kenyan representatives abroad, the back-end administrative process often falls short of the expectations set by high-level political promises.

The lack of a transparent ledger for these disbursements means that athletes often rely on verbal assurances rather than legally binding agreements. Without a clear regulatory mandate that compels the Ministry to honour specific pledges, minority sports participants remain vulnerable to shifting budgetary priorities and administrative apathy. This cycle mirrors other sectors where institutional accountability struggles to keep pace with operational demands, a recurring theme seen in recent high-profile disputes, such as when [Hannah Benta threatened legal action regarding unauthorized image use](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-03-23-hannah-benta-warns-of-legal-action-following-unauthorized-p).

### Why it matters now

The Kiarie case serves as a mirror for the lack of formal policy regarding the support of athletes in niche sports. By failing to bridge the gap between initial pledges and final payouts, the Ministry risks alienating the next generation of professional skaters who look to the state for stability. The systemic failure to process these funds reflects a policy environment that is ill-equipped to nurture emerging disciplines, leaving the athletes to bear the weight of administrative incompetence.

### What to watch next

The primary focus will be whether the Ministry of Sports issues a formal clarification regarding the KSh 1.91 million deficit. Without a clear path to payment, Kiarie may pursue further legal or administrative avenues to demand the release of the funds. Analysts will be observing if this standoff forces the government to implement a standardized, public-facing portal for tracking sports grants, or if the current bureaucratic bottleneck continues to stifle athlete development for those outside the mainstream sporting fraternity.
