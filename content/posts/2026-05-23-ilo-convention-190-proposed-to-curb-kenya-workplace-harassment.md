## ILO Convention 190 and the Performance of Protection

The government insists that ratifying ILO Convention 190 will fix Kenya's toxic workplace culture. It is a comforting fairy tale, but one that ignores the harsh reality of our economy. This legislation is a mirage that leaves the most vulnerable behind.

The narrative surrounding the proposed ratification is seductive. Policy makers claim that by adopting this international framework, Kenya will finally address the Sh95.5 billion productivity loss caused by workplace harassment. They speak of safer offices and empowered employees, painting a future where legal signatures magically translate into respectful conduct. It is a neat, tidy vision. Unfortunately, it is entirely disconnected from the Kenyan experience.

### The Echo Chamber at Lower Kabete

Next week, the Kenya School of Government at Lower Kabete will host a conference to deliberate on these protections. It will be a gathering of suits, legal consultants, and bureaucrats, all nodding in agreement while sipping lukewarm coffee. This event will likely produce a mountain of policy documents and flowery press releases, yet it remains fundamentally insulated from reality. These conferences are designed to manufacture consensus among the elite, while failing to address why the existing labor laws have already failed to protect the vast majority of our workers. If a law exists but cannot be enforced in the slums, the streets, or the small kiosks where most Kenyans earn their bread, then that law is not a shield; it is a decoration.

### The Myth of Universal Protection

First, the Convention assumes a structured employment relationship that simply does not exist for the 80 percent of Kenyans operating in the informal sector. How does a regulation meant for HR departments and corporate boardrooms protect a market vendor, a casual laborer, or a domestic worker? It does not. By focusing on formal structures, the government is essentially legislating for the minority while ignoring the masses who face the most egregious forms of harassment with no recourse.

Second, the economic focus on the Sh95.5 billion loss reveals the true priority: corporate output, not human dignity. When the conversation starts with profit margins, it inevitably ends with box-ticking. Organizations will prioritize compliance reports to avoid litigation rather than fostering a genuine culture of safety.

Third, the implementation hurdles are insurmountable without a radical shift in our judiciary and police force. We already have laws against assault and workplace discrimination. Adding a new convention to the statute books does not solve the fact that victims are rarely taken seriously, or that justice is a luxury good. Without institutional accountability, Convention 190 will just be another expensive administrative layer.

### Why it matters now

We are currently witnessing a cycle where public discourse is distracted by legislative optics. While the political class obsesses over ILO stamps of approval, real, daily workplace abuse remains unchecked. This performative governance allows the state to claim it is doing something while keeping the status quo intact. Whether it is the local drama of political families or the broader, more tedious disputes like those seen in Mbeere North, the public is often steered toward sensationalism rather than structural reform.

### What to watch next

Pay attention to who stands to gain from the consultancy contracts and the specialized training programs that will sprout up following the ratification. Watch for the absence of informal worker representation in the implementation committees. If the focus remains on high-level administrative benchmarks instead of community-based enforcement mechanisms, we will know exactly who this law was written for, and it certainly won't be the person working behind a counter for a daily wage.

Instead of cheering for a new legal framework, we should demand the enforcement of the basic protections that already exist. If we cannot hold employers accountable under current laws, another convention is just another expensive piece of paper. The silence that follows this ratification will be deafening.
