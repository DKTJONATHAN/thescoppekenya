## The Paper Trail of Deception: Why Kenyans Believe the MMUST Scandal is Just the Tip of the Iceberg

Public trust in the hallowed halls of academia is cracking under the weight of a seven-million-shilling lie. As the Kakamega courts handle the fallout of a forged-degree scandal, the streets are asking how one person fooled a major university for eight long years.

Julius Ogamba appeared before the Kakamega Anti-Corruption Court on May 12, 2026, to face charges of forging academic certificates to secure a job at Masinde Muliro University of Science and Technology. Prosecutors allege that between January 2015 and May 2023, the accused used fraudulent documents to draw a salary and benefits totaling Ksh7,584,574. Despite a career path that spanned entities from the Nairobi City Water and Sewerage Company to Kaimosi Friends University, the facade finally crumbled when the Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission intervened. Now out on a Ksh500,000 cash bail, the accused awaits a hearing scheduled for May 28, 2026. The case highlights a systemic failure in human resource vetting that allowed a false resume to bypass internal audit boards for nearly a decade.

### Voices from the Pavement

In the bustling streets of Kakamega, the mood is one of exhausted cynicism. For many, this case confirms a growing suspicion that the hiring process in public institutions has become a playground for the well-connected and the dishonest.

Local small-business owner Benson Ochieng expresses the frustration shared by many graduates who are struggling to find work despite having legitimate qualifications. He notes that while honest job seekers are pushed to the fringes, individuals with fake papers are eating the taxpayers' money for breakfast. He believes that if one man can pull this off for eight years, it is proof that nobody is actually checking the files at the end of the day.

For Mary Wanjiku, a teacher based in the region, the scandal feels like a personal insult to the effort required to earn a degree. She argues that the failure lies not just with the individual, but with the university's internal leadership. She points out that the silence from university boards is deafening, suggesting that if they had been doing their jobs, this fraud would have been caught in the first week, not after eight years of looting the public coffers.

University student activist David Mwasame views the situation as a byproduct of a culture that values the look of success over actual skill. He draws a parallel to the broader issues in the country, noting that just as some pretend to run businesses in the creative sector-where [All Vibes, No Balance Sheets: The Fake 'CEO' Culture Killing Kenyan Entertainment Businesses](https://zandani.co.ke/article/all-vibes-no-balance-sheets-the-fake-ceo-culture-killing-kenyan-entert) has become a normalized hustle-many in the public sector are simply faking their credentials to secure a paycheck. He says that until vetting becomes a digital and transparent process, the universities will remain vulnerable to these parasites.

### Why it Matters Now

The discovery that a fraud could persist undetected from 2015 to 2023 exposes a massive hole in how Kenya screens its public servants. When institutional oversight fails, the cost is not just financial; it is the degradation of the meritocracy. If taxpayers cannot trust that their money is paying for genuine expertise, the foundation of public service crumbles. This incident forces a hard look at whether internal audit boards are complicit, negligent, or simply incompetent.

### What to Watch Next

The court proceedings on May 28, 2026, will be the next major hurdle. Beyond the trial of the individual, the public is waiting to see if any university administrators will be held accountable for the lack of due diligence during the hiring process. If the EACC chooses to widen the net, it could trigger a series of audits across other public universities, potentially exposing a much larger network of fake academic paper-chasers who have been hiding in plain sight.

For now, the story remains a grim reminder that in the race for government jobs, some are skipping the books and heading straight for the printer. The public mood is best described as a mix of weary resignation and a demand for a total cleanup of our public payrolls.
