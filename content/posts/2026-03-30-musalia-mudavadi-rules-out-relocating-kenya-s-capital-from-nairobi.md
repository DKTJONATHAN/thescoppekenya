Prime Cabinet Secretary Musalia Mudavadi on Monday, March 30, 2026, dismissed proposals to relocate Kenya's capital from Nairobi. Appearing before the Senate Committee on Devolution and Intergovernmental Relations, Mudavadi cited legal and practical constraints, including Kenya's land tenure system, as insurmountable obstacles to such a move.

## Capital Relocation Ruled Out

### Legal and Practical Constraints Undermine Proposals

Mudavadi firmly stated that relocating the capital is not feasible due to the nation's unique land tenure system. He explained that a significant portion of urban land is privately owned, making the acquisition required for a new capital impractical. "We cannot relocate the capital to another city," Mudavadi affirmed. "Our land tenure system cannot provide a solution like the one in Tanzania and Nigeria." He further highlighted the substantial financial implications of such a project, pointing to the immense costs associated with moving major infrastructure, including the Standard Gauge Railway (SGR).

### International Examples, Local Realities

While acknowledging that other nations, such as Nigeria and Tanzania, successfully moved their capitals from Lagos to Abuja and Dar es Salaam to Dodoma respectively, Mudavadi stressed that Kenya's circumstances are distinct. He reiterated that the private ownership of urban land would necessitate compromising citizens' property rights to establish a new city, a measure deemed unachievable under current legal frameworks.

### Focus on Nairobi's Urban Improvement

Instead of pursuing a relocation or expansion of the capital, Mudavadi advocated for alternative interventions aimed at enhancing Nairobi's existing infrastructure and urban management. He defended the Ksh80 billion cooperation agreement between the Nairobi County Government and the national government, designed to bolster the capital's performance across four critical areas. This agreement addresses persistent challenges such as flooding and unregulated construction on riparian land, which were raised by the Senate committee.

The cooperation focuses on improving water and sewerage systems, the construction and rehabilitation of roads, bridges, and drainage, housing and related infrastructure development, and comprehensive solid waste management alongside the regeneration of the Nairobi River. Mudavadi confirmed that the joint steering committee, which he chairs, oversees the strategic direction, with Governor Sakaja leading the implementation through a dedicated committee working closely with technical teams from both national and county governments. These efforts underscore the complex and multifaceted nature of national governance, which consistently addresses a spectrum of issues, from critical infrastructure development to managing diverse public concerns, including significant cultural achievements and responses to international travel disruptions.
