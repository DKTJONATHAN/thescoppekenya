## Ksh100 Billion Green Bonds: Hope or Hype for Farmers?

The government’s ambitious plan to raise Ksh100 billion through green bonds for sustainable agriculture has hit the ground with an immediate wave of skepticism and cautious optimism. Kenyans, from the smallest farm gate to the high-stakes trading floors, are weighing the practical implications of such a grand financial instrument against everyday realities.

A collective sigh of "here we go again" ripples through Kenya's farming communities following the announcement. Many smallholder farmers question how a sophisticated mechanism like green bonds will genuinely translate into improved livelihoods and regenerative practices on their scattered plots, especially with the 50 per cent credit risk cover offered to lenders. The immediate sentiment leans heavily towards a 'prove it first' stance, highlighting a familiar gap between high-level policy announcements and grassroots implementation.

The National Treasury recently unveiled its intention to secure Ksh100 billion by 2027, primarily through green and climate bonds, earmarking the funds for sustainable agricultural infrastructure and regenerative farming methods. This push is part of the broader National Agriculture Sector Investment Plan (NASIP), aiming to increase private sector funding in agriculture to 45 per cent by 2030. To sweeten the deal for lenders, the Agricultural Finance Corporation (AFC) is introducing a credit guarantee scheme covering 50 per cent of loan risks. There are also plans for a County Agricultural Investment Fund to provide seed capital for agri-tech startups, a move designed to draw private sector participation into the food system.

### Market Watchers Remain Cautious

On the Nairobi Securities Exchange (NSE), early reactions from analysts are cautiously optimistic, focusing on the potential for new investment vehicles. "The idea of leveraging carbon credit revenues to back these bonds is innovative and could attract international impact investors," noted a senior market analyst, who preferred not to be named. "However, the devil is in the details of the issuance structure and the transparency of how these funds will flow. We are watching for the first tranche to gauge genuine market appetite." The market is keen to see if this initiative can truly de-risk a sector often seen as high-risk, high-reward, particularly when similar pledges, like the [Ksh4.5 Billion UoN Science Complex Project](https://zandani.co.ke/article/ruto-macron-unveil-ksh4-5-billion-uon-science-complex), require careful monitoring for long-term impact.

### Smallholders Seek Tangible Benefits

For Mary Njeri, a maize farmer in Bungoma and a representative of a local cooperative, the terms 'green bonds' and 'carbon credits' sound distant from her daily struggle. "Fifty per cent risk cover for banks sounds good for them, but what about us?" she asked, her voice tinged with skepticism. "Will the loans reach us at reasonable interest rates? And what does 'regenerative farming' mean when my harvest is already struggling with unpredictable rains? We need direct support, not just another complex financial scheme that might not trickle down." Her concerns highlight the deep-seated fear among smallholders that new financing models, however well-intentioned, often bypass the very people they are meant to help.

### Agri-Tech Founders Eye the Seed Fund

Meanwhile, the agri-tech startup scene is a mix of hopeful anticipation and pragmatic skepticism. Jane Akinyi, founder of an agri-tech firm developing drought-resistant seeds, welcomed the mention of a County Agricultural Investment Fund. "Access to seed capital is always the biggest hurdle for innovators like us," she explained. "We’ve seen good ideas fail to scale because of a lack of early-stage funding. If this fund genuinely offers accessible capital between, say, Ksh10 million to Ksh50 million, it could be a massive boost. But we've heard promises before, so our eyes are on the actual implementation and how quickly these funds become available, not just in policy documents." Her sentiment reflects a broader desire within the tech sector for concrete action beyond mere announcements.

The next few weeks will be crucial as the National Treasury prepares the ground for these bond issuances. Stakeholders are not just looking for numbers; they are looking for clear mechanisms that ensure transparency, accessibility, and genuine impact on the ground. The public wants to know how these sophisticated financial instruments will transform into tangible benefits for Kenya's agricultural backbone, not just another policy paper.

As the financial machinery begins to turn, the question remains: will this Ksh100 billion initiative truly cultivate a greener, more prosperous future for Kenyan agriculture, or will it be another grand promise lost in translation? The country watches, keenly aware that the proof of the pudding is in the eating.
