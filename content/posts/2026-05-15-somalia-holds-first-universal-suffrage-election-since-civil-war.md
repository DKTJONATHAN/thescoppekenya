## Somalia and the Fragile Beauty of Universal Suffrage

History arrived in the quiet, dust-swept corridors of Baidoa this week. As Somalia conducts its first universal suffrage election since 1991, the moment serves as a poignant reminder that democracy is never a finished product. Instead, it remains a demanding, daily commitment to the reclamation of the individual voice.

The sight of voters across 13 districts in the Southwest State standing in line to cast their ballots transcended a mere political milestone. It acted as a powerful rebuttal to thirty years of elite consensus. For decades, the international community viewed Somalia through a narrow lens: one dominated by clan elders and quiet rooms where power was brokered away from the public eye. The success of the Justice and Unity Party (JSP) in securing a parliamentary majority through individual votes signals a deep, structural shift. The nation is moving away from the tradition of lineage-based representation toward a system defined by the popular mandate.

### The Logistics of Hope

Observing the reports coming out of the Bay, Bakool, and Lower Shabelle regions, one cannot help but admire the immense logistical courage required to facilitate this transition. While we often fixate on the flaws within our own electoral systems, our neighbors are laboring to build a foundation from the ground up. To hold a one-person, one-vote election across these specific regions is far more than a bureaucratic achievement. It represents a bold act of defiance against the lingering shadows of 1991.

In Kenya, we frequently take for granted the normalcy of parliamentary debate and the noise of our campaign rallies. We treat democratic imperfections as excuses to disengage, distracted by the trivialities of pop culture or the tactical frustrations of European football. Yet, for the people of the Southwest State, the journey to a ballot box carries significant weight. It is the sound of a long-silent population finally finding its voice.

### Reflections on a New Mandate

The rise of the JSP is less about specific partisan ideology and more about an urgent appetite for a clear, direct bond between the citizen and the state. Viewing this through a Kenyan perspective reveals several important insights.

First, the policy gap is striking. We have spent decades refining our institutions, yet a persistent disconnect remains between the casting of a ballot and the actual implementation of policy. Somalia, by contrast, is bypassing years of incremental institutional development by leaping directly into the deep end of universal suffrage. The result is a raw, unpolished, and remarkably authentic form of civic expression.

Second, our tendency to view neighboring nations as static blocks of geopolitical interest obscures the truth. The events in Baidoa demonstrate that true stability is not merely the absence of conflict; it is the presence of legitimate, transparent processes. When citizens believe their individual vote holds genuine weight, the motivation to pursue change through violence naturally begins to wane.

Third, we must remain mindful of the fragility inherent in this process. Moving away from deep-seated clan power structures is never a linear journey. The JSP victory serves as a strong signal of intent, but the ultimate test lies in whether these fragile structures can withstand the inevitable pressures of governance.

### Why it matters now

The Somali experiment serves as a reminder that democracy functions much like a muscle. When left unused, it inevitably withers. By choosing direct elections, the residents of the Southwest State are committing to a system that requires politicians to earn their support through persuasion rather than command. This reality challenges us to stop viewing our own civic participation as a tedious chore, encouraging us instead to value it as a hard-won right that our neighbors are currently striving to secure.

### What to watch next

The national conversation now shifts toward the presidential election scheduled for May 23, 2026. This date represents the final hurdle in cementing the region's new trajectory. If the momentum generated by these parliamentary contests continues, it could effectively close the door on an era defined by birthright and personal connection.

The quiet that settles in after a vote is often the most significant aspect of the process. It is the sound of a people waiting to discover if their collective choice will truly alter the architecture of their daily existence. We should be watching closely-not just to see who wins, but to witness the resilience of the democratic idea itself.
