## The Cost of Regional Ambition in the Safaricom Ledger

Safaricom has posted record revenues of KSh 427.56 billion for the 2026 fiscal year, yet these top-line figures obscure mounting internal pressure. The company’s financial health is increasingly tethered to an ambitious Ethiopian expansion that has pushed net debt to KSh 139.81 billion. This shift forces a difficult question regarding the long-term sustainability of dividend payouts and whether the firm is overextending its capital base to secure regional influence.

### The M-PESA Financing Model

Under the leadership of Peter Ndegwa, Safaricom has pivoted from traditional telecommunications dominance toward a model centered on M-PESA as its primary funding mechanism. With annual transaction throughput reaching KSh 41.68 trillion, the platform now serves as the firm’s essential liquidity source. Rather than reinvesting these proceeds into domestic infrastructure projects like the local data initiatives championed by current policy, the organization is directing this capital toward the resource-heavy build-out of its Ethiopian network.

This represents a departure from the strategy established under Bob Collymore, who prioritized lean debt profiles and robust domestic margins. Current balance sheet metrics depict a firm operating with significant leverage relative to its growth objectives. While market capitalization remains anchored at 1.25 trillion, the strain on shareholders is tangible. The company’s KSh 95.61 billion in net profits is being consumed by the relentless capital expenditure required to maintain operations in Addis Ababa. This regional venture, characterized by a volatile regulatory and operational landscape, is effectively competing with shareholder returns for the company's surplus cash.

### Dividends and the Shareholder Burden

The financial reality for investors is stark. A net debt position of KSh 139.81 billion limits the company’s capacity to provide competitive dividend yields. For retail investors who have long treated Safaricom as a stable, blue-chip utility stock, the move toward a debt-heavy, growth-oriented model represents a fundamental change in risk profile. Shareholders are essentially underwriting a major portion of the US$ 2.65 billion consortium investment in Ethiopia. They are absorbing the volatility of a foreign market while the Kenyan business acts as the sole, reliable cash provider.

Historically, surplus revenue was distributed to shareholders, cementing the firm's reputation as a reliable income generator. Under the current management, capital is being diverted toward massive infrastructure deployment. This reallocation of wealth from the investor to the balance sheet is rarely discussed in official disclosures. While corporate leadership celebrates revenue growth, the underlying cash flow mechanics reveal a transition. The company is sacrificing immediate shareholder satisfaction for the high-stakes objective of becoming a pan-African telecommunications powerhouse. 

### The Strategic Dependency

The Ethiopian expansion has created a structural dependency on the high-margin M-PESA business. This service must consistently generate enough liquidity to absorb the ongoing operational losses and heavy overheads associated with the northern expansion. Unlike historical network upgrades that focused on domestic connectivity and offered clear returns, the current strategy demands constant, large-scale funding injections.

This reality places the board in a position of forced commitment. They must decide whether to protect the interests of Kenyan investors or continue to feed the Ethiopian venture to prevent the loss of sunk costs. The current trajectory suggests the latter. As management favors debt reduction and regional investment over meaningful increases in dividend payouts, the central concern for investors is clear. At what point does regional ambition transition from a strategic masterstroke into a permanent anchor on local value?

The margin for error is shrinking. If M-PESA were to encounter even moderate transaction stagnation or new regulatory hurdles, the firm’s ability to balance its significant debt obligations with investor demands would collapse. The current leadership team is walking a narrow path, balancing the pursuit of long-term dominance against the risk of alienating the domestic investor base that provided the foundation for Safaricom’s growth. Whether this strategy ultimately delivers value or merely depletes the company’s domestic core remains the defining question of the current fiscal era.
