## National Bank of Kenya Achieves Robust Profit Growth Amidst Strategic Realignment National Bank of Kenya reported a KSh 2.39 billion profit after tax for 2025, more than doubling 2024 figures, driven by a 37.0% reduction in bad loan provisioning. This strong performance marks its best earnings since recapitalization, reflecting deliberate portfolio rationalization and a strategic shift towards government securities under new ownership.

Strong Financial Performance For the full year ended 31 December 2025, National Bank of Kenya recorded a profit after tax of KSh 2.39 billion, significantly surpassing the KSh 1.06 billion posted in 2024. This achievement represents the bank's strongest earnings performance since its recapitalization during the KCB-era. The substantial increase in profitability was largely propelled by a 37.0% decline in bad loans provisioning, which dropped to KSh 1.52 billion. Consequently, gross Non-Performing Loans (NPLs) halved to KSh 15.66 billion, reaching their lowest level since 2022.

Balance Sheet Transformation The bank's balance sheet reflects a strategic shift, with net loans experiencing a significant 32.3% contraction to KSh 50.71 billion. This KSh 24.16 billion reduction represents the sharpest single-year decline in the bank's recorded history, indicating a deliberate portfolio rationalization strategy by the new owner. Capital freed from traditional lending activities has been reallocated into government securities, which rose to KSh 59.93 billion, and Central Bank of Kenya balances, signaling a recalibrated risk appetite and asset allocation.

Historical Context and Ownership Transition NBK's recent performance is understood against a backdrop of historical challenges. After a decade of consistent profitability from 2005 to 2014, with total assets growing to KSh 123.1 billion and profit after tax reaching KSh 2.02 billion in 2010, the bank experienced a significant downturn. Deterioration commenced in 2015 with a KSh 3.72 billion loan loss provision leading to a pre-tax loss of KSh 1.64 billion. Gross NPLs surged from KSh 7.24 billion in 2014 to KSh 29.99 billion by 2016, driven by problematic public sector lending and governance issues, resulting in core capital falling below statutory minimums by 2018. KCB Group completed a Central Bank of Kenya-supervised share swap takeover in September 2019, leading to NBK's delisting from the Nairobi Securities Exchange on 16 September 2019. The KCB tenure brought recapitalization but also uneven results, including a KSh 3.49 billion pre-tax loss in 2023, the worst in the bank's history, which ultimately prompted the sale to Access Bank.

Access Bank's Strategic Acquisition The acquisition proved profitable for KCB, yielding a KSh 3.1 billion gain on the transaction. This gain funded a special dividend of KSh 4 per share, lifting KCB's total 2025 payout to KSh 7 per share from KSh 3 in 2024. For Access Bank, the acquisition of NBK provides immediate scale and synergy with its existing Kenyan subsidiary. By September 2025, both institutions had initiated shared services across their combined network of over 100 branches, with full systems and operational integration continuing to progress. This strategic move by Access Bank strengthens its footprint in the Kenyan market, a critical region for economic growth, where initiatives such as [Digital Learning Kenya’s Frontier Counties Taking Root](https://zandani.co.ke/article/digital-learning-kenyas-frontier-counties-taking-root) point to evolving opportunities.
