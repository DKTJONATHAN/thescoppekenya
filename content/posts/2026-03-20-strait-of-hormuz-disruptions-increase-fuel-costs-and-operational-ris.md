## Kenyan Businesses Confront Rising Costs Amidst Strait of Hormuz Disruptions Disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz are intensifying cost and risk for Kenyan businesses, impacting fuel, petrochemicals, and Gulf-linked cargo. This situation exposes existing weaknesses in balance sheets through increased energy prices, foreign exchange pressure, and extended transit times, necessitating strategic recalibration for executives.

Energy Price Shocks and Economic Exposure Disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz directly increase the cost and risk associated with transporting fuel, petrochemicals, and other Gulf-linked cargo. These energy price shocks reveal pre-existing balance sheet weaknesses rather than creating new ones. Kenya, as a significant net importer of petroleum products, experiences a direct increase in its national fuel import bill with every sustained $10 USD (KES ~1,295) rise in global oil prices. This impact quickly cascades through the economy, escalating logistics, manufacturing input, and transport costs, while also intensifying foreign-exchange demand. Should oil prices return to the $100-120 USD (KSh ~12,955 - 15,546) range last seen in 2022, Kenya could face a significant supply-side shock. The government-to-government fuel import arrangement offers some buffer by allowing gradual conversion of domestic shilling sales to dollars, smoothing foreign-exchange demand without reducing global oil price exposure.

Currency Volatility and Debt Implications A crucial macro risk emerges from the interplay between elevated oil prices and a depreciating shilling. A sustained oil shock inflates Kenya’s dollar import bill and can exert pressure on the exchange rate. Businesses with USD liabilities and KES revenues face a dual exposure: rising import costs in dollar terms coupled with a simultaneous increase in the local-currency value of external debt. This combination can rapidly compress covenant headroom for highly leveraged firms. While Kenya benefits from nearly 90% renewable electricity generation, offering a long-term hedge against imported fuel volatility, the immediate currency-oil price dynamic remains challenging. Export-oriented agricultural producers and tourism operators may, however, benefit from converting foreign earnings into a depreciating local currency, despite domestic energy cost increases and commodity-linked agricultural input prices like fertilizer.

Logistics and Supply Chain Strain Kenyan businesses face logistics exposure through two primary channels: the Gulf itself and the Red Sea/Suez system. Disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz elevate costs and risks for Gulf-linked cargo. Even when Kenyan cargo does not directly transit Suez, the global container network is affected as carriers suspend Gulf calls, reroute vessels, reduce capacity, and incur higher insurance costs. This results in a broader deterioration of reliability, shipping capacity, transit times, and landed costs for Kenyan importers. War-risk insurance premiums for Gulf transits have surged from approximately 0.25% to over 2.5% of hull value in some cases, translating to millions of dollars per voyage for large vessels. Rerouting around the Cape of Good Hope can extend transit times by up to two weeks, imposing substantial working-capital impacts; a 15-day delay for a company with KSh 10 billion annual revenue and 40% imported inputs could tie up approximately KSh 150-170 million in additional working capital. Amidst these geopolitical tensions, Kenya recently issued a [Middle East security advisory](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenya-issues-middle-east-security-advisory-u-s-israel-strikes-on-iran), highlighting the volatile operational environment.

Strategic Responses for Kenyan Firms Executives navigating this environment must prioritize visibility, balance sheet assessment, and cost structure recalibration. Establishing weekly cross-functional reviews involving finance, treasury, procurement, operations, and commercial teams is crucial for translating fuel, freight, and foreign exchange exposures into operational decisions. CFOs should reassess unhedged USD liabilities, short-term refinancing needs, and covenant headroom under weaker currency scenarios. Early engagement with commercial banks is vital for managing working capital, reviewing inventory days against longer transit assumptions, and exploring tools like factoring. Rather than blanket price increases, selective repricing based on product pricing power and customer segments can better protect margins. Companies should also plan for duration by stress-testing capital expenditure under tighter financing, diversifying sourcing beyond single-country dependencies, and re-evaluating just-in-time inventory strategies for critical inputs. Crises like these underscore the importance of understanding structural exposure to fuel, freight, and currency, quantifying EBITDA, and possessing sufficient balance sheet headroom to absorb prolonged shocks.
