Kenya is poised for a significant expansion of its agricultural exports into the United States market, following a productive trade mission to Nairobi by the U.S. Africa Trade Desk (USATD). This development is seen as a pivotal moment for the nation's agricultural sector, which remains a cornerstone of the economy. The high-level USATD delegation, led by CEO Gavin van der Burgh, engaged extensively with key government agencies and private-sector entities across floriculture, coffee, macadamia, apparel, grains, and animal feed sectors. Cabinet Secretary for Agriculture and Livestock Development, Senator Mutahi Kagwe, formally received the delegation, emphasising the government's steadfast commitment to bolstering Kenya's agricultural exports through a private-sector-led model. This strategy prioritises compliance, traceability, and value addition to meet global demands. CS Kagwe reiterated the Ministry's dedication to investing in systems that ensure Kenyan products consistently meet stringent U.S. market standards, aligning with President William Ruto’s Bottom-Up Economic Transformation Agenda (BETA), which champions agricultural transformation and export diversification. While agriculture is a primary pillar of BETA, the sector faces a substantial funding shortfall of Sh59.864 billion in the 2026/27 budget, as highlighted in the 2026 Budget Policy Statement. Despite this, the government's fiscal policy for FY 2026/27 and the medium term aims to transform the economy from a net importer to a net exporter of agricultural and manufactured products. In a move to further stimulate agricultural exports and enhance global competitiveness, the government plans to reduce Value Added Tax (VAT) on agricultural inputs to 8% under the upcoming 2026 finance bill. Additionally, excise duties and export promotion taxes on packaging materials for horticulture exporters are set to be removed, and VAT refunds accelerated. These reforms are expected to unlock capital and accelerate investment across key agricultural value chains. The USATD team identified fresh cut flowers, coffee, and macadamia nuts as immediate priority sectors for U.S. retail markets, including major chains such as Walmart, with tea slated for a second phase. This builds on CS Kagwe’s earlier visit to the U.S. in September, which secured a commitment to expand the import window for Kenyan agricultural goods. A notable success from the trade mission is Nouvelle Blooms Ltd, an export firm based at Jomo Kenyatta International Airport (JKIA), which secured agreements to export over four million stems of premium Kenyan roses per month to the U.S. market. The company will also commence supplying specialty Kenyan coffee directly to American buyers. Kenya remains a leading global exporter of cut flowers, with the global market valued at approximately $35 billion to $39 billion as of 2024-2025, projected to grow to over $50 billion by 2030. In the macadamia value chain, companies like Macnut Consortium showcased their production capacity and adherence to U.S. food safety standards, securing macadamia nuts a place in the initial wave of Kenyan products destined for American retail outlets. The global macadamia market is anticipated to expand steadily, from USD 3.0 billion in 2026 to USD 6.7 billion by 2036, driven by demand for premium snacking and plant-based trends, with Kenya contributing significantly to global supply. To ensure market access and meet international standards, Kenya has significantly tightened horticultural traceability rules. Amendments to the Crops (Horticultural Crops) Regulations in 2025 now mandate growers to register with the National Horticulture Traceability System and maintain detailed, auditable records for each crop, encompassing planting, harvesting, pest and disease management, and pesticide applications. The Kenya Plant Health Inspectorate Service has also implemented the Integrated Export Import Certification System for automated phytosanitary certificates. Similar reforms are underway in the coffee sub-sector to comply with European Union deforestation regulations, with about a third of Kenya's coffee acreage already mapped. Furthermore, the government, through the Kenya Agricultural Livestock Research Organization (KALRO) in partnership with the Korean government, is advancing programs to strengthen pest management. These initiatives focus on developing new technologies and practices, including biological control, to reduce reliance on chemical pesticides. Proposed legislation, including the Plant Protection Bill, 2025 and the Pest Control Products Bill, 2025, aims to reinforce regulatory frameworks for pest management and control products, thereby enhancing food safety and security. These efforts complement the withdrawal of 77 pesticide products and the restriction of 202 others following a comprehensive review by the Pest Control Products Board. Stakeholders are also pushing for strategic public education on safe pesticide use through initiatives such as aak-GROW's "Stewardship Mashinani". This strengthened partnership with the U.S. is expected not only to open new markets for Kenyan exports but also to facilitate strategic imports of raw materials such as cotton for apparel and inputs for livestock feed, supporting local manufacturing and food security. These imports are anticipated to reduce production costs and stabilise supply chains across Kenya’s dairy, poultry, and livestock sectors, boosting competitiveness and sustainability. The visit marks a landmark step towards unlocking Kenya’s full agricultural export potential, positioning the nation to become a global agri-export powerhouse, and ultimately transforming livelihoods for local farmers.
