## Ethio Telecom Listing and the New Regional Competition

Ethiopia has officially opened its doors to public ownership by listing its biggest telecommunications firm, creating a massive shift in how capital flows through East Africa. This move challenges Kenya's long-standing position as the region's undisputed financial hub for small investors.

The revelation that Ethio Telecom generated 162 billion birr in revenue for its 2025 financial year provides a rare look into a previously closed market. For a Kenyan business owner looking to expand north, these numbers prove that the Ethiopian market is no longer a speculative venture but a concrete landscape with documented buying power. This listing on the Ethiopian Securities Exchange (ESX) under the ticker TELE marks the first time a state-owned firm in Addis Ababa has invited the public to own a piece of the action. This is a sharp contrast to the Nairobi Securities Exchange (NSE), where the wait for new state-owned listings has stretched for over a decade.

### Transparency and the Kenyan Supplier

For years, Kenyan SMEs looking to partner with Ethiopian entities faced a wall of secrecy. Without public financial records, it was hard to tell if a potential partner was solvent or struggling. The disclosure of 162 billion birr in revenue and a profit of 42.36 billion birr changes the math for every Kenyan contractor, from software developers to logistics providers.

When a company lists, it must play by the rules of transparency. This newfound visibility means Kenyan firms can now assess the creditworthiness of the Ethiopian market with more precision. ### The Retail Investor Shift

The most striking part of this listing is the verification of 45,000 IPO investors. These are not just large banks; they are everyday citizens and small business owners who now have skin in the game. These investors represent approximately 10.1 million shares ready for trading. This is a wake-up call for the NSE. While Kenya has traditionally been the home of the retail investor in East Africa, the enthusiasm in Addis Ababa shows that the appetite for ownership is moving.

Kenyan traders should watch this closely. As capital starts to flow into the ESX, the regional liquidity might pivot. If the NSE continues to suffer from a privatization drought while the ESX lists its biggest assets, the "smart money" from Kenyan and international investors will inevitably look toward Ethiopia. This shift could impact interest rates and the availability of credit for local SMEs as banks look to fund cross-border investment opportunities instead of local expansion.
