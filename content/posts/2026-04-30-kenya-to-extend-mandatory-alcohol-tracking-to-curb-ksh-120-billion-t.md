## Curbing Illicit Alcohol to Recover KSh 120 Billion in Tax Revenue

Kenya plans to extend mandatory excise stamps to all alcoholic beverages, including second-generation brews, to curb an estimated KSh 120 billion annual tax loss from illicit trade. This initiative seeks to differentiate compliant products from untaxed ones and generate real-time supply chain data.

### Market Dynamics and Tax Evasion

The illicit alcohol market, while representing approximately 6% of the overall market, disproportionately contributes to lost tax revenue due to underreporting and fragmented distribution channels. Research commissioned by the Alcoholic Beverages Association of Kenya (ABAK) indicates that illicit alcohol consumption volume has surpassed legal alcohol, with adults consuming an estimated 3.6 litres of pure illicit alcohol in 2024 compared to 2.5 litres of legal alcohol. Legal producers, already burdened by excise duties and compliance costs, face significant competition from these lower-priced alternatives that often bypass quality controls and fiscal obligations, creating a price distortion. This draws consumers into the black market, particularly in low-income segments where illicit variants can be less than half the cost of legal spirits.

### Enforcement Challenges and Strategic Responses

Combating illicit alcohol trade faces significant hurdles, notably the absence of a formalized structure with clear accountability and funding for enforcement agencies. Anti-Counterfeit Authority CEO Dr. Robi King'a has identified border points across the western, southern, and northern corridors as key conduits for smuggled ethanol and finished spirits. This challenge persists despite multiple agencies operating within the same enforcement space. To streamline coordination among tax authorities, standards regulators, anti-counterfeit officials, and law enforcement, policymakers are considering anchoring a permanent multi-agency framework in law. Such a framework aims to shift strategy from episodic crackdowns to systemic control mechanisms embedded in production and distribution. This approach aligns with broader government efforts to strengthen institutions and uphold legal frameworks, as seen with directives concerning police conduct and other significant appointments.

### Consumer Behavior and Health Risks

Consumer behavior in the illicit alcohol market is primarily driven by financial incentives, not ignorance of risks. An ABAK study reveals that 94% of consumers acknowledge the significantly lower price of illegal alcohol compared to regulated products. Concurrently, 61% of consumers associate illicit alcohol with severe health consequences, including blindness, organ failure, or death. This paradox underscores the powerful economic incentive that sustains illicit supply, which continues to expand as excise taxes rise and enforcement struggles to keep pace. The proposed extension of mandatory excise stamps is a critical step towards re-establishing market equilibrium and protecting both public health and government revenue.
