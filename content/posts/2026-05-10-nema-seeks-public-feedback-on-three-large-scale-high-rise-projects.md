## Concrete Jungle Fatigue: Why Nairobi and Mombasa Residents are Pushing Back on High-Rise Expansion

Nairobi and Mombasa residents are hitting a breaking point as NEMA opens the floor for public feedback on three massive new high-rise developments. Tired of shrinking skylines and strained infrastructure, locals are questioning if their neighborhoods can handle more.

The National Environment Management Authority has officially invited public scrutiny into three proposed construction projects located in the heart of Kileleshwa, Kilimani, and Nyali. These proposals include a 24-story residential block in Kileleshwa, a 23-story serviced apartment tower in Kilimani, and a 13-story development in Nyali. The Environmental Impact Assessment reports submitted by the developers acknowledge risks like soil erosion and increased demand on public utilities, while outlining density targets that reach up to 75 percent. With the 30-day clock now ticking for public comments, residents are already organising to challenge how these projects will exacerbate the chronic water shortages and traffic gridlock currently plaguing their streets.

### Voices from the Pavement

The frustration is palpable on the ground, especially for those living near the proposed sites on Gatundu Road and Chania Avenue. Simon, a long-term Kileleshwa resident, feels like his neighborhood is being suffocated by concrete. He argues that the existing sewage and water systems were built for single-family homes, not for a single block housing hundreds of apartments. He questions how the city can approve another 24-story building when his taps have been dry for the better part of the week.

In Kilimani, the sentiment is equally critical. Martha, who has operated a small business near the site of the proposed 23-story tower, is worried about the construction phase alone. She points out that her daily commute on existing roads is already a test of patience, and adding 483 serviced apartment units to the area seems like a recipe for permanent traffic paralysis. She believes developers are prioritizing profit margins over the livability of the very areas they are marketing as premium.

Down in Nyali, the conversation shifts to environmental preservation and the loss of coastal charm. David, a resident, says that the 75 percent density target mentioned in the reports is fundamentally incompatible with the neighborhood's character. He worries that the scale of these projects will create heat islands and strip away the remaining greenery that makes the area habitable. For him, the question is not whether the buildings are safe, but whether the neighborhood will still be a home once they are finished.

### Why it Matters Now

The pushback against these developments follows recent political shifts, including Sakaja Reshuffles Five Nairobi County Chief Officers, which has left many residents feeling uncertain about who is holding developers accountable. While the rest of the nation watches news like Wycliffe Oparanya threatens to withdraw Western region support for ODM, ordinary citizens in urban centers are focused on the immediate battle for their local environment.
