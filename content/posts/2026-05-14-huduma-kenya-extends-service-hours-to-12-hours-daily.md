## Huduma Kenya Extends Service Hours at 20 Centres Nationwide

The state promises a twelve-hour workday for twenty select centres, framing the move as a gift of convenience to the Kenyan worker. Yet, behind this veneer of progress lies a stubborn silence on the staff and system failures that cause the queues in the first place.

### The Illusion of Longer Access

Starting today, May 14, citizens are invited to visit twenty major Huduma branches between 7 am and 7 pm. The narrative is simple: the government is working harder so you do not have to. But extending operating hours is a superficial band-aid on a gaping structural wound. While the doors may stay open longer, the internal engine remains fundamentally broken. The current staff-to-citizen ratio at high-traffic branches like GPO and City Square is already at a breaking point. Expecting the same exhausted personnel to maintain efficiency during an additional four hours of operation without a corresponding increase in human capital is not just optimistic; it is a recipe for employee burnout and further decline in service quality.

A deeper look at the operational costs reveals a fiscal paradox. Funding extended shifts requires electricity, security, and cleaning services for buildings that are already straining under the weight of outdated infrastructure. Meanwhile, the digital backbone of these centres remains erratic. Civil society observers note that the frequent system downtimes are rarely caused by a lack of hours in the day. Instead, they point to a lack of investment in robust digital architecture and the persistent, unaddressed shadow of ghost workers within the civil service. Simply put, if the server is offline or the database is sluggish at 10 am, it will be equally useless at 6 pm. The government is asking taxpayers to queue for an extra four hours, but they have yet to prove that the systems processing those applications are actually capable of performing any faster.

### The Paper Trail of Inefficiency

The list of twenty designated centres, including Kisumu, Mombasa, Nakuru, and Kiambu, covers the most congested urban hubs. These locations are the primary testing grounds for government digital initiatives, yet they continue to be defined by backlogs in passport processing and tax compliance. While the political class focuses on optics, such as recent spectacles like [Embarambamba meeting Kipchumba Murkomen](https://zandani.co.ke/article/embarambamba-meets-kipchumba-murkomen-after-viral-muddy-video), the mundane reality of administrative bottlenecks remains a secondary concern.

There is a distinct lack of transparency regarding the budgetary reallocation required for this expansion. When resources are shifted to cover overtime pay and extended utility usage, other critical service areas often suffer. This decision appears to be a reactive maneuver designed to pacify the public during a time of high document demand, rather than a proactive fix to the fundamental flaws in the public service delivery model. If the goal were true efficiency, the focus would be on digitizing the process to remove the need for physical presence entirely, rather than forcing citizens to inhabit these centres for longer portions of their day.

### Why it matters now

The expansion of Huduma operating hours is a pivot point for public sector performance metrics. If this experiment fails, it confirms that the government prefers performative accessibility over actual system reform. As political shifts continue to capture headlines, such as [the appointment of Oburu Odinga as the new ODM party leader](https://zandani.co.ke/article/odm-appoints-oburu-odinga-as-new-party-leader), the daily struggle of the average citizen to secure a basic document remains a static, frustrating baseline.

### What to watch next

Monitor the downtime reports and average wait times at these twenty locations over the next month. If the system uptime does not improve to match the extended physical hours, the entire initiative will be exposed as nothing more than a marketing exercise.

When the lights stay on for twelve hours but the printers remain jammed and the servers remain stagnant, who truly benefits from this extended schedule?
