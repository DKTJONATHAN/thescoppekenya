## Safaricom Financial Results and the Ethiopia Expansion

Safaricom recently reported KES 400.8 billion in service revenue from its Kenyan operations. While this demonstrates the company's domestic strength, a KES 37.7 billion loss from its Ethiopian subsidiary has created a complex situation for investors. Shareholders are currently weighing the benefits of regional expansion against the impact on their immediate financial returns.

### The Balancing Act: Kenya Profits vs. Ethiopia Costs

Safaricom remains a pillar of the Nairobi Securities Exchange. Its Kenyan performance is driven by 40 million M-PESA users and a robust network of 3.1 million merchants, resulting in a net income of KES 119.1 billion. This segment functions as the company's primary source of stability and cash flow.

In contrast, the Ethiopia operation presents a different picture. Although the business has successfully acquired 13.6 million active customers, it is currently operating at a deficit. The KES 37.7 billion loss before interest and taxes highlights the high cost of building infrastructure in a new market. This spending influences the company's dividend policy, which is currently set at KES 2.00 per share. The board must balance the need to compensate existing shareholders with the necessity of funding the capital-intensive rollout required to establish a footprint in Ethiopia.

### Moving Beyond Inflationary Fog

Financial analysis of Safaricom's regional progress has been complicated by the application of IAS 29, an accounting standard for hyperinflationary economies. This standard adds layers of complexity that often obscure the actual performance of the Ethiopian unit, making it difficult for the average investor to track organic growth.

The company has announced that it will cease using this accounting classification for its Ethiopian business by June 30, 2025. This transition is a significant milestone for transparency. Once the change is implemented, stakeholders will be able to evaluate the Ethiopian division's financial health without the distortion caused by hyperinflation adjustments. Management appears optimistic that the local market will stabilize, which serves as a signal of confidence to long-term investors. While general political news often captures public attention, this technical change in financial reporting will likely provide a more accurate picture of the firm's long-term value.

### Investing Through the Ziidi Trader Platform

Safaricom is taking steps to deepen local retail participation in capital markets. The recent introduction of the Ziidi Trader platform aims to lower the barrier to entry for individual Kenyans. By streamlining the process of buying and selling shares, the company intends to foster a broader and more resilient base of small-scale investors.

This initiative suggests that while the company is allocating significant capital to foreign infrastructure, it remains committed to strengthening the domestic ecosystem. By making the Nairobi Securities Exchange more accessible to digital-first users, Safaricom is attempting to modernize how local investors interact with the equity market. The success of this platform will be measured by its ability to increase the number of active retail participants over the coming years.

### Why it matters now

The company is effectively operating as two distinct entities. It manages a mature, profitable utility in Kenya while simultaneously nurturing a capital-intensive, high-growth startup in Ethiopia. Investors are currently feeling the impact of this dual strategy. Every unit of currency directed toward building towers and fiber optics in Addis Ababa is capital that is not being returned to shareholders. Understanding this trade-off is critical for anyone assessing the stock or relying on the firm's growth trajectory for their financial planning.

### What to watch next

The company's next major milestone is the Annual General Meeting, which is slated for July 31, 2026. At this event, the final dividend payout will be confirmed. Beyond the immediate fiscal decision, investors should monitor the performance of the Ethiopian unit following the removal of hyperinflationary accounting. If the operational loss does not show a clear downward trend after mid-2025, the board may face increasing pressure to re-evaluate the scale and pace of its regional investment strategy. Monitoring these developments will be essential for gauging the long-term sustainability of the current expansion model.
