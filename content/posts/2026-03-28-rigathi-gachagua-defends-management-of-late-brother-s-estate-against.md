Rigathi Gachagua, former Deputy President, on Friday, March 27, 2026, defended his management of his late brother Nderitu Gachagua's estate. His remarks follow claims from the former Nyeri Governor's family, who petitioned President William Ruto to intervene in an ongoing succession dispute, alleging fraud and irregular handling of assets.

## Gachagua Addresses Estate Succession Dispute

### Family Alleges Fraud and Contested Will

The dispute stems from a letter dated March 23, 2026, sent to President Ruto by members of the late Governor's family. They requested an investigation into alleged fraud, forgery, and irregular management of the estate. The family contested the will, citing inconsistencies and alleging a close relative orchestrated schemes to disinherit them. They further claimed the late governor was critically ill when the will was executed, arguing his medical condition made it impossible to prepare such a complex document. ### Rigathi Gachagua's Defense of Legal Process

In response to the allegations, Rigathi Gachagua dismissed them on Friday, March 27, stating the succession process was concluded legally years ago. He clarified that his late brother prepared a comprehensive will, encompassing 21 beneficiaries, including his wives, children, siblings, and other dependents. Gachagua explained the will outlined a clear distribution formula with varying percentages for beneficiaries, and three executors were appointed to oversee asset sales, debt settlement, and distribution of proceeds. He affirmed that in 2018, all beneficiaries appeared before a court, agreed to the will's contents, and the court subsequently adopted it, granting executors authority to implement its provisions.

### Political Motivation Claims and Presidential Authority

Gachagua questioned the timing of the revived dispute, suggesting it is politically motivated. He accused President Ruto of attempting to interfere in a private family matter for political gain. Gachagua asserted that succession matters are exclusively handled by the courts, not the presidency, and dissatisfied family members should seek legal intervention rather than appealing to Ruto. He further dismissed claims that the President possesses authority to reverse transactions completed under a court-sanctioned process, labeling such assertions as misleading and legally impossible.
