## The Safaricom Dividend Paradox: Why Record Payouts Leave the Ordinary User Behind

Safaricom is handing out a staggering KSh 80.13 billion to shareholders, the largest payout in Kenyan corporate history. While the boardroom celebrates this 83.7 percent dividend ratio, the average consumer continues to struggle under the weight of persistent digital transaction costs.

### The Math of Corporate Wealth vs User Burden

The numbers are designed to dazzle. Safaricom has posted an attributable profit of KSh 95.61 billion, leading to a record dividend proposal of KSh 2.00 per share. Board chairman Adil Khawaja frames this as a win for the Nairobi Securities Exchange. Yet, for the ordinary Kenyan who keeps the telco’s engines running, this windfall carries a bitter aftertaste. 

This massive wealth transfer occurs while the cost of living remains stubbornly high. The average mobile user spends a disproportionate amount of their monthly income on data bundles and M-Pesa transaction fees. When you compare the KSh 2.00 per share payout against the annual per-capita spending on digital services, a clear picture emerges: the individual user is effectively subsidizing the returns for institutional investors and major shareholders. 

The telco justifies its pricing models as necessary for infrastructure expansion, particularly in Ethiopia. However, an 83.7 percent payout ratio suggests that the company is prioritizing aggressive capital distribution over the reduction of service costs for the people who actually use the network. If the company is profitable enough to break dividend records, why does the cost of connectivity remain a primary barrier for the bottom of the pyramid?

### The Accountability Gap

Corporate responsibility often becomes a hollow term in the heat of a profitable fiscal year. In an economy where inflation has eroded purchasing power, the decision to maximize payouts to shareholders instead of reinvesting those billions into lower tariffs or enhanced digital access is a choice. It is a prioritization of capital gains over social equity.

We see this disconnect across several sectors. Just as we recently witnessed authorities deflect accountability regarding the [Nairobi Expressway Flooding](https://zandani.co.ke/article/nairobi-expressway-flooding-shocking-blame-2024), there is a similar tendency in the corporate world to hide behind market metrics to avoid addressing the direct economic impact on the public. Whether it is political accountability or corporate performance, the narrative remains consistent: those at the top are insulated, while the public is left to bear the cost.

### Why it matters now

The record dividend happens during a period of intense economic strain for many Kenyans. When a dominant market player extracts record profits and immediately funnels the bulk of that cash out to shareholders, it limits the potential for the company to play a genuine role in economic relief for its user base. It highlights a widening chasm between corporate success and public welfare.

### What to watch next

The proposal must still pass the annual general meeting on July 31, 2026. While institutional shareholders will undoubtedly back the motion, the discussion at the AGM will reveal whether any voices are raised regarding the sustainability of this model. Investors should keep a close watch on whether the company maintains this payout level next year if the expansion into Ethiopia requires further capital injections, or if this is merely a temporary attempt to placate a restless investor base.

The ultimate question remains: How much longer can Safaricom demand high fees from the Kenyan consumer to fund dividend payouts that primarily benefit those who are already wealthy, while the average user finds their digital participation increasingly unaffordable?
