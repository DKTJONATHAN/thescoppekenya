The Communications and Multimedia Appeals Tribunal on Friday, March 27, 2026, authorized the Communications Authority of Kenya (CA) to revoke six broadcasting licenses belonging to the Standard Media Group. This decision follows the media house's outstanding debt of Ksh48.9 million in unpaid annual fees and levies.

Licences Affected by Tribunal Ruling The ruling impacts Radio Maisha, Spice FM, Vybez Radio, Berur FM, KTN Burudani, and KTN News. The tribunal dismissed an appeal filed by The Standard Media Group PLC, affirming that the CA's planned revocation is lawful, valid, and consistent with the Kenya Information and Communications Act (KICA).

Financial Arrears and Regulatory Compliance The tribunal cited the media group’s multi-year failure to pay outstanding annual licence fees and the Universal Service Fund (USF) levy, despite repeated notices from the authority. The CA stated the outstanding amount currently totals Ksh48,874,524.10, comprising Ksh13,880,334.37 in licence fees and Ksh34,994,189.73 in USF levies. Each licence requires timely annual payment of these fees, a requirement the Mombasa-Road-based media outlet failed to meet despite several extensions and concessions provided by the regulator.

Timeline of Non-Compliance and Appeals The CA issued a Notice of Contravention to the Standard Group on December 4, 2025, which lapsed on January 17, 2026, highlighting non-compliance with licence terms. Following continued non-payment, the CA subsequently issued Notices of Revocation to all Standard Group stations on September 24, 2025, after the initial contravention notices expired. Earlier engagements between the CA and Standard Group occurred on June 14, 2025, December 4, 2025, and February 9, 2026, aimed at resolving the unpaid regulatory fees. On April 9, 2026, the CA informed the media group it would proceed to publish revocation notifications in the Kenya Gazette, as previous revocation notices had lapsed on March 24, 2026. While the media outlet did not dispute the debt in its appeal, it referenced a December 24, 2025, agreement outlining a payment plan with an initial Ksh10 million settlement. The group argued that the CA's revocation notices breached this agreement and infringed upon constitutional rights concerning freedom of expression and public communication. However, the tribunal ruled that the CA had provided ample opportunities for compliance over an extended period, emphasizing that obligations under KICA are clear and non-negotiable. This comes as some financial institutions, such as Diamond Trust Bank, have recently reported significant profit increases, underscoring the broader financial landscape businesses operate within. The tribunal ultimately dismissed the Standard Group's appeal.
