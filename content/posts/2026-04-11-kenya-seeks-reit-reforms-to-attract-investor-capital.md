## Kenyan REITs Push for Regulatory Overhaul Kenya's Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT) sector, with a market capitalization of US$189.5 million, is advocating for substantial reforms to its tax and regulatory framework. Industry stakeholders are pushing for reinstated tax incentives, clearer asset definitions, and flexible structural requirements to attract greater investor capital and unlock the market's full potential, aiming to bridge domestic funding with large-scale infrastructure projects.

Reforming the Tax Landscape for Predictability The African REIT market, totaling approximately US$30 billion, remains relatively small by global standards, with South Africa accounting for about 95% of its capitalization. In Kenya, a key impediment to growth has been the inconsistent application of tax incentives. Temporary exemptions on stamp duty and other transaction taxes previously stimulated REIT activity but lapsed in 2022, leading to a slowdown in deal flow. Currently, registered REITs in Kenya benefit from exemptions on corporate income tax for qualifying property income, and property transfers into REIT structures may qualify for VAT exemptions. However, industry participants emphasize that gaps persist. The REITs Association of Kenya (RAK) is actively campaigning for the reinstatement of stamp duty and capital gains exemptions, highlighting that eliminating the 4% levy on property transfers within REIT structures would significantly reduce entry costs and enhance the economic viability of listing infrastructure assets. A stable and predictable tax framework, aligned with global standards, is seen as crucial for lowering transaction costs and protecting investor returns.

Expanding Eligible Asset Definitions Regulatory ambiguity surrounding what constitutes an eligible asset is another constraint on the Kenyan REIT market. Inconsistent interpretations risk narrowing the scope of potential investments. Current regulations differentiate "real estate" in the legal sense from broader REIT-eligible assets, fostering uncertainty among investors. This lack of clarity has resulted in investors primarily defaulting to traditional property classes like land and buildings, thereby hindering innovation in emerging sectors such as digital and telecom infrastructure. Stakeholders are advocating for clearer policy direction to explicitly include non-traditional assets like telecom towers and data centers, a move that would align Africa with more developed REIT markets, such as the United States, where such infrastructure assets are already integrated into REIT frameworks.

Addressing Structural and Legal Complexities Beyond asset definitions, structural requirements, including minimum public float thresholds and investor participation rules, also influence market development. While these rules are intended to broaden ownership, regional comparisons suggest they may be ill-suited to early-stage markets with limited investor bases. REIT stakeholders propose a more flexible approach, such as allowing privately held or closely held REITs in initial phases, to build momentum before transitioning to wider public participation. Kenya's REIT framework further contends with structural uncertainty rooted in the legal complexity of trust-based vehicles. The concept of an "incorporated common law trust" remains poorly understood, creating confusion among legal practitioners, fund managers, and potential investors. This ambiguity has challenged the strengthening of trust in REIT structures, compounding existing tax considerations that have moderated adoption. Addressing such foundational legal clarity is crucial for any market, echoing sentiments around consistent application of legal frameworks in other sectors, such as those highlighted in the [TSC's Cruel Blunder: Fred Apima Obita TSC Ruling Exposes Rot](https://zandani.co.ke/article/tsc-cruel-blunder-fred-apima-obita-tsc-ruling-exposes-rot). Regulators and industry stakeholders are now examining recent reforms in collective investment schemes as a potential reference point for enhancing clarity and strengthening the framework. Updated rules in 2023 expanded permissible structures to include companies and limited liability partnerships, offering greater flexibility than the traditional unit trust model currently used by REITs. Comparisons with mature markets, where REITs are typically structured as companies, suggest that a shift toward a corporate REIT model in Kenya could address governance and alignment concerns, providing investors with more familiar oversight mechanisms and positioning REITs as a more attractive channel for long-term capital mobilization.
