## The EPL Dependency Trap: Why Your Jersey Spend Isn't the Real Villain

The common lament across Nairobi and Kampala is that our obsession with Arsenal or Manchester United is a form of cultural betrayal. This narrative suggests that if we simply stopped buying foreign merchandise, our local clubs would suddenly thrive. That is a lazy myth.

## The Myth of the Unpatriotic Fan

Blaming the fan for preferring the English Premier League (EPL) over the Kenyan Premier League is like blaming a passenger for choosing a jet over a donkey cart. The English Premier League generates over £340 million in international broadcast licensing alone, while local clubs often struggle to secure shirt sponsorships reaching a fraction of that in shillings. This disparity is not a failure of consumer patriotism. It is a failure of productization. When fans shell out cash for a jersey, they are paying for a complete experience: broadcast quality, security, professional marketing, and consistent scheduling.

To demand that Kenyans abandon their loyalty to Bukayo Saka in favor of a local league that frequently changes its kickoff times at the last minute is to ignore the reality of consumer behavior. People do not buy products out of moral obligation. They buy them because the quality meets their standards for entertainment. If our domestic clubs continue to provide a match day experience defined by crumbling stadium infrastructure and a total lack of digital engagement, no amount of shaming will shift the needle.

The institutional failure here is profound. While the EPL functions as a global media machine that sells a lifestyle, our clubs are often run as amateur entities that cannot even account for their own gate collections. We are witnessing the result of a sports industry that refuses to treat the fan as a customer. Much like we have seen in recent debates regarding [Understanding Data Privacy and Digital Tracking Practices](https://zandani.co.ke/article/understanding-data-privacy-and-digital-tracking-practices), organizations that fail to respect the data and preferences of their users will always lose them to platforms that provide better utility.

## The Infrastructure of Irrelevance

The disparity in revenue streams is the ultimate indictment of local management. The EPL operates with a sophisticated model where clubs earn millions through commercial partnerships and global licensing, while many of our teams rely on the whims of political patrons or corporate charity. There is no clear pathway for a club like AFC Leopards or Gor Mahia to monetize their massive, passionate fanbases because they do not own the vertical chain of their product. They do not control the ticketing systems, they do not own the stadia, and they do not create content that keeps fans engaged outside of the ninety minutes on the pitch.

When President Yoweri Museveni and others lament the drain of resources to foreign leagues, they miss the target. The problem is not the £300,000 being spent on a kit import. The problem is the vacuum in local management that makes that kit the only viable professional option available to the average fan. Investors will not touch a league where the governance is opaque and the commercial brand is non-existent.

## Reclaiming the Market through Professionalism

We must move away from the expectation that local support is a charity drive. The burden of proof lies with the club administrations. They need to professionalize their internal data management, invest in stadiums that serve as year-round entertainment hubs, and market their players as stars who are accessible to the public. If a local team can provide a high-quality match day experience and a digital product that creates a sense of community, the fans will return. 

Stop asking fans to ignore the world-class spectacle of European football. Start building a product that can actually compete with it. The solution to our football crisis is not moralizing to the youth; it is professionalizing the boardroom. Unless clubs shift from being vanity projects to being commercial enterprises, they will remain exactly where they are-on the periphery of their own fans' attention.
