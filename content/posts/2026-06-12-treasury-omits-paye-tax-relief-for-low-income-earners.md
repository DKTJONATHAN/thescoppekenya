## The Missing Forty Billion Shilling Promise

Treasury Cabinet Secretary John Mbadi omitted the long-awaited PAYE tax relief for low-income workers from the 2026/2027 fiscal budget presented to Parliament on Friday, leaving over one million Kenyans earning below 30,000 shillings without the financial reprieve they were promised.

The omission marks a significant pivot from earlier executive assurances that suggested a revised tax structure for the country's most vulnerable earners would be a centerpiece of the current financial cycle. Instead of the expected relief, the budget speech focused on revenue stabilization measures. This shift leaves a specific gap in household liquidity for workers who have seen the cost of living climb steadily since the turn of the year. While the focus remains on macro-economic stability, the immediate result is that the anticipated monthly boost to basic take-home pay has been sidelined indefinitely.

### The Fiscal Math Behind the Decision

Government analysts have cited a projected revenue shortfall of 40 billion shillings as the primary driver for excluding the tax changes. This figure represents the total amount the state would have forfeited had it implemented the zero-rated or reduced PAYE status for those earning under the 30,000 shilling threshold. Officials maintain that the current economic climate requires strict adherence to revenue targets to avoid expanding the existing deficit. By prioritizing the collection of this 40 billion shillings, the Treasury has effectively opted to balance the national ledger at the direct expense of the lowest wage bracket.

For an employee earning 30,000 shillings, the absence of this relief means a continued monthly deduction that consumes a significant portion of their disposable income. Without the promised adjustment, these workers must continue to stretch their net earnings across rising transport, energy, and food costs. While the government has emphasized that the proposal is not off the table entirely, the lack of a formal implementation timeline in the official budget documents signals that no immediate change is expected in the near term. ### The Policy Gap and Future Uncertainty

The government's stance is that the tax relief measure remains under active consideration as part of broader fiscal reforms. However, parliamentary sources indicate that no specific budgetary allocation has been ring-fenced to initiate this relief during the upcoming quarters. The administration is instead focusing on optimizing existing collection methods to meet its ambitious targets for the 2026/2027 period. This strategy essentially treats the 40 billion shillings as a necessary buffer to prevent further borrowing, even though the political optics of leaving low-income earners behind remain contentious.

Observers are now looking toward the supplementary budget cycles that typically occur later in the fiscal year. These secondary windows serve as the most likely venue for the Treasury to reintroduce the relief package if revenue performance exceeds expectations.
