The High Court, in a ruling by Justice Lawrence Mugambi on Thursday, March 19, 2026, has prohibited telecommunication companies from reassigning registered mobile phone numbers without the owner's explicit consent. The court declared these numbers constitute personal data, protected under the Constitution.

Safeguarding Digital Identities in Kenya

Court Affirms Constitutional Protection Justice Lawrence Mugambi, delivering the ruling on Thursday, March 19, affirmed that mobile numbers function as digital identifiers, intrinsically linking various pieces of information to an individual's private affairs. The judgment referenced Articles 31 (c) and (d) of the Constitution, which protect the right against unnecessary exposure of private information. The court highlighted that mobile numbers frequently carry data revealing an individual's financial, social, and personal activities, making their protection crucial in the current digital landscape.

Mandate for Attorney General The court has further directed the Office of the Attorney General to implement all necessary measures within six months to safeguard digital identities connected with registered numbers. This directive underscores the urgency of establishing robust frameworks to protect citizens' digital footprints.

Origins of the Landmark Petition The ruling originated from a petition filed in June 2024, which aimed to prevent telecommunication companies from recycling mobile numbers. Petitioners argued that the reassignment of deactivated, yet previously registered, phone numbers posed a significant threat to user privacy. They asserted that such numbers are an integral component of an individual's digital identity, linked to sensitive personal information, and their reassignment could expose users' data to third parties without explicit consent. The petition sought a declaration affirming the existence of a personal digital identity and that a registered phone number is a core part of it, comparable to a national ID or passport. This concern aligns with ongoing discussions about digital financial regulations, such as the Kenya Treasury's proposed draft regulations for virtual asset service providers.

Protections for Vulnerable Individuals The case also brought to light specific concerns affecting incarcerated individuals, who often lose access to their registered numbers during extended periods of non-use. This situation creates a vulnerability where their personal data could be exposed if their numbers are reassigned.

Strict Conditions for Number Reassignment Justice Mugambi emphasized that any reassignment of mobile numbers must occur under stringent conditions designed to protect the privacy of the former owner. The court mandated that the previous owner's informed and verifiable consent must be secured prior to any reassignment. Additionally, reassignment is permissible only after a reasonable period following a public notice, which must be preceded by a documented verification process confirming that the original owner cannot be reached or has unequivocally relinquished rights to the number. Technical safeguards are also required to prevent unauthorized exposure or transfer of personal data linked to the previous owner when a number is reassigned or recycled.
