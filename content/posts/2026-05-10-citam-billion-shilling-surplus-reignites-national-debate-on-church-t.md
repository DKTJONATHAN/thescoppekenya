The Christ is the Answer Ministries (CITAM) organization revealed a surplus exceeding one billion shillings in its 2023 annual report, sparking intense nationwide debate regarding the commercialization of faith, the tax-exempt status of religious institutions, and the growing financial burden placed upon congregants.

## Financial Transparency and the Corporate Pulpit

### The Business of Salvation
The disclosure of a billion-shilling surplus has transformed the discourse surrounding religious institutions in Kenya. While some supporters interpret this figure as a hallmark of sound organizational management, a growing segment of the public views the church as a modern, profit-driven corporation. This perspective highlights a fundamental tension between traditional houses of worship and the contemporary model that emphasizes fiscal growth. As economic conditions tighten for the average Kenyan, the sight of a religious institution accumulating wealth on this scale has led to claims that the modern church prioritizes corporate revenue over spiritual service.

### The Debate Over Tax-Exempt Status
At the heart of the national conversation is the legal status that grants religious organizations immunity from standard taxation. Critics argue that when a church generates a surplus comparable to that of a mid-sized financial institution, the traditional justification for tax exemption no longer applies. The argument posits that these organizations should be treated as commercial entities if their financial output mirrors that of blue-chip corporations. As the government seeks new avenues for revenue collection, many citizens are calling for a policy shift that would strip religious bodies of their tax-exempt status to ensure equitable contributions to the national economy.

### Economic Disillusionment Among Congregants
The disparity between the opulent growth of large religious organizations and the financial instability of the average attendee has become a focal point of public frustration. Many congregants, particularly the younger generation, feel that their contributions are fueling a surplus rather than supporting community welfare. This sense of disillusionment is further exacerbated by the harsh economic climate, which makes the constant demand for tithes and offerings increasingly difficult to satisfy. The prevailing public sentiment suggests that if the church adopts the operational structure of a business, it must also accept the financial responsibilities and fiscal oversight inherent in the corporate sector.
