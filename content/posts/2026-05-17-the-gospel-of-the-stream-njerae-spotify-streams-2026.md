In the year of our Lord 1455, a whisper began in Mainz, a subtle hum from the workshop of Johannes Gutenberg. Not the hallowed chant of the Gregorian, but the rhythmic clatter of metal types, a sound that promised a new kind of revelation. Before this, the divine word, and indeed all worthy thought, was a jewel guarded by scribes in monasteries, painstakingly copied, page by arduous page. A single tome, heavy with parchment and wisdom, cost a king's ransom and took a lifetime to reproduce. Access to knowledge, therefore, was a privilege, a sacred trust bestowed upon the chosen few by divine appointment or noble birth.

Then came the press, a contraption of wood and iron, spewing forth copies with an alarming, almost impious, speed. Suddenly, the sacred texts, once rare as dragon’s teeth, began to multiply like loaves and fishes. It was not long before a certain Augustinian friar, Martin Luther, gazed upon the lavish sale of indulgences - papal pardons for sins, peddled by the likes of Johann Tetzel throughout the German territories. These parchment slips, promising to spring souls from purgatory for a coin, were the Church’s most lucrative revenue stream, endorsed by none other than Pope Leo X himself, ever in need of ducats for St. Peter's Basilica and his own Medici extravagances. Luther, a man of God with a scholar’s keen eye, saw not salvation, but a blatant market transaction. On October 31, 1517, he famously nailed his Ninety-five Theses to the door of the Castle Church in Wittenberg, a simple act intended for academic debate, yet, thanks to Gutenberg’s marvel, it became a rallying cry heard across a continent. Theses, once mere Latin points for scholarly discourse, were translated into German, printed, and circulated with astonishing velocity, bypassing the bishops and chancellors, going directly to the common man, sparking a fire that the Pope’s mighty pronouncements could not extinguish.

The irony, of course, was that the very technology that printed the Pope's indulgences also printed Luther's indictment of them. The unmediated word, once the exclusive domain of the pulpit, was now in the hands of the ploughman, the weaver, the tavern keeper. New interpretations bloomed like wildflowers after a spring rain, challenging centuries of established authority. The old gatekeepers suddenly found their gates rendered obsolete, their pronouncements drowned out by a cacophony of newly literate voices. The sacred became profane, or perhaps, the profane became sacred, depending on one’s allegiance.
