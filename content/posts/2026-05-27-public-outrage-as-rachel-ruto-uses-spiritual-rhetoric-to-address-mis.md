## Spiritual Rhetoric and National Accountability

The First Lady has frequently framed the rising cost of maternal healthcare and the surge in reports of missing children as spiritual challenges. Rather than proposing specific policy reforms or increased security measures, she has advocated for fasting and divine intervention. This approach has frustrated families who are demanding tangible police action and affordable medical services. The recurring use of religious language suggests that the current administration views administrative responsibilities as spiritual battles rather than state obligations.

### Prayer as a Substitute for Policy

Kenyans are feeling the financial pressure as the cost of delivery and postnatal care becomes increasingly unaffordable for the average citizen. While families seek government subsidies or healthcare reform, the First Lady continues to offer petitions to the heavens. Many parents of missing children feel that the focus on spiritual warfare diminishes the urgency of police investigations. This reliance on faith to explain systemic failures has led to a growing disconnect between the executive office and the daily struggles of the population.

### Political Ambitions Disguised as Faith

Observers have noted that the First Lady often mentions double double blessings during these religious convocations. Critics interpret this specific phrasing as a subtle political campaign for the Ruto administration to secure a second term in office. This focus on securing a future electoral mandate through spiritual narratives occurs while the public is struggling with immediate economic instability. The alignment of religious faith with political longevity has raised concerns about the transparency of the current leadership and its priorities.

### The Dangers of State Sponsored Mysticism

The rhetoric used at State House has drawn comparisons to the late cult leader Yehova Wanyonyi. By treating economic and security failures as issues that require miracles, the administration risks being seen as detached from the reality of governance. Many citizens find it insensitive to offer prayers as a substitute for lowering hospital fees. There is a palpable sense of exhaustion among Kenyans who seek concrete legislative solutions to the high cost of living instead of theological explanations for government inaction.
