## Border Security and the 2027 Voter Roll Integrity

Residents in Busia are questioning the legitimacy of local identification processes as reports emerge of foreign nationals securing Kenyan documentation. This administrative friction creates significant anxiety about the accuracy of the voter register as the country moves toward the 2027 General Elections.

The porous nature of the border between Kenya and Uganda has long been a part of life for communities in Busia. However, when administrative loopholes allow non-citizens to obtain Kenyan identity cards, the conversation shifts from regional cooperation to national security. Many local residents now fear that these documentation discrepancies are not just isolated bureaucratic errors, but systemic issues that could compromise the integrity of the next national vote.

### Is the voter register becoming vulnerable to manipulation?

The core of the concern lies in the link between a national identity card and the voter registration process. In Kenya, the ID card is the foundational document for verifying citizenship and eligibility to participate in an election. If the registration process is compromised at the point of issuance, the ripple effects are felt at the ballot box. 

Local voters have raised alarms, claiming that vetting procedures appear to be applied unevenly. Some residents report that the process for verifying Kenyan heritage feels more strenuous for them than the documentation process offered to foreign residents. These allegations of discriminatory vetting have eroded public confidence in the local administrative offices tasked with maintaining the integrity of the registry. When a community feels that the foundational document of their citizenship is being diluted, the perceived legitimacy of every future ballot is called into question.

### What is the government's current position?

Immigration Principal Secretary Belio Kipsang has addressed the issue by directing local administrators to formalize the status of Ugandan nationals who have married into Kenyan families. The official stance is that these administrative steps are necessary to account for the reality of cross-border families and regional integration. 

However, for many in Busia, the instruction to formalize these documents raises as many questions as it answers. Citizens are asking for transparency regarding how these formalizations are verified to ensure that only those legally entitled to Kenyan citizenship are granted the right to vote. Without clear, public-facing safeguards to prevent the inclusion of ineligible individuals on the voter roll, the government risks losing the trust of the electorate long before the campaign season begins.
