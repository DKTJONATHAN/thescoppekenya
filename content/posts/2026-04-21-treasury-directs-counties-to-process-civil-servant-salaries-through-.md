The National Treasury has mandated that all county government civil servant salaries for April 2026 must be processed exclusively through the integrated HRIS-Kenya Payroll System and IFMIS platform. This directive, issued on April 8, 2026, by Principal Secretary Chris Kiptoo, aims to enhance payroll management across state institutions.

## Treasury Streamlines County Payroll Processing

### Integrated Systems for Enhanced Efficiency
The directive from Treasury Principal Secretary Chris Kiptoo, detailed in a circular dated April 8, 2026, requires all County Executive Committee Members for Finance and County Assembly Clerks to utilize the combined HRIS-Kenya Payroll System and Integrated Financial Management Information System (IFMIS). This integrated approach manages government employee records and salaries, alongside budgeting, procurement, and public fund tracking. The primary objective is to streamline statutory deductions, including Pay as You Earn (PAYE), Social Health Authority (SHA), and National Social Security Fund (NSSF), ensuring their automatic processing and direct remittance to relevant government entities. The integration has undergone successful piloting across various national government state departments and some county assemblies.

### Compliance Mandate and Consequences
Kiptoo underscored that the Office of the Controller of Budget will only approve exchequer requests for salaries submitted through the integrated module. This means that any county officers' salaries not processed via the new integrated system risk delays or rejection for the April 2026 payroll. All accounting officers are urged to fully comply to facilitate constitutional entities in executing their mandatory duties and to prevent disruptions in salary disbursement this April. This measure aligns with the Public Finance Management Act, 2012, intending to improve accountability and efficiency in public fund management across both national and county governments.

### Tackling Payroll Fraud and Ghost Workers
The Treasury's directive follows concerning reports from Auditor-General Nancy Gathungu. Her audit for the 2024/25 financial year uncovered potential losses exceeding Ksh33 billion attributed to ghost workers in counties, signaling widespread payroll fraud. The audit specifically identified Ksh978 million paid to suspected ghost workers after nearly 600 county employees failed physical verification across 26 counties. Machakos, Mandera, and Kajiado counties were noted for significant payroll integrity concerns. According to the Auditor-General's findings, several counties made payments to staff not recorded in the official payroll system, contrary to public finance regulations. In some instances, casual workers, contract staff, and alleged ‘special duty’ officers received payments through vouchers and bank transfers without proper documentation or approval from the County Public Service Boards, with some beneficiaries' names absent from any official employment records.
