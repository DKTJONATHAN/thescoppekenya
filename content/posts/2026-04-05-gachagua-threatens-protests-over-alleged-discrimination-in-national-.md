Celestine Nzioka, For Za Ndani Former Deputy President Rigathi Gachagua today threatened to mobilize Gen Zs for government office occupations, alleging discrimination in national identification document issuance. Speaking at a Gatundu North church service, Gachagua claimed a ploy to hinder youth from regions not supporting the Kenya Kwanza regime from obtaining IDs.

Allegations of ID Issuance Bias

Gachagua's Demands and Upcoming Meeting During his address, Mr. Gachagua stated that reports indicate national IDs are not being issued in areas perceived as unsupportive of William Ruto. He announced that opposition figures would convene on Monday, April 6, 2026, to determine a course of action. The former Deputy President vowed to initiate protests should this alleged discriminatory practice persist, specifically threatening to appeal to Gen Zs to occupy government offices across the country, citing them as the "President's weakness". Without these crucial identification documents, individuals cannot register as new voters. This directly impacts the Independent Electoral and Boundaries Commission's (IEBC) ongoing Continuous Voter Registration exercise, which commenced on March 30, 2026, targeting 2.5 million new registrations.

Uncollected Documents and IEBC Voter Register Scrutiny Mr. Gachagua's remarks coincide with reports from Huduma Kenya indicating a substantial backlog of uncollected documents. Currently, 152,398 national IDs, 85,550 smart driving licenses, and 28,550 birth certificates remain idle in government offices, raising questions about both public access and administrative efficiency. These administrative challenges add to a broader spectrum of public concerns, including environmental issues where, for instance, Senator Omtatah warns of deforestation as companies switch to firewood fuel. Additionally, Mr. Gachagua criticized the IEBC regarding its recent statements on voters registered before 2012. The commission initially caused apprehension by suggesting some individuals might need to re-register. However, the IEBC later clarified that only those who failed to undergo biometric registration in 2012 are required to re-register, confirming the credibility of the current biometric voter register. Mr. Gachagua confirmed that the opposition plans another meeting to discuss the IEBC's operational specifics before issuing a formal statement.
