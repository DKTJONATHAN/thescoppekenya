The KUCCPS portal opened yesterday, April 7, 2026, giving the nearly one million 2025 KCSE candidates until May 6 to select their university and college courses and secure their academic futures before the month-long window closes. <p>I have seen this cycle repeat for over a decade, and if there is one thing I have learned, it is that the first week of the KUCCPS application is always a mix of adrenaline and anxiety. Yesterday, Education Cabinet Secretary Julius Ogamba met with the KUCCPS team, led by CEO Dr. Agnes Wahome, to give the green light. Now, the 270,715 students who scored a C+ and above are scrambling to figure out if their dreams of becoming doctors or engineers align with the realities of their cluster points.</p> <h2>The Deadline and the First Steps</h2> <p>You have exactly four weeks. The portal closes on May 6, 2026. My advice? Do not wait until May 5. Over the years, I have watched the system crawl to a standstill in the final hours because everyone decided to hit 'submit' at the same time. The process starts at the official portal: kuccps.net/login. You will need three things to get in: your KCSE index number, the year you sat your exams (2025), and a password. For most of you, your password will be either your KCPE index number or your birth certificate number.</p> <p>If you are one of the 993,226 candidates who sat for the exams last year, you should already be thinking about your options. Even if you didn't hit the C+ mark for a degree, there are diploma and certificate slots in TVETs that are arguably more marketable in this current economy. The transition from the 8-4-4 system is nearly complete, and the stakes for the 2026/2027 academic year placement are higher than ever.</p> <h2>Understanding Cluster Points and How They Work</h2> <p>I often tell parents that a C+ is not just a C+. Depending on which subjects you performed well in, your "weighted cluster point" will determine if you get into that prestigious law school or if you have to pivot to another social science. KUCCPS uses 20 distinct clusters. Each course, from Medicine to Fine Arts, has four mandatory subjects that are used to calculate your points. </p> <p>For instance, if you want to study Engineering, the system looks at your performance in Mathematics, Physics, Chemistry, and either English or Geography. You calculate your cluster points by comparing your performance in these four subjects against the maximum possible points. For this 2026 cycle, KUCCPS has uploaded the 2024 cut-off points as a reference. Use them. They are the best barometer you have to see if your 38 points can realistically get you into a competitive course at the University of Nairobi or if you should look at newer institutions like the Open University of Kenya.</p> <h2>Choosing the Right Institution: Public vs Private</h2> <p>This is where things get tricky for the pocket. In my years covering this, the financial conversation has become just as important as the academic one. There are 42 public universities available this year. If you get placed in any of these, you are eligible for both government scholarships and HELB loans. This is the gold standard for most Kenyan families because it significantly lowers the out-of-pocket costs.</p> <p>Then there are the 30 private universities. While they offer great facilities and often more stable academic calendars, there is a catch. If you choose a private university through KUCCPS, you can get a HELB loan, but you will not receive a government scholarship. I have seen many families get excited about a placement in a top-tier private school only to realize later that the tuition gap is more than they can handle. Talk to your parents or guardians now before you click that final button.</p> <h2>How to Select Your Courses Wisely</h2> <p>The application form allows you to pick several choices. Most students make the mistake of filling all their slots with the same competitive course across different universities. If you put "Bachelor of Medicine" as your first, second, and third choice in three different top-tier universities, and you don't meet the cut-off for the first one, you likely won't meet it for the others either. You are essentially wasting your slots.</p> <p>Spread your risks. Your first choice should be your dream course. Your second and third choices should be realistic alternatives that you would still be happy to study. Your fourth choice should be a safety net—a course you are absolutely certain your points will cover. I remember a friend's daughter who missed out on a placement entirely because she only chose competitive nursing programs in Nairobi and ignored the colleges in the periphery. She had to wait for the second revision, which is a stressful experience nobody needs.</p> <h2>Final Thoughts for the Class of 2025</h2> <p>The 2025 KCSE results showed a significant improvement, with over 270,000 students qualifying for degrees. This means the competition for slots in traditional "power" courses will be fierce. Do not just follow your friends. I have seen students pick courses because their 'squad' is going to a certain university in Nakuru or Eldoret, only to regret it two years later when they realize the course doesn't fit their career goals.</p> <p>Take this month to research. Visit the portals of the universities you are considering. Check their faculty strength and their proximity to home. Once you have made your decision, log into the KUCCPS portal, follow the prompts, pay the application fee via the provided mobile money channel, and ensure you get a confirmation message. This is the first real adult decision you are making; take it with the seriousness it deserves.</p>
