## Nairobi Shivers While Mitumba Traders Make a Killing Kenya Meteorological Service Extends Cold Weather Alert to June 2 is the central subject of the update.

The Kenya Meteorological Service Authority has stretched its cold weather alert until June 22, forcing residents to swap light layers for heavy sweaters as temperatures in highland regions plummet to single digits. This week-long deep freeze is changing daily routines from Githurai to Eldoret.

While the coast enjoys the typical sun, the rest of the country is currently bracing for an extended period of biting air and grey skies. The latest forecast confirms that low-lying fog and persistent drizzle will remain the status quo for at least another seven days. From the rolling hills of Bomet to the expansive landscapes of Laikipia, the mornings have turned into a battle against the elements. Those in Nyahururu and Eldoret are reporting lows dipping between 4 and 8 degrees Celsius, temperatures that have transformed the daily commute into an endurance test for many.

### The Survivalist Economy of Warmth

Marketplaces are seeing a frantic shift in consumer behavior as the mercury drops. Second-hand clothes sellers, known locally as mitumba traders, are reporting their best sales figures in months. Woolen coats, heavy blankets, and thick socks have become the most sought-after commodities in downtown Nairobi and regional hubs. Even with the recent economic shifts that saw [investors re-evaluating their portfolios after the Saudi spending spree review](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-17-end-of-the-saudi-spending-spree-pif-review-sparks-shockwave), regular Kenyans are prioritizing warmth above all else. Local tea kiosks are doing brisk business, with vendors noting that customers are lingering longer to escape the damp chill, often pairing their cups with heavy snacks to keep the internal heat running. Some are even finding comfort in gadgets designed for long-term endurance, like the recently released [vivo Y31d with its massive 7200mAh battery](https://zandani.co.ke/article/vivo-y31d-launches-in-kenya-7200mAh-battery), which helps users stay connected for longer stretches without needing to leave their warm blankets to find a power socket.

### Voices from the Frost

In Nyahururu, residents describe the current conditions as some of the most uncomfortable in recent memory. Samuel Maina, a local farmer, says that the frost is so thick in the early mornings that the grass looks like it has been dusted with sugar. He notes that getting out of bed at five in the morning is now the hardest part of the day, with many choosing to wait until the sun makes a brief, weak appearance before starting their chores. Meanwhile, in Eldoret, college students are banding together to share resources. Sarah Wanjiku, a student at a local institution, mentioned that everyone is living in their heaviest hoodies and using extra blankets scavenged from home just to study at night. In the bustling streets of Nakuru, motorcycle riders are struggling to maintain their schedules as the wind bites through even the thickest jackets. Many riders are now pulling over frequently just to rub their hands together near exhaust pipes or seek shelter in roadside shops, turning the simple act of transport into a series of short, shivering sprints.

### Looking Ahead to June 22

Public sentiment online is a mix of fatigue and resignation. On social media platforms, users are sharing memes about how the June 22 deadline feels like an eternity away. The conversation has moved beyond mere weather updates into a collective ritual of sharing survival tips, from traditional recipes for herbal teas that promise to keep the chest warm to debates on the most effective layering techniques. The meteorological authority warns that the Lake Victoria Basin and the Rift Valley should remain alert for more rain, which threatens to dampen the spirits of those hoping for a sudden return to the warm, sunny days typical of this season. As the country waits for the clouds to break, the focus remains on endurance. With seven more days of cold on the horizon, the culture of communal warmth and the reliance on local, hardy solutions continue to define the Kenyan experience. Everyone is counting down the days until the calendar flips past the 22nd, hoping that the arrival of clearer, warmer skies will finally allow the country to thaw out and return to the normal pace of life.

The mood remains stoic, with every cup of hot chai and every heavy coat serving as a quiet defiance against the biting frost.
