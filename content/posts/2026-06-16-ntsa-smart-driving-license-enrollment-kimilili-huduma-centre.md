## Bringing Digital Licensing to Bungoma Doorsteps

Motorists in Kimilili no longer have to endure the arduous journey to regional headquarters to secure their credentials. A three-day mobile registration drive at the local Huduma Centre has finally bridged the gap for drivers who have historically been sidelined by geography.

### Why is this shift away from central processing hubs necessary?

For years, transitioning from the outdated, bulky red booklet license to the modern smart card felt like an impossible hurdle for many rural drivers. To obtain the biometric-enabled card, residents of Bungoma often had to travel as far as Kisumu or Nairobi. This created a hidden tax on the rural motorist. Beyond the Ksh3,050 processing fee, drivers faced significant loss of income from missed workdays and the high cost of cross-county travel.

By bringing the biometric capture equipment directly to the Kimilili Huduma Centre, the National Transport and Safety Authority is essentially removing the barrier of distance. This initiative recognizes that a driver operating a tractor or a matatu in Western Kenya requires the same digital utility as a motorist in the capital. The older booklet system was prone to forgery and lacked integration with modern enforcement databases, whereas the new card provides a reliable, traceable record of one’s driving history.

### What are local motorists saying about this convenience?

The atmosphere at the Huduma Centre this week reflects a sense of relief among local transport operators. John Wekesa, a commercial driver based in Kimilili, shared that he had put off upgrading his license for over three years due to the logistical nightmare of the registration process. He noted that paying his fee and having his fingerprints captured locally took less than an hour, a stark contrast to the multi-day trip he once dreaded.

For many, the smart license is more than just a piece of plastic. It represents a shift toward a more equitable system where government services follow the citizen rather than the other way around. Drivers who previously relied on the physical booklet felt disconnected from the national transport infrastructure. With the enrollment window remaining open through June 19, there is a visible push to ensure that local motorists are finally brought into the digital fold.

### How does this move change the road licensing experience?

The move toward full digitization reflects a broader national strategy to secure and streamline documentation. While recent [national cybersecurity improvements](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-05-06-kenya-reports-decline-in-national-cybersecurity-threats) have helped bolster confidence in government portals, the primary win here is accessibility. The smart license serves as a single, durable source of truth for traffic officers. It eliminates the confusion often caused by worn-out paper permits that became illegible over time.

As the government moves away from the legacy systems that defined the industry in 2017 and 2018, the decentralization of these services will likely set a precedent for other regions. The efficiency of this three-day window suggests that the infrastructure for digital inclusion is becoming more robust. If the Kimilili pilot demonstrates success, it could easily serve as a blueprint for other sub-counties where motorists remain trapped in the analog era. Localized service delivery is not just about convenience; it is about ensuring that every Kenyan driver, regardless of their location, has the same opportunity to operate within the law without undue hardship.

Localizing biometric capture means no more lost wages or expensive travel to chase a simple document.
