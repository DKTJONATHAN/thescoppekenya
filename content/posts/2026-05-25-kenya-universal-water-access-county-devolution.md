## Kenya’s Water Sector: From State Mandate to Market Logic

Kenya is transitioning from a state-led water management model to a commercialized, devolved system. Through the Water (Amendment) Act 2024, the government seeks to address systemic gaps by empowering counties and inviting private capital to the table.

The history of water in the republic has often been a story of unmet expectations. For decades, the centralized model operated on the assumption that the state could indefinitely carry the burden of infrastructure and delivery. This period viewed water as a social right, funded by the national treasury. However, as urbanization accelerated and existing pipes aged, the state's ability to maintain this social contract withered. We now face a landscape where the old ways are no longer sustainable, leading to a pragmatic, if somewhat weary, embrace of the private sector.

### The Commercialization of a Resource

The Water (Amendment) Act 2024 represents a definitive pivot away from the post-independence mindset. It shifts the focus toward bulk water development through Public-Private Partnerships. This move effectively transfers the weight of infrastructure investment from the taxpayer to the private investor. In this new era, water is treated less as a guaranteed state gift and more as a performance-based commodity. The introduction of smart metering and performance-based financing is designed to ensure financial viability, but it also signals that the days of heavily subsidized access are fading.

This shift creates a clear distinction between regions. Counties with the resources to prove their revenue-collection capabilities will likely see a surge in private interest. Meanwhile, resource-poor areas may find themselves struggling to attract the same level of investment. The 2024 Act tells a clear story: the government is moving away from a system of universal state responsibility toward a model of selective efficiency.

### The Reality of the Infrastructure Gap

To understand why this change is occurring, one must look at the sobering data regarding current service levels. National service coverage currently stands at 44 percent. While this figure is the focus of much debate, the sanitation statistics reveal a deeper crisis. According to current data, sanitation access stands at 40.9 percent across the country. More specifically, sewerage coverage is a mere 16 percent. These figures highlight a historical neglect of the wastewater side of the equation, a failure that the new legislation seeks to rectify by requiring counties to integrate sanitation into their development plans.

### Devolution as the New Mandate

The role of the Council of Governors, led by figures like Mary Mwiti, has become central to this evolution. As service delivery is fully devolved to the 47 counties, the pressure to meet the 2030 universal access targets is immense. The 2024 legislative framework requires Water Service Providers to act with the efficiency of private corporations. This is a significant burden for counties that lack the massive capital injections once promised by the central government.

By shifting the responsibility to the local level and the private sector, the state is making a calculated bet. It assumes that the market can succeed where centralized ministries have failed. The success of this move will be measured by whether the country can bridge the gap from 44 percent coverage to the 100 percent goal in less than a decade. It is a formidable challenge set against a backdrop of increasing debt and rising costs for the end user.

### Why it matters now

The implementation of the 2024 Act will fundamentally change the relationship between Kenyans and their water supply. As the 2030 deadline for universal access approaches, the move toward private bulk water development means that costs will likely reflect the true price of service delivery. For many, the era of the state-subsidized faucet is coming to a close.

### What to watch next

The negotiations between the Council of Governors and national regulators like WASREB will be critical. These discussions will determine if counties retain control over their resources or if they will function primarily as intermediaries for private developers. The initial rollout of smart metering in urban centers will serve as the first real test of whether this commercialized model can actually deliver on its promises.
