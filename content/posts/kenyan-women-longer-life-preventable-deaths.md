**The Scoop**
Walahi, this news headline saying Kenyan women live longer, then immediately hitting us with "but still die from preventable diseases" feels like a whole mood swing, bana. Like, are we celebrating or crying? The latest Population Situation Analysis (PSA) confirms what we low-key knew: our Kenyan mamas are outliving men. Their life expectancy is nearly 67 years now, which is kinda cool, signaling better survival for kids and public health steps. But then you deep-dive, and it's like, what's the actual flex if preventable stuff is still taking lives? It's giving "two steps forward, one step back," jo.

**The Evidence**
So, why do women live longer, eh? Apparently, itâ€™s a mix of biology na behavior. Angela Nguku from White Ribbon Alliance Kenya anasema women tend to seek care early, hawajiingizi kwa high-risk stuff kama kukurukakara za barabara, and build strong social networks. Biologically, the two X chromosomes and estrogen give women some protection, while high testosterone in men is linked to higher risks. Plus, women are just better at using healthcare, from antenatal visits hadi kupeleka watoto chanjo, absorbing all that health info. Men, on the other hand, wanadread hospitals, ati until it's too late. Manze, sisi huona!

Lakini the twist comes hapa: despite all that, preventable deaths are still mad high, especially for mothers, newborns, children, and the poor. Dr. Grace Kanyi, OB/GYN, calls maternal health the "loudest contradiction." Ati 355 deaths per 100,000 live births? That's way off the Sustainable Development Goal target ya 70. And the causes aren't even mysterious - severe bleeding, infection, obstructed labor. Things modern healthcare *should* handle. She says a pregnant woman needs skilled staff, essential medicines, blood ready, and a referral system that actually works. But the PSA shows our health system snaps at these points: too few doctors and nurses, uneven distribution, stock-outs. Health coverage even dropped between 2018 na 2024. Walae, it's a mess.

Chronic diseases like hypertension, diabetes, and cancer are also stacking up, accounting for almost 40% of deaths. And you know how it goes - women often delay screening because family needs come first. Kwanza, the asks from women, like respectful care, skilled providers, and blood in emergencies, are "not big asks," according to Ms. Nguku. They're just the basics! Young women and adolescents are also at serious risk, with anaemia, cancers, and teenage pregnancies at 15%-a reality fueled by poverty and harmful norms. And to top it all off, we don't even count deaths properly. Death registration is barely 45%, meaning most deaths happen bila cause documented. How can we fix what we don't even track, jo? **The Verdict**
So, the long and short of it is, yeah, biology might give Kenyan women a slight edge, but chromosomes alone won't protect them from a system that's still letting them down. It's not about if women are strong enough, but if our health infrastructure and societal structures are. The Ministry of Health has this Rapid Response Initiative going till June 2026 to cut preventable deaths, which is a start. But bana, we need more than initiatives. We need real, functional systems and accountability. Because living longer shouldn't mean just surviving to face preventable tragedies. We deserve better, manze.
