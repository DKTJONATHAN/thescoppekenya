## Glasgow 2026: A Blueprint Kenya Cannot Afford to Ignore

As Glasgow officially opens its doors for the 2026 Commonwealth Games, the disparity between Scottish multi-sport readiness and the dilapidated state of Kenyan facilities becomes impossible to ignore for those watching from Nairobi.

While global headlines focus on the preliminary bowls competitions and the designated flagbearers for Scotland, Jersey, and Guernsey, the real story for Kenya lies in the legacy planning. Glasgow is utilizing existing world-class infrastructure to host this international gathering, ensuring that the taxpayer investment is not buried in white elephant projects. This model of sustainable venue utilization stands in stark contrast to the trend seen back home, where [government-led infrastructure projects](https://zandani.co.ke/article/government-launches-multi-billion-shilling-infrastructure-projects-i) often prioritize short-term political optics over the long-term utility required by elite athletes.

### The Myth of Temporary Readiness

Preparation for these Games is not a last-minute scramble. Teams from Wales, Northern Ireland, and Scotland have spent years integrating athlete support services into their established sporting hubs. Athletes like Merriens, De Sousa, and Woodward are training in facilities designed for consistency rather than the patch-work renovations that plague Kenyan stadiums. In Glasgow, the infrastructure supports a 36-year cycle of maintenance and usage. In Kenya, we are still debating the basic completion of regional tracks.

The current atmosphere in Scotland demonstrates that international sporting success is rooted in institutional stability. When we look at the preparation of individuals like Kelly, Marshall, or Adlington, we see the result of a system that views sports as a permanent industrial asset. Kenya currently struggles with a different kind of scarcity; even while we grapple with a [deepening student loan crisis](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenya-student-loan-crisis-deepens-helb-defaults-surge), the national conversation on athletic funding remains superficial. We talk about medal tallies every four years, yet we fail to build the training environments that make those medals a statistical inevitability rather than a miracle.

### Lessons in Legacy Planning

The Glasgow model forces a rethink of how we allocate public resources. Host nations today are moving away from the construction of massive, singular-use stadiums. Instead, they focus on retrofitting urban spaces to serve local communities long after the closing ceremony. This is an efficient use of capital that Kenyan administrators seem unable to grasp. We continue to see massive budget outlays on projects that lack a clear, functional future beyond a singular sporting event. 

The logistical machine behind these Games-coordinating international teams and media profiling for hundreds of athletes-is a testament to professionalized sports management. The home nations are not just playing for the podium; they are managing a multi-sport ecosystem. For a country that prides itself on world-beating track talent, our lack of home-grown, world-standard competition sites is a strategic failure. We produce world-class runners in spite of our infrastructure, not because of it. If the Commonwealth Games show us anything, it is that elite performance is an institutional product.

As the bowls events settle into their rhythm and the opening festivities formalize the competition, the Scottish approach to hosting serves as a stark reminder of our own policy gaps. The Kenyan sports ministry has much to learn from a city that understands how to leverage a multi-sport event to improve the city fabric rather than just burning through a budget. While our athletes prepare to face the best in the Commonwealth, they do so against rivals whose home support structures are significantly more robust. The window to modernize our approach to athletic development is closing, and until we stop treating sports infrastructure as a seasonal political project, we will continue to watch the rest of the world leave us behind at the starting blocks.
