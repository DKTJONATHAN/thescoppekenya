## A Wall of Doubt Stands Where Dormitories Once Burned

For many parents, the fresh paint and reinforced exits at Utumishi Girls Academy cannot cover the scorched memories of a tragedy that remains vivid in their minds. While the Ministry of Education pushes for a return to normalcy, the atmosphere among families is thick with a cautious, almost fragile, skepticism.

The Ministry of Education, led by Cabinet Secretary Julius Ogamba, has officially greenlit a phased return to the campus. Students in senior classes are expected back starting July 2, with the remaining cohorts following by July 6. This decision follows heated consultations where parents demanded accountability for the systemic failures that allowed a deadly fire to occur. In response, the school management has seen a major shake-up, including the principal being sent on compulsory leave, while the campus has undergone a Ksh 30 million overhaul to install modern fire suppression systems and emergency egress points. Meanwhile, the legal saga surrounding the eight students accused of murder is moving into a clinical phase, as the Kibera High Court has ordered mandatory mental health evaluations at Mathare Mental Hospital.

### Voices from the School Gate

The consensus among parents is far from celebratory. One mother, who travelled from Wajir for the stakeholder meeting, noted that while the new steel fire doors look professional, they do little to silence the inner voice asking if her daughter will ever truly be safe behind boarding school walls. She argued that money spent on renovations is just surface level, and that until the institutional culture changes, the trauma will linger regardless of how many new fire extinguishers are mounted in the corridors.

Another parent, a father from Naivasha, expressed deep frustration over the pace of the investigation and the reopening timeline. He questioned the rush to get girls back into classrooms when the psychological wounds of the survivors have barely been addressed. He expressed concern that the school is treating the tragedy as an infrastructure project rather than a human crisis, noting that his daughter still wakes up screaming at night, making the talk of academic schedules seem disconnected from the reality of their living rooms.

A third voice, a community leader representing a group of guardians, highlighted the divide between the Ministry's administrative logic and parental sentiment. He pointed out that while Barbara Akinyi and other school administrators might have signed off on the safety compliance documents, the trust that was incinerated on that night cannot be bought or rebuilt through government memos. He emphasized that for the parents, the reopening is not a sign of recovery but a test of whether the authorities can actually protect children when the cameras are off.

### The Long Road to Oversight

This standoff puts the Ministry of Education in a difficult position. They are trying to balance the urgent need to maintain the academic calendar against the growing national anxiety regarding boarding school safety. The situation at Utumishi is being watched closely, not just by those with children currently enrolled, but by parents across the country who are increasingly skeptical of how institutions manage risk. Much like recent discussions on systemic corruption that have hit national headlines, the demand for transparency at the school level is reaching a boiling point.

The coming week serves as a high-stakes litmus test. As students begin to return to the dormitories, the Ministry will need to prove that these reforms are more than just cosmetic. For now, the mood in the hallways and at the school gates is one of heavy silence. The infrastructure is ready, the legal processes are in motion, and the dates are set, but the community remains unconvinced that the walls of the academy can once again be trusted to hold a child’s future. The classrooms will fill up again, yet the shadow of the fire remains, casting doubt over every new safety measure until proven otherwise.
