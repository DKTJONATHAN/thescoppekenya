## Kenyan Regulators Probe Meta Smart Glasses over Privacy Kenya's data protection regulator has initiated a formal investigation into Meta's Ray-Ban smart glasses, responding to concerns from digital rights advocates. The probe examines privacy implications and the use of recorded footage for training Meta's AI systems, highlighting significant data protection and consent issues.

Regulatory Action Initiated The Office of the Data Protection Commissioner (ODPC) formally launched an investigation into Meta's Ray-Ban smart glasses on March 31. This self-initiated probe focuses on the privacy concerns surrounding the glasses and the methods by which captured footage is utilized to train Meta's artificial intelligence systems. The action follows sustained advocacy from digital rights groups.

Digital Rights and Surveillance Concerns The ODPC's move comes after The Oversight Lab, a Nairobi-based digital rights organization, filed a petition on March 6. The petition urged the regulator to investigate the glasses' potential for mass surveillance and their capability to record individuals without their explicit knowledge or consent. Over 150 organizations and individuals endorsed this call, advocating for a transparent and open investigation into the human rights implications.

Unconsented Data Collection and AI Training A joint investigation by Swedish publications Svenska Dagbladet and Göteborgs-Posten revealed that footage recorded by the glasses-including sensitive personal moments, financial details, and private content-was being reviewed by human contractors in Nairobi. These workers, employed by outsourcing firm Sama, were tasked with labeling the content to enhance Meta's AI understanding of real-world environments. Individuals captured in these recordings were reportedly unaware that their private moments were being processed in a data center in Kenya. The core issue extends beyond what the glasses record to include the subsequent handling of the footage, who accesses it, and the absence of consent for such data processing, particularly concerning its use in training Meta's AI tools in compliance with Kenya's Data Protection Act 2019.

Local Precedents and Broader Systemic Challenges The investigation gains local urgency from incidents like that involving "Yaytseslav," a Russian man widely discussed on social media for secretly recording private encounters with multiple women in Africa, including Kenya, using Ray-Ban Meta glasses. The Oversight Lab cited this case as a tangible example of how the glasses facilitate non-consensual recording in private settings, beyond the AI training concerns. This pattern, identified by The Oversight Lab's Executive Director Mercy Mutemi, underscores Kenya's recurrent role as a site for challenging, often hidden, global technology operations, where associated costs are disproportionately borne by local workers. The situation draws parallels to the Worldcoin scandal, where thousands of Kenyans had their iris data collected under a cryptocurrency project.

Meta's Existing Legal Battles in Kenya This is not Meta's first encounter with legal scrutiny in Kenya. The company is currently defending against two separate lawsuits filed by former content moderators who worked for its subcontractor, Sama. A collective of 187 former moderators is seeking $1.6 billion in compensation, citing unfair dismissal, substandard working conditions, and inadequate mental health support after exposure to gruesome content. Despite Meta's initial arguments against being sued in Kenyan courts, the Court of Appeal ruled in September 2024, paving the way for these trials.

Global Resonance of Kenya's Findings Kenya now joins the United Kingdom and the United States in formally scrutinizing Meta over these privacy and data handling concerns. The ODPC has committed to publicly sharing its findings upon the conclusion of its investigation. The outcome and the regulatory actions taken will have implications extending far beyond Kenya's borders, potentially influencing global data protection standards for smart technologies.
