## The Hormuz Pump: Why Kenya's Cost of Living is Held Hostage in a 33km Strait

Most Kenyans view the Middle East as a collection of distant, oil-rich deserts whose conflicts offer nothing more than predictable soundbites on the evening news. This perspective is a luxury we can no longer afford; our household budgets are directly shackled to a narrow stretch of water thousands of kilometres away.

### The Myth of Distant Diplomacy

The prevailing narrative suggests that the Strait of Hormuz is a private stage for Iranian posturing and Western military chest-thumping. It is an easy story to sell. It frames the issue as a foreign diplomatic puzzle that happens "over there," far removed from the boda boda riders in Nairobi or the farmers in Eldoret. This is a dangerous simplification. When you pay for petrol or struggle with the soaring cost of food, you are not just paying for the fuel itself; you are paying a "Hormuz Premium."

That 33km chokepoint is the world's most critical artery for energy. Nearly 20 percent of global oil transit passes through this bottleneck. When geopolitical tensions flare in the Persian Gulf, the price of every barrel of oil jumps in response to the perceived risk of closure. For an import-dependent economy like ours, this is not abstract. It is the reason our transport costs spike, which in turn forces traders to hike the price of maize, vegetables, and household goods. We are paying the price for the colonial legacy of maritime mapping and 1950s resource struggles every single time we visit a petrol station.

### Historical Anchors in Our Fuel Tank

The instability we experience at the pump today is the direct result of decisions made generations ago. The 1951 nationalization of the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company by Mohammad Mosaddegh was not just an Iranian historical footnote; it set the template for the modern energy insecurity that plagues developing nations. When Western powers intervened to secure their interests in Iranian oil, they cemented a global dependency on a singular, volatile maritime route.

This reliance is a colonial-era ghost that still haunts the Global South. We remain trapped in a supply chain designed to favor Western energy security, with little room for African economic autonomy. Just as the [Afreximbank’s recent commitments](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-08-afreximbank-commits-10-billion-to-support-african-and-caribbean-econ) aim to bridge gaps in our internal trade, we must recognize that our vulnerability to the Strait of Hormuz is an structural weakness. We rely on a global system where every minor dispute in the Gulf of Oman is instantly reflected as a tax on the Kenyan consumer. 

### From Imperial Power to Global Inflation

To believe that the Strait of Hormuz is merely a military zone is to ignore the economic reality of the 21st century. This region has been a focal point of control since the Portuguese arrived in the 1500s. The dominance exerted by British naval forces in the 19th century created the framework for the current geopolitical friction. It is a history of extraction and oversight that keeps the price of our energy tied to the whims of great powers and regional actors.

We must stop treating our economic struggles as entirely domestic. The high cost of living is not always the result of local mismanagement; it is often the invisible consequence of a global system that forces us to subsidize the security of foreign maritime routes. If we want to stabilize our economy, we must acknowledge that our national sovereignty is currently limited by our dependence on the flow of oil through that narrow, contested passage.

### Why it matters now
Inflation in Kenya is currently being imported. Because we lack significant domestic energy production, every shock at the Strait of Hormuz forces the government to choose between absorbing losses or passing costs to the public. As we have seen with [recent policy debates](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-25-hussein-mohamed-rejects-fkf-suspension-over-sh42m-chan-claims), the budget is already strained. Rising fuel costs threaten to undermine the entire economic recovery effort.

### What to watch next
Keep a close eye on the volume of tankers successfully clearing the strait. Any increase in maritime insurance premiums for vessels entering the Persian Gulf will be felt at Kenyan pumps within three weeks. We are living in an era where global energy politics are effectively our own local domestic policy.
