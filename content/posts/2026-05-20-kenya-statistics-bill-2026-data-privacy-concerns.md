## Legislative Proposal Targets Data Oversight: Kenya Statistics Bill 2026 Explained

The Kenyan government has introduced the Statistics Bill 2026, a legislative proposal designed to transition from the current Kenya National Bureau of Statistics to a newly formed Kenya Statistics Authority. The bill seeks to modernize data collection by decentralizing information gathering to the county level while mandating biometric data acquisition. As the proposal moves through the legislative process, legal and civil society groups are examining how its provisions intersect with existing national privacy frameworks.

### Assessing Legal Supremacy and Data Rights

The central issue in the proposed legislation is the potential for institutional overlap between the new authority and the Data Protection Act 2019. The bill outlines a framework for permanent, local statistical offices across Kenya’s 47 counties to facilitate real-time economic data collection. However, legal analysts have noted that the text currently lacks clear language deferring to the Data Commissioner regarding how sensitive information, specifically biometric identifiers, is processed, stored, or disposed of.

Under the 2006 Statistics Act, data collection was focused primarily on administrative and socio-economic indicators. The 2026 Bill marks a shift by authorizing the collection of personal biometric data for statistical accuracy. Critics of the current draft argue that if the Kenya Statistics Authority is granted primacy over these processes, it could limit the ability of citizens to exercise their rights to data privacy, such as the right to object to data collection or request the deletion of personal records.

Furthermore, the bill introduces stringent enforcement mechanisms. Organizations and individuals that do not comply with the new mandates face penalties as high as KES 5 million. This level of enforcement represents a significant shift from the administrative procedures that previously defined national statistical reporting.

### Governance Restructuring and Technical Security

The administration identifies the dissolution of the existing bureau as a measure to capture data from the informal sector more effectively. By establishing 47 county branches, the government intends to improve planning and resource allocation through a decentralized, data-driven governance model. Nevertheless, the transition presents logistical and technical challenges. Bureau staff have questioned the impact of this transition on existing employment protections and the maintenance of longitudinal data consistency.

Technical experts have also raised concerns regarding the security architecture of the proposed county-level offices. Creating 47 distinct entry points for biometric and demographic information expands the potential surface area for unauthorized access. Unless these county offices implement standards consistent with the Data Protection Act 2019, there is a risk that the new infrastructure could compromise the security of personal information. Similar to other large-scale national infrastructure projects-such as the 20-year master plan for JKIA terminal modernization-the success of this initiative will depend on balancing operational goals with long-term security safeguards.

### The Legislative Path Forward

The Statistics Bill 2026 is currently undergoing a public participation phase, allowing technology advocates and the public to submit feedback. The primary concern among observers is the legislative hierarchy of the bill. Once enacted, the potential for the new authority to bypass the Data Protection Act could become difficult to challenge in court.

The forthcoming parliamentary debate is expected to determine whether specific safeguards are included to harmonize the bill with current privacy laws. A key point of contention for lawmakers will be clarifying whether the Kenya Statistics Authority is categorized as a data controller or a data processor. As public participation sessions continue, stakeholders are closely monitoring whether future revisions will prioritize the alignment of state mandates with individual rights or favor administrative reach. The outcome of these discussions will define the state’s access to private data for the next decade.
