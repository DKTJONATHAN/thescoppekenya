## The Empty Pockets Behind the Runway

The government has committed Ksh375.4 billion to China Communications Construction Company for a massive overhaul of Jomo Kenyatta International Airport, yet the financing model relies on cannibalizing the capital reserves of the Kenya Pipeline Company to sustain the project.

### Harvesting the Pipeline for Aviation Ambitions

The administration is framing the 20-year master plan for JKIA as a necessary leap into regional aviation dominance, aiming to push capacity well beyond the current 12 million passenger limit. However, the architecture of this deal is far more precarious than the sleek 230,000-square-metre terminal designs suggest. By pulling Ksh20 billion in initial seed capital from the planned privatization of the Kenya Pipeline Company, the state is effectively treating a critical energy utility as a slush fund for an aviation gamble. This is not mere fiscal maneuvering; it is the structural repurposing of revenue derived from a vital public asset to fuel a contract that exposes the national budget to significant debt-servicing risks over the next two decades.

The logic of stripping KPC of its operational autonomy to bankroll an airport expansion creates a dangerous inter-agency dependency. KPC has historically functioned as a self-sustaining entity, its revenues tethered to the maintenance and expansion of the country’s fuel transport network. By redirecting the dividends of its privatization toward the airport project, the government risks undermining the very entity responsible for national energy security. If the aviation sector fails to generate the projected returns, the state will have compromised the financial health of one of its most reliable parastatals to cover the shortfall of another. This is an economic shell game where the cost of failure is the degradation of two essential industries simultaneously.

### The Math of a Debt-Laden Master Plan

The sheer scale of the Ksh375.4 billion contract demands scrutiny that goes beyond the glossy renderings of the new runway and terminal facilities. With an end date set for 2045, the project locks the country into a long-term debt cycle that assumes perpetual growth in passenger numbers and cargo throughput. As officials celebrate the promise of a 4,500-metre runway capable of hosting modern heavy aircraft, they remain conspicuously silent on the interest rates and repayment terms embedded in the Chinese state-owned firm's contract.

This financial structure mimics the patterns seen in previous infrastructure projects that pushed national debt toward unsustainable levels. When infrastructure becomes a vehicle for debt rather than a generator of independent revenue, the public interest is sidelined in favor of external obligations. While citizens deal with the ongoing economic shifts and infrastructure projects that continue to reshape regional ties, the reality remains that the primary beneficiaries of this capital flow are foreign contractors who are insulated from the localized fiscal shocks that will inevitably follow.

Just as local administrators faced scrutiny earlier this year regarding the transparency of major public initiatives-as seen in the [2026-04-05-diana-luvanda-and-nairobi-officials-question-match-integrity-after-u](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-05-diana-luvanda-and-nairobi-officials-question-match-integrit)-this aviation deal follows a pattern of opaque, top-down decision-making that skirts public oversight. The government is betting the country's fiscal future on the hope that global aviation demand remains constant for the next twenty years. Should that demand falter, or should the redirected KPC funds fail to stabilize the project's cash flow, the burden of this Ksh355 billion balance will fall squarely on the taxpayer.

The strategy ignores the volatility inherent in massive infrastructure development, preferring the appearance of rapid progress over the stability of sound fiscal policy. As construction commences, one must ask: when the fuel infrastructure is stripped of its resources and the aviation market fluctuates, who will be left to service the debt when the runway is empty?
