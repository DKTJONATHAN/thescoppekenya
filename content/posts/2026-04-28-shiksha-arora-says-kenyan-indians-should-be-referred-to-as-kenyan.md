Media personality Shiksha Arora recently ignited a spirited conversation across Kenya, advocating for Kenyan Indians to be simply referred to as Kenyan. Her powerful reflections on identity, shared on the Mic Cheque Podcast, are resonating deeply, sparking vital national discourse about belonging for many.

## Shiksha Arora Ignites Identity Discourse

Arora's candid remarks have struck a national chord, prompting many to consider the nuances of identity in contemporary Kenya. During a recent appearance on the Mic Cheque Podcast, she posed a poignant question: "In India, you’re told you don’t belong. In Kenya, you’re told you’re not Kenyan enough… so what are you?" This query encapsulates the emotional tension she experiences, feeling like an outsider in both Kenya and India, a sentiment resonating with many young people navigating life between diverse cultures.

### A Legacy in Kenya

Kenyans of Indian descent boast a rich history in East Africa, with their presence dating back to the late 19th century during the construction of the Uganda Railway. Over generations, they have cultivated businesses, built families, and woven themselves into the country's social and economic fabric. The community, currently estimated at approximately 50,000 people, achieved a significant milestone in 2017 when they were officially recognized as Kenya's 44th tribe, a move intended to affirm their rightful place within the nation's identity. Despite this landmark recognition, Arora stresses that the conversation about belonging is far from over, asserting, "Kenyan Indians should not be called Kenyan Indians… we are Kenyan."

### Echoes from Other Voices

Arora's call for inclusive identity comes amidst broader discussions about race and belonging among Kenyans globally. She recounted an incident in Malibu this year where a driver questioned her identity after she requested a ride, demanding, “Proof to me it’s you… you have to show me your phone,” while keeping the car door locked. Despite this, Majimbo noted that her residency in diverse areas like Los Angeles, West Hollywood, and Beverly Hills largely shields her from frequent similar experiences.

Similarly, content creator Natalie Tewa opened up about experiencing what she describes as "silent racism" while pursuing her studies in the UK. She highlighted subtle yet impactful moments, such as people expressing surprise that she spoke fluent English.
