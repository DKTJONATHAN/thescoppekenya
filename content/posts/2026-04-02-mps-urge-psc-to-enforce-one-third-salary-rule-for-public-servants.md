The Public Accounts Committee (PAC) on Thursday, April 2, 2026, urged the Public Service Commission (PSC) to establish stringent regulations ensuring public servants receive at least one-third of their basic salary. This directive follows persistent non-compliance with the Employment Act, 2007, observed across various government institutions.

Enforcing Salary Thresholds During a parliamentary committee meeting today, PAC unequivocally called upon the PSC to enforce the one-third rule on every public servant's net pay. This statutory requirement mandates that employees must take home a minimum of one-third of their basic salary after all deductions. The committee's demand arose during its examination of Auditor-General’s reports for the financial years ending June 30, 2024, and 2025, which highlighted widespread breaches of this legal provision.

State House Non-Compliance Identified The audit report for the 2023/2024 financial year revealed that over 78 staff at State House received net salaries below the legal threshold. These discrepancies were primarily attributed to excessive deductions, often linked to loan repayments, salary advances, check-off systems, and court-ordered recoveries. The Public Accounts Committee underscored that Section 19(3) of the Employment Act, 2007, explicitly limits total deductions to no more than two-thirds of an employee's basic salary, safeguarding against financial distress.

Management Interventions and Recurring Issues State House Comptroller Katoo Ole Metito informed the committee that management interventions had significantly reduced the number of affected staff. Ole Metito stated that by the end of March 2026, only 14 out of the initial 78 individuals from the 2023/2024 audit were still taking home less than one-third of their pay, with projections to reduce this to four by the end of April 2026. Despite these efforts, committee Chairperson Tindi Mwale, MP for Butere constituency, expressed concern that this issue is not isolated to State House but recurs across other state agencies and government arms. He urged the PSC to develop policy and regulations to resolve the problem definitively. The committee maintained that continued cases of non-compliance point to weaknesses in payroll oversight and called for urgent regulatory action to address the matter across the public service, a commitment to systemic improvement that resonates with efforts to foster digital literacy and educational equity in Kenya’s frontier counties. Protecting Public Servants The one-third rule is critical for protecting employees from over-indebtedness and ensuring they retain sufficient funds for daily living expenses. For example, a public servant with a basic salary of Ksh 30,000 should receive a minimum of Ksh 10,000 after deductions. If deductions exceed Ksh 20,000, leaving the employee with less than Ksh 10,000, it constitutes a violation of the law. The committee's latest call emphasizes the urgency for the PSC to implement comprehensive measures to uphold this fundamental protection for public servants.
