## The Arbitration Shield: Why Kenya's Corporate Disputes are Moving Out of Courtrooms

The recent High Court dismissal of Digital Mara Media Limited’s attempt to halt the termination of a KSh 300 million advertising contract with Kenya Airways signals a definitive shift in Kenya’s legal landscape. By refusing to intervene in the matter, Justice Mulwa has reinforced a growing judicial preference for private arbitration over traditional public litigation, particularly concerning complex commercial agreements.

This ruling acts as a clear advisory to the corporate sector. The judiciary is demonstrating a diminishing appetite to function as a tool for ongoing contract management when parties have previously committed to binding arbitration clauses. Such moves are designed to alleviate the chronic backlog in the commercial division of the High Court, steering firms toward specialized resolution mechanisms that favor private, expedited outcomes over the prolonged, often performative nature of courtroom proceedings.

### The Limits of Judicial Intervention

The dispute centered on the abrupt conclusion of a business partnership. Digital Mara Media Limited petitioned the court for an injunction to preserve the contract, asserting that the airline’s exit was premature and legally unjustified. Conversely, Kenya Airways contended that its actions were fully compliant with the agreement, specifically pointing to a clause that grants the right of termination subject to a 60-day notice period.

Rather than delving into the nuances of the breach of contract claims, Justice Mulwa focused strictly on the procedural obligations defined in the parties' original contract. By leveraging the Arbitration Act, the court effectively directed the disputants toward an appointed arbitrator. This reflects a modernizing legal system that views commercial contracts as autonomous, self-governing documents. In this framework, the judiciary acts more as a gatekeeper than a primary decision-maker, ensuring that the sanctity of the initial agreement is upheld rather than circumvented through court intervention.

### Why it Matters Now

For corporate executives and legal departments, this outcome is a pivotal lesson in litigation strategy. The era of utilizing court injunctions as a reliable mechanism to pause the operational consequences of contract terminations is rapidly closing. Courts are clarifying that they will no longer disrupt the commercial functions of major entities like the national carrier when a contractually mandated dispute process remains available.

This evolution highlights the necessity for rigorous contract drafting. If a business anticipates judicial protection, it must treat dispute resolution clauses as critical strategic assets rather than standard boilerplate text.
