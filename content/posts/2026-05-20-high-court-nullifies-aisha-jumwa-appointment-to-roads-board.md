## The Nullification Cycle: Governance and the High Court

The High Court ruling invalidating the appointment of Aisha Jumwa to the Kenya Roads Board is the latest instance of a recurring administrative failure. This pattern reveals a persistent disconnect between executive patronage and the mandatory legal standards for public service appointments.

### A Chronology of Procedural Fails

The decision by Justice Bahati Mwamuye on May 20 is not an isolated event. It fits into a broader timeline of executive appointments that have hit a legal wall due to procedural oversight.

*   Early 2024: Several appointments to independent commissions were suspended after the court found that the executive bypassed mandatory public participation requirements, effectively rendering the nominations dead on arrival.
*   Mid 2025: A series of board appointments for state corporations were invalidated because the vetting committees failed to verify the academic credentials and conflict of interest declarations as required by the Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission guidelines.
*   Present Day: The nullification of the Kenya Roads Board chairmanship confirms that even high-profile roles remain susceptible to the same vetting shortcuts that have stalled governance for years.

These events are bound by a common thread: the executive branch consistently prioritizes political expediency over the rigorous, transparent vetting processes enshrined in the 2010 Constitution. While the state argues for the need to maintain political momentum, the judiciary continues to hold the line on the statutory requirements of the Kenya Roads Board Act and the principles of Article 232, which demand values like accountability, transparency, and competitive selection.

### The Cost of Repeat Hiring

The intersection between political patronage and the strict requirements of the Kenya Roads Board Act has created a bottleneck in public service delivery. When the state forces an appointment that ignores Section 7 of the Act-which dictates the specific qualifications and the professional profile necessary for board leadership-it effectively invites a lawsuit. This is not merely a political grievance; it is a fundamental institutional failure.

Government agencies often fall into a trap where they seek to fill positions based on political loyalty rather than objective merit. As discussed in our piece on why hiring leaders who mirror the past kills organizational growth, this strategy serves short-term stability but ensures long-term stagnation. When a court strikes down these appointments, the resulting vacuum causes projects to stall, procurement processes to freeze, and the public to lose faith in the integrity of state institutions.

Legal experts have noted that the state has become comfortable with a strategy of "appoint first, defend later." This creates a recurring fiscal cost, as the taxpayer ultimately funds the litigation fees and the inevitable repeat of the recruitment process. The judiciary, under the guidance of the Constitution, remains the only barrier against this institutional complacency.

### Why it matters now

The invalidation of the appointment highlights that constitutional adherence is not a suggestion but a requirement for administrative legitimacy. When the executive branch treats the law as a hurdle to be jumped rather than a framework to be followed, the entire civil service suffers from a lack of clear leadership. This specific ruling serves as a warning that no appointment is immune to the rigorous test of legal compliance.

### What to watch next

The government must now return to the drawing board to initiate a selection process that actually respects the letter of the law. Citizens should observe whether the subsequent search for a chairperson adheres to the public participation requirements and the technical qualifications mandated by the KRB Act. If the cycle repeats, it suggests that the failure is not accidental but structural.

Past procedural neglect almost always dictates future judicial intervention.
