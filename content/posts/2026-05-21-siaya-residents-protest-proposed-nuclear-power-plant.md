## Siaya Residents Protest Against Proposed Nuclear Power Plant

Angry residents in Sakwa village, Bondo sub-county, disrupted a public participation forum on Thursday to reject the government's plans to host a massive nuclear power facility that has been relocated from the coast after intense local opposition.

The Nuclear Power and Energy Agency (NuPEA) is pushing to establish a project valued at 500 billion shillings, aiming to generate between 1,000 and 3,000 megawatts of electricity. This initiative is a central pillar of the national energy master plan, which requires a site to be secured and prepared ahead of a scheduled 2027 construction launch.

The relocation of the project to Siaya follows a failed attempt to establish the plant in Kilifi County. While the government views the movement of the project as a necessary step for national development, residents of Sakwa perceive the shift as a search for a more compliant population rather than a strategic site selection. 

For the government, the 500 billion shilling investment represents a leverage point for regional economic transformation. Officials argue the project will spur infrastructure growth, employment, and regional development. However, locals remain skeptical, questioning whether the promise of development justifies the risks associated with the facility.

### A Tale of Two Regions

The contrast between the Kilifi rejection and the current struggle in Siaya highlights the limitations of public participation in Kenya. Kilifi activists successfully mobilized against the plant by highlighting environmental and health concerns, effectively forcing the state to retreat. In Siaya, the government appears to be applying a more aggressive strategy to secure local approval, leading to the friction witnessed at today’s forum.

Residents expressed frustration over the perceived lack of transparency, arguing that the project was brought to their doorstep without meaningful consultation. While the government frames the public participation process as a democratic necessity, many in Sakwa view it as a procedural hurdle the state intends to clear regardless of local sentiment. This zero-sum dynamic pits the national goal of energy security against the autonomy of regional communities.

### Why it matters now

The urgency behind this project is driven by a rigid 2027 timeline, forcing NuPEA to secure a host site at any cost. This pressure has transformed the selection process into a battle for legitimacy. If the government fails to convince Siaya residents, the project faces further delays that could threaten the national energy grid’s expansion. The outcome of the standoff in Bondo will set a critical precedent for how the state handles community resistance to large-scale infrastructure projects.

Just as many Kenyans are monitoring shifting fiscal pressures-seen in broader financial trends like the [Fuliza User Base Hits 17 Million Amid Declining Loan Sizes](https://zandani.co.ke/article/fuliza-user-base-hits-17-million-amid-declining-loan-sizes)-local communities are increasingly wary of government projects that promise prosperity but carry heavy long-term burdens. The Sakwa protest signals that residents are no longer willing to accept state projects on blind faith.

### What to watch next

The government is expected to announce the next phase of its site-vetting process within the coming weeks. Observers should monitor whether the state will choose to intensify its community outreach, offer new economic incentives to sway opinion, or if this latest wave of opposition will force yet another relocation of the national project.
