Young Kenyan men are increasingly postponing marriage to pursue a state of financial perfection, sparking a national debate about the validity of this strategy. This shift in priorities suggests that many are choosing personal wealth over the commitment of building a life with a partner.

## The Case Against Financial Perfection in Marriage

### The myth of the finished man
Many young people have adopted the belief that they must reach a specific financial peak before they are ready for a spouse. They treat their lives like a construction project that must be entirely completed before a partner is allowed to enter. This mindset ignores the reality that life is not a linear progression leading toward a final, polished product. Instead of building a future alongside a partner, many men are isolating themselves in a pursuit of a goalpost that continually shifts further away.

### Lessons from recent economic shifts
The obsession with solitary financial success often creates a fragile existence that cannot withstand major life challenges. When broader economic shifts occur, such as the sudden conclusion of major international investment trends or the tightening of local tax policies, those who have established strong interpersonal foundations fare significantly better than those who are flying solo. Relying exclusively on an individual bank account leaves one vulnerable to personal storms that money alone cannot resolve. Strong social and domestic foundations provide a layer of stability that material wealth cannot replicate.

### The trap of performative success
There is a rising tendency to prioritize the appearance of wealth over the substance of human connection. Many men spend their prime years chasing status symbols while neglecting the emotional maturity and partnership skills that marriage requires. When an individual waits until they believe they are perfect to settle down, they often discover that they have simply spent their best years in isolation. Life demands that people learn to navigate unpredictable challenges together rather than waiting for a moment of financial ease that may never arrive. Choosing to wait often results in a life that is economically stable but emotionally hollow, leaving individuals to realize that material success is a poor substitute for companionship.
