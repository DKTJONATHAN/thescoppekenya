## The Thermal Seesaw: How Kenyans are navigating the Triple Threat weather pattern

"I leave the house in a sweater, start sweating in traffic by noon, and by the time I am heading home, I am shivering again. It feels like my body is being forced to live in three different climate zones in one single day."

This is the consensus across Kenya as the Meteorological Department confirms a volatile weather streak lasting until May 18. Kenyans are facing a meteorological triple threat: soaking rains, aggressive daytime heat topping 30 degrees Celsius, and freezing nighttime dips below 10 degrees in the highlands. This erratic cycle is playing havoc with daily routines, health, and urban infrastructure, forcing citizens to adapt their commutes and wardrobes to a rapidly changing, unpredictable environment.

### Voices from the Streets

Social media is buzzing with stories of the "thermal seesaw." In Nairobi, commuters are voicing frustration over the logistical nightmare of preparing for multiple seasons in twenty-four hours. Mama Sarah, a trader in Gikomba, says she carries an umbrella for the afternoon rain, a light shirt for the noon sun, and a thick jacket for the evening chill. She notes that the constant shift in temperature has left her children battling persistent coughs. Meanwhile, in the highlands of Nyandarua, residents are reporting a spike in respiratory infections, with local clinics seeing more patients seeking treatment for chest complications. In Mombasa, the conversation is slightly different, focusing on the humidity that follows the heavy downpours, which makes the heat feel significantly more suffocating than the thermometer suggests.

### Health Tips for the Seesaw

Medical professionals are urging caution as the human body struggles to regulate itself under such extreme fluctuations. To mitigate the risks of colds and heat-related exhaustion, experts suggest keeping a "base layer" approach to clothing. This allows for quick adjustments as the day progresses, preventing the body from overheating or chilling too rapidly. Hydration is vital, even when it feels cold outside, as dry wind in highland areas can lead to silent dehydration. Parents are encouraged to keep children warm during the early morning hours and to ensure that wet clothes are changed immediately to avoid lingering dampness that invites illness. This comes at a somber time for the regional arts community, which is still mourning losses like the recent passing of [Queen of Katwe actress Esteri Tebandeke](https://zandani.co.ke/article/queen-of-katwe-actress-esteri-tebandeke-dies-after-kidney-complicati), reminding everyone that health remains the most fragile and precious asset during times of environmental stress.

### Preparedness and What Comes Next

For those living in flood-prone zones across the country, the message is one of heightened vigilance. Thunderstorms are often sudden, turning dry paths into water traps within minutes. The checklist for the coming week is simple: keep emergency contacts accessible, secure loose roof materials, and avoid crossing flooded roads, regardless of how shallow they might appear. While national leaders like [Uhuru Kenyatta continue to emphasize the need for social harmony](https://zandani.co.ke/article/uhuru-kenyatta-calls-for-national-unity-and-an-end-to-tribal-politic), the current priority for the common mwananchi is the fundamental need to protect one’s household from the elements.

**Why it matters now:**
The rapid oscillation between heat, rain, and cold is not just a weather anomaly; it is a health risk. Respiratory systems, particularly in vulnerable populations, are being pushed to their limits. Understanding how to manage these 20-degree temperature swings is now a prerequisite for basic survival in urban and rural centers alike.

**What to watch next:**
The Meteorological Department will be providing daily updates. Pay attention to regional warnings between now and May 18. If you are in a flood-prone county, identify the nearest high-ground refuge points before the next round of heavy rainfall arrives.
