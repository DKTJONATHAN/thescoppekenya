## The Judiciary as a Broker of Rights

The High Court has effectively declared that constitutional violations carry a price tag, ruling that Rigathi Gachagua’s removal was legally flawed yet practically irreversible. By awarding Ksh50 million in damages while validating the impeachment, the court has turned the Constitution into a transactional document rather than a safeguard for democratic process.

Rigathi Gachagua is now taking his fight to the Court of Appeal, challenging a logic that permits a legal process to be simultaneously toxic and binding. When Judges Eric Ogolla, Anthony Mrima, and Fridah Mugambi found that the Senate proceedings failed Za Ndanis set by Articles 25, 47, and 50, they acknowledged a systemic collapse of fair administrative action. Yet, by keeping the impeachment intact, the bench signaled that the procedural rot discovered within the Senate does not necessarily invalidate the political execution of an office holder. This ruling creates a dangerous hybrid status in Kenyan law: an illegal process that produces a perfectly legal result.

### The Price of Constitutional Failure

The Ksh50 million award functions as a sterile, moral consolation that side-steps the core procedural integrity issues plaguing the legislative chamber. Legal analysts are already questioning if this sum acts as a form of judicial hush money, intended to normalize the breach of fundamental rights by monetizing the damage. If a violation of Articles 25 and 47-which mandate the right to a fair hearing and administrative justice-can be rectified with a cheque, then the Constitution is being demoted to a liability in a tort case rather than a supreme shield for the public office.

This precedent suggests that state institutions can bypass the mandatory requirements of constitutional due process, secure the desired political outcome, and simply settle the bill later. It mirrors the persistent disregard for fiscal accountability seen elsewhere in the public sector, much like the [EACC warnings against using public resources for political mobilization](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-03-23-eacc-warns-politicians-against-using-public-resources-for-e). If the Judiciary treats constitutional rights as negotiable commodities, the barrier between legitimate governance and institutionalized overreach dissolves. When rights are commodified, the protection of the individual becomes secondary to the preservation of a tainted political status quo.

### A Blueprint for Future Removals

The High Court ruling has effectively provided a roadmap for future legislative purges. Any future assembly seeking to oust a political target now understands the cost of doing business: bypass fair hearing requirements, ignore the spirit of Article 50, and prepare to pay a fine. The damage award does not undo the political reality of the impeachment; it merely attaches an invoice to the misconduct.

By validating the outcome despite the admitted constitutional breaches, the court has signaled that political utility outweighs procedural sanctity. This echoes the broader crisis of institutional integrity, reminiscent of when the [ODPP attempts to challenge cybercrime laws at the Supreme Court](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-15-odpp-challenges-ruling-on-cybercrime-law-at-supreme-court), highlighting how legal channels are frequently caught between executive will and judicial restraint. If the Court of Appeal upholds this logic, it will solidify a regime where constitutional breaches are treated as minor regulatory infractions rather than foundational failures of the state.

The judiciary is tasked with protecting the integrity of the law, yet it has opted to act as an arbiter of damages for a dying democratic process. If a violation of one’s constitutional rights can be cured by a payout, what exactly is there to prevent the powerful from continuing to trample over the law to achieve their ends?
