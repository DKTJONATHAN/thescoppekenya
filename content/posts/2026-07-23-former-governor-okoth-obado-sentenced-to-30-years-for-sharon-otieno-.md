Former Migori Governor Okoth Obado began a 30-year prison sentence at Kamiti Maximum Security Prison this week. He was convicted for the 2018 murder of Rongo University student Sharon Otieno, concluding one of the most high-profile criminal cases in recent Kenyan history.

## The Harsh Reality of Life at Kamiti

### A Drastic Change in Circumstances
The former county leader has traded his former life of luxury for a confined cell. While some observers cynically characterize his new living situation as a government-owned Airbnb, the reality within the prison walls is stark. This transition from a regional power broker to a convicted inmate serves as a pointed reminder that even the most influential public figures are not immune to the judicial process.

### The Hidden Tax of Prison Politics
Life inside the facility is governed by rigid social hierarchies known as prison politics. New inmates, particularly those with high profiles, quickly discover that institutional walls do not guarantee safety from fellow prisoners. Dangerous inmate gangs, frequently identified as estate committees, control the internal distribution of resources. These groups enforce a mandatory service charge, which functions as extortion money demanded from newcomers to guarantee their physical protection. Failure to pay this illicit levy often leads to immediate physical confrontation.

### Navigating the Internal Hierarchy
Without the ability to pay these powerful inmate syndicates, a new prisoner faces significant threats to their safety. The irony of a man who once held supreme power in Migori being forced to negotiate his own protection is significant. It underscores how quickly fortunes shift when an individual is stripped of political influence. As Obado serves his term, he remains sequestered far from the halls of power he once commanded, serving as a reminder that legal accountability eventually reaches even those who believe themselves to be above the law.
