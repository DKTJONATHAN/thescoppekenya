## Africa Braces for Fuel Rationing Amid Middle East Conflict Trade Cabinet Secretary Moses Kuria has issued a stark warning that Africa, including this nation, is highly susceptible to severe fuel supply shocks and potential rationing. The ongoing Middle East conflict, now in its third week, has led to the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, significantly disrupting global oil flows and causing a surge in crude oil prices since February 28, 2026.

Strait of Hormuz Closure Triggers Crisis The crisis stems directly from the closure of the Strait of Hormuz to most commercial maritime traffic following an escalation in the U.S.-Israel war with Iran. Historically, this strait facilitated 20 percent of the world’s daily oil supply. Since March 2, 2026, Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) has declared the strait closed, threatening any unauthorized vessels. This has resulted in a approximately 97 percent plummet in vessel transits since the conflict began. Moses Kuria, in a statement on X on Saturday, March 14, highlighted Africa's particular vulnerability due to a lack of alternative fuel sources and insufficient stockpiles. He noted that other continents possess alternative sources like Russia or have strategic stockpiles such as China, leaving Africa, despite being a producer, exposed to the severest impact of supply shocks. Kuria criticized the absence of a coordinated energy strategy within the African Union, stating, “We are on our own”.

Economic Repercussions and Local Impact The direct consequence of the strait's closure has been a significant increase in crude oil prices. Murban crude oil, for example, surged to Ksh11,916 (USD 92.13) per barrel on March 12, up from Ksh9,862 (USD 76.25) just a week prior, as reported by the CBK bulletin on Friday, March 14. Motorists in Nairobi have already reported petrol stations rationing fuel purchases, with some capping sales at Ksh2,000 in anticipation of the monthly pump price review. This echoes similar measures implemented in some countries in the far east. Kwame Owino, CEO of the Institute of Economic Affairs, emphasized the need for African nations to prepare for a "catastrophic scenario". In an interview with CNN this week, he advised governments to adjust fiscal policy to enhance affordability, even if it means a lower government revenue. While these economic pressures mount, other sectors also face review, with the Kenya Wildlife Service proposing new access and conservation fees, a development of interest to those tracking government revenue adjustments.

Government Response and Alternative Supplies Daniel Kiptoo, Director General of the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA), informed journalists last week that the government is closely monitoring the loading of an oil ship in the Red Sea to mitigate potential shortages. The nation anticipates a delivery later in April. EPRA is actively seeking alternative supplies from India and Oman, with arrivals expected by early April. Kiptoo stressed, “The government will be concerned if the scheduled cargo doesn’t load".
