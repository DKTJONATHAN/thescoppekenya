<p>Nairobi’s tech hubs in Kilimani and Westlands are buzzing this Monday, March 23, 2026, as a new generation of developers discovers that while AI can write their code, it is quietly stripping away their ability to think for themselves.</p>

<h2>The Illusion of the Shortcut</h2>
<p>I have spent over a decade sitting in cafes from Ngong Road to the CBD, watching the Kenyan tech scene evolve from basic SMS services to the "Silicon Savannah" we boast about today. In all that time, the one thing that separated the masters from the pretenders was the "grind"—the hours spent breaking things and fixing them. But lately, the vibe has changed. I see junior developers finishing complex tasks in minutes, leaning heavily on AI tools to generate entire libraries of code. On the surface, it looks like a win for productivity. If you look closer, you see a generation of professionals who are becoming hollow.</p>

<p>The reality is that AI-assisted productivity is not a shortcut to competence. When you delegate the heavy lifting of thinking to an algorithm, you stop building the mental muscles required to understand the "why" behind the "how." I’ve watched brilliant young minds in our local startups struggle to debug a single line of code the moment the AI isn't there to hold their hand. They can produce the work, but they don't understand the work. This isn't just a hunch; the data shows that while AI helps novices get things done, it actively impairs their conceptual understanding and code-reading abilities. We are trading long-term mastery for short-term speed, and that is a dangerous bargain.</p>

<h2>The Crisis of Supervision</h2>
<p>The biggest problem we face in the near future is a massive gap in supervision. In any industry, whether it’s accounting, law, or software engineering, the senior’s job is to check the junior’s work. But if the junior used AI to do the work and never learned the basics, how will they ever become a senior capable of identifying errors? We are creating a pipeline of "supervisors" who lack the technical depth to actually supervise.</p>

<p>In safety-critical domains—think of the systems running our mobile banking or the digital infrastructure of our hospitals—this is a disaster waiting to happen. If a human engineer doesn't have the skill to spot a subtle logic flaw in AI-generated code, that flaw goes live. We are moving toward a world where humans are legally liable for the mistakes of machines they aren't skilled enough to audit. I’ve seen this play out in smaller ways in the entertainment industry, where AI-generated contracts or marketing plans miss the cultural nuances of the Kenyan market because the person "prompting" the AI doesn't actually understand the business. When the mistake hits the fan, "the AI did it" won't be a valid defense.</p>

<h2>AI is Changing the Face of Conflict</h2>
<p>While we talk about jobs, we have to look at how this same technology is revolutionizing modern warfare. This isn't science fiction anymore. AI is shifting the battlefield from human bravery to algorithmic efficiency. We are seeing the rise of autonomous systems that can identify targets and make split-second decisions without a human in the loop. The "skill" in war is moving away from the soldier on the ground to the programmer in the bunker.</p>

<p>This revolution in warfare brings a chilling lack of accountability. Just as a junior dev trusts an AI to write a function, military commanders are starting to trust AI to analyze battlefield data and suggest strikes. The danger here is the same: the loss of human judgment. When war becomes a series of automated calculations, the moral and ethical weight of taking a life gets buried under layers of code. In our region, where security is always a sensitive topic, the introduction of AI-driven surveillance and drone tech means that the person "supervising" the machine might not even understand the bias built into the system they are deploying.</p>

<h2>The Real Job Thief</h2>
<p>Is AI taking your job? That’s the question everyone asks me at every cocktail party in the city. The answer isn't a simple yes or no. AI isn't necessarily going to walk into your office and sit at your desk. Instead, it’s going to make you obsolete by making you lazy. The job isn't being "taken" as much as it is being "devalued." If a task that used to take a skilled human five hours now takes an unskilled human five minutes with an AI, the market price for that skill drops to zero.</p>

<p>The people who will survive this shift are the ones who use AI as a tool for cognitive engagement, not as a replacement for it. I’ve observed two types of workers lately. The first type delegates everything to the AI and checks out mentally. They are the ones who should be worried. The second type uses AI to explore different perspectives, to brainstorm, and to double-check their own logic while still doing the hard work of learning the core concepts. These are the people who will still have jobs in 2030 because they possess the one thing the AI can't replicate: genuine, experience-based human intuition.</p>

<p>We have to stop looking at AI as a magic wand that solves our problems. It is a powerful engine, but if you don't know how to drive, you’re just a passenger in a car that’s eventually going to crash. For the young Kenyans trying to make it in this new economy, my advice is simple: don't let the convenience of the tool rob you of the skill you need to master it. If you can’t do the job without the AI, you can’t do the job at all.</p>
