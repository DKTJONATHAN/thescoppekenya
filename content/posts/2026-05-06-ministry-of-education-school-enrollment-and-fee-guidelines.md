## Expanding National School Enrollment: Opportunity or Overcrowding?

The Ministry of Education is rolling out a plan to increase capacity in top-tier high schools. While parents see this as a chance for better education, many are asking if this expansion provides true equality or simply stretches existing infrastructure to its breaking point.

The Kenyan education sector is once again at a crossroads. As Basic Education Principal Secretary Julius Bitok announced the push to enroll more students in high-performing national and extra-county institutions, a familiar question emerges. Does stuffing more students into prestigious schools like Alliance Girls actually lift educational standards, or is it a cosmetic fix for a systemic problem? While the government shifts focus, the country continues to process major national events, such as the ongoing [High Court orders for a partial recount in Mbeere North](https://zandani.co.ke/article/high-court-orders-partial-recount-in-mbeere-north-by-election), reminding us that public systems-whether electoral or educational-require deep structural integrity to function.

### Does expanding elite schools fix the inequality gap?

There is a long-standing myth that a national school tag guarantees a superior future. The reality is that these institutions are often at maximum capacity. When the Ministry pushes for increased enrollment in these centers, it risks eroding the very environment that made them successful. 

True inequality in Kenya is not found in the popularity of national schools, but in the severe under-funding and under-enrollment of sub-county schools. These local institutions often lack basic laboratory equipment and adequate staffing, creating a massive gap in service delivery compared to their national counterparts. By pouring resources into already established giants while rural schools crumble, the government maintains a two-tier system rather than bridging the divide. If the goal is genuine equity, infrastructure investment must prioritize the schools currently struggling to fill desks, rather than just adding more students to schools that are already bursting at the seams.

### What does the Ksh53,000 fee cap actually cover?

Amidst the anxiety over admission spots, the Ministry has reaffirmed the secondary school fee cap of Ksh53,000 for senior levels. This directive is designed to protect parents from predatory levies that have become common in recent years. 

However, parents are often caught between government mandates and school board demands for extra development levies. The Ksh53,000 threshold is intended to cover tuition, board, and essential operations. When schools exceed this, they often cite maintenance costs or capital projects that are not reflected in official policy. Families should view this cap as their baseline protection. If a school pushes for payments beyond this total, that institution is effectively shifting the burden of infrastructure development onto the pockets of parents-a practice that contradicts the spirit of the current fee guidelines.

### Why it matters now

Education remains the primary engine for social mobility in Kenya. With national economic pressures-including [falling foreign reserves amid global conflicts](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenya-s-foreign-reserves-fall-amid-iran-war-and-mounting-pressures)-household budgets are tighter than ever. Parents need to know that their school fees are being used for student learning rather than excessive expansion projects that prioritize school prestige over student performance.

### What to watch next

Keep a close eye on the actual infrastructure audits conducted in the coming months. If the government follows through on its promise to invest in under-enrolled schools, it may signal a shift toward regional parity. If the policy remains fixated on expanding only the top 10 percent of schools, expect the admission lottery to become even more frantic as infrastructure gaps widen. 

Access to quality education should be a standard right, not a competitive prize won through school-level expansion.
