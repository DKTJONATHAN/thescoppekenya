## Infrastructure Development and Education Equity in Kenya

President Ruto's latest announcement for Maseno School has ignited a fierce debate among parents and education stakeholders. While the promise of expansion offers immediate relief to one institution, citizens are questioning if this approach addresses the systemic neglect facing thousands of other public schools across the country.

During the 120th anniversary celebration of Maseno School in Kisumu, the President laid out an ambitious plan to transform the institution into a model for secondary education. The centerpiece of the announcement is the conversion of 25 Board of Management teachers into permanent Teachers Service Commission staff, alongside a massive infrastructure overhaul. This includes the construction of 40 classrooms, new dormitory facilities to accommodate an extra 2,000 students, and a multipurpose hall. The government also pledged Ksh 3 million to support lunch programs and extracurricular activities, marking a significant investment for a single campus.

### The Voices from the Ground

The reception to these promises is as divided as the country's economic landscape. At the center of the discussion is the disparity in how government funds reach the classroom.

For many parents in rural areas, the news feels like a slap in the face. A primary school teacher from Homa Bay remarked that while the staff at Maseno deserve their promotions, it is frustrating to see the state pump millions into an already elite institution while their own school roofs leak and textbooks remain a luxury. To them, the Maseno model feels like a political vanity project rather than a structural solution for the education sector.

Within the corridors of teacher unions, the sentiment is more cautious. A local education advocate pointed out that the TSC permanent employment for BOM teachers is a welcome relief, but it exposes a wider crisis of understaffing. They argue that if the government can find the budget to regularize staff at a high-profile school, there is no excuse for the perpetual teacher shortages in marginalized counties where the teacher-to-student ratio is dangerously high.

Others are looking at the fiscal sustainability of these mega-projects. A Nairobi-based policy analyst noted that building 40 classrooms is the easy part. The real challenge, he suggests, is the maintenance budget. He questions who will pay for the electricity, water, and cleaning services for these massive new structures five years from now, especially when the government is already struggling with the fiscal burden on the exchequer. He believes the focus should shift from opening new rooms to ensuring that existing facilities are not falling into decay.

This event forces a conversation about whether the state should be playing the role of a benefactor to top-tier schools or acting as a stabilizer for the hundreds of crumbling day schools that serve the vast majority of Kenyan children.

### What to Watch Next

Public attention will now shift to whether these promises are codified in the upcoming national budget. Watch for how other school boards react; if the Maseno model becomes Za Ndani, expect a wave of demands from other national and regional schools for similar infrastructure cash injections. Additionally, the process of transitioning the 25 BOM teachers will be closely monitored to see if it follows a meritocratic path or if it triggers fresh allegations of political patronage. The true test will be whether the government can balance these localized gestures with a broader policy that ensures no school is left behind, unlike the current situation where students in remote regions continue to struggle with basic needs while others receive state-funded upgrades.

The mood on the ground remains skeptical, as citizens wait to see if this is a genuine shift in education policy or merely a momentary headline.
