## Fuel VAT Reduction Targets Economic Stability President William Ruto has assented to a law halving Value Added Tax on petroleum products to eight percent. This measure aims to alleviate household and business pressure, with immediate pump price adjustments observed in Nairobi and Mombasa. The government cites external shocks as the primary driver for this economic intervention.

Immediate Market Response and Consumer Relief The legislative change has promptly triggered a downward adjustment in fuel prices following recalculations by the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA). In Nairobi, super petrol decreased by KSh 9.37 to KSh 197.60 per litre, with diesel falling by KSh 10.21 to KSh 196.63. Mombasa saw super petrol retail at KSh 194.32 and diesel at KSh 193.35. These revisions bring pump prices in the capital below the KSh 200 mark, partially reversing earlier increases. The reduction is intended to ease the cost of living and operational expenses for businesses, mitigating the ripple effect on consumer goods and services.

Legislative Rationale Amidst Global Volatility Deputy Majority Leader Owen Baya emphasized that the VAT reduction was necessitated by external shocks impacting Kenya's position within the global financial ecosystem. Disruptions in the Middle East, in particular, have significantly affected the country, exhausting existing legal provisions for mitigation. This policy move underscores the government's responsiveness to international economic pressures and its commitment to safeguarding domestic economic stability. The government's proactive stance in managing such challenges is also reflected in its preparedness for other national concerns, including heavy rainfall and thunderstorms across Nairobi and Western Kenya.

Long-term Economic Reforms and Supply Chain Scrutiny While supporting the VAT cut, lawmakers advocated for deeper structural reforms to address persistently high fuel costs. Kitui Central MP Makali Mulu urged a review of additional levies embedded in fuel pricing, noting discrepancies with neighboring countries despite sourcing from the same markets. Central Imenti MP Moses Kirima called for accelerated development of Kenya’s own petroleum resources to enhance national resilience against external shocks. Concerns were also raised regarding the efficiency of the government-to-government (G-to-G) fuel import framework, with Suba South MP Caroli Omondi cautioning that market concentration could dilute the intended benefits of the tax reduction. Kabuchai MP Majimbo Kalasinga, meanwhile, warned transport operators against maintaining high fares, insisting that fuel cost savings must be passed on to consumers.
