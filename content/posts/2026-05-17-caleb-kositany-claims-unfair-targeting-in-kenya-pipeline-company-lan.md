Former Soy Member of Parliament Caleb Kositany is currently under investigation for his role in the illegal acquisition of land belonging to the Kenya Pipeline Company. This May 2026 probe has prompted the former lawmaker to publicly defend his actions against accusations of misconduct.

## Analysis of the Kositany Land Acquisition Defense

### The logic of comparative criminality
Kositany has openly admitted to obtaining portions of the Kenya Pipeline Company land. However, he maintains that the investigative focus on his actions constitutes unfair targeting. His primary defense rests on the assertion that numerous other high-ranking public figures acquired significantly larger tracts of the same property yet have not faced similar scrutiny from law enforcement agencies. By highlighting these omissions, Kositany attempts to shift the narrative from his personal culpability to the perceived inconsistency of the state.

### Examining the culture of impunity
The argument that one should only be held accountable if all other participants are treated equally reflects a deep-seated culture of impunity within political circles. When public assets are treated as private resources, those involved often view legal action as a personal grievance rather than a necessary step toward justice. This specific brand of entitlement creates a framework where corruption is normalized as a collective habit, leading perpetrators to feel genuinely victimized when they are singled out for investigation while their peers remain undisturbed.

### Accountability versus collective deflection
The ongoing investigation into the Kenya Pipeline Company parcel serves as a stark reminder of the challenges surrounding institutional integrity. By framing his potential prosecution as a matter of selective justice, Kositany avoids addressing the fundamental illegality of the land transfer. This strategy of deflection underscores a broader trend in which individuals caught in the unauthorized possession of public property prioritize personal grievances over the rule of law. Ultimately, asserting that others have committed more significant offenses does not absolve an individual of legal responsibility, nor does it mitigate the ethical implications of the original land acquisition.
