## The Pipeline Paradox: Why Capping KPC Tariffs Won't Brake Fuel Inflation

The Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority has moved to curb rising transport costs by capping Kenya Pipeline Company tariffs for the next three years. While this measure offers temporary relief to motorists, it risks creating long-term structural deficits in critical infrastructure maintenance and investment.

The decision arrives as Nairobi and other major towns face severe gridlock. Today, May 18, 2026, matatu operators have launched a nationwide strike, protesting the combined impact of recent fuel price hikes and stagnant operational margins. As commuters walk to work, the government seeks to stem the bleeding by limiting how much KPC can charge to move petroleum products from Mombasa to the hinterland. By capping the transport rate at Ksh 5.44 per cubic metre per kilometre for the 2025/2026 cycle, rising to Ksh 5.83 by 2027/2028, regulators are essentially betting that price suppression can substitute for systemic economic reform. 

### A History of Deferred Maintenance

The current tariff structure is not an isolated policy shift but the latest chapter in a long-running effort to manage public frustration over energy costs. This regulatory dance follows a familiar rhythm:

- 2023: The government faced mounting pressure as global market volatility forced consecutive retail price increases, leading to a temporary suspension of certain levies to pacify the transport sector.
- 2024: A series of technical audits revealed that consistent under-funding of KPC infrastructure led to localized pipeline integrity issues, necessitating a push for higher tariffs that were ultimately denied.
- 2025: As retail costs surged again-noted in reports on global market instability-the debate shifted from whether to raise tariffs to how much of the cost the state could absorb before the utility became insolvent.

For years, KPC has argued that its proposed tariff increases were essential to maintain the aging pipeline network feeding depots in Nairobi, Nakuru, Eldoret, and Kisumu. By denying these requests and enforcing a lower cap, the regulator is essentially shifting the burden of cost management away from the consumer's immediate checkout experience and onto the utility's balance sheet. 

### The Infrastructure Bottleneck

When the regulator forces KPC to operate on a margin thinner than its actual cost of service, the immediate impact is visible in the depreciation of maintenance budgets. The approved terminal tariffs of Ksh 2,851.80 for Nairobi and upwards of Ksh 4,200 for Western Kenya hubs serve as a rigid ceiling. If these rates remain below the actual cost of upgrading pumping stations or replacing pipe sections, the pipeline itself becomes the victim of the very fuel price inflation it is meant to regulate.

History suggests that these price-capping strategies create a compounding debt cycle. When infrastructure eventually fails due to under-investment, the cost of emergency repairs-combined with the economic losses from supply disruptions-far exceeds the initial savings realized by capping tariffs. Unlike the volatile retail market, where prices move monthly, pipeline infrastructure requires predictable, long-term funding cycles to prevent total system stagnation.

### Why it Matters Now

The matatu strike serves as a blunt reminder of the fragility of the transport-fuel nexus. With petrol prices having spiked by Ksh 16.65 and diesel by Ksh 46.29 recently, the average operator is past the breaking point. Capping pipeline tariffs is a fiscal tourniquet applied to a systemic injury. While it may provide enough breathing room to suppress retail price hikes for this quarter, it does nothing to address the structural reliance on imported fuel or the underlying operational costs of the national pipeline network.

### What to Watch Next

The coming months will test whether this three-year tariff schedule can hold against the reality of global oil prices. If the currency fluctuates or international crude costs spike, the gap between the capped KPC rate and the actual cost of operation will widen. Watch for signs of delayed pipeline maintenance or reduced pumping throughput in the Western Kenya depots. Policy stop-gaps eventually lose their effectiveness when the underlying economic pressures exceed the regulatory limit. 

Today’s policy patch will inevitably become tomorrow’s fiscal crisis.
