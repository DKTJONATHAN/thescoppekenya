## Treasury Maintains Fuel Tax Stance Amid Market Volatility

The National Treasury has reaffirmed its commitment to existing fuel levies despite ongoing transport strikes across the country. Officials have identified the 270 KSh per litre threshold as an essential component of the government's fiscal framework. By maintaining these tax levels, the state aims to mitigate the impact of global supply chain instabilities on the national budget.

### Examining the Revenue Baseline

Treasury Cabinet Secretary John Mbadi has dismissed requests to repeal fuel taxes, framing current pump prices as a requirement for long-term economic stability. Transport operators have responded to rising operational costs with nationwide industrial action, yet the Ministry maintains that the differential between the current 242 KSh diesel price and the projected 270 KSh unsubsidized cost is a necessary buffer.

This 28 KSh margin serves as a vital safeguard for the national exchequer. Global energy market data highlights the volatility driving this decision, with diesel costs climbing from 642 USD to 1120 USD per metric tonne in recent periods. If the government were to absorb these costs through tax reductions, the resulting loss in revenue would jeopardize existing financial commitments. This includes the funding required for various infrastructure and public-private partnership initiatives currently managed by the state. Consequently, the government is prioritizing the preservation of sovereign liquidity over immediate concessions to consumers.

### Geopolitical Exposure and Fiscal Resilience

The refusal to alter tax policy is largely a response to risks originating in the Strait of Hormuz. As a net importer of refined petroleum, Kenya remains vulnerable to fluctuations within this maritime chokepoint. Escalations in Middle Eastern instability lead to immediate increases in insurance premiums and transit fees, which inflate the landing cost of fuel at the port of Mombasa.

The Treasury’s refusal to eliminate these levies functions as a defensive measure against external shocks. Should the government remove these taxes now, any further disruption in international shipping corridors would leave the national budget without the necessary flexibility to navigate a potential balance-of-payments crisis. By retaining the levies, the administration preserves the capacity to intervene if price spikes intensify, rather than leaving the domestic economy entirely dependent on the unpredictability of international spot markets. This approach reflects a broader economic strategy-emphasized during the recent Africa Forward Summit-to shield the local currency and state finances through consistent internal revenue collection.

### Economic Implications for Investors

For the investment community, this policy indicates that the government prioritizes inflation management over immediate growth stimulus. The transport sector is integral to regional logistics, and an extended strike poses a clear threat to manufacturing and retail supply chains. Businesses should anticipate that increased logistics costs will continue to propagate through the value chain. While the Treasury has suggested it will hold future consultations with transport stakeholders, the structural rigidity of the current fuel pricing formula appears unlikely to shift. The government has signaled a willingness to endure industrial unrest to avoid the structural deficits that would arise from a reduction in tax revenue.

### Forward-Looking Indicators

Market observers are now directing their attention toward the upcoming review cycle from the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority. The resilience of the 270 KSh threshold will face significant pressure if global diesel prices consistently exceed the 1120 USD per metric tonne mark. 

Investors should monitor parliamentary briefings where the Treasury is expected to clarify how these fuel levies influence the broader national infrastructure budget. Additionally, market participants should observe whether the government alters its rhetoric concerning fuel subsidies if the current transport strike leads to a measurable increase in official inflation figures. Further clarity regarding the country's fiscal trajectory will be provided in the upcoming June Supplementary Budget estimates, which will serve as the primary indicator of the government's long-term budgetary health.
