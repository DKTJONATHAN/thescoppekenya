## Regulating AI-Generated Exploitation on Social Media Platforms

TikTok has recently banned 20 accounts following an investigation that exposed a network of AI-generated Black female personas promoting sexually explicit content. This development highlights the urgent need for robust content moderation and ethical AI application, particularly as regulatory bodies like Kenya's Communications Authority intensify oversight on digital platforms.

### Global Misuse of AI-Generated Personas

An investigation by the BBC, in collaboration with analysts from Riddance, identified at least 60 accounts, predominantly on Meta-owned Instagram and several on TikTok, featuring highly sexualized AI-generated avatars of dark-skinned women. These accounts used racialized language like “ebony,” “noir,” and “dark,” and did not label their content as AI-generated, violating platform guidelines. One manipulated video, featuring an AI-generated face superimposed onto Malaysian model Riya Ulan's body without her consent, garnered 35 million views on TikTok and 173 million on Instagram. Researchers have criticized this trend as racist and exploitative, noting how AI tools facilitate the caricature of Black bodies with no social repercussions for the creators. TikTok stated it has zero tolerance for content promoting off-platform sexual services and prohibits unlabelled AI-generated content used without individuals' permission. This aligns with TikTok's community guidelines, which require disclosure of AI-generated content to prevent misinformation and prohibit synthetic media of private figures without permission. Previous studies have also revealed TikTok videos with AI-generated imagery of minors in sexualized outfits accumulating millions of likes.

### Kenya's Escalating Stance on Social Media Oversight

This issue resonates particularly in Kenya, where the Communications Authority of Kenya (CA) announced a formal inquiry into TikTok in March 2025. This followed a separate BBC investigation alleging the platform profited from sexualized livestreams involving Kenyan teenagers. The CA issued five formal demands to TikTok, requiring the removal of exploitative content involving minors, an explanation for moderation failures, and a plan to enhance child protection mechanisms.

TikTok's enforcement data in Kenya demonstrates increasing pressure. In Q2 2025, the platform removed nearly 600,000 Kenyan videos for community guideline violations, a rise from 450,000 in Q1 2025 and 360,000 the previous year. The Q3 2025 report showed 580,000 Kenyan videos removed between July and September, with 99.7% proactively identified and 94.6% deleted within 24 hours. Approximately 90,000 Kenyan live streams were also shut down mid-session during this quarter.

Kenya has maintained regulatory oversight on TikTok since 2023, having directed the platform to address content moderation, establish a local office, and publish quarterly compliance reports. The CA is prepared to impose sanctions and pursue legal action under both the Computer Misuse and Cybercrimes Act and the Films and Stage Plays Act. This heightened government scrutiny is a global trend, with Spain, for instance, initiating a prosecutorial investigation in February over allegations of platforms facilitating the spread of AI-generated child sexual abuse material.

### The Imperative for Ethical AI Governance in Africa

The incidents involving AI-generated personas underscore a critical challenge for the African tech landscape: ensuring ethical innovation. As AI tools become more accessible, the potential for misuse, particularly in ways that exploit vulnerable populations or perpetuate harmful stereotypes, grows. This calls for a proactive approach to AI governance, emphasizing transparency and accountability from platform providers. The ongoing regulatory actions by the CA in Kenya signal a clear intent to protect its digital citizens, fostering a safer online environment.
