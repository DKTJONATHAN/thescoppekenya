Over the years, I have stood at many gravesides in Lang'ata and sat through countless vigils in leafy suburbs and cramped living rooms, watching the Kenyan entertainment scene lose its brightest stars and dearest legends. We celebrate their lives with loud music and long tributes, but we rarely talk about the cold, hard mechanics of what actually happens when the curtain finally falls on a human life. <h2>The Slow Fade Before the Final Bow</h2> <p>Most people think death is like a light switch—on one second, off the next. In reality, unless it is a sudden accident on the Thika Superhighway, dying is usually a slow, deliberate process. I have sat with families who were confused why their loved one stopped eating or why they seemed to prefer staring at a wall rather than talking to the cousins who traveled from the village to see them. This isn't rudeness; it is the body preparing to exit.</p> <p>Your body is a high-maintenance machine that requires immense energy just to keep you awake. When you are dying, that energy is gone. You aren't sleeping to "recharge" anymore; you are sleeping because the heart can no longer pump enough oxygen-rich blood to keep the brain engaged. You might notice the skin becoming thin, almost like parchment paper, and easily bruised. This happens because the body stops prioritizing the repair of skin cells. It is putting all its remaining resources into the core organs, trying to keep the heart and lungs going for just a few more beats.</p> <p>One of the hardest things for Kenyan families to accept is the loss of appetite. In our culture, food is love. We try to force-feed porridge or soup, thinking it will bring back strength. But the digestive system is one of the first things to pack up. Pushing food at this stage often causes more distress than comfort because the body can no longer process it. Eventually, even the muscles that control when you pee or go to the bathroom lose their grip. It is a humbling, quiet decline that signals the end is near.</p> <h2>The Brain’s Secret Fireworks Display</h2> <p>There is a reason why so many people, including world-famous figures like Steve Jobs, have those "Oh wow" moments right before they go. For a long time, we thought the brain just flickered out like a dying candle. We now know that isn't true. Even after the heart stops beating and the lungs stop pulling in air, the brain often stages a massive, coordinated grand finale.</p> <p>In those final thirty seconds after clinical death—meaning your heart has stopped—there is often a massive spike in neural activity. It is a surge of electrical energy that looks remarkably like the brain waves we see when someone is in a state of heightened consciousness or deep meditation. This is the brain’s last dance. It is likely why people who have been revived talk about seeing their life flash before their eyes or feeling a profound sense of peace. This isn't just spiritual talk; it is a documented chemical event.</p> <p>The brain releases a cocktail of neurochemicals that would make any chemist’s head spin. You get a massive hit of endorphins—the body’s natural painkillers—which explains why people who have suffered horrific injuries often look peaceful in their final moments. There is also a surge of serotonin, which can cause vivid hallucinations, and potentially DMT, a powerful psychedelic that alters perception of time and space. When someone looks into the corner of the room and smiles at someone you cannot see, it is likely this chemical surge at work.</p> <h2>When the Body Becomes an Object</h2> <p>Once the brain finally goes quiet, we move from clinical death to biological death. This is when the "living" part truly ends and the "decay" part begins. It starts at a cellular level. Without oxygen, carbon dioxide builds up in the cells, making them acidic. This acidity causes the cell membranes to burst, releasing enzymes that begin to digest the body from the inside out. It is a process called autolysis, and it starts within minutes of the final breath.</p> <p>Then comes the stage that every mortician at Lee Funeral Home knows well: rigor mortis. About two to six hours after death, the body begins to stiffen. This isn't because the body is "fighting" death, but because the muscles require a specific type of energy (ATP) to relax. Since the body isn't making energy anymore, the muscles stay locked in whatever position they were in. This stiffness peaks at about twelve hours and then slowly fades away after two days as the tissues themselves begin to break down and liquefy.</p> <p>If the body isn't preserved, the next stage is putrefaction. The bacteria that lived peacefully in your gut while you were alive suddenly realize the "police" (your immune system) have left the building. They begin to feast on the tissues, producing gases that cause the body to bloat and emit those distinct, heavy odors we associate with death. The hemoglobin in the blood breaks down, which can turn the skin a greenish hue. It is a grisly process, but it is nature’s way of recycling the body back into the earth.</p> <h2>The Finality of the Physical</h2> <p>Watching this process over the years has taught me that we put so much stock in the physical shell, but that shell is incredibly fragile. We spend our lives dressing it up, taking it to the gym, and worrying about how it looks on a red carpet. Yet, in the end, the transition from a person to a body is remarkably fast. The "you" that people love is found in that electrical surge in the brain, the memories stored in those neurons, and the chemical reactions that allow you to feel joy.</p> <p>When the heart stops, the body doesn't just "quit." It goes through a highly organized, biological exit strategy. It protects you with endorphins, gives you a final burst of consciousness, and then slowly dissolves back into the elements. Knowing the mechanics doesn't make the loss of a friend any easier, but it does take away some of the mystery. Death isn't a dark hole; it is the final, essential process our bodies perform for us before we leave the stage for good.</p>
