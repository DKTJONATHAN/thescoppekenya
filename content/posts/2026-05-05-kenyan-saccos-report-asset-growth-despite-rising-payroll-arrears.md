## The Billion-Shilling Hold: How Public Sector Arrears Cripple Member Dividends

The Kenyan SACCO sector is parading a KSh 1.076 trillion asset base like a trophy, but behind the glossy annual reports lies a grim reality. While the industry celebrates trillion-shilling growth, thousands of civil servants are effectively being forced to subsidize state liquidity gaps.

The math is simple and deeply predatory. Public sector employers are holding onto KSh 3.49 billion in unremitted payroll deductions that rightfully belong to SACCO members. This is not just a bookkeeping oversight. It is a calculated delay that turns private savings into an interest-free loan for the government, all while SACCOs scramble to maintain the liquidity required to pay dividends to their 55,602 impacted members.

### Institutional Growth versus Individual Erosion

The industry narrative is one of triumph. Gross loan portfolios have ballooned to KSh 845.11 billion, and regulators point to an 8.39 percent non-performing loan ratio as a sign of institutional health. But these numbers mask the rot. When a SACCO holds 73 percent of its holdings in loans, it relies on steady monthly cash flows to remain solvent. When the employer fails to remit deductions, that cash flow evaporates.

SASRA has signaled that these arrears represent a systemic risk to the sector. This is not hyperbole. When member money is stuck in the state's coffers, SACCOs are forced to freeze credit lines or delay dividend payouts to keep the ship afloat. The members suffer twice: their savings are frozen in a stalled bureaucracy, and their potential returns are cannibalized to cover the gaps left by the very entity that is supposed to be paying them. It is a closed loop of financial extraction where the institution grows on paper while the individual member pays the price for the government's fiscal mismanagement.

### The Hidden Tax on Civil Servants

This situation mirrors the wider fiscal crisis where the state leans on local lenders to plug massive budget deficits, as seen when the government added 1.3 trillion to the national debt in 2025. Now, that pressure has reached the SACCO level. By failing to remit deductions on time, the government is essentially treating the SACCO sector as a captive lender, forcing it to provide liquidity that the Treasury cannot source elsewhere.

The 55,602 affected members are being used as involuntary shock absorbers for the national budget. They are not merely saving for homes or retirement; they are financing the state's inability to manage its own cash flow. While the sector reports record assets, the reality for the average member is stagnant interest on deposits and a shrinking dividend pool. The money that should be compounding in an investment account is instead serving as a zero-cost bridge for public sector payroll delays.

### Why it matters now
The persistence of these arrears creates a dangerous precedent. If the government continues to treat SACCO remittances as discretionary spending, it destabilizes the entire cooperative movement. This is no longer a matter of administrative delay; it is a fundamental breach of the trust between the employer and the employee. When liquidity dries up, the most vulnerable members are always the first to be denied emergency loans or withdrawals, regardless of how healthy the sector's total asset base looks on a balance sheet.

### What to watch next
The focus must turn to the regulatory enforcement of these remittances. SASRA needs to move beyond publishing warnings and start demanding punitive measures against government agencies that treat payroll deductions as an optional expense. Until there is a mechanism to penalize the state for these delays, the practice will continue to fester. Watch for increased agitation from SACCO delegates who are beginning to realize that their institution's stability is being traded for the government's convenience.

Who wins when the state keeps the cash while the member loses the dividend?
