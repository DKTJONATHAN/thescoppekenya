## The Ksh4.5 Billion UoN Engineering and Science Complex: A Decade of Paperwork

The University of Nairobi has finally secured the green light for its Ksh4.5 billion engineering complex, a project touted as a milestone in Kenya-France relations. Yet, behind the official handshakes, the timeline reveals a decade of administrative inertia and stalled promises.

### The Geography of Stagnation

For ten years, the proposed engineering and science hub at the Chiromo campus existed as little more than a phantom infrastructure. The initial concepts were drawn up in 2015, during the previous administration. By March 2019, the project had shifted from a hopeful architectural sketch to a stalled diplomatic talking point. Every subsequent fiscal year saw the same ritual: high-level summits, promises of French funding, and eventual silence.

Between 2019 and 2022, the project suffered under the weight of shifting political priorities and the classic Kenyan bureaucratic crawl. While funding for other state projects flowed, this critical investment in AI and green energy languished in a state of suspended animation. It was not a lack of resources, but a lack of political continuity. As [Moi University faces potential auction after court order freezes bank accounts](https://zandani.co.ke/article/moi-university-faces-potential-auction-after-court-order-freezes-ban), the University of Nairobi project serves as a stark reminder of how public infrastructure in Kenya remains vulnerable to the whims of changing administrations and the slow death of inter-agency cooperation.

### Procurement as the Latest Hurdle

The December 2025 contract award to Scope Design Systems is the latest attempt to breathe life into this dormant plan. By bringing in a specialized firm, the government aims to signal that this is no longer just a diplomatic gesture but a concrete development. However, the optics of announcing this partnership at the Africa Forward Summit cannot hide the reality of the 2015-2025 timeline.

This is the cycle of Kenyan governance. A project starts under one president, stalls during the transition, and is rebranded under the next. The design contracts and architectural plans that finally materialized in late 2025 are essentially a decade-late solution to a problem identified during the previous decade. While officials cheer the partnership as a victory for modern research, they remain conspicuously silent on why it took ten years to move from a basic feasibility study to an actual design contract. The cost of this delay is not just measured in the inflation of construction materials, but in the lost opportunities for the thousands of engineering students who passed through UoN while the facility remained a concept.

### Why it matters now

Education infrastructure is the backbone of the workforce. When projects like the UoN Engineering and Science Complex stall for a decade, the ripple effects are felt in the quality of graduates entering the market. With [Treasury Tax Reduction Conditions Set for PAYE, VAT, and Income Tax Reduction](https://zandani.co.ke/article/treasury-tax-reduction-conditions-paye-vat-income-tax-reduction) tightening the belt on public spending, the government needs to demonstrate that large-scale loans and partnerships actually translate to functional buildings rather than just perpetual planning phases.

### What to watch next

The focus must now shift to the ground. Construction is scheduled to follow the design phase, but history provides a cautionary tale. Taxpayers should keep a close watch on the actual breaking of ground in the coming months. Will Scope Design Systems be given the independence to execute the project without the usual political interference, or will this complex become another monument to bureaucratic delay?

If the government can secure billions for a project, why did it take two different presidential terms and ten years of diplomatic summits to reach the point where a design contract is considered a major victory?
