The campaign trail for the Real Madrid presidency has descended into a spectacle of amateurish delusion, with candidate Enrique Riquelme treating Manchester City’s squad like personal merchandise. As the June 7 election approaches, Riquelme’s reliance on using world-class players as campaign props has finally triggered a sharp rebuke from the Etihad Stadium.

### Intellectual Property as a Political Weapon

The desperation in Riquelme’s camp is palpable. Attempting to gain traction in a stagnant race, the candidate has featured Erling Haaland in his promotional materials, acting as if the Norwegian striker were an unpaid campaign staffer rather than a contracted asset of a Premier League giant. Manchester City, a club that guards its brand image with ruthless efficiency, has made it clear that this unauthorized usage will not stand.

This is far beyond standard transfer rumors. It is a fundamental disregard for intellectual property. Riquelme’s campaign has adopted a strategy of manufacturing "inevitability," asserting that players like Rodri are essentially destined for the Bernabéu. These claims are entirely fictitious. Neither the players nor their representatives have engaged with Riquelme, yet the campaign continues to leverage these falsehoods to manipulate fan sentiment. 

Much like the broader instability seen in the global economy, where tensions in the Strait of Hormuz create artificial fluctuations in fuel and shipping costs, Riquelme’s rhetoric creates a localized volatility in the transfer market. He is weaponizing the hopes of a fanbase that craves star power, peddling fantasies to secure votes from supporters who are being sold a bridge that does not exist. It is a hollow maneuver, built on the exploitation of fan loyalty rather than legitimate sporting ambition.

### Florentino Pérez and the Defensive Perimeter

Florentino Pérez, ever the tactician, has treated this entire ordeal as a nuisance to be swatted away. Recognizing the potential for the Riquelme campaign to gain unearned momentum, Pérez moved to fortify his position by calling an early election and shifting the narrative toward concrete action.

The recent acquisitions of Ibrahima Konate and Denzel Dumfries are not necessarily the flashy, ego-driven transfers Madrid fans obsess over, but they serve a clear purpose: they are barricades. Pérez is effectively signaling to the electorate that while his opponent deals in imaginary signings and PR stunts, the current administration is busy executing the logistical requirements of managing a premier club. 

It is a calculated display of power. By focusing on club operations, Pérez reinforces the idea that the presidency is not an entry-level position for grandstanders. Much like the current consolidation trends in the streaming industry, where dominant players prioritize market stability and asset protection, Pérez is protecting his firm grip on the Bernabéu. He has successfully framed the upcoming election as a choice between professional stewardship and the chaotic posturing of an outsider who is clearly out of his depth.

### The Cost of Amateurism

Manchester City’s intervention acts as a stark reality check for the Riquelme campaign. Using other clubs’ players as electoral billboards is not clever politics; it is a liability. Riquelme appears to operate under the assumption that he can speak a squad into existence through sheer bravado, completely ignoring the complex legal frameworks that govern modern football. 

Candidates in this race are offering voters a mirage. They are promising a future built on assets that are owned by others, hoping to secure a seat at a table that is not vacant. When the ballots are counted this weekend, the fallout from this charade will leave a stain on the club’s institutional prestige. Real Madrid is currently projecting the image of a disorganized, underfunded regional entity rather than a sophisticated European powerhouse. 

The campaign’s reliance on these tactics has backfired. Manchester City has demanded a public retraction of the claims linking their players to the Riquelme camp, effectively forcing the candidate to admit that his primary electoral promises were built on thin air. As the clock runs down to Saturday, the farce is reaching its inevitable, embarrassing conclusion. The voters are left to decide whether they prefer the cold, calculated security of the incumbent or a candidate who seems to believe that his campaign is immune to the basic rules of contract law.
