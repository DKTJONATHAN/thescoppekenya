## Kenyan Family Seeks Answers for Kin Recruited into Russian Army

A Kenyan family has urgently appealed to the government for assistance in locating their relative, Humphrey Kimani, who was recruited into the Russian army and has since gone silent. This incident underscores broader concerns regarding Kenyan citizens unlawfully engaged in the Russia-Ukraine conflict, prompting intensified governmental efforts to address the crisis.

### Family's Urgent Appeal

The family of Humphrey Kimani expressed profound alarm after losing contact with him, raising fears he may have perished in the ongoing war. According to family members, reaching Kimani by phone has become impossible, fueling anxieties given the confirmed deaths of other Kenyans recruited into the conflict. This situation highlights the desperate circumstances many families face when their loved ones, drawn by promises of high remuneration, disappear on foreign battlefields.

### Growing Casualties Among Kenyans

Reports indicate a grim toll on Kenyan nationals involved in the conflict. Over 18 Kenyans recruited into the Russian army have been confirmed dead, with their families often struggling to repatriate bodies for proper burial. The National Intelligence Service's February 2026 parliamentary report revealed that over 1,000 Kenyan citizens were unlawfully recruited, enticed by monthly salaries up to Ksh 350,000 and bonuses exceeding Ksh 1.2 million. However, these promises frequently lead to tragic outcomes, leaving families in distress over unaccounted for relatives and unfulfilled financial expectations, a stark reminder of the vulnerabilities exploited, similar to salary disputes seen in other sectors. Beyond the fatalities, 28 Kenyans are currently missing, 39 have been hospitalized, and over 80 remain on the conflict's frontlines. A family in Kisii recently held a funeral service, burying an empty coffin due to the inability to bring their kin's body home.

### Government Repatriation and Prevention Efforts

The Kenyan government has significantly escalated its efforts to repatriate citizens trapped in the conflict zones. Foreign Affairs Principal Secretary Korir Singoei has engaged in discussions with both Russian and Ukrainian ambassadors to Kenya, raising concerns about affected nationals and exploring repatriation mechanisms. In a further high-level intervention, Prime Cabinet Secretary Musalia Mudavadi is expected to travel to Russia this month, March 2026, to negotiate structured agreements with Russian authorities aimed at curbing unlawful military recruitment. Simultaneously, domestic authorities are bolstering measures to prevent further recruitment. The Directorate of Criminal Investigations (DCI) recently apprehended a suspect believed to be part of a human trafficking syndicate that lures vulnerable individuals with promises of legitimate employment in European countries, only to funnel them into military service.
