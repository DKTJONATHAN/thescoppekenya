## DCI Foils Major Jet Fuel Theft Attempt at Wilson Airport

Detectives from the Directorate of Criminal Investigations (DCI) and the Kenya Airport Police Unit (KAPU) have successfully thwarted an attempt to siphon jet fuel from an aircraft undergoing maintenance at Wilson Airport. Four suspects were arrested in connection with the sophisticated DCI jet fuel theft operation, driven by the fuel's high market value and versatility as an additive.

### Suspicious Vehicle Leads to Arrests

Officers intercepted a white Mitsubishi Canter truck at Wilson Airport after its movement through routine activity aroused suspicion. A subsequent search of the vehicle revealed four drums, each containing 200 litres of jet fuel. Investigators believe the fuel was drained from a plane undergoing maintenance, a situation potentially providing cover for the illicit siphoning. The DCI stated that a "covert attempt to siphon jet fuel and sneak it out under the cover of routine airport activity has been thwarted." The vehicle was "carrying a heavy secret: four drums, each brimming with 200 litres of jet fuel." The vehicle's owner, the driver, and two additional suspects have been taken into custody.

### Regulatory Breaches Uncovered

Further investigation by detectives established that the alleged owner of the consignment lacked a gate pass, which is mandatory for access to the restricted airport area. Additionally, the Mitsubishi Canter truck itself did not possess the necessary clearance from the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA) required for the transportation of petroleum products. These breaches highlighted the criminal intent behind the operation. The vehicle and the four drums of fuel are now held as exhibits for the ongoing DCI investigation.

### The Lure of Jet Fuel Theft

Jet fuel theft is often motivated by its high market value and its versatility. Due to being taxed differently and generally cheaper than diesel, it is sometimes mixed with diesel to increase volume and profit margins for unscrupulous dealers. In Kenya, jet fuel is also illicitly sold for substance abuse, with individuals sniffing it for intoxicating effects. These factors contribute to the persistent challenge of DCI jet fuel theft.

The DCI continues its thorough investigation into the matter.
