## The Digital Burden: A Call to Reform Kenya’s Health Records

The transition to electronic medical records is being heralded as a hallmark of national advancement. However, for those on the front lines of care, this technological pivot feels less like progress and more like an unsustainable administrative load. If digitization is intended to support patient outcomes, our current trajectory is moving in the opposite direction by trapping critical clinical work beneath layers of redundant data entry.

### The Illusion of Efficiency at the Bedside

Dear Clinician, the current expectation placed upon you is illogical and exhausting. While the Ministry of Health champions a paperless vision, your daily experience remains defined by the grueling reality of dual-reporting. Across our clinics, from the busy wards in Nairobi to the most isolated rural outposts, you are effectively working two jobs. You are required to maintain legacy MoH 711 registers while simultaneously entering identical information into a digital portal. This is not innovation. It is a systematic doubling of your labor that redirects your attention from the person in need to the keyboard in front of you.

Consider a parent arriving at a clinic in Turkana. That mother is not concerned with the server stability of TIBABU or the user interface of KenyaEMR. Her priority is her child’s health. Yet, because these platforms lack interoperability, you are frequently compelled to navigate between disconnected digital tabs instead of focusing on direct examination. The digital record, intended to be a tool for efficiency, has become a cumbersome barrier. It consumes the time that should be reserved for listening, diagnosing, and healing.

### Structural Failures and the Human Cost

Our move toward devolved governance has resulted in a fragmented landscape of incompatible systems. Afya Ke, KenyaEMR, and TIBABU operate in silos, rarely communicating with one another. In regions where internet access is erratic and equipment supply chains are fragile, these digital mandates are destined to falter. We have witnessed how environmental volatility, such as the recent April flooding in Nairobi, can cripple essential infrastructure. A digital health strategy that relies on the fantasy of constant connectivity is fundamentally flawed. When the software freezes, patient wait times balloon, and Za Ndani of care inevitably drops.

There is a disturbing irony in this transformation. By elevating the importance of data for centralized reporting, the state has inadvertently sidelined the human core of medicine. You are being recast as an administrative clerk rather than a healthcare professional. When you are forced to prioritize a database entry over the comfort of a suffering patient, the system has clearly failed its primary objective. The promise of streamlining your practice has been replaced by the struggle to master unintuitive interfaces that fail to account for clinical intuition.

### Addressing the Crisis

We are currently witnessing an IT project masquerading as a clinical strategy. If this persists, we should expect a sharp increase in provider burnout and a steady decline in patient wellness. We are sacrificing the efficiency of traditional record-keeping for the opacity of disconnected software that ignores the realities of our local medical landscape.

### A Path Toward Meaningful Change

It is time to scrutinize how county governments handle system consolidation. We must watch to see if there is a sincere commitment to unifying these platforms or if the trend of investing in expensive, disparate licenses will prevail. Ultimately, success should not be measured by the volume of records digitized. Instead, we must ask if the time spent per patient is decreasing.

I urge you to advocate for an immediate cessation of dual-reporting requirements. We need a single, coherent interface designed for clinical utility rather than aggregate data extraction. Let us restore the focus of our healthcare workers to the people they serve, ensuring that screens no longer obscure the vital connection between doctor and patient.

Signed,

A Concerned Observer of Our Healthcare Reality
