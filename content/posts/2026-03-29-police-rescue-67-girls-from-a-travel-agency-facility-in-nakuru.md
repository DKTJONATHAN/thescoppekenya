Police in Njoro, Nakuru County, successfully rescued 67 girls on Sunday, March 29, 2026, from a facility in Nakuru. The girls were reportedly confined by a travel agency, awaiting planned travel to Gulf countries for purported job opportunities, facing threats and unhygienic conditions during their stay.

Facility Closure and Ongoing Investigations Njoro Sub-County Police Commander Nelson Koech addressed the press on Sunday, March 29, confirming the rescue operation. The victims recounted being threatened and assaulted while held at the facility. The police raid was initiated after one of the girls alerted security agencies, detailing their months-long confinement awaiting travel documents. Koech noted the highly unsanitary conditions, describing an overcrowded space infested with insects, which forced the girls to sleep during the day. The facility is reportedly operated by a travel agency whose license expired in late 2025. Following the rescue, authorities have closed the premises to facilitate comprehensive investigations.

Legal Counterarguments and Official Warnings Lawyers representing the facility's operators have stated their intention to challenge the closure in court, vehemently denying the girls' allegations. One lawyer expressed surprise at the claims of torture, asserting that as a human rights advocate, they would have actively championed the girls' cause had such allegations been truthful, and criticized what they termed "blanket statements." Meanwhile, the government has consistently advised individuals seeking employment abroad to utilize officially recognized channels to ensure a secure process. The Ministry of Labour urges individuals to verify job opportunities and recruitment agencies through the National Employment Authority (NEA) website. On Tuesday, January 20, 2026, Labour and Social Protection Cabinet Secretary Alfred Mutua disclosed that 390 cases of alleged labour and travel agency fraud are currently under investigation. The Ministry has issued strong warnings against traveling on tourist visas for employment, making payments via personal numbers, or engaging in cash transactions without formal written agreements or official receipts. Mutua highlighted that some entities accused of defrauding people are not registered with the NEA or the Business Registration Service (BRS, an offense in itself), and affirmed the Ministry's commitment to sanitizing the sector and safeguarding job seekers. This commitment to combating fraudulent schemes underscores a broader governmental push against corruption and illicit financial activities, echoing actions like the recent [EACC arrests Kilifi County revenue officer for demanding Ksh435,000 bribe](https://zandani.co.ke/article/eacc-arrests-kilifi-county-revenue-officer-for-demanding-ksh435-000-).
