## The Road Maintenance Levy Tug-of-War: Whose Potholes Get Fixed?

While the government boasts of grand infrastructure projects, the average motorist is busy counting the cost of shredded tires and broken suspensions. Behind the scenes, a 120 billion KSh fund is trapped in a bureaucratic stalemate between the Senate and the Ministry of Roads.

The Ministry of Roads and Transport, led by Davis Chirchir, paints a picture of a national road network needing prioritized, high-density traffic management. Yet, the balance sheet tells a different story. The counties are tasked with managing 76 percent of the country’s road network, yet they are being starved of the funds required to keep those arteries functional. Currently, the national government clings to a funding model that ignores the reality of rural and regional connectivity, choosing instead to focus on trunk roads that serve the narrative of rapid industrial progress while the backroads of Kenya disintegrate into craters.

### The Math of Managed Decay

The central tension lies in a simple, brutal disparity: 76 percent of the road network is managed by counties, but they are consistently sidelined when it comes to accessing the Road Maintenance Levy Fund. The Senate has pushed for a more equitable 51 percent national to 49 percent county split to reflect these responsibilities. The Ministry, however, remains unmoved, citing traffic volume as the ultimate arbiter of where cash flows.

This is not merely a debate about administrative efficiency; it is about whose vehicles get to roll without damage. When 12 KSh of every litre of fuel is pledged to investors for the securitization of infrastructure bonds, that money is effectively earmarked before it even touches the road. By locking away such a significant portion of the levy for debt servicing, the Ministry has stripped itself of the flexibility needed to address emergency repairs at the local level. It is a classic case of paying the ghosts of past projects while ignoring the present catastrophe. The Court of Appeal suspension of the 2025 High Court ruling has only served to solidify this gatekeeping, leaving county governors with nothing but empty pockets and deteriorating earth roads.

### Follow the Money to the Potholes

The securitization of the fuel levy has created a dangerous feedback loop. By committing 12 KSh per litre to service long-term debt, the state has effectively mortgaged the convenience of the Kenyan motorist to satisfy the appetite of financiers. The result is a maintenance budget that acts as a debt repayment mechanism rather than a public service tool. 

While the political elite fight over which office gets to sign the checks, the taxpayer is the one footing the bill for a crumbling transport system. It is a familiar rhythm in our public discourse-much like the personal regrets aired by public figures who prioritize the wrong things, such as [Khalif Kairo’s recent reflections on personal timing](https://zandani.co.ke/article/khalif-kairo-reveals-regret-over-not-marrying-before-mother-died) or the intense scrutiny on [Arsenal’s Premier League prospects](https://zandani.co.ke/article/jamie-carragher-warns-arsenal-face-real-problem-if-they-miss-premier), the mismanagement of the fuel levy feels like a missed opportunity that will carry a heavy, long-term interest rate. The roads will not wait for the Ministry and the Senate to settle their power struggle.

### Why it matters now
The current stalemate ensures that the rainy season will exacerbate the degradation of the 76 percent of roads under county control. With no clear mechanism for emergency funding reaching the local level, the cycle of road destruction will accelerate, directly impacting the cost of living as transport prices for goods and passengers rise.

### What to watch next
Keep an eye on the upcoming parliamentary sessions where amendments to the Roads Act will likely be tabled again. Specifically, monitor whether the Ministry concedes to a performance-based funding model that separates maintenance cash from debt servicing obligations.

If the 120 billion KSh collected from fuel taxes is legally tethered to investor returns rather than the actual state of the tarmac, when exactly does the Kenyan motorist stop being a lender and start being a beneficiary?
