## The Price of Proof: How ARA’s Failed Forfeiture Bids Impact Property Security

If you own land or a home, could you prove exactly how every shilling used to buy it was earned years or even decades ago? A recent High Court ruling shows that for many Kenyans, the answer to that question is now a vital line of defense against state asset seizure.

Justice Benjamin Musyoki recently dismissed a petition by the Assets Recovery Agency (ARA) to seize three Mombasa apartments linked to Asif Amirali Alibhai Jetha and Lyn Henderson. While the case centered on these specific properties in Nyali, the underlying message is clear: the state must meet a high evidentiary bar before it can take private property. This decision acts as a reminder for every working Kenyan that in the face of financial scrutiny, your personal record keeping is your most valuable asset.

### What is the evidentiary burden in civil forfeiture cases?

In Kenya, the ARA acts on the suspicion that assets are the proceeds of crime. When they seek civil forfeiture, they are essentially asking the court to strip ownership based on the premise that the money used to buy the property cannot be linked to a legitimate source. However, as seen in the Mombasa apartments case, suspicion alone is not enough.

Justice Musyoki ruled that the state failed to provide sufficient evidence that the apartments were linked to illicit activities. The property owners successfully countered by providing a clear financial trail, including evidence of initial capital from savings and gifts, as well as profits from previous real estate deals. This highlights a crucial policy gap: the Agency needs more than a hunch to override property rights. The judiciary is signaling that the burden of proof rests heavily on the state to demonstrate a criminal origin, rather than forcing the property owner to prove their innocence in a vacuum.

### How documentation acts as a shield for property owners

For the average citizen, this case emphasizes why detailed financial documentation is not just for tax filing-it is a legal shield. The couple involved managed to track their capital back to specific transactions, such as a KSh 26.5 million plot sale and documented savings. Without these records, they might have faced a much harder battle.

Prolonged legal battles over property create significant economic friction. When assets are tied up in litigation, they lose their liquidity. You cannot sell, rent, or leverage a property that is caught in a legal freeze. This uncertainty ripples through the market, as investors become wary of assets that could be flagged by the state without clear, immediate evidence. Protecting your investment requires maintaining an organized paper trail. If you buy land or build apartments, keep your sale agreements, bank statements, and investment records in a secure, easily accessible format for years to come.

### Why it matters now

In a cooling economy, property owners are feeling the pressure of inflation and the need to protect their tangible investments. When state agencies initiate seizures that later fail in court, it creates an environment of instability. For the ordinary worker striving to build a portfolio, the security of their title deed is paramount. Knowing that the courts hold the ARA to a strict standard of evidence provides some comfort, but it also places the onus on the individual to be prepared for the worst-case scenario.

### What to watch next

The real test will be whether the ARA adjusts its investigative standards following this ruling. If they continue to pursue assets without robust evidence, we may see more cases dismissed, leading to questions about the efficiency of state resources. Keep an eye on how these legal precedents influence future land transactions. As the market evolves, it is possible that financial due diligence requirements will become more stringent for private buyers.
