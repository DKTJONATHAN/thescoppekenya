## The High Cost of Weak Evidence in Kenya's Corruption Trials

Today, a Milimani Anti-Corruption Court magistrate finally closed the book on a 12-year legal saga involving former Lands Minister Amos Kimunya. While the acquittal brings closure to the defendant, it leaves the Kenyan taxpayer counting the heavy cost of a failed prosecution.

For ordinary citizens, the acquittal of Amos Kimunya on May 6, 2026, is not just a legal headline. It is a harsh reminder of how systemic failures in investigative work and prosecution strategy turn our judicial system into a bottomless pit for public money. Since 2014, the state has poured immense resources into a case that Magistrate Harrison Barasa ultimately dismissed due to lack of sufficient evidence. This raises a difficult question: how many public services could we have funded with the millions of shillings spent on a dozen years of litigation that led nowhere?

### How much did the 12-year trial drain from public coffers?

When a court case stretches from 2014 to 2026, the financial impact extends far beyond the lawyers' fees. We are talking about the salary costs of state prosecutors, investigators from the Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission, and the judicial time spent on endless hearings, appeals, and a retrial ordered back in 2022. 

The original dispute involved a 25-acre piece of land in Nyandarua valued at Ksh 60 million. When the state takes a case to court and loses due to weak evidence, the financial loss is twofold. First, there is the direct expenditure on the trial itself. Second, there is the opportunity cost. If that Ksh 60 million land parcel had been managed effectively for public use, or if the funds spent on the 12-year litigation had been channeled into basic infrastructure or health, ordinary Kenyans would have seen a tangible return on their taxes. Instead, those funds were essentially burned to sustain a legal process that failed to meet the threshold of criminal proof.

### Why do corruption cases frequently collapse due to evidence gaps?

The court’s ruling today pointed to a familiar pattern: the gap between an allegation and a provable crime. In the Kenyan justice system, successful corruption prosecutions require meticulous gathering of documents, chain-of-custody logs, and clear evidence of personal enrichment. 

When investigative agencies bring cases to court that are later described as weak, it highlights a failure in the initial stages of investigation. If the evidence is not watertight from day one, the defense will inevitably find cracks. While some might focus on the defendant, the real concern for the public is the internal quality control of the Office of the Director of Public Prosecutions and the investigative agencies. When these institutions fail to do their homework, they are not just losing a case; they are failing to hold anyone accountable for genuine public loss. This mirrors the frustration seen in other sectors, such as the frustrations surrounding [infrastructure and safety standards](https://zandani.co.ke/article/narok-governor-two-men-swept-away-mara-river-search-bridge-safety), where poor planning and execution cost lives and livelihoods.

### Why this matters now

This acquittal is a fiscal wake-up call. We are watching a cycle where high-profile cases are initiated with public fanfare, only to collapse years later because the state failed to provide sufficient evidence. For a country with limited resources, this represents a significant drain on our national budget. Every shilling spent on a failed decade-long legal battle is a shilling taken away from schools, hospitals, or road maintenance.

### What to watch next

The focus must now shift to the accountability of the investigative agencies. It is time to ask for a public audit of the costs associated with stalled or failed corruption cases. If the ODPP and investigative wings cannot improve the success rate of their prosecutions, taxpayers should demand a structural overhaul. Without a change in how evidence is gathered and managed, the cycle of expensive, fruitless trials will continue to deplete our resources, while justice for genuine corruption remains elusive.

A weak case is more than a legal failure; it is a direct theft of the public's time and money.
