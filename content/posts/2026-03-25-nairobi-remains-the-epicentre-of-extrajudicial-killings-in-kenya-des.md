Nairobi City County has ranked as Kenya's epicentre for extrajudicial executions for a fourth consecutive year, according to the recently released Missing Voices 2025 Annual Report, "Unbowed for Justice." Despite a gradual decline in total cases since 2022, the capital continued to record the highest numbers nationwide in 2025.

Nairobi Remains Epicentre of Extrajudicial Killings

Decline in Cases, Persistent Disparity The Missing Voices 2025 Annual Report reveals that while extrajudicial killings in Nairobi saw a reduction from 60 cases in 2022 to 32 in 2025, the capital still accounts for a disproportionate share of incidents across the country. This trend marks a fourth consecutive year of decline in overall cases, yet Nairobi's figures remain higher than any other county.

Police Stations Under Scrutiny Several police stations were identified in the report for their high numbers of extrajudicial executions. Pangani Police Station ranked highest in executions during 2025, while Central Police Station was specifically cited for cases involving deaths in custody. Other stations associated with such incidents include the Nairobi County Police Command, Kasarani Police Station, and Villa Police Station.

Shift to Protest-Related Fatalities A notable shift in police brutality drivers occurred in 2025. Protest-related and crowd-control operations became the primary cause of killings, moving away from anti-crime operations, which historically led such incidents. This change was particularly evident during the Gen-Z and Saba Saba protests held in June and July 2025, which contributed to over half of all killings nationwide, with Nairobi experiencing a significant portion of these fatalities. The report highlighted young men aged 19 to 35 as the most targeted demographic, often due to officers associating this age group with criminal activities. Among the victims from this period, activist Albert Ojwang lost his life in police custody, and 22-year-old mask vendor Boniface Kariuki was shot on June 25 while selling masks to protesters. These cases remain unresolved.

Systemic Barriers to Justice The Missing Voices report also underscores systemic challenges fostering impunity, including the underfunding and understaffing of oversight bodies like the Independent Policing Oversight Authority (IPOA), leading to protracted investigation and prosecution delays. Further complicating accountability are overlapping mandates between investigative bodies such as the Directorate of Criminal Investigations (DCI), the Office of the Director of Public Prosecution (ODPP), and IPOA. The ODPP recently attributed justice delays for police brutality victims to investigative agencies' failure to provide comprehensive, evidence-backed case files. Legal gaps also hinder accountability, with enforced disappearance not yet recognized as a distinct offense under Kenyan law, limiting families' avenues for justice when individuals vanish in police custody. Such domestic legal gaps underscore the broader challenges in achieving justice, even as the nation participates in international legal forums, exemplified by President Ruto's nomination of Supreme Court Judge Njoki Ndung’u for an International Criminal Court seat.
