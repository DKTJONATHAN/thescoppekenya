## Wajir: Beyond the Madaraka Day Spotlight

As the national flag flies over Wajir today for the 2026 Madaraka Day celebrations, the true significance lies beneath the surface of the grand speeches. This moment marks a deliberate shift from treating Northern Kenya as a peripheral humanitarian zone to acknowledging it as a core participant in our national economic engine.

### Building Resilience Through Water Security

The presence of the National Water Harvesting and Storage Authority in Wajir South, Habaswein, and Wajir North represents a departure from reactive drought relief. By focusing on critical water infrastructure, the state is finally addressing the foundational stability of the region. The construction of the 34-kilometre pipeline projects is the most vital development here; it moves the pastoralist economy away from the cycle of desperate migration and towards predictable, stationary agricultural growth. 

When reliable water reaches these communities, we see more than just a public works project. We see the mitigation of the high-stakes risks that have historically hampered investment in the North. Just as our energy sector is being cleaned up after the [irregularities involving former officials](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-04-dci-arrests-former-energy-officials-over-irregular-ksh-4-bi), the water sector must demonstrate similar transparency. Providing water security is the essential de-risking mechanism required to encourage private sector confidence in the arid lands of Northern Kenya.

### Aviation as a Gateway for Regional Trade

Alongside the water projects, the Kenya Airports Authority is meticulously upgrading Wajir’s aviation infrastructure to serve more than just local passenger travel. The expansion of runways and terminal facilities is a strategic maneuver to position the town as a logistics hub for cross-border commerce with Ethiopia and Somalia. Wajir is no longer merely a remote outpost but a critical point of entry for regional trade networks.

This is not a development occurring in isolation. While the government pursues [international trade talks in Azerbaijan and Kazakhstan](https://zandani.co.ke/article/ruto-travels-to-azerbaijan-kazakhstan-trade-talks), the domestic reality is that our own border regions must be equipped to handle the movement of goods and people. By tightening the link between Wajir and the international market, the state creates an environment where pastoralism can evolve into trade-led enterprise. The aviation upgrade is the physical manifestation of an integrated Kenyan market that refuses to leave its frontier counties behind.

### The Responsibility of Sustained Growth

The celebration of our independence in Wajir serves as a deadline for accountability. It is easy for a government to bring fanfare and temporary improvements to a county during a national holiday. The true challenge is ensuring that the technical projects initiated by the National Water Harvesting and Storage Authority receive the maintenance and funding required to last beyond the current administration. 

The people of Wajir deserve to know that the focus on their water and transport needs will continue after the dignitaries depart on Monday morning. We must move past the era of seeing these developments as charitable acts. They are constitutional requirements for every citizen, regardless of their proximity to the capital. The success of this hydraulic and logistical leap will be measured not by the crowds seen in the stadium today, but by the volume of water moving through the new pipes and the volume of trade moving across the new runways in the coming years.

I ask the government to treat these infrastructure projects as permanent features of the national budget, not as temporary political gestures. Commit to the long-term oversight of these water systems and maintain the air corridors as competitive trade routes, ensuring that Wajir remains a priority long after the last Independence Day song fades.
