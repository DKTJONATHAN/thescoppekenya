Kenyan authorities have detained multiple students this week following a surge in dormitory fires across the country. As schools assess the damage to their facilities, police are summoning parents to station houses to address the criminal involvement of their children in these arson incidents.

## The Shifting Narrative of School Arson

### The End of Parental Denial
For years, the standard response to school fires involved parents storming campus gates to demand justice for children they viewed exclusively as victims of academic pressure. The current wave of investigations has inverted this dynamic. Parents who once blamed school administrations or harsh teachers are now finding themselves seated in police stations facing formal charge sheets. The narrative that school arson is merely a cry for help from overburdened students is collapsing under the weight of evidence showing that the arsonists are often the students themselves. This shift forces a confrontation with the uncomfortable reality that the child holding the match is frequently the same one whose home life was shielded from the consequences of poor behavior.

### Beyond the School Gate
The rise in juvenile delinquency is creating a ripple effect that compromises both local security and the national focus on broader stability. Observers have noted the irony in the current situation where parents, who long maintained their children were incapable of such defiance, are forced to reconcile their expectations with evidence from security footage. This trend highlights a decline in domestic discipline, suggesting a systemic failure in homes where moral guidance was entirely outsourced to the teaching staff. The result is a series of destructive acts that sabotage the educational environment and expose deep fractures in parental supervision.

### Accountability Hits Close to Home
The era of reflexively blaming the school menu or the quality of instruction for a student's violent outburst is coming to a definitive end. By mandating parental presence at police stations, authorities are establishing that accountability for a child’s actions originates at home. Despite this legal pressure, many parents remain in a state of denial, often characterizing their children as misguided or easily led by peers. This refusal to accept fault mirrors a broader societal struggle to admit failure. However, the current legal crackdown serves as a stern warning: when a student burns down a school building, the parents will no longer be treated as sympathetic observers, but as stakeholders in the criminal conduct of their children.
