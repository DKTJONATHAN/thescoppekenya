## Closing the Internship Loophole

The formal transition of 20,000 interns into permanent roles marks the quiet end of a controversial fiscal experiment that defined the teaching profession for four years. This move signals a pivot from emergency workforce measures back toward long-term civil service stability.

### The Rise of the Managed Precarity Model

In September 2022, the government introduced the intern model as a fiscal shock absorber to mitigate a severe teacher shortage while grappling with a constrained national budget. By classifying new recruits as contract staff rather than permanent employees, the Teachers Service Commission could bypass pension contributions and permanent salary increments. This created a generation of educators living in institutional limbo. While the strategy successfully reduced the classroom shortfall, it sparked constant friction with labor unions like KUPPET, who argued that the system institutionalized exploitation under the guise of staffing reform. The policy effectively allowed the state to test the feasibility of a massive workforce expansion without committing to the full lifelong financial obligations that define Za Ndani Kenyan civil service employment contract.

### A Timeline of the Shift

- September 2022: The government launches the intern recruitment drive, setting the initial benchmark for a flexible, low-cost teacher supply chain.
- May 7, 2026: Negotiations intensify in Naivasha as the TSC and KUPPET reach a landmark agreement to resolve long-standing promotion backlogs and service term disputes.
- June 23, 2026: President Ruto formalizes the transition of 20,000 existing interns to permanent and pensionable terms while green-lighting a fresh recruitment drive for 24,000 additional staff.

### Fiscal Sustainability and the Education Ceiling

The current Ksh783 billion education budget serves as the bedrock for this structural expansion. Transitioning 20,000 teachers from contract to permanent status is not merely a bureaucratic change but a significant long-term commitment that impacts the national wage bill for decades. This decision acknowledges that the intern model, while useful for managing immediate deficits, was never sustainable as a permanent feature of the public sector. By folding these individuals into the permanent payroll, the state is effectively admitting that the cost of teacher retention-specifically regarding benefits and seniority-is now a necessary line item.

The recruitment of the additional 24,000 teachers suggests the government is finally aiming to reach the long-discussed target of 124,000 total new staff members since 2022. This comes at a time when the broader economy is seeking stability, much like recent efforts to diversify export markets by securing deals for flowers and macadamia in the U.S. By formalizing these positions, the Ministry of Education is shifting away from the stopgap measures that characterized the early years of the current administration.

This transformation does not necessarily signal the end of budgetary pressures. Instead, it represents a transition from managing a temporary crisis to maintaining a ballooning permanent payroll. History suggests that when government-funded employment cycles reach this level of saturation, the focus inevitably shifts from the speed of recruitment to the quality of service delivery and the ability of the exchequer to maintain these salaries during future fiscal downturns. Policies born of necessity often crystallize into fixed overheads that become the new baseline for every subsequent budget cycle.
