Political tensions are escalating across the Mt. Kenya region as residents express widespread dissatisfaction with President William Ruto. While some government factions dismiss this unrest as ethnic entitlement, observers argue these grievances are rooted in failed economic promises rather than tribal bias.

## Understanding the regional resistance

### Debunking the narrative of tribal entitlement
The argument that Mt. Kenya’s political opposition stems from ethnic chauvinism ignores the fundamental realities of the 2022 general election. Critics of the current administration are often quick to label any dissent as tribalism. However, this perspective overlooks the fact that the Mt. Kenya region was instrumental in securing President Ruto’s victory. Voters in the region actively opposed former President Uhuru Kenyatta to support the current leadership, demonstrating a commitment to political agendas and promises rather than a desire to keep power within their own community.

### Economic survival as the primary driver
For residents in counties like Nyeri and Meru, the primary concerns are the rising cost of living and the accessibility of essential farming inputs. The current frustration is fundamentally economic, not ethnic. As tax policies impact livelihoods, the primary focus for the average citizen is the dwindling profitability of tea and coffee production. When basic economic security is threatened, the tribe of the individual in the presidency becomes secondary to the immediate survival of the household and the regional economy.

### Political deflection and the power struggle
The rhetoric regarding tribal entitlement is increasingly viewed as a political smokescreen intended to deflect from the internal failures of the ruling coalition. By framing the discourse around ethnic divisions, political elites successfully divert attention from the structural struggles that have plagued their alliances. While political leaders focus on territorial influence and power dynamics, the public remains focused on the lack of genuine welfare policies. If the government continues to minimize legitimate economic hardships as mere tribal pride, it risks losing the trust of a constituency that once served as its primary support base.
