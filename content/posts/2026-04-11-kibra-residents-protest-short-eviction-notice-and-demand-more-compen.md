Residents in Nairobi's Kibra Soweto Zones C and D protested on Saturday, April 11, 2026, against a government directive to vacate their homes within 13 days for an urban upgrading project. They cited the short timeline and the inadequacy of the Ksh60,000 compensation as impractical and unfair, disrupting their livelihoods.

Kibra Residents Protest Eviction Directive

Short Eviction Notice Causes Disarray The government directive, issued by the Ministry of Lands, Public Works, Housing and Urban Development, requires residents of Kibra Soweto Zones C and D to vacate by April 23, 2026. Site clearing is scheduled to commence the following day. Residents argue the 13-day window is too brief for practical relocation, throwing their lives into disarray. Many noted the directive has disrupted daily routines and livelihoods, leaving them uncertain about their future. Businesses within the affected area have reportedly come to a halt following the notice, severely impacting income sources. Nathan Marenge, an elderly resident, expressed severe hardship. “I do not have any means and alternative to survive; whatever the government is doing is unjust and unfair to me. I ask the government to help me relocate or provide me with a job so that I can be able to sustain my needs; otherwise, this looks like a death sentence to me,” Marenge stated. The abrupt halt to his livelihood reflects a common plight for many facing uncertain futures, perhaps prompting broader questions about viable [careers in a rapidly changing urban landscape](https://zandani.co.ke/article/careers-unvarnished-reality-pan-african-entertainment).

Calls for Increased Compensation and Consultation Concerns over the adequacy of the Ksh60,000 compensation package are widespread, even as some residents began moving out. Angela Khabaye highlighted the financial strain, stating, “This demolition has negatively impacted us despite the Ksh60,000 that we were given. That amount cannot be enough to pay a house deposit, look for an alternative school for kids, and channel it to business since whatever little I was doing here has been flattened already.” Residents are urging the government to review and increase the resettlement support to reflect current economic realities. They also demand authorities engage them in meaningful consultations, aiming for a smoother transition that prevents further destabilisation of vulnerable households. Demolitions have already commenced in parts of the settlement, with several structures brought down in preparation for the project. The government maintains that these evictions are integral to a wider urban renewal program. This initiative seeks to replace informal settlements with modern housing units under the Affordable Housing initiative.
