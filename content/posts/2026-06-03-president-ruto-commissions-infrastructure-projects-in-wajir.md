## Concrete Does Not Erase History

President William Ruto recently unveiled the Ahmed Khalif Stadium and a slate of road projects in Wajir to coincide with the 63rd Madaraka Day. While regional leaders praise these as milestones of progress, they function more as political stagecraft than solutions to systemic exclusion.

The prevailing narrative suggests that pouring asphalt and erecting concrete stands in Wajir finally signals the end of Northern Kenya's 63-year isolation. This view is dangerous because it mistakes the aesthetics of government presence for the substance of regional integration. If historical neglect were merely a lack of stadiums, the problem would have vanished decades ago. Instead, we are witnessing a recurring cycle where ribbon-cutting ceremonies are deployed to pacify restive peripheries while the underlying structures of economic and political disenfranchisement remain untouched.

### The Myth of the Structural Quick-Fix

The government-led discourse treats these new infrastructure projects as high-value assets for the people of Wajir. Yet, this ignores the vast gulf between physical construction and human development. A stadium, however grand, does not provide water security, resolve chronic livestock disease outbreaks, or improve the quality of education in a region that consistently lags behind Central or Nyanza in national literacy and health indicators. When the state prioritizes projects that offer high visibility during an election cycle, it avoids the more difficult, less photogenic work of decentralizing economic opportunity. We have seen this performance before; shiny facades often hide the rot of institutional neglect.

### The Trap of Centralized Paternalism

There is a fundamental tension in how these projects are conceptualized. By focusing on central government-led initiatives like the Northern Water Works Development Agency or state-sponsored stadium construction, the administration reinforces a top-down model of governance. Real autonomy requires local control over resources and policy-making, not just the receipt of projects designed in Nairobi boardrooms. When the state arrives with a pre-packaged plan for a town, it inherently bypasses the local agency required to make these developments sustainable. This creates a dependency loop where the region remains beholden to the political whims of the center rather than building its own independent economic foundation.

### Symbolic Sweeteners Before 2027

We must be clear about the timing of these inaugurations. With the 2027 electoral cycle looming, these infrastructure commitments function as political sweeteners designed to secure regional loyalty. It is a cynical transaction. The state promises to fix the physical landscape to distract from the fact that Northern Kenya remains excluded from the broader national narrative of prosperity. This is not to say that the people of Wajir do not deserve roads or stadiums; they do. But we should be wary of any development agenda that relies on the "mirage" of construction to paper over the gaps in constitutional implementation and resource allocation.

Just as we have seen in recent public discourse-from the heated outbursts at industry events regarding [Tourette's syndrome](https://zandani.co.ke/article/bafta-attendee-shouted-n-word-sinners-stars-explains-outburst) to the debates surrounding personal habits like those [Goldie Hawn confirmed regarding her punctuality](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-05-07-goldie-hawn-confirms-habitual-lateness-during-death-becomes-her)-people are often more interested in the performative aspects of a situation than its root cause. We prioritize the loud, visible moment over the quiet, necessary work of reform. 

The Wajir projects are a reminder that the state is perfectly capable of building walls and roads, yet remains perpetually unable to build the institutional trust that is actually required to bind the nation together. Until policy focuses on the lived reality of the marginalized rather than the optics of state presence, these structures will remain nothing more than expensive tombstones for broken promises. We need to stop applauding the bricks and start demanding the inclusion that was promised at independence.
