## The Billionaire Shield: Why Finance Bill 2026 Protects Elite Assets While the Matatu Stalls

Za Ndani line from government corridors suggests that the Finance Bill 2026 is a painful but necessary medicine to lift our abysmal 14.1 per cent tax-to-GDP ratio toward healthier, OECD-adjacent levels. This is a fairy tale. Far from a balanced fiscal strategy, the bill is a meticulously crafted shield for the ultra-wealthy that prioritizes the preservation of elite capital over the survival of the Kenyan commuter.

### A Fiscal Structure That Starves the Many
When the matatu industry ground to a halt on May 15, the protest was not merely about the pump price. It was a visceral reaction to a 46 per cent surge in diesel costs. While the global price of crude rose by 10.7 per cent, Kenyan operators are paying Sh242.92 per litre for diesel, a staggering hike from the previous Sh196.63. This is not an act of God or a mere byproduct of global markets. It is the consequence of a tax regime that views the transport sector as a bottomless piggy bank while keeping the vaults of the top 0.01 per cent tightly sealed.

The math of the Finance Bill 2026 is insulting. It offers a 15 per cent exemption on capital gains tax for select asset classes, effectively subsidizing the portfolios of Kenya's 6,800 dollar millionaires. When we demand a wealth tax, the Treasury feigns concern about investor flight. Yet, they see no issue with forcing a matatu driver to pay a disproportionate share of his daily earnings to move workers across Nairobi. We are witnessing a transfer of wealth disguised as fiscal policy, where the consumption of the poor is taxed to death to ensure the returns of the wealthy remain untouched.

### The Myth of the Struggling Elite
Defenders of this status quo argue that taxing the 16 centi-millionaires who hold over Sh13 billion each would stifle innovation and investment. This is a tired deflection. These individuals do not need a 15 per cent capital gains tax shield to remain profitable; they need a stable, functional economy where the workforce can afford to reach their places of employment. By shielding the elite, the state is actively strangling the very market these centi-millionaires rely on. If the matatus stop, commerce stops. If the commute becomes unaffordable, the economy stagnates. 

There is a hollow irony in watching banks like DTB post record earnings while the informal sector, which acts as the backbone of our economy, faces a total squeeze. Just as we have seen with the systemic failures in local football clubs like AFC Leopards and Shabana FC, we are watching a leadership class choose quick, extraction-based fixes rather than addressing the structural rot. The Finance Bill 2026 does not fix the tax-to-GDP gap; it widens the chasm between those who contribute to the nation and those who siphon from it.

### Why it matters now
The social contract is frayed. When the cost of moving people to work exceeds the value of the work itself, the state loses its primary justification for taxation. We are effectively taxing the movement of the poor to protect the stillness of billionaire assets.

### What to watch next
Watch the parliamentary debate on the capital gains clauses. If the 15 per cent exemption survives, it will be the clearest indicator yet that the state has abandoned the concept of progressive taxation. Expect further industrial action from the transport sector if the diesel levy remains untethered from meaningful wealth redistribution.

The Finance Bill 2026 is not a road to fiscal health. It is a toll booth operated by the few, demanding payment from the many, while the owners of the road keep their pockets firmly shut.
