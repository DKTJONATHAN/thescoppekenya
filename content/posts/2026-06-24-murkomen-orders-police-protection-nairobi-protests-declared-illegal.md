## Security Orders Clash Over Planned June 25 Protests Murkomen orders police protection as CBD protests declared illega is the central subject of the update.

Interior Cabinet Secretary Kipchumba Murkomen has ordered the National Police Service to provide security for peaceful demonstrators marking the anniversary of the 2024 Gen Z movement, setting up a sharp administrative conflict with regional commanders who have declared tomorrow's Nairobi protests illegal.

This directive places Kenya’s security apparatus in a state of operational dissonance. While Murkomen advocates for the protection of constitutional rights, Nairobi police officials maintain that the lack of formal permits renders any gathering within the Central Business District a violation of public order. This policy gap leaves law enforcement officers on the ground without clear operational guidelines for the planned June 25 marches.

The contradiction highlights a deepening divide between executive rhetoric and the bureaucratic requirements enforced by local police. Under existing regulations, protest organizers are often expected to provide notification and secure permits for assemblies. However, the High Court has frequently clarified that the right to assemble is constitutional and not contingent upon police authorization. By declaring the protests illegal, local authorities are asserting a procedural mandate that directly undermines the Cabinet Secretary's public commitment to peaceful assembly.

The confusion is exacerbated by wider security operations reported across the country. In Kajiado County, authorities confirmed the arrest of activist Bob Njagi, while counter-protests were recorded in Kisumu. These scattered incidents illustrate a volatile climate where local law enforcement remains focused on pre-emptive containment, often disregarding the broader policy direction emanating from the Interior Ministry.

### Conflicting Directives on Public Order

Legal analysts point out that this dissonance creates a dangerous environment for both the police and participants. If officers are instructed to protect demonstrators by the national leadership but ordered to disperse them by local commanders, the chain of command effectively collapses. Such ambiguity places individual officers at risk of accountability failures if force is applied against protesters who believe they are operating under the ministry's protection.

The situation is further complicated by President William Ruto’s recent warnings against acts of violence or the destruction of public property. While the government maintains that property security remains a top priority, the lack of a unified approach to managing the demonstrations suggests a breakdown in administrative cohesion.
