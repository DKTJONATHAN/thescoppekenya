General Roland Walker, the British Army's top commander in Kenya, has recently addressed long-standing allegations of misconduct against soldiers from the British Army Training Unit in Kenya (BATUK). In an interview, Walker acknowledged the deep strain these issues, including murder, sexual violence, and environmental damage, have placed on the defence relationship between the two nations, committing to closer collaboration with the Ministry of Defence.

Misconduct Allegations Strain UK-Kenya Ties General Walker, the head of BATUK, broke his silence amidst escalating pressure concerning numerous unresolved cases. He affirmed the British Army's commitment to the highest standards, stating, "Where we have failed, then that's on us to remedy our own behaviours." He maintained that British soldiers operate within the law, despite public anger over thousands of complaints filed without subsequent prosecutions. Walker firmly rejected the notion that his troops consider themselves above the law, asserting a clear commitment to local legal processes and full investigations from start to courtroom.

Unresolved Justice for Agnes Wanjiru Among the most damaging cases impacting BATUK's standing is the 2012 murder of Agnes Wanjiru, a 21-year-old woman last seen with British soldiers in Nanyuki. Her body was discovered two months later in a septic tank. An inquest concluded she was murdered by British soldiers, and while reports of a soldier's confession emerged, no one has faced charges to date. This case has become a stark symbol of accountability failures within the unit.

Persistent Sexual Violence and Abandoned Children Sexual violence represents another enduring grievance. A parliamentary inquiry conducted in 2025 highlighted a troubling pattern of rape and assault. Earlier investigations between 2003 and 2004 reportedly shelved many complaints, with their findings never publicly released. Additionally, hundreds of children fathered by British soldiers have been left without financial support or recognition, enduring significant social stigma within their communities.

Environmental Damage and Unexploded Ordnance Communities residing near BATUK's training grounds at Archer's Post have suffered fatalities and permanent injuries from unexploded ordnance. In 2018, one victim who lost both arms received approximately Ksh13 million in compensation, though the UK Ministry of Defence did not admit liability for the incident.

Parliament Signals New Era of Accountability In a decisive move in April 2024, the Parliament voted to amend the defence agreement, enabling British soldiers to face local prosecution for crimes committed while off duty. This legislative change signals a new era for accountability concerning BATUK. General Walker acknowledged the need for change and emphasized achieving this by working closely within existing legal frameworks. He reiterated, "What we need, as best as possible, is to work even more closely with the Ministry of Defence to resolve some of these deep-seated grievances."
