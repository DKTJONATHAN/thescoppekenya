## The Likoni Ferry Paradox: Why KPA is subsidizing the cart to save the commuter

The Kenya Ports Authority has quietly restructured Likoni ferry tariffs to balance the books as commuter habits shift. By hiking costs for private motorists while slashing fees for informal transit operators, the state is effectively forcing a new socio-economic order at the coast.

For years, the Likoni crossing has been a chaotic, essential artery for Mombasa, carrying up to 500,000 pedestrians and 6,000 vehicles daily. However, the completion of the Dongo Kundu bypass in August 2024 fundamentally altered this dynamic. Where motorists once sat in hour-long queues, they now have a 45-minute alternative route that bypasses the bottleneck entirely. As high-paying vehicle traffic migrates to the bypass to save time, the Kenya Ports Authority (KPA) has found its operational revenue under significant strain. The recent tariff revision is not merely an attempt to close a budget gap; it is a calculated decision to turn the ferry into a specialized utility for the working class rather than a general-purpose transit hub.

### A shift in priorities

Under the new regime, the financial burden has been redistributed. Private car owners now pay Ksh180, up from the previous Ksh120, to use the channel. Conversely, the KPA has provided direct economic relief to the most vulnerable users of the Likoni and Mtongwe channels. Tuk-tuk operators, who were previously paying Ksh100, now pay Ksh75. Even more significant is the reduction for handcart operators, whose fees have been dropped by 50 per cent. By keeping pedestrian transit free and subsidizing these small-scale logistics, the state is acknowledging that while the middle class has graduated to the Dongo Kundu bypass, the informal economy remains anchored to the ferry.

This redistribution serves a cold fiscal reality. Operating a fleet of six vessels is expensive, with annual maintenance costs often reaching Ksh3 billion. With vehicle revenue declining as drivers opt for the bypass, the KPA had to choose between letting the service decay or squeezing the remaining motorists to cross-subsidize the informal sector. It is a quiet admission that the ferry is no longer the primary transit mode for those who can afford alternatives.

### The cost of convenience

The economic contrast between the ferry and the bypass is stark. A driver using the Dongo Kundu road might spend more on fuel due to the distance but saves on the wear and tear of urban congestion. Meanwhile, a handcart operator or a tuk-tuk driver cannot utilize the highway. For them, the ferry is the only viable link between the mainland and the island. The KPA is essentially pivoting its business model to act as a social welfare provider for those who cannot participate in the luxury of bypass-based travel.

This follows broader trends in how transport authorities handle data-heavy logistics, similar to the recent mandates requiring transport operators to appoint data protection officers to manage the flow of digital passenger information. As transit systems become smarter, they also become more stratified, separating those who pay for time from those who pay for survival.

### Why it matters now

The KPA is currently managing a fleet that is aging, with three of the six vessels requiring urgent replacement. The government has signaled an intent to procure two new ferries at a cost of approximately Ksh5 billion. Without this tariff adjustment, the authority would struggle to justify the capital expenditure required to keep the channel viable for its remaining 300,000 daily commuters. 

### What to watch next

The long-term sustainability of this model remains fragile. As traffic volume on the Dongo Kundu bypass increases, the KPA will likely see a further drop in high-value vehicle tolls at the ferry. If the gap between ferry maintenance costs and revenue continues to widen, the pressure will mount to either further increase vehicle tariffs or seek a more permanent government bailout. Those who rely on the ferry are effectively being tethered to a system that is rapidly becoming a relic of the pre-bypass era.

Infrastructure evolves, but the bill for the old ways always lands on those with the fewest options.
