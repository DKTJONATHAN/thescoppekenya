### The bystander effect in public transit
The death of Mangera has exposed a disturbing culture of apathy within the public transport sector. Witnesses reported that as the altercation between the student and the crew turned physical, the remaining passengers remained silent. This collective inaction has raised serious concerns regarding the bystander effect, where individuals choose to remain passive observers rather than intervene during acts of violence. The incident highlights a troubling trend in which personal safety is prioritized to the point of total indifference toward the life and well-being of fellow commuters.

### Normalizing violence and moral decay
The aggressive behavior displayed by matatu crews has become an increasingly normalized feature of urban travel. By failing to intervene when a student is targeted for questioning a fare increase, bystanders inadvertently foster an environment where such cruelty can persist. This incident serves as a grim reflection of a society that has seemingly abandoned the principle of community care. When an entire busload of passengers witnesses a lethal assault without attempting to prevent it, the event stands as a testament to the erosion of moral responsibility in public spaces.

### Accountability and the call for change
Authorities are actively investigating the incident and working to apprehend the crew responsible for the act. Beyond the legal pursuit of those involved, the tragedy has prompted a wider discussion about the lack of collective courage in Kenya. The public is now demanding systemic changes to ensure that matatu operations are held to stricter safety and behavioral standards. This case remains a painful reminder that the silence of witnesses is often as damaging as the aggression of perpetrators, emphasizing the urgent need for a shift in how society responds to injustice on the road.
