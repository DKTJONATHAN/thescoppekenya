## Ruto Launches Ksh45 Billion Solar Project: A New Dawn for Kenyan Education President William Ruto inaugurated a Ksh45 billion solar project on Sunday, March 1, 2026, aimed at providing clean energy solutions for cooking, lighting, and other needs to over 3,000 Kenyan schools, marking a significant step towards reducing carbon footprint and promoting sustainable development nationwide. The ambitious programme underscores the government's commitment to transitioning the education sector towards renewable energy sources. Speaking at the Alliance High School's centenary celebrations, President Ruto detailed the extensive scope of the project, which is set to benefit thousands of public learning institutions across the country. This initiative is a concerted effort between the Ministry of Energy and the Ministry of Education, bolstered by partnerships within the banking sector, with Kenya Commercial Bank (KCB) reportedly extending a credit facility with a 30 percent discount to facilitate its implementation. The President emphasised that this is a deliberate move to reduce Kenya's carbon footprint and decrease reliance on biomass, ensuring that learning environments are powered by green energy.

Impact of the Ksh45 Billion Solar Project on Energy Access The newly launched initiative is projected to generate an impressive 780 megawatts of energy, a substantial boost to the nation's renewable energy capacity. A crucial aspect of the programme addresses the utilisation of excess power generated when schools are not in session; President Ruto has instructed the Ministry of Energy to purchase this surplus energy, even if at a discount, to provide schools with additional resources for infrastructure development. This innovative approach ensures maximum efficiency and financial benefit for the participating institutions. The project's feasibility has been demonstrated through a successful pilot phase involving over 200 schools, with plans for a rapid expansion to other institutions within the next year.

Broader National Efforts to Power Kenyan Schools and Institutions This national solarisation programme aligns with a broader governmental push towards integrating clean energy across various sectors. Kenya's commitment to renewable energy is evident in its Vision 2030 goals, which aim for universal access to sustainable energy and a significant increase in the renewable energy mix by 2030. Beyond the school initiative, other institutions are also making substantial strides. Earlier this year, the Cooperative University of Kenya received approval from the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA) for its Ksh6.45 billion (approximately US$50 million) solar plant project. This project, revealed by University Chancellor Benard William Chitunga, is designed to generate 40 megawatts of electricity for the national grid, positioning the university as the first in the country to supply power on such a large scale. The Cooperative University's project, implemented in partnership with China's Dahai Shandong company, exemplifies an operational innovative financing model where the partner finances, constructs, and operates the plant before transferring ownership to the university. The Future of Clean Energy: Powering 3,000 Kenyan Schools The President's initiative is poised to fundamentally transform the energy landscape for educational institutions, drastically reducing their reliance on traditional, carbon-intensive fuels like firewood and charcoal. By moving towards solar energy for cooking, lighting, and other needs, the project is expected to lower operational costs for schools while contributing significantly to Kenya's climate action objectives. This extensive programme not only promises environmental benefits but also aims to create green jobs, strengthening Kenya's climate resilience and accelerating its journey towards green growth. The government's ambitious plan to solarise all schools before the next general election signifies a clear and strong commitment to a sustainable future for the nation's youth.
