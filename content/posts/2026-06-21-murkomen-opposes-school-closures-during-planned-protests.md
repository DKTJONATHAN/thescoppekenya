## Schools Stand at the Center of a Political Tug-of-War

Kenyans are grappling with a deepening divide as the push for June 25 protests collides with the academic calendar. While the streets prepare for activism, a fierce debate rages over whether the classroom should remain an untouchable sanctuary or a space for civic growth.

The tension spiked this Sunday after Cabinet Secretary Kipchumba Murkomen took to the pulpit at AIC Langata. He argued that the constitutional right to assemble is not a blank check to disrupt the education of millions. His comments followed suggestions from opposition leaders, including Siaya Governor James Orengo, that the planned protests should extend to a national shutdown. For the government, the academic calendar is a sacred timeline that must remain undisturbed by political volatility. For the opposition, these demonstrations are a necessary act of national reflection and resistance, often compared to historical moments where civic action took precedence over daily routines.

### Voices from the Estates and the Law

In the bustling residential areas of Nairobi, parents are trapped in a quiet state of anxiety. Mary Wanjiku, a mother of two in Donholm, expresses the exhaustion of many families who feel caught in the middle. She notes that while she supports the right to voice grievances, the prospect of her children missing days of study brings more stress than the protest itself. For her, the school gate is one of the few places where children are shielded from the intensity of adult political arguments. Her sentiment reflects a broader concern in Nairobi estates where the cost of a missed school day is measured not just in academic progress, but in the lost safety of a supervised environment during times of unrest.

From a legal standpoint, the clash presents a difficult puzzle. Constitutional experts point out that the right to assembly is balanced by the state's duty to provide an environment for children to exercise their own right to education. There is no simple path where both exist in total isolation. While activists argue that witnessing history is a form of civic education, security experts warn that schools can become high-risk zones if they remain open during unpredictable street actions. This tension is not entirely new; Kenya has historically balanced academic timelines against moments of national disruption, though the current polarized environment makes every decision feel heavier than in previous years.

Meanwhile, the perspective from the youth remains arguably the most complex. A final-year university student from Nairobi shares that for many young people, the classroom and the street are no longer separate worlds. He suggests that today’s generation views participation in national discourse as an essential component of their development. He argues that being shielded from these events does not make students safer, but rather leaves them unprepared for the reality of the country they will eventually inherit. This viewpoint highlights a generational gap where the traditional view of schools as static learning bubbles is increasingly challenged by a reality where information and political awakening travel instantly through digital networks.

### The Uncertainty Ahead

As June 25 approaches, the government appears determined to keep the wheels of the country turning, with officials signaling that businesses and schools should proceed as planned. Meanwhile, opposition figures maintain that the weight of the moment justifies a temporary pause in national activities.

This standoff leaves headteachers and school administrators in a precarious position. They are tasked with ensuring the safety of thousands of learners while navigating conflicting directives from political leaders. Many are now quietly preparing contingency plans, weighing the risks of closure against the risks of keeping their doors open during a day that promises to be anything but ordinary. While the headlines focus on the shouting match between Nairobi’s political elite, the real pressure is felt in the staff rooms and living rooms of the families who are still weighing the risks of a child's commute against the demands of an unpredictable week. The city is holding its breath, waiting to see if the rhythm of the school day will hold or if the streets will claim the calendar.
