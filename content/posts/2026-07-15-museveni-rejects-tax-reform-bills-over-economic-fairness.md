## The Plastic Protectionism Play

President Yoweri Museveni has returned the Income Tax and Excise Duty Amendment Bills to Parliament. The official justification cites a need to protect the Ugandan economy from immediate fiscal volatility. Although the State House frames this veto as a safeguard for small-scale enterprises, the decision to reject a 25 percent excise duty on plastics raises questions about which interests are actually being served.

### The Math of Monopoly

Lawmakers initially proposed raising the excise duty on single-use plastics from 2.5 percent to 25 percent. The move was publicly framed as an environmental initiative designed to reduce the non-biodegradable waste clogging Kampala’s drainage networks. Behind this narrative, however, lies a complex economic dynamic. Raw materials for plastic production cost roughly 70 USD per tonne, while the final goods sell for nearly 1,500 USD per tonne. A ten-fold tax increase would have created significant pressure on the margins of cottage manufacturers and small-scale recyclers who lack the capital to absorb such costs.

Large-scale manufacturers maintain a different operational reality. Through sophisticated supply chains and existing government tax incentive packages, these industrial titans are largely shielded from standard levy fluctuations. President Museveni asserts that the veto is necessary to prevent consumer price hikes and ensure market equity. Yet, this action reinforces the dominance of existing industry leaders. When tax burdens remain static, smaller firms struggle to secure the capital required for innovation or market expansion. This keeps the industrial landscape stagnant, favoring companies that already possess established assembly lines.

A truly equitable approach might have involved tiered taxation based on production volume. Instead, the current veto preserves a status quo where high-volume, low-cost plastic production remains highly profitable. As a result, the environmental burden of waste management persists, subsidized by the public rather than the producers.

### Industrial Stagnation in 2026

The Ugandan economic climate in 2026 remains focused on the push for industrial self-reliance. However, this legislative block suggests a reluctance to challenge established revenue streams. The government has aggressively championed import substitution in other sectors, such as textiles and construction. The plastics industry, conversely, continues to receive a notably lighter regulatory touch.

This inconsistency likely stems from the deep connections between industrial output and political influence. Plastics serve as a foundational element of the fast-moving consumer goods supply chain. Any disruption to this sector would inevitably cause price spikes across the food and beverage industries. Given the current fiscal cycle, the administration appears eager to avoid any policies that might trigger widespread public discontent.

Uganda is following a pattern common to several East African markets, where governments prioritize the protection of domestic industrial bases even when those sectors rely on environmentally damaging practices. While neighboring nations like Ethiopia are working to formalize market structures through initiatives like a national stock exchange, Uganda seems fixed in a cycle of protectionism. This preference for short-term political stability over systemic economic or environmental reform hinders the modernization of the manufacturing sector.

The veto ensures the survival of business models that are increasingly outdated. If the executive branch were genuinely focused on the welfare of small and medium-sized enterprises, there would be a clear path to use tax revenues to subsidize sustainable alternatives. Instead, no such proposal has emerged. Whether viewed as a move to stifle inflation or a capitulation to industrial lobbyists, the result is identical. Ugandan consumers remain caught between a legislature seeking higher tax yields and a presidency committed to preserving an unchanged, inefficient market structure. The environment continues to absorb the costs of this inertia, while the fundamental issues of waste management and industrial evolution remain unaddressed.
