## Kenya Electricity Prices Set to Drop as Dams Overflow

The Kenya Electricity Generating Company (KenGen) announced on Thursday, April 30, 2026, a potential reduction in nationwide electricity costs for households and businesses. This follows a significant increase in water levels across key hydroelectric dams, which has substantially boosted power generation output and eased pressure on the national grid, signaling a positive shift for Kenya electricity prices.

### Hydro Generation Exceeds Targets

KenGen's latest dispatch report confirmed that hydro generation has surpassed expectations, with production levels exceeding projected targets in recent days due to optimal dam inflows. On April 28, the main hydro plants generated 11.7 million kilowatt-hours, exceeding a forecast of 10.95 million kilowatt-hours. The Eastern Hydros contributed the majority of this output, at approximately 9.13 million kilowatt-hours. Reservoir levels at key dams remain strong and stable, with Masinga Dam recorded at 1,056.54 metres, Kamburu at 1,006.07 metres, Gitaru at 923.69 metres, and Kindaruma at 780.28 metres, all comfortably above minimum operating thresholds. This improved performance is attributed to sustained rainfall patterns and efficient water management practices.

### Impact on Electricity Pricing Trends

The current hydrological conditions place KenGen's hydro system at nearly optimal capacity, which has stabilized the grid supply and reduced reliance on more expensive thermal generation methods. KenGen Acting MD and CEO Ahmed Issack stated that strong reservoir levels, averaging about 99 percent of operating capacity, position the company to continue maximizing hydropower generation, which is the cheapest source of electricity on Kenya's grid. Issack noted this provides greater flexibility to expand renewable supply while reducing dependence on costly thermal generation, potentially leading to lower Kenya electricity prices in the coming months if conditions persist.

### Assurances on Dam Safety

Amid concerns regarding rising water levels potentially posing a threat to communities downstream of the Seven Forks cascade, Issack assured that KenGen is deploying all necessary resources to avoid spillage risks. The company affirmed that its engineers operate a robust water management program designed to maintain reservoirs within safe operating ranges, and uncontrolled spilling is not expected even as Masinga Dam nears maximum levels, provided inflows remain manageable.

KenGen's installed generation capacity totals 1,786 megawatts (MW), with over 90 percent derived from renewable sources: hydro (826 MW), geothermal (754 MW), and wind (25.5 MW). This focus on green energy aligns with broader efforts to enhance energy security and affordability in Kenya.
