President William Ruto's recent condemnation of strikes targeting Gulf nations is not the decisive leadership Kenya needs; it is a belated, self-serving exercise in diplomatic platitudes that rings hollow for the hundreds of thousands of Kenyans caught in the crossfire. This is a crisis long brewing, and his pronouncements on Monday, March 2, 2026, are merely a reactive whimper after Iran unleashed its retaliatory barrages since Saturday, February 28, a direct response to U.S.-Israeli strikes that reportedly killed Ayatollah Ali Khamenei. Where was this urgency when the geopolitical storm clouds gathered?

## Kenya's Hidden Stakes Gulf War: A Delusion, Not a Secret

The notion that Kenya has "hidden stakes" in the Gulf conflict is a dangerous delusion propagated by those in power. There is nothing hidden about the monumental economic and human stakes involved. With over 400,000 Kenyans toiling in these volatile lands, as confirmed by data from Diaspora Principal Secretary Roseline Njogu, their safety is not a side-note; it is the central, agonizing truth. Their remittances, the very lifeline of countless Kenyan families, are now directly threatened by every missile and drone screaming across those skies. To pretend this is somehow a revelation is to insult the intelligence of every Kenyan who sends their meager earnings home, praying for their family's survival. This isn't about lofty international peace; it's about cold, hard cash and human lives, a reality that the government conveniently ignores until catastrophe looms.

### Ruto's Moral Grandstanding Over Kenyans in UAE Safety

President Ruto's statement, warning that "the regionalisation of this conflict poses a grave threat to international peace and security," is precisely the kind of empty rhetoric that offers zero comfort to a Kenyan cleaning toilets in Dubai or building skyscrapers in Riyadh. While he pontificates about multilateral institutions being "indispensable frameworks for the resolution of the current crisis," the real crisis is the palpable fear gripping Kenyan households dependent on those Gulf wages. His call for urgent de-escalation middle east is too little, too late. Where was this eloquent call for multilateralism when our foreign policy decisions were being made? Was he too busy chasing bilateral deals that now look woefully short-sighted in the face of regional meltdown? This is not leadership; it is a desperate scramble to appear relevant when the house is already on fire.

### Diaspora Principal Secretary: A Title Without Teeth

And what of Diaspora Principal Secretary Roseline Njogu? Her role, ostensibly to protect and promote the interests of Kenyans abroad, seems reduced to merely reciting statistics: "over 400,000 Kenyans living and working in these countries." What tangible action has been taken to safeguard these citizens *before* the missiles started flying? This office, like many others, appears content to exist as a bureaucratic formality, offering no real shield to our people. The government's advice for Kenyans in the Gulf to "exercise extreme caution" and "avoid non-essential movement" is a grotesque understatement of the dangers they face. Are they supposed to build bomb shelters with their bare hands? Their vulnerability is a direct indictment of a government that prioritizes grandstanding over genuine proactive protection.

### The Hypocrisy of Kenya Economy Impact Gulf Conflict

Kenya's economic relationship with the Gulf is a delicate dance of hypocrisy, now brutally exposed by this conflict. The UAE stands as a major source of imports and a top destination for Kenyan exports, particularly tea. This transactional dependency means that every blast in the Gulf is a direct hit to Kenya's pockets. The very notion of `kenya economy impact gulf conflict` is not a theoretical exercise; it is a daily reality. This government, quick to condemn strikes *after* they happen, must answer for its failure to diversify our economic partners or, at the very least, establish robust contingency plans for the `gulf remittances kenya affected` by such predictable volatility. It is a stunning display of short-sighted greed, sacrificing the long-term stability of our nation for immediate, precarious gains. The `ruto condemnation gulf strikes` feels like an afterthought, a feigned concern for peace when the real worry is the bottom line.

For too long, Kenya has played a dangerous game, cozying up to nations embroiled in perpetual conflict, driven by the lure of easy remittances and trade deals. The moral compass of our foreign policy has been wildly off course, swayed by transient economic benefits rather than the enduring safety of our citizens. It is an appalling betrayal of the hardworking Kenyans whose remittances prop up our economy, yet whose lives are treated as expendable pawns on the global chessboard. We linked up with dubious partners, and now the bill is due. The price? The blood and treasure of our own people. President Ruto, your administration’s belated, insipid statements do nothing but highlight the fundamental failure to protect its most vulnerable citizens. This is not about diplomatic niceties; it is about accountability for the lives and livelihoods of over 400,000 Kenyans now staring down the barrel of a regional war, a crisis you were clearly unprepared for, despite all the obvious signs. ### A Reckoning for Ruto's Diplomacy

Ruto's administration consistently touts its diplomatic prowess, yet when faced with a genuine regional conflagration, we get a toothless statement lamenting "international peace." This is not the bold, decisive leadership promised. This is a government caught flat-footed, reduced to echoing the generic calls for de-escalation that any secondary school student could issue. The truth is, his administration has failed to provide a robust framework for our diaspora's protection, beyond offering vague warnings to "monitor official communications." Such dereliction of duty is unforgivable.

Kenya needs more than hollow condemnations and belated calls for dialogue. President Ruto must move beyond performative diplomacy and outline a clear, actionable plan to safeguard Kenyans and secure our economic interests. This includes immediate, robust evacuation contingency plans, aggressive advocacy for our citizens' safety, and a complete overhaul of our foreign policy to ensure it prioritizes human lives over precarious petrodollars. Anything less is a continuation of this cowardly, incompetent approach.
