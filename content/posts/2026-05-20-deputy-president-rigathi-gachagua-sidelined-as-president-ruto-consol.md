Deputy President Rigathi Gachagua faces increasing isolation at Harambee House as President William Ruto reshuffles his administration and cements a new unity with opposition factions. This executive shift has left the Deputy President excluded from key government decision-making processes and critical policy meetings.

## The erosion of executive influence

### The silent seat at the table
The recent fuel crisis summit at State House highlighted the growing distance between the President and his deputy. While President Ruto convened with new political allies to deliberate on energy policy, Gachagua was conspicuously absent. This absence follows a consistent pattern of exclusion from high-stakes government operations, including recent oil importation agreements. The Deputy President, once positioned as the second-most powerful figure in the republic, is now effectively watching from the periphery as administrative priorities are set by a new coalition of leaders.

### From perceived dominance to vocal spectator
Political analysts suggest that the influence of the Deputy President has shifted from central governance to mere observation. Critics argue that his public warnings, once feared for their impact on government direction, have lost their potency. While his political base continues to deploy hired supporters to counter the influence of newly appointed ministers aligned with opposition factions, the core of executive power has clearly migrated away from his office. This struggle for relevance suggests a weakening grip on his official mandate, as he finds himself sidelined in his own administration.

### The critique of fiscal and travel priorities
Reports of frequent and costly travel have drawn sharp criticism from opponents who claim the Deputy President is prioritizing personal transit over the substantive work of policy management. Critics accuse him of inconsistent governance, often moving between international destinations while his domestic political influence wanes. This perceived lack of focus on critical national issues stands in contrast to the President’s attempts to maintain control over economic challenges. As the administration continues to consolidate power, Gachagua appears increasingly trapped in a battle of public optics, struggling to maintain his status while his role in the inner circle continues to diminish.
