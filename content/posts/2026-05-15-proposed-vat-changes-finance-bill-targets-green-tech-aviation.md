## The VAT Seesaw: How Finance Bill 2026 Reshapes the Cost of Living and Industrial Output

As the Finance Bill 2026 takes shape, working Kenyans face a stark choice between cheaper household tech and rising costs in transport. This shift in tax policy creates a distinct divide between green consumer incentives and heavier burdens on the industrial sector.

### Are your household gadgets and green energy costs finally coming down?

The government is betting that making consumer electronics and clean energy more accessible will spur digital adoption and climate resilience. By removing VAT on mobile phones, electric vehicles, and medical dialyzers, the bill aims to lower the initial barrier to entry for many middle-class households. If you have been waiting to upgrade your smartphone or invest in an electric motorbike, this proposal directly targets your wallet by reducing the final purchase price.

For SMEs, this is a clear signal. The inclusion of agricultural inputs and scrap metal under the VAT-exempt category means that production costs for small-scale farmers and recyclers should theoretically stabilize. However, the catch lies in the broader industrial shift. While you pay less for your phone, companies that rely on heavy logistics or aviation to move goods are looking at higher operational overheads. The 16 percent VAT on denatured ethanol will hit chemical and manufacturing firms, likely forcing them to pass those costs onto consumers in the form of higher prices for finished goods.

### Why is the aviation and tourism sector facing a tax hike?

The proposal to strip VAT exemptions from aviation equipment and aircraft is a significant change. Previously, this sector enjoyed a buffer that kept maintenance and fleet expansion costs relatively low. Under the new rules, airlines and tour operators will face the full brunt of 16 percent VAT on these big-ticket items. 

The economic trade-off here is sharp. While the government provides relief for green technology, it is simultaneously widening the tax net on the infrastructure that drives our tourism sector. If you are planning a domestic flight or a safari package, expect the price of those services to reflect these new tax realities as operators move to protect their shrinking margins. This is not just a change for big corporations; it affects the entire ecosystem of hotels, transport providers, and service staff who rely on the steady flow of international and local travelers.

### What happens to current infrastructure and aid projects?

Transitions in tax policy can often derail projects already in progress. To prevent this, the bill introduces a fiscal grandparenting clause. Any aid-funded infrastructure project that received official approval or exemptions before June 30, 2026, will be protected. This safeguard ensures that current roadworks, energy plants, or hospital constructions do not stall mid-stream due to sudden tax shocks. 

This stability is necessary, especially as recent performance reports show that firms like Shri Krishana Overseas are already struggling with sharp declines in earnings. Without such protections, the added tax burden could lead to project abandonment or significant delays. For the average citizen, this means that major projects funded by international partners will remain shielded from the current tax fluctuations until their completion.

### Why it matters now

The Finance Bill 2026 represents a pivot toward a service-led and green-leaning economy. It favors the digital consumer and the agricultural entrepreneur while demanding higher contributions from heavy industry and transport. As the economy shifts, firms that cannot pivot to cleaner or more efficient methods will find themselves burdened by these new taxes, which will inevitably show up in your monthly shopping basket.

### What to watch next

Keep a close eye on the parliamentary debates regarding the implementation timeline for the aviation tax. If the tourism lobby manages to secure a phased rollout, the impact on your holiday budget might be softer than initially expected. Additionally, watch how retail prices for mobile devices adjust once the bill officially passes into law; if history is any guide, retailers may be slow to pass on these savings to you, so monitor your local tech stores carefully.
