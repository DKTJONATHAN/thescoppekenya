## Kenyan Police Force and Protest Fatalities

A recent report analysing police responses to protests from 2020 to 2025 reveals a concerning pattern of excessive force, including tear gas and live bullets, leading to 159 deaths and over 1,200 injuries. This systemic response casts a long shadow over ongoing demonstrations against rising fuel prices.

The streets of Kenya have once again borne witness to tragic loss this week, as public demonstrations over spiralling fuel prices escalated into violent clashes. As of May 26, 2026, at least 17 Kenyans have been killed in locations including Nairobi, Naivasha, Kiambu, Busia, Thika, and Nakuru, following recent transport sector strikes on May 18 and 19. These recent fatalities, attributed largely to police intervention, are not isolated incidents but rather the latest chapter in a grim, well-documented narrative of how state security apparatus has historically engaged with public dissent in the country. It is a story that, for many, feels all too familiar, hinting at deeply entrenched practices rather than isolated missteps.

The immediate focus is on the human cost of the current fuel price protests. The outcry over the cost of living, a consistent pain point for many Kenyans, has been amplified by the transport sector. Analysts like Mbote Njogu have repeatedly raised concerns over Kenya's fuel pricing strategy, pointing to its ripple effect across the economy. However, the response to these grievances, particularly from law enforcement, has frequently veered into lethal territory, transforming legitimate protest into a confrontation with devastating consequences. The 17 deaths recorded just this week serve as a stark reminder of this often-fatal trajectory.

### A Consistent Pattern of Force

A comprehensive analysis, conducted as part of the Freedom Index report, examined police responses to 1,002 protests across Kenya between 2020 and 2025. The findings paint a troubling picture of consistent and often lethal crowd control methods. The report meticulously documented 159 deaths, 1,245 injuries, and 2,603 arrests directly linked to these protest events over the five-year period. These numbers are a stark metric of the heavy toll on citizens exercising their constitutional right to assembly and expression.

Tear gas canisters emerged as the most frequently deployed crowd-control weapon, recorded 230 times during the period. Following closely, and far more lethally, live bullets were documented in 87 instances. This pairing of 'non-lethal' and 'lethal' force suggests a readiness to escalate, often bypassing internationally recognised protocols for managing public demonstrations. Rubber bullets, water cannons, and batons were also part of the arsenal, but it is the prevalence of tear gas and live ammunition that underscores the severe approach taken.

### Youth at the Forefront

The data further highlights a disproportionate impact on youth-led protests. Demonstrations organised primarily by younger Kenyans experienced weapon use in 114 recorded instances, making them the most affected group. This contrasts with 62 cases for residents and community-led demonstrations, and even fewer for other types of protests. This trend suggests a particular vulnerability for younger generations articulating their frustrations, often over economic hardship and governance issues. The energy and directness of youth activism, while critical for democratic discourse, have frequently met with the state's most aggressive responses.

The persistent recurrence of high fatalities and injuries across varied protest triggers - from economic grievances to political dissent - indicates a deeply ingrained operational culture within elements of the police force. This isn't merely about isolated incidents or rogue officers; it speaks to a systemic issue where the threshold for deploying excessive and lethal force against unarmed citizens appears consistently low. The recent deaths during fuel price protests therefore stand not as an anomaly, but as a chilling echo of the past five years' findings. The pattern observed between 2020 and 2025, where crowd control frequently transitioned from tear gas to live bullets, continues to manifest, turning public outcry into public mourning. It reflects a difficult truth about how swiftly peaceful assembly can become a site of confrontation and tragedy.

### Why it matters now

This report provides vital context to the ongoing loss of life. It compels a re-evaluation of police training and engagement protocols, especially as economic pressures continue to mount and public discontent, whether over fuel prices or other issues, is unlikely to abate. Understanding this historical trajectory is crucial for comprehending the current crisis and for preventing further casualties.

All eyes will be on how the government and police leadership respond to these findings and the ongoing fatalities. Will there be concrete steps towards reform, accountability, and a shift away from lethal crowd control? Or will the familiar cycle of protest, violence, and tragic outcomes simply repeat?
