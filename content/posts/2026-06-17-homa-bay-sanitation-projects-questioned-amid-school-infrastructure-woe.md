## The Cost of a Hole in the Ground

Homa Bay County officials claim a two-door pit latrine is worth half a million shillings, yet schools like Nyaburi Primary are left with crumbling, hazardous facilities. This budget discrepancy reveals a glaring disconnect between executive accounting and the harsh, dangerous reality facing rural students.

The math simply does not add up for anyone who has ever held a hammer or purchased a bag of cement. Before the Senate Public Accounts Committee, Governor Gladys Wanga stood firm, justifying a procurement cost of Ksh 500,000 for a standard two-door pit latrine. In the competitive world of rural construction, independent contractors estimate that such a facility-even accounting for materials, transport, and labor-should rarely exceed Ksh 150,000. That leaves a phantom gap of Ksh 350,000 that seems to vanish into the thin air of county procurement processes, even as infrastructure projects across the region struggle to provide even basic utility.

### The View from the Ground

At Nyaburi Primary School in Karachuonyo, the conversation about unit costs feels like a cruel joke. Here, the reality of sanitation is not measured in abstract budget lines but in the daily anxiety of students and staff. The existing facilities are dilapidated, failing to meet the most basic hygiene standards for hundreds of learners.

Jared Osati, a local educator, observes that the situation goes beyond mere inconvenience. For students with mobility needs, the lack of support infrastructure turns a trip to the latrine into a genuine safety hazard. There is no ramp access, the structures are structurally unsound, and there is a persistent lack of clean water for handwashing. While officials talk about market rates in air-conditioned committee rooms, children at Nyaburi are navigating floorboards that could give way at any moment. The irony is sharp: the county treasury processes payments for gold-plated sanitation, while the actual schools remain stuck in a state of primitive disrepair.

### Fiscal Priorities vs. Classroom Survival

The disparity between the official invoice and the school-level infrastructure is not just a statistical anomaly; it is a policy failure. When the county prioritizes high-cost procurement models that yield crumbling results, the students are the ones paying the price. Educators report that the sanitation crisis is compounded by a lack of food provisions, leaving many children hungry and vulnerable to illnesses that the school environment is poorly equipped to manage.

The disconnect is pervasive. While the arts and entertainment sectors in Kenya continue to celebrate milestones like [Insignia Productions Wins Big at 14th Kalasha Awards](https://zandani.co.ke/article/insignia-productions-wins-at-kalasha-awards), the administrative focus in Homa Bay seems fixed on inflating infrastructure budgets rather than improving the quality of life for the next generation. The Senate's role is to scrutinize how public funds are deployed, yet the evidence from Karachuonyo suggests that the oversight mechanisms are failing to capture the true cost of inefficiency. Even as the nation looks toward modernizing public services, such as the [Kenya to Launch Nairobi Metropolitan Police Unit Modeled on NYPD](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenya-to-launch-nairobi-metropolitan-police-unit-modeled-on-nypd), the most fundamental infrastructure in Homa Bay remains trapped in a cycle of questionable spending and neglected maintenance.

The Ksh 500,000 price tag for a single two-door latrine serves as a clear indicator of where the county government places its fiscal value. It is a valuation that favors complex procurement documentation over the safety of a child walking into a school bathroom. When the ledger claims a structure is worth half a million, but the ground tells a story of rot and risk, who is actually profiting from the project?
