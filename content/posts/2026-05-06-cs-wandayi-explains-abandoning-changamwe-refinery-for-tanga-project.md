## The Tanga Bet: Why Kenya is outsourcing its energy security to Tanzania

Cabinet Secretary for Energy and Petroleum Opiyo Wandayi officially announced the government’s abandonment of the Changamwe oil refinery during a Senate session on Wednesday, May 6, 2026, signaling a major shift toward a regional processing hub in Tanga, Tanzania.

This pivot marks a departure from decades of intent to modernize Kenyan petroleum infrastructure. Government officials confirmed that the Changamwe facility, once the heart of the coastal energy sector, is now considered commercially unviable. The decision reflects a calculated move to prioritize cross-border infrastructure over the costly rehabilitation of aging local assets.

The administration’s shift is rooted in the limited output from the South Lokichar oil fields in Turkana. According to the Ministry of Energy, domestic crude volumes are currently insufficient to justify the high operational costs required to keep a dedicated local refinery functioning at capacity. By participating in the Tanga project, the state aims to integrate into a more robust regional supply chain that leverages larger throughput volumes from across East Africa.

This move effectively writes off the Changamwe facility as a sunk cost, ending years of speculation regarding its potential refurbishment. Instead of funneling capital into a site with declining utility, the government is repositioning itself as a partner in a multi-state energy venture. This transition aligns with a broader strategy to streamline regional petroleum logistics, though it leaves the future of the Mombasa site in legal and economic limbo.

### The risks of sovereign dependency

Outsourcing critical processing capacity to Tanzania introduces new layers of geopolitical vulnerability. By relying on a regional neighbor for petroleum refinement, Kenya surrenders a degree of control over its own energy supply chain. While the economic logic rests on economies of scale, the decision raises questions about national energy sovereignty.

Energy sector stakeholders remain divided on the long-term implications. Proponents argue that the regional model reduces the financial burden on the Kenyan taxpayer. Critics, however, warn that tying the country's energy fate to a foreign refinery exposes the domestic market to external administrative and diplomatic risks. Unlike the recent [Nairobi water supply interruptions](https://zandani.co.ke/article/nairobi-water-reports-supply-interruption-following-kabete-kilimani-) which were resolved within internal jurisdiction, a bottleneck in Tanga would be beyond the reach of Kenyan policy makers.

### Senate reaction and the cost of abandonment

During the Senate proceedings, lawmakers expressed sharp concern regarding the loss of domestic industrial capacity. Senator Mohamed Faki questioned the transparency of the shift, noting that abandoning Changamwe removes a vital pillar of the coastal economy. He pressed the Cabinet Secretary on whether the administration had exhausted all private-public partnership options before turning to the Tanga proposal.

The government maintains that the decision is purely fiscal. Officials pointed to the current global energy market, where large-scale refineries like the one proposed in Tanga offer a safer hedge against price volatility than smaller, outdated local plants. While private sector giants like Aliko Dangote have demonstrated the potential of regional refining capacity elsewhere on the continent, the Kenyan government insists that the Tanga partnership is the only path that balances fiscal discipline with energy needs.

### Why it matters now

The pivot toward Tanga signifies a formal end to Kenya’s pursuit of domestic refining self-sufficiency. As the country moves away from the legacy costs associated with Changamwe, it creates a new dependency on regional cooperation. This move is indicative of a broader trend where nations choose integrated regional frameworks over isolated industrial projects to manage limited natural resources.

### What to watch next

The immediate focus will shift to the formalization of the Tanga agreement and the legal status of the land occupied by the Changamwe refinery. Observers should monitor whether the government will initiate a formal decommissioning process for the Mombasa site or if the facility will be repurposed for petroleum storage, which would maintain some level of local supply chain control. Future parliamentary hearings are expected to drill down into the specific investment figures and the exact nature of the Kenyan state’s equity in the Tanga project.
