## The Silent Deadline: Why Kenya's Newsrooms Are Facing a Future of Financial Fragility

The newsroom hums with the relentless pulse of breaking stories, yet a quiet, persistent anxiety lingers beneath the surface. As we track the nation preparing for the Idd weekend amidst heavy rainfall warnings from the Meteorological Department, it strikes me that journalists are experts at reporting on the safety of others while remaining blind to their own crumbling foundations.

This past week, the conversation between the Media Council and the Retirement Benefits Authority exposed a sobering truth. Our industry is drifting toward a retirement crisis, trading the robust stability of formal pension schemes for the hollow comfort of informal welfare funds that dissolve the moment a contract ends.

### The Myth of the Hustle

Kenyan journalism is defined by a culture that equates success with the ability to chase endless deadlines or land another freelance gig. We wear our hustle as a badge of honor, yet this rugged independence masks a severe structural vulnerability.

Consider the Public Service Superannuation Scheme (PSSS) as a benchmark. It represents a level of security that feels like a fantasy to many media workers. Public servants benefit from a combined 22.5 percent contribution, split between a 7.5 percent employee stake and a 15 percent employer match. This mechanism has built a fund of 291 billion shillings for 511,000 members. In contrast, the average media house welfare fund is voluntary, erratic, and utterly insufficient for life after the newsroom.

We are effectively mimicking the national struggle where 83 percent of the informal sector lacks a retirement buffer, resulting in a dismal 6 percent coverage rate. Despite our professional status, journalists are increasingly trapped in this precarious, informal cycle.

### Structural Security versus Individual Saving

It is easy to point fingers at a perceived lack of financial literacy when journalists face a retirement of poverty. However, this narrative is a convenient distraction from the real culprit: systemic failure. No amount of personal discipline can compensate for the absence of a contributory pension scheme integrated into one's employment terms.

The PSSS model confirms that long-term security is not merely a matter of individual discipline, but an institutional necessity. When an employer commits to a significant portion of retirement savings, the weight of financial survival shifts from the individual to the organization. Currently, many media houses treat retirement benefits as a luxury rather than a core component of a professional contract.

### Why it matters now

The financial fragility of those who report the news is more than a personal burden; it is a direct threat to the integrity of the press. A journalist paralyzed by fear for their own future is far more vulnerable to external pressures, including corruption or compromised reporting. When we see entrepreneurs like Khaligraph Jones constructing massive business headquarters in Ruaka, it serves as a stinging reminder of what is possible. Why can our own industry not build similarly durable assets for its practitioners? The lack of pension infrastructure is a ticking clock that threatens to eventually silence the very voices meant to hold power to account.

### What to watch next

The focus of our industry unions and stakeholders must shift toward mandatory pension enrollment for every media worker. We need to see sustained, aggressive pressure on media owners to match Za Ndanis established by public sector schemes. If the industry fails to transition from haphazard, short-term welfare pools to formal, reliable pension accounts, we will continue to watch our brightest minds exit the profession the moment they realize their service offers no path to a dignified life.

True security is not found in the adrenaline rush of a breaking story. It is built through the slow, consistent accumulation of contributions that honor the years of service given to the public. Without this foundation, the silence that follows a long career in the news will be defined by regret rather than the peace of a well-earned rest.
