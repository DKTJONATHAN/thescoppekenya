## EPRA Fuel Price Freeze: The Hidden Cost of Stability

The Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority has kept pump prices unchanged for the cycle ending August 14, 2026. While motorists in Nairobi continue to pay Ksh214.03 for super petrol, this consistency is being bankrolled by specific fiscal interventions rather than market trends.

This price ceiling represents a temporary truce between the state and global market volatility. Behind the scenes, the government is juggling tax extensions and fund drawdowns to ensure the cost of fuel does not spike at the pump. This move prevents an immediate shock to the household budget but masks the underlying pressures that would otherwise push costs higher.

### Where is the money coming from?

To prevent a price hike, the government has authorized a Ksh945 million drawdown from the Petroleum Development Levy Fund. This fund was originally designed to provide the necessary capital for developing petroleum infrastructure, including storage facilities and distribution networks. By shifting this cash toward price stabilization, the treasury is essentially consuming future infrastructure capacity to cover present-day operational costs.

If this cushion had not been applied, Kenyan motorists would be paying significantly more at the pump. Market analysis suggests that real global costs would have necessitated an increase of nearly 21 percent for diesel and 19.8 percent for kerosene, with super petrol facing an 11.7 percent hike. These numbers represent the difference between the regulated price and the actual landing cost of imported refined products. By tapping into the levy, the state is absorbing this gap, effectively transferring the burden from the consumer's wallet today to the nation's broader fiscal balance sheet tomorrow.

### Why is the government delaying VAT collection?

Alongside the cash injection, the state has extended the 8 percent Value Added Tax waiver on petroleum products until October 2026. This tax is a major revenue stream for the exchequer, and keeping it off the books creates a substantial shortfall in the national budget. While this keeps the cost of transport and logistics manageable for local businesses, it forces the government to find alternative funding sources elsewhere or increase public borrowing to plug the resulting gap.

Every shilling lost to this VAT extension is a shilling that cannot be spent on building roads, expanding rural electricity, or funding [automated trading initiatives](https://zandani.co.ke/article/thee-pluto-highlights-automated-trading-bmm-nairobi-launch) that seek to modernize the financial sector. When the waiver eventually expires, the sudden reintroduction of that 8 percent tax will likely cause a sharp jump in prices, unless global oil prices drop significantly by then. The current policy acts as a shock absorber, but it does not remove the hazard; it merely pushes the impact further down the timeline.

### How does this affect the Kenyan household?

For the ordinary person, the freeze is a welcome reprieve. Stable fuel prices mean that matatu fares are unlikely to spike this month and the cost of transporting food from farms to urban markets remains predictable. This level of stability helps in planning monthly expenses and keeps a lid on headline inflation. However, the reliance on fiscal maneuvers means that the economy is walking a fine line.

The current stability is a trade-off. By sacrificing long-term infrastructure funding and delaying tax revenue, the government is betting that international oil market prices will settle before these emergency measures run dry. If global markets remain high, the options to protect the consumer will dwindle, potentially leading to a more painful price correction in the final quarter of the year. For now, the pump price holds, but the bill for this stability is quietly accumulating in the background.

Short-term price relief is being bought with long-term fiscal trade-offs that shift the burden from today's pump price to tomorrow's national debt.
