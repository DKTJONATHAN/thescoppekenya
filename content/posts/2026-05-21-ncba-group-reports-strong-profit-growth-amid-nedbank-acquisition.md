# NCBA Group Q1 2026: Profit Growth or Valuation Theater?

NCBA Group’s first-quarter results for 2026 paint a picture of relentless growth, but a forensic look at the numbers suggests a more tactical agenda. While management touts a 9 percent profit jump, the underlying surge in bad debt and the impending 66 percent acquisition by Nedbank point to a firm positioning itself for a high-stakes exit.

### Asset Dominance and the Provisioning Warning

NCBA reported a net profit of KSh 5.96 billion for the opening quarter of 2026. The bank’s leadership anchors this success on the performance of its Boosta SME product and a dominant 32 percent share of the asset finance market. They are framing the institution as an efficient engine of Kenyan enterprise, but the balance sheet tells a more complicated story.

The bank tucked away KSh 2.54 billion to cover potential loan losses. This isn't just routine bookkeeping. It is a loud signal that their aggressive SME lending strategy is facing significant headwinds. With gross non-performing loans climbing to KSh 39.28 billion, stakeholders should question whether this reflects genuine market growth or an intentional effort to inflate the balance sheet before the Nedbank Group secures its 66 percent majority stake next week.

The strategy here is transparent. By aggressively deploying capital into higher-risk SME accounts, the bank successfully pads its interest income and secures impressive market penetration statistics. Investors might view this as a masterclass in valuation optimization. However, from the perspective of the Kenyan borrower, it looks suspiciously like a lender loading up its portfolio with fragile debt to maximize its appeal to a foreign buyer.

### The Pretoria Pivot and Credit Risk

CEO John Gachora has framed the impending Nedbank deal as a catalyst for regional stability and integration. The narrative is one of expanded capacity for MSMEs throughout Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania, and Rwanda. Yet, history tells us that when foreign giants acquire super-majorities in regional banks, the institution's risk appetite typically contracts.

If the current loan book is as precarious as the KSh 2.54 billion provision suggests, the incoming South African ownership will likely demand a swift shift toward conservative lending. Local entrepreneurs currently benefiting from the ease of the Boosta product should anticipate a sudden tightening of credit conditions once the ink dries on the May 28 deal. This is a recurring playbook in corporate finance: inflate the asset base, sell the vision of scale, and leave the new board to reconcile the inevitable fallout of elevated credit defaults.

This pattern of consolidation mirrors the volatility seen elsewhere in the local economy, where long-term commitments are being traded for short-term exit premiums. The bank’s 32 percent market share in asset finance is currently serving as leverage for a sale rather than acting as a foundation for sustainable domestic lending.

### Why the Stakes Are High

The Nedbank acquisition is more than a change in branding or boardroom leadership. It represents a fundamental shift in the risk profile of a major East African lender. If NCBA has used a lending surge to dress up its valuation, the consequences will fall squarely on borrowers. They are the ones who will bear the burden of harsher terms or sudden liquidity crunches once new management takes the reins in Nairobi.

### Outlook for the Second Quarter

The commencement of the Nedbank offer on May 28 will reveal whether the market truly buys the narrative behind NCBA's current books. Investors should monitor the non-performing loan ratio throughout the second quarter with extreme scrutiny. Should the bank abruptly restrict lending criteria immediately following the acquisition, it will validate the suspicion that this Q1 growth was a tactical sprint meant to boost valuation rather than a long-term commitment to SME inclusion.

Is the Boosta product a genuine tool for financial inclusion, or was it a credit-loading exercise intended to inflate the purchase price before the keys were handed over to Pretoria? The next three months will provide the answer.
