## The Poverty Trap of Exporting Raw Potential

Farmers in Murang'a and Kericho are tired of watching their hard-earned harvests travel thousands of kilometers only to return in fancy retail packaging that costs ten times more than the raw product they sold. While officials speak of industrialization, the reality on the ground remains a cycle where foreign processors capture the vast majority of the value chain, leaving local producers to survive on the smallest margins of the global agricultural trade.

Cabinet Secretary Mutahi Kagwe recently addressed the World Food Prize Foundation DialogueNext Forum, calling for an urgent pivot away from the export of raw agricultural commodities. He argued that the current global trade architecture is fundamentally rigged against African nations, forcing a reliance on raw exports that suppresses domestic manufacturing and creates a permanent deficit in job creation. The CS specifically pointed to the coffee, tea, and macadamia sectors as prime examples of lost wealth, noting that the combination of punitive tariffs on finished goods and the high cost of local processing machinery effectively handcuffs Kenyan farmers to the bottom of the value pyramid.

### From Farm to Retail: The Mathematics of Loss

The arithmetic of the raw export model is brutal. A kilogram of premium Kenyan coffee beans often fetches a modest sum for a smallholder farmer in Murang'a, yet once those beans are roasted, branded, and packaged by firms in Europe or Asia, the retail price spikes by hundreds of percent. This gap represents the wealth that is essentially drained from the local economy. Farmers report that they are effectively subsidizing foreign labor and infrastructure because they lack the capital to process their own goods locally. Even as sophisticated financial instruments like the Satrix MSCI World Feeder ETF list on the Nairobi Securities Exchange to provide new investment avenues for the elite, the average smallholder remains trapped in a system that makes purchasing modern roasting or packing equipment prohibitively expensive due to high import taxes.

### Voices from the Fields

Local farmers view the official rhetoric with a mixture of hope and skepticism. In Kericho, tea growers point out that while they understand the need for local value addition, the barrier is not just ambition but basic industrial infrastructure. They argue that one cannot process tea without a reliable energy supply and the capital to invest in sophisticated machinery, both of which remain out of reach for cooperatives struggling with fluctuating market prices. Meanwhile, macadamia farmers in central Kenya report that their biggest hurdle is the lack of policy consistency. They note that previous attempts to process locally were stifled by sudden shifts in export bans and inconsistent support for indigenous manufacturers, leading many to view current government promises as yet another cycle of grand announcements that fail to reach the farm gate. Observers elsewhere note that countries like Vietnam, which successfully shifted from being a low-end coffee exporter to a global powerhouse in processing, did so by aggressively lowering duties on industrial equipment and providing low-interest credit to farmers, a model that remains a distant ambition for most Kenyan producers.

### Obstacles to Industrial Sovereignty

The systemic failure to shift this model is highlighted by the high cost of doing business locally. Small-scale entrepreneurs attempting to enter the processing space often find themselves entangled in complex regulatory requirements that favor established, larger corporations. For those trying to modernize, the government tax structure on imported industrial machinery acts as a significant deterrent. Instead of shielding the local processor, these tariffs make the initial capital expenditure for a small packaging plant or a processing mill nearly impossible for a collective of farmers to absorb. Without a fundamental restructuring of how the state treats industrial equipment compared to finished raw materials, the dream of capturing the full value of the harvest will remain a distant target. The path forward requires more than speeches; it demands a radical dismantling of the financial and tax barriers that prevent the farmer from becoming the manufacturer.

The mood across the agricultural heartlands remains one of cautious anticipation, as farmers wait to see if the rhetoric of value addition will finally be matched by the cold, hard reality of policy reform.
