President William Ruto turned a church service into a political venue this past Sunday, launching a series of disparaging remarks against opposition leader Kalonzo Musyoka. The event sparked immediate controversy as members of the clergy were seen openly applauding the President’s aggressive political rhetoric.

## The Erosion of Sacred Spaces

### The Pulpit as a Political Stage
The boundary between statecraft and the sanctuary has become increasingly invisible. During the recent service, the President deviated from traditional religious liturgy to launch a direct verbal assault on Kalonzo Musyoka. The atmosphere in the church shifted instantly from one of reverence to a partisan arena, confirming that for many, the altar is now viewed as a high-profile podium for settling personal and political scores.

### Clergy Complicity in Toxic Talk
The most jarring element of the afternoon was not the President's sharp tongue, but the visible reaction of the men and women of the cloth. Rather than offering a prayer for unity or maintaining a neutral spiritual stance, the clergy members in attendance were observed laughing and nodding in vigorous agreement as political vitriol filled the room. By choosing to validate these attacks, these spiritual leaders have effectively traded their moral authority for proximity to power, proving they are often more comfortable with the machinations of the state than the teachings of scripture.

### A Growing Moral Deficit
Observers are now questioning the integrity of religious institutions that allow themselves to become extensions of a government agenda. The rot currently residing in the pulpit represents a significant crisis for the nation. When religious leaders abandon their role as the moral conscience of the country to act as cheerleaders for political insults, they forfeit their standing to critique the decline of national discourse. When the shepherd begins dancing to the politician’s tune, it becomes clear that the sanctuary has been compromised by those tasked with protecting it.
