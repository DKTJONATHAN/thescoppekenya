## Understanding Police Accountability and the NPS Disciplinary Process

When a high-ranking officer like OCS Dishen Angoya avoids a public court appearance despite allegations of misconduct, many Kenyans ask the same question: Why do police officers seem to face a different set of rules than ordinary citizens?

The National Police Service recently clarified that Central Police Station OCS Dishen Angoya would not be appearing in court following his discharge from Langata Hospital. This follows public outcry surrounding the release of 64 suspects detained during recent fuel protests. While the service confirms that the OCS has been released unconditionally, the lack of a public trial has ignited a debate about how the police force holds its own members accountable.

### How does the Internal Affairs Unit handle misconduct?

The National Police Service often relies on the Internal Affairs Unit (IAU) to manage cases of suspected misconduct or breach of duty. This unit operates as an internal mechanism designed to investigate complaints against officers without immediately escalating them to the criminal justice system. 

When an officer is accused of wrongdoing, the IAU conducts an administrative review rather than a police investigation leading to a court file. This process is focused on internal disciplinary measures, such as demotions, transfers, or forced retirement, rather than jail time or fines imposed by a judge. The primary objective is to maintain order within the ranks. However, this creates a situation where the public, who are the complainants, often remain unaware of the actual penalties an officer faces. If the matter is kept internal, the courtroom doors remain shut to the public, effectively shielding the officer from the scrutiny of a public trial.

### Why are police treated under internal rules instead of public law?

The constitutional framework of the National Police Service allows for specific administrative regulations that govern the conduct of officers. These rules serve as a layer between the officer and the criminal justice system. While ordinary Kenyans facing similar allegations regarding the handling of suspects would likely undergo a standard arrest, booking, and arraignment process, police officers often benefit from these internal policies.

This system creates a distinction between administrative discipline and judicial accountability. When an officer is processed internally, they are subject to service standing orders. These orders allow the leadership of the police service to decide whether an issue constitutes a breach of professional conduct or a criminal offense. If the leadership opts for the former, the officer avoids the public record of a court proceeding. This creates a perception of impunity, as the public cannot observe the transparency of the disciplinary process or verify the severity of the consequences.

### Why it matters now

The situation regarding the OCS raises fundamental questions about equity in the justice system. When 64 citizens are detained during protests and the officer responsible for their release faces no public legal consequences, it signals a disconnect between public expectations and police policy. For a country already grappling with public trust, as seen in the recent [Nairobi transport strikes](https://zandani.co.ke/article/nairobi-transport-strike-continues-as-matatu-operators-reject-deal), any perceived shielding of authorities from public scrutiny threatens to erode the stability of our institutions. Just as international observers monitor [foreign military partnerships](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenya-faces-backlash-over-expanding-foreign-military-partnerships) for signs of overreach, Kenyans are becoming increasingly sensitive to how the state handles its own power.

### What to watch next

Keep an eye on how the Internal Affairs Unit communicates the final findings of the Angoya case. While a court appearance is now off the table, the police service may eventually issue a report on whether administrative sanctions were applied. Public pressure will likely force a shift in how these cases are handled, potentially moving the service toward greater transparency to avoid further speculation. 

The public deserves to know if internal justice leads to actual accountability or just internal shuffling.
