## DCI Homicide Unit Makes Arrest in Mwiki Gospel Singer Murder

DCI homicide detectives have apprehended Josiah Njeru Njiri in Kasarani regarding the fatal arson attack on gospel artist Rachel Wandeto. The singer succumbed to severe burn injuries at Kenyatta National Hospital six days after the assault in Mwiki.

The arrest of Josiah Njeru Njiri marks a pivotal shift in a case that has rattled Nairobi's creative community. On May 16, Rachel Wandeto was targeted in an act of extreme violence involving a flammable substance at her residence in Mwiki. While emergency responders rushed her to Kenyatta National Hospital, the extent of her injuries proved fatal, leading to her death while under professional medical care. 

This tragedy highlights a growing pattern of vulnerability for Nairobi’s creative professionals who operate as solo workers in residential hubs. For many entertainers, the lines between their private residence and their workspace are increasingly blurred, leaving them susceptible to risks that larger, more formal organizations might mitigate. The attack on Wandeto is not an isolated incident but a symptom of a systemic gap in security for gig-economy workers who lack corporate-level protection.

The DCI homicide unit has confirmed that the investigation is now in a critical phase, focusing on the interactions leading up to the attack. Detectives are currently analyzing digital footprints and witness accounts to establish the sequence of events that brought the suspect to the singer’s doorstep. This case follows a series of high-profile investigations handled by the DCI this year, including the ongoing inquiries into public figures like [Sahara’s Nywele Ngumu drama](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-27-bare-sahara-s-nywele-ngumu-drama-dci-says-she-s-not-going-a), which underscore the agency’s tightening grip on criminal activity targeting Nairobi’s entertainment class.

### The Vulnerability of Nairobi's Creative Freelancers

The Mwiki incident has reignited a difficult conversation about the lack of safety protocols for solo entertainers in the city. Unlike mainstream corporate employees who benefit from secure office environments and private transport, the creative class often relies on informal transport arrangements and works from unsecured residential locations. This precarity makes them easy targets for individuals who seek to exploit personal or financial disputes.

Industry peers are now demanding more robust security interventions. The expectation is that the arrest of Njiri will provide a focal point for a broader discussion on duty-of-care, specifically for women working independently in the entertainment sector. Just as [Home Afrika has faced recent scrutiny](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-05-01-home-afrika-reports-profit-as-liabilities-exceed-total-asse) regarding their institutional liabilities, the creative industry must now grapple with its own lack of protective structures for those who contribute to the city’s cultural output.
