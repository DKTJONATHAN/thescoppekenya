## Amboseli Airstrip Temporarily Closed Amid Flooding The main airstrip at Amboseli National Park has been temporarily closed due to severe flooding caused by ongoing heavy rainfall. The Kenya Wildlife Service (KWS) announced the closure as a safety precaution, diverting all aircraft operations to the alternative Tawi Airstrip. This disruption affects numerous local and international tourists seeking access to the renowned wildlife destination.

Main Airstrip Operations Suspended The Kenya Wildlife Service confirmed that the Main Amboseli Airstrip is currently inundated with water, making it unsafe for aircraft landings and departures. "The Kenya Wildlife Service wishes to notify all visitors, tour operators and airlines operating flights to Amboseli National Park that the Main Amboseli Airstrip is currently flooded due to ongoing heavy rainfall in the area," stated KWS. The temporary suspension will remain in effect until weather conditions improve and the airstrip is deemed safe for use. This aligns with broader reports of heavy rainfall affecting various parts of Kenya in early March 2026.

Alternative Landing Point Designated Airlines and charter pilots are directed to use Tawi Airstrip as the temporary landing point. This alternative facility is located approximately five kilometres from the Kimana Gate, ensuring that visitors with pre-booked game drives can still access Amboseli National Park despite the flight disruptions into the main airstrip this week. KWS is closely monitoring the situation at the flooded airstrip and anticipates issuing an update on its reopening shortly.

Wider Impact of Persistent Rainfall This closure follows a KWS announcement on February 28, 2026, warning the public that heavy rainfall across Kenya had rendered several roads within national parks and protected areas flooded, impassable, or extremely slippery. The advisory urged visitors to exercise caution and adhere to ranger guidance. The prevailing wet conditions have created widespread challenges, impacting various wildlife and public spaces. In a related incident, a tourist Land Cruiser was swept away by the swollen Mara River in Narok West, Kenya, between February 25-26, 2026, with passengers still missing as of February 27, 2026. KWS attributed the incident to heavy rains causing the river to overflow. Furthermore, Karura Forest in Nairobi faced partial closure on March 6, 2026, with access restricted to trails near the caves, waterfalls, and the Ruaka Picnic Site due to flooding. The Kenya Forest Service warned that these paths are unsafe and submerged, advising visitors to avoid them until further notice. Other parks, including Nairobi National Park and Maasai Mara National Reserve, have also experienced road disruptions and accessibility challenges in early March 2026.
