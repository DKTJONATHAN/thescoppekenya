Inspector General Douglas Kanja, speaking from Vigilance House in Nairobi on Friday, April 17, 2026, issued a stern warning against attacks on police stations while directing officers to strictly adhere to the Constitution, amidst rising public discontent and planned protests.

Police Chiefs Issue Public and Internal Directives

Warning Against Attacks and Lawlessness The National Police Service (NPS) has issued a strong warning to individuals and groups contemplating or engaging in attacks on police stations, destruction of public infrastructure, and other acts of lawlessness. Such actions, Kanja cautioned, will be met with the full force of the law without exception. He emphasized that police stations are fundamental to public safety and emergency response. "Police stations are not just buildings; they are the bedrock of public safety, emergency response, and the first line of defense. To attack a police station is to attack the very security of the public it serves," Kanja stated. The Inspector General additionally cautioned against increasing political incitement and the mobilization of criminal gangs for violent activities, vowing that orchestrators and financiers would face prosecution regardless of their social or political standing. The NPS, in collaboration with the public and partner security agencies, has activated comprehensive strategies, including intensified operations to bring perpetrators swiftly to justice. This commitment aligns with broader efforts to combat serious crimes, as seen in cases like the recent conviction of a Somali national for recruiting a minor into the Al-Shabaab network.

Officers Ordered to Uphold Constitution Concurrently, Kanja directed all officers to strictly adhere to the Constitution, the National Police Service Act, and service standing orders. He stressed that professionalism, restraint, accountability, and respect for human rights are non-negotiable in all operations. This directive follows a recent incident in Embu on April 14, 2026, where police officers allegedly shot two civilians protesting the poor state of the Ishiara Level IV Hospital in Mbeere North. "Stern and immediate action will be taken against any misconduct, in line with the robust frameworks for accountability in Kenya," the police boss warned.

Planned Protests and Official Response Kanja’s remarks came hours after Nairobi Regional Police Commander Issa Mohamud issued a separate warning regarding planned demonstrations scheduled for Tuesday, April 21, 2026, particularly in the Central Business District. Mohamud affirmed the right to peaceful assembly but insisted that all protests must strictly comply with the law. He noted that police had not received formal notification from protest organizers, a mandatory legal requirement for coordination and security planning, urging engagement with authorities to prevent confrontation and disruption of public order. The planned demonstrations are linked to public concern over rising fuel prices and the high cost of living, with sections of the populace expressing frustration over increased transport, food, and essential commodity costs. Despite police caution, some groups maintain the protests will proceed, citing the urgency of public expression on the economic situation, while others accuse authorities of attempting to suppress legitimate dissent. Political leaders have also weighed in, with the opposition issuing a seven-day ultimatum for action on the fuel crisis, while President William Ruto and his Deputy Kithure Kindiki have urged critics to seek practical solutions instead of resorting to mass protests.
