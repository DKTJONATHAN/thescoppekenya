## Kenya Targets Oil Sector Expansion with New SEZ Legislation Kenya's National Assembly has initiated debate on proposed legislation to extend Special Economic Zone (SEZ) incentives to petroleum operators, aiming to significantly boost investor interest and capital in the nascent oil extraction sector. This strategic shift seeks to replace previous short-term arrangements with a comprehensive statutory framework designed for long-term, capital-intensive projects requiring substantial upfront investment and extended development timelines. The government's objective is to establish a more competitive fiscal environment, positioning Kenya to unlock the commercial potential of its oil reserves.

Regulatory Streamlining for Petroleum Investment The proposed law seeks to embed long-term incentives within statute, moving beyond project-specific agreements which have often been perceived as insufficient for large-scale petroleum ventures. This regulatory overhaul is intended to standardize treatment for investors, providing a predictable and stable environment crucial for attracting the billions in capital required. Lawmakers supporting the Bill emphasize that this approach could accelerate development across multiple blocks, particularly in the Turkana basin.

Comprehensive Fiscal Incentives Central to the new legislation is a suite of fiscal incentives designed to reduce project costs and enhance investor appeal. Proposed amendments include extending value-added tax (VAT) relief to supplies for SEZ operators. Furthermore, time limits on specific withholding tax exemptions for payments to non-residents would be removed, offering greater financial predictability. Additional changes would exempt key oil equipment from select levies, notably those linked to rail transport, thereby facilitating the movement of heavy infrastructure into remote production areas. These measures collectively aim to lower the financial hurdles currently impeding investment.

Generalizing Successful Incentive Models The move to generalize these incentives across the petroleum sector draws parallels from past successes. In 2025, the government granted extensive tax and duty exemptions to Gulf Energy for Block T7 in Turkana under a restructured production-sharing contract. That agreement eliminated VAT on petroleum inputs, scrapped import levies, and eased withholding tax obligations, while also expanding cost recovery limits and clarifying eligible capital expenditures. This earlier initiative underscored the significant fiscal support deemed necessary to render the Turkana basin commercially viable, a model the new legislation now seeks to extend widely.

Unlocking Turkana's Potential Amidst Global Dynamics The Turkana fields, estimated to hold over 300 million recoverable barrels, have long been recognized for their commercial promise despite facing financial constraints due to high development costs and infrastructure challenges. By extending SEZ benefits-traditionally reserved for manufacturing and export-oriented industries-policymakers are strategically repositioning the sector. This comes as Kenya, like many nations, navigates a complex global economic landscape. Investors often consider broader regional stability when committing to large-scale projects, with events such as those prompting the recent Kenya Middle East security advisory highlighting the need for robust domestic policy to counterbalance external uncertainties. The generalization of SEZ incentives is a calculated move to enhance the attractiveness and viability of Kenya's oil sector on the international stage.
