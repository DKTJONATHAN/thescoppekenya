## Understanding the Market Disconnect: Why Flame Tree Group’s Stock Performance Does Not Lower Your Grocery Bill

Recent market activity involving Flame Tree Group has left many observers puzzled. The company announced a net loss of 15.9 million shillings, yet its share price on the Nairobi Securities Exchange jumped by 22 percent. To a household managing a tight budget, this looks like a contradiction. It is natural to wonder why a company struggling with losses sees its valuation rise while the cost of consumer goods stays high. The answer is found in the pressure of corporate debt, which prevents manufacturers from passing savings on to the public.

### Why market valuation can rise despite a net loss

Stock investors and everyday shoppers track different metrics. While Flame Tree reported a bottom-line loss, the company’s internal operations showed notable progress. Its EBITDA grew by 18 percent to hit 384.5 million shillings. This indicates that the core activity of manufacturing goods is becoming more efficient. Furthermore, the company improved its cash flow from a negative position to an inflow of 157.2 million shillings. Investors often interpret these signs of operational stability as evidence that the company is moving in the right direction, prompting them to buy shares in anticipation of future recovery.

However, this market confidence does not mean the firm has achieved total financial health. Efficiency on the factory floor is an important step, but it does not immediately translate into lower retail prices. The company remains locked in a difficult struggle to balance its books, as its overall debt obligations overshadow its recent operational gains.

### The impact of debt on consumer prices

The core challenge for Flame Tree and many similar manufacturers is a heavy reliance on external financing. The company currently holds 1.32 billion shillings in total borrowings, a figure that includes a 470 million shilling bank overdraft. These financial obligations resulted in finance costs rising to 307 million shillings this year.

Consider how this affects your shopping basket. When a company generates revenue, it must prioritize interest payments to lenders before it can fund other business activities. By dedicating over 300 million shillings to interest expenses, the firm loses the ability to reinvest in production efficiencies that could lower the final price of goods. Consequently, even as the company improves its manufacturing processes, the resulting profit is consumed by the cost of servicing debt. The interest bill acts as a barrier that prevents operational efficiency from reaching the consumer in the form of cheaper products.

### How financial burdens affect workforce stability

High levels of debt also create a precarious situation for employees. When a business carries over a billion shillings in debt, management must focus on survival rather than expansion or large-scale hiring. Although the firm reported a gross margin of 37.8 percent, the continuous need to cover interest on overdrafts keeps the company in a defensive mode. 

For staff, this often translates to limited growth in wages or restricted job creation. Businesses that are forced to prioritize debt repayment over all else find it difficult to invest in their workforce. While national discussions continue regarding the Division of Revenue Bill and other fiscal policies, local manufacturers are managing their own internal budget crises. If the cost of borrowing remains high throughout the economy, the manufacturing sector will struggle to provide the price relief that many Kenyan families need.

### Why this dynamic matters today

There is a growing disconnect between the optimism seen on the stock exchange and the reality faced by consumers. A rising stock price represents an investor’s bet that a company will survive and eventually become profitable, not a signal that products are becoming more affordable. High interest rates effectively act as a hidden tax on the manufacturing sector, which keeps retail prices elevated regardless of how well a company runs its factories.

### What to monitor moving forward

In the coming year, the primary indicator of change will be how these companies manage their debt portfolios. If firms continue to rely heavily on expensive bank overdrafts, the downward pressure on consumer prices will remain weak. As policymakers debate public salary regulations and broader economic adjustments, it is worth watching whether local firms can successfully restructure their liabilities. Reducing the interest burden is the most reliable path toward converting improved factory productivity into tangible savings for the average household.
