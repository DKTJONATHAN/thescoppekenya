## Digital Asset Governance: The Billion-Shilling Liability

The High Court of Kenya recently dismissed an application by Bonfire Adventures seeking to secure 48 Safaricom lines currently registered to Simon Kabu. This judicial outcome exposes a fundamental risk for domestic companies that rely on telecommunications infrastructure registered to individuals rather than the corporate entity itself. The ruling highlights a recurring failure in the local startup sector, where digital assets are frequently managed as personal extensions of the founder rather than as core company property. By failing to migrate subscriber registration to the business entity after incorporation, the firm now faces a significant financial challenge: a demand for KSh 1.868 billion in historical usage fees and a continuous monthly rental obligation of KSh 14.4 million. Because the court found no legal basis to grant injunctive relief, the case serves as a definitive reminder that legal ownership of digital lines resides solely with the individual named in the official registry.

### The Myth of Founder-Company Symmetry

During the rapid growth phase of Kenyan SMEs, founders often conflate their personal identity with that of their enterprise. Legal frameworks, however, recognize the company as a distinct, separate entity. When assets such as mobile numbers, domain names, or proprietary social media handles remain linked to a founder’s personal identity, they lose their status as institutional assets. This creates a dangerous vulnerability, granting the individual the power to disrupt or withhold access to essential operational tools during disputes or restructuring.

This lack of structural governance has tangible implications for corporate valuation. Prospective investors prioritize the integrity of a company’s asset base during due diligence. When core revenue drivers, such as a primary customer-service line, are anchored to an individual rather than a corporate tax identity, the business faces an immediate decline in valuation due to the heightened risk of operational discontinuity. Much like the complex asset control issues observed in larger corporate sell-offs, the Bonfire dispute demonstrates that the stability of a company is only as robust as the legal documentation securing its infrastructure. Founders must recognize that professionalizing a firm requires more than just scaling revenue; it demands the formalization of every digital touchpoint that drives the business forward.

### Regulatory Gaps and Risk Mitigation

The current regulatory landscape managed by the Communications Authority of Kenya faces growing scrutiny regarding these structural blind spots. Existing SIM registration protocols prioritize individual accountability and security, yet they lack a specific framework for the seamless migration of legacy numbers into corporate ownership. This creates a difficult bottleneck for firms that evolved from small operations into established entities. Without a clear mechanism to formalize these transitions, many companies remain trapped in a state of administrative limbo, often requiring judicial intervention to rectify ownership errors.

To mitigate such risks, businesses must treat digital identity with the same procedural rigor applied to physical real estate or tangible machinery. A comprehensive digital audit should be a mandatory exercise for any growing firm. This process involves verifying that every business-critical SIM card, software license, and digital platform is registered under the company’s KRA PIN and certificate of incorporation. Failure to execute these bureaucratic updates leaves a firm entirely dependent on the cooperation of the original registrant, creating a precarious dependency that can threaten the entire enterprise. 

For the broader investment community, the lesson is straightforward: a firm that cannot substantiate absolute ownership of its operational infrastructure presents an inherently unstable risk profile. Moving forward, governance experts anticipate that the aftermath of the Bonfire case will trigger an industry-wide reassessment. Transparency concerning the ownership of digital real estate is likely to become a non-negotiable requirement in future term sheets. While the Communications Authority may eventually introduce policies to assist corporations in claiming these lines without the consent of the original owner, the current climate dictates that structural integrity must precede expansion. Until such regulations are established, firms that prioritize short-term growth over the formalization of their digital assets remain susceptible to catastrophic, self-inflicted liabilities.
