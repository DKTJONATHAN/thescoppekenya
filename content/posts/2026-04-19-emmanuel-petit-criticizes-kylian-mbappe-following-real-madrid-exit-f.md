## Real Madrid's Perpetual Blame Game Emmanuel Petit has lambasted Kylian Mbappe, accusing him of injecting selfishness into Real Madrid's dressing room after their Champions League exit. While Petit blames Camavinga more for the Bayern Munich loss, the Bernabéu's ongoing malaise points to systemic issues beyond individual player performance.

Mbappe: The Selfish Scapegoat? Former Arsenal and Chelsea midfielder Emmanuel Petit pulled no punches, declaring that Kylian Mbappe's arrival "filled the Real Madrid dressing room with selfishness." He labeled the situation a "fiasco". This accusation comes after Mbappe's return from a minor injury saw Real Madrid lose three and draw one of their last four matches, a damning run that has critics sharpening their knives. The irony is not lost on anyone watching Paris Saint-Germain, who, according to Petit, have become "phenomenal" and "united, like the fingers of a hand" since Mbappe’s departure. The timing, as Petit dryly notes, "really works against him".

Camavinga's Costly Card Despite his criticism of Mbappe, Petit reserves his harshest judgment for Eduardo Camavinga. He asserts that "If anyone needs to be blamed, it’s Camavinga," calling his late foul and subsequent red card against Bayern Munich "catastrophic". While acknowledging the 86th-minute red card was "very harsh," Petit conceded that referee Slavko Vincic was "simply applying the rules," a point often conveniently overlooked by Real Madrid, who, in Petit’s cynical view, "always hide behind the referee’s performance". It’s an old trick, trotted out whenever things go sideways.

Bernabéu's Deeper Rot The issues at the Santiago Bernabéu extend far beyond individual errors or perceived selfishness. This season has been a masterclass in dysfunction. Real Madrid sacked Xabi Alonso back in January after a Spanish Super Cup loss to Barcelona, amid whispers of squad unhappiness with team selection and tactics. Florentino Perez immediately brought in Alvaro Arbeloa, yet the team's fortunes haven't improved; they languish nine points behind leaders Barcelona in La Liga and are now out of the Champions League. Radio Marca analyst Sergio Valentin offers a more measured, though equally damning, perspective, insisting Mbappe isn't the sole problem. Valentin highlights Mbappe's defensive work against Bayern, asking: "did Real Madrid win?" He argues that reducing the team's struggles to Mbappe's perceived lack of defense unfairly burdens one player when the "crux of the problem" lies elsewhere. The persistent underperformance highlights a club grappling with internal discord, where individual brilliance is overshadowed by collective disarray. While Real Madrid grapples with its identity crisis, other major clubs are already plotting their next tactical overhauls and midfield maestros.
