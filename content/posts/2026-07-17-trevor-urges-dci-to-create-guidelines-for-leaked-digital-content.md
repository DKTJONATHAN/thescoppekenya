## Privacy Protection or Selective Policing? 

Kenyan content executive Trevor has officially challenged the Directorate of Criminal Investigations to clarify how it handles leaked private digital media. The formal request seeks to bridge the enforcement gap within the 2018 Computer Misuse and Cybercrimes Act during an era of viral content volatility.

The digital streets are currently ablaze, but for once, the conversation has shifted from the content itself to the glaring inconsistency of the law. Following the recent circulation of private media involving influencers like Sheryl Gabriella and Tyler Mbaya, Kenyans are taking to X and TikTok not just to gossip, but to demand a definitive legal framework. The consensus among the digital populace is clear: the current application of cybercrime laws feels like a game of musical chairs where the rules change depending on who is in the seat.

### The DCI Privacy Paradox: Why Kenyan Fans are Demanding More Than Just Clickbait Justice

While figures like Geoffrey Mosiria have publicly pushed for aggressive investigations and arrests to stem the tide of non-consensual digital sharing, the online community remains skeptical. Fans are arguing that the law is being applied in a vacuum. The frustration stems from a feeling that the 2018 legislation is being treated as a suggestion rather than a mandate. For many observers, this creates a situation where digital rights are only protected for those with a certain level of social or political currency, leaving regular citizens vulnerable to the whims of platform algorithms and unchecked doxxing.

The discourse is moving fast, and here is how the observers are reacting:

*   One vocal user on X pointed out that we have laws on paper, but in practice, the DCI seems to wait for a public outcry before moving. They argued that if the law were applied equally, we would see fewer of these leaks because the consequences would be predictable and severe for everyone, not just for trending names.
*   A TikTok creator noted that the ambiguity surrounding the Computer Misuse and Cybercrimes Act is the actual problem. They suggested that because the guidelines are not clear, police departments across Nairobi often dismiss these cases as personal disputes rather than clear-cut cybercrimes, creating a massive vacuum in accountability.
*   Another observer commented that the current panic is just a symptom of a larger rot. They noted that whether it is socialites looking to pivot into politics like [Socialite Mercy Masai Rebrands for Kajiado Women Representative Political Bid](https://zandani.co.ke/article/socialite-mercy-masai-rebrands-for-kajiado-women-representative-poli) or influencers caught in a scandal, the common denominator is that our digital privacy is treated as a secondary concern until it becomes a national nuisance.

### Moving Beyond the Viral Feedback Loop

This call for institutional clarity is a departure from the usual reactions that follow digital leaks. Trevor’s formal appeal suggests that the industry is finally realizing that reactive policing is insufficient. If the DCI adopts clear guidelines, it would force a standard protocol for reporting, investigating, and prosecuting cases of non-consensual media distribution. This would, in theory, remove the subjective nature of current enforcement.

Observers are also noting that this demand for transparency aligns with a growing weariness regarding how the internet consumes lives. Much like the mixed critical reception seen in [Toy Story 5 Reviews: Critics Divided on Technology, Playtime Themes](https://zandani.co.ke/article/toy-story-5-reviews-technology-playtime-critics-divided), where people are debating the impact of unchecked technology on our cultural output, the public is now debating the ethics of our digital existence. People want to know that if they fall victim to a digital breach, the law will provide a shield rather than a barrier to entry.

The pressure is now squarely on the DCI to demonstrate whether the 2018 Act is a functional tool for justice or merely a stagnant piece of legislation collecting dust. For now, the public remains vigilant, waiting to see if this request for clarity leads to a change in policy or if it will be buried under the next viral cycle. The mood is one of restless anticipation, as the digital generation grows tired of being part of an unregulated experiment where their private lives remain the main currency for online discourse.
