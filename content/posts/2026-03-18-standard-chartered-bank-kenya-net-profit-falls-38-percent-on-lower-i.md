## Kenyan Banking Sector Faces Revenue Headwinds Standard Chartered Bank Kenya reported a 38.0% fall in its 2025 net profit, reaching KSh 12.44 billion. This decline stemmed from reduced interest rates and the stabilization of the Kenyan shilling, which collectively compressed the bank's total operating income.

Profitability Decline Amidst Market Shifts For the year ended December 31, 2025, Standard Chartered Bank Kenya's net profit decreased by 38.0% to KSh 12.44 billion. Total operating income also saw a 16.5% drop, settling at KSh 42.30 billion. This figure aligns closely with the KSh 41.73 billion recorded in FY2023, indicating that the exceptional foreign exchange conditions of FY2024 had inflated previous results rather than establishing a new baseline for earnings.

Interest and Forex Income Compression Net interest income, the bank's primary revenue source, declined by 13.2% to KSh 28.89 billion. This was largely driven by a 25.9% decrease in interest on loans and advances, falling to KSh 16.92 billion due to Kenya's declining interest rate environment. The bank mitigated some margin compression by reducing expensive deposits and increasing government securities income, which rose 14.3% to KSh 11.15 billion. The government securities portfolio stood at KSh 96.90 billion, near the FY2024 level of KSh 98.00 billion, significantly up from KSh 63.83 billion at the end of 2023. Interest expenses decreased by 29.1% to KSh 3.94 billion. Non-interest income fell 23.0% to KSh 13.41 billion. Foreign exchange trading income experienced a substantial collapse of 58.6%, plummeting to KSh 3.42 billion from KSh 8.27 billion, as the shilling's stabilization through 2025 unwound the forex tailwind of FY2024. The bank attributed the broader non-interest income decline to lower transactional volumes and margins in Transaction Services and Markets, partially offset by growth in Wealth Solutions. Assets under management grew 29% year on year to KSh 302 billion.

Operational Costs and Asset Quality Reported operating expenses increased by 13.3% to KSh 25.47 billion. However, the bank clarified that underlying expenses grew by 4%, with the variance attributed to a one-off employee past service cost of KSh 2.6 billion. The reported cost-to-income ratio deteriorated to 60.2% from 44.3%, although stripping out the pension charge, the underlying ratio would be closer to 54%, within the bank's historical range. Loan loss provisions declined by 16.3% to KSh 1.99 billion, representing the lowest level since FY2019. The non-performing loan ratio improved by 200 basis points to 5.4%, with gross NPLs falling 26.5% to KSh 8.83 billion from a peak of KSh 23.28 billion in FY2021.

Balance Sheet Dynamics and Capital Strength Total assets contracted by 5.5% to KSh 363.49 billion, continuing a decline from the FY2023 peak of KSh 428.96 billion. Customer deposits fell 4.1% to KSh 283.45 billion, with current and savings accounts comprising 97% of the deposit base. Net loans, however, edged up 1.8% to KSh 154.31 billion, driven by growth in Corporate Finance and Wealth Solutions, which offset declines in Transaction Services, Personal Loans, and Mortgages. Cash and cash equivalents halved to KSh 35.68 billion from KSh 72.66 billion. The financing outflow of KSh 17.52 billion was largely due to the FY2024 final ordinary dividend of KSh 13.98 billion, which at KSh 36.75 per share was the largest single dividend payment in the bank's history, paid during the year. Core capital adequacy remained strong at 20.36%, well above the statutory minimum of 10.50%. The liquidity coverage ratio closed at 300% and the net stable funding ratio at 155%, both comfortably exceeding the 100% regulatory threshold.

Shareholder Returns and Leadership Transition The bank's payout ratio reached a record 95%, extending a dividend streak spanning at least 38 consecutive years. The FY2025 dividend per share (DPS) of KSh 31.00 is the second-highest in the bank's history, following FY2024. Outgoing Managing Director Kariuki Ngari will transition leadership to Birju Sanghrajka.
