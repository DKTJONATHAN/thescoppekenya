Veteran Nollywood actor Kanayo O. Kanayo has ignited a crucial industry conversation, proposing lifetime streaming royalty payments for Nigerian actors. This call for the Actors Guild of Nigeria (AGN) to implement a new policy aims to ensure performers earn from their work long after production concludes, directly addressing widespread financial struggles among beloved industry figures.

Nollywood's Royalty Reckoning Kanayo O. Kanayo, an award-winning actor and lawyer, recently put forth a proposal for the Actors Guild of Nigeria (AGN) to mandate lifetime royalty payments for all Nigerian actors and actresses featured in films uploaded to streaming platforms. He advocates for a dedicated agency to oversee and enforce compliance nationwide, asserting this would guarantee artists benefit from their craft well after cameras cease rolling. This proposition arrives amid numerous reports of financial hardship faced by seasoned Nigerian actors, highlighting a systemic issue within the industry. Just last month, veteran actor Abiodun Ayoyinka, known as Papa Ajasco, openly discussed his financial difficulties despite decades in the entertainment sector. Similar challenges faced by Nollywood actors, including broader economic conditions, have also been voiced by figures like Kunle Remi. Two years prior, Hanks Anuku also made headlines with public appeals for financial assistance following a period of personal hardship after his peak Nollywood career. Patience Ozokwor articulated the core problem bluntly: "The reason why Nollywood actors and actresses are poor is that we don't get royalties for what we do, we only get paid for our appearance at the shoot." This sentiment reflects a consistent and urgent demand from many performers.

Divided Opinions on Implementation The proposal has elicited varied responses from key industry stakeholders. Writer-director Jadesola Osiberu responded with sarcasm, suggesting that if actors desire royalties, they should also contribute to covering a producer's losses proportional to their screen time. This highlights a perceived imbalance in the demand. Producer and actress Bolaji Ogunmola offered a more direct counter-argument, stating that if actors seek backend earnings, they should negotiate equity stakes and invest in projects upfront rather than expecting guaranteed payouts after production is complete. She encouraged actors to "put your money in the film" and negotiate a reduced fee or no fee at all for a percentage of the profits.

Systemic Hurdles to Royalties The comparison often drawn to the music industry's royalty model does not fully align with Nollywood's current structure. In more established film industries globally, residuals are linked to meticulously negotiated distribution contracts and are enforced by robust unions and organizations built over decades, supported by comprehensive legal infrastructure and industry-wide consensus. However, in Nollywood, platforms such as Netflix typically provide producers with a fixed licensing fee for exclusive rights, without any contractual obligation to distribute further payments to the cast. Without this foundational structure, implementing a royalties mandate would require significant industry collaboration, including a complete renegotiation of how Nollywood finances, contracts, and distributes its content. AGN President Emeka Rollas has previously acknowledged this gap, noting that Nigerian actors lack the residuals and royalties that constitute a primary source of income for actors in more developed industries. Nollywood actors generally receive one-off payments per movie or project, lacking ongoing financial rewards for work that continues to generate income long after its initial creation. The conversation initiated by Kanayo is undoubtedly vital for the future financial stability of Nigerian actors. However, at present, the industry appears to lack both the collective will and the established framework required to fully act on such an extensive proposal.
