## Why Lower Interest Rates Are Not Fueling Corporate Growth

The Central Bank of Kenya has spent the better part of a year loosening monetary policy to encourage borrowing, yet local firms remain remarkably cold to the idea of taking on new debt. This widening gap between policy intentions and market behavior reveals a stalling economy where global pressures are silencing domestic stimulus.

### Are We Trapped in a Cost of Living and Operating Loop?

The financial strategy of Kenyan companies has shifted sharply. While the Central Bank began cutting rates in August 2024 to make credit cheaper, firms are actively avoiding bank loans. Data shows a significant drop in reliance on commercial bank funding, which plummeted from 40.1 percent to 24.1 percent in less than a year. Instead, businesses are retreating into their own reserves, hoarding cash rather than investing in new machinery or additional staff.

This hesitation is not born of a lack of ambition, but rather a calculation of risk. While 62.5 percent of businesses acknowledge that lending rates have indeed fallen, those savings are being swallowed by the rising cost of inputs. Conflict and instability across the Middle East are disrupting global supply chains, leading to a situation where 68 percent of local firms report a surge in purchase prices. Even if money is cheaper to borrow, the profit margins are currently too thin to justify the risk of expansion. Companies are effectively choosing to survive on internal cash rather than gamble on growth that the current market cannot support.

### Is the Middle East Affecting Your Job Security?

The impact of this corporate defensive posture extends far beyond the boardroom. When companies stop expanding, the first thing to stagnate is the workforce. Current sentiment data highlights this chilling effect, with corporate growth expectations for the coming year hitting a record low. Only 38.9 percent of firms surveyed believe they will grow over the next twelve months, the lowest confidence level seen in five central bank reports.

For the average Kenyan, this translates into a job market that is not creating new opportunities and a consumer sector that is slowing down. With 41.8 percent of firms reporting weaker sales in the second quarter, businesses are keeping their purse strings tight. This is a far cry from the environment seen in healthier sectors, such as the shifts we have observed with [HACO Industries and their move toward regional manufacturing under AfCFTA](https://zandani.co.ke/article/haco-industries-pivots-to-regional-manufacturing-under-afcfta). While some firms find stability by looking toward new trade blocs, the majority are currently paralyzed by the rising costs of raw materials that are imported through routes affected by global instability.

### Why Corporate Caution Leads to Stagnation

The failure of the interest rate cuts to spark a fire in the real economy is the defining policy challenge of this mid-year period. By prioritizing internal funding, companies are ensuring they stay afloat, but they are also effectively capping their own potential. When a business relies solely on its own cash, it cannot move quickly or scale up to meet potential spikes in demand. 

This environment leaves the local economy in a precarious holding pattern. If purchase prices continue to climb due to geopolitical factors, the money saved from lower interest rates will remain insufficient to offset the operational strain. Business owners are waiting for a sign that the international environment will stabilize before they commit to major projects or significant hiring drives. Until that confidence returns, the disconnect between the Central Bank's monetary easing and the reality on the ground will likely continue to stifle the national economic outlook. For now, the safest bet for most firms is to wait out the volatility, which means keeping a tight lid on spending and maintaining a very low growth profile.
