## The Uniform Trap: Why Kenya’s Police Training is Stuck in the Colonial Past

**The National Police Service is currently enforcing a disciplinary policy that is legally bankrupt and constitutionally indefensible.** By dismissing female recruits upon the discovery of pregnancy, the service is not upholding standards of discipline; it is clinging to an outdated, patriarchal framework that views motherhood as an administrative failure rather than a protected human right.

### A Constitutionally Incompatible Practice

The primary issue here is a stubborn refusal by the National Police Service to align its internal standing orders with the superior law of the land. Article 27 of the Constitution is explicit: the state shall not discriminate directly or indirectly against any person on any ground, including sex, pregnancy, or marital status. When the Inspector-General of Police permits the termination of recruits based on pregnancy, the service essentially declares itself an island where constitutional protections do not land.

This is not a matter of operational readiness or physical agility. If a recruit meets the intellectual and tactical requirements of the academy, a pregnancy does not render her suddenly incompetent. Yet, the service treats it as a breach of contract. This archaic mindset ignores the Employment Act 2007, which provides a clear roadmap for how employers should accommodate maternity. While police training is inherently rigorous, the solution is not summary dismissal. The solution is a pause, a deferment, or a transition to specialized administrative duties until the recruit is ready to resume training. We do not stop expecting our police to be moral; why do we force them to choose between a career in public service and their reproductive autonomy?

### The Failure of Administrative Modernization

We often obsess over the technological upgrades in our public sector, like the new [Google-backed urban flood warning systems](https://zandani.co.ke/article/google-launches-urban-flash-flood-warning-system-in-kenya) that aim to modernize our infrastructure. Yet, our administrative policies remain frozen in a colonial-era rigidity. The current disciplinary approach fails to mirror International Labour Organization (ILO) maternity standards, which emphasize protection against dismissal during pregnancy and the right to return to work after childbirth. 

The National Police Service is effectively operating under a policy gap. They are using internal manuals as a shield against the legal evolution of the Kenyan workforce. If the EACC can modernize its regional operations-as seen in the [recent appointment of Abdi Mohamud to lead regional anti-graft efforts](https://zandani.co.ke/article/eacc-ceo-abdi-mohamud-elected-to-lead-regional-anti-graft-body)-surely the police service can modernize its human resources management. Retaining talent is an asset, not a liability. Forcing women out of the force because of biology is a waste of state resources spent on recruitment and early training.

### Why It Matters Now

The legal environment in Kenya has shifted, and the police force is increasingly finding itself on the losing end of employment disputes. Courts have already signaled a shift toward individual rights over administrative tradition. By continuing this punitive cycle, the National Police Service invites costly litigation that the taxpayer ultimately funds. We are watching the slow-motion collision between century-old administrative dogma and a modern constitutional reality that will no longer tolerate this level of exclusion.

### What To Watch Next

The immediate test lies in whether the Inspector-General will preemptively reform administrative policy or wait for a landmark court ruling to force the change. Watch for the introduction of a specific maternity leave policy for recruits that offers a pathway for deferred graduation rather than immediate termination. If the service refuses to budge, expect parliament to be pressured into formalizing protections through a legislative amendment to the National Police Service Act, rendering current dismissal practices illegal by statute rather than just by judicial interpretation.

The uniform is meant to protect the rights of Kenyans. It is time the National Police Service proved it can extend those same protections to the women who wear it.
