By Celestine Nzioka Co-operatives and MSMEs Development Principal Secretary Kiburi Kilemi announced proposed stringent rules for SACCO directors and senior managers on March 30, 2026, during Harambee Sacco's Annual Delegates Meeting in Nairobi. The reforms aim to safeguard members' deposits and instill confidence.

Sweeping Reforms Target SACCO Leadership The government's proposed reforms are already in motion, with some awaiting parliamentary or ministerial approval. Principal Secretary Kilemi emphasized these changes are critical to addressing past instances of mismanagement and protecting members' financial interests.

Enhanced Vetting and Term Limits Under the new proposals, individuals seeking leadership positions in SACCOs will be subject to a fit-and-proper test. This assessment will scrutinize their personal history, professional conduct, and overall suitability, ensuring that those with questionable legacies in one SACCO cannot obtain roles within the same ecosystem. Additionally, term limits are being considered to cap the length of service for directors and other leaders, fostering new ideas and fresh leadership within these institutions.

Stricter Education and Professional Standards Education requirements are also part of the proposed plan, mandating a minimum O-Level certificate for individuals to qualify for a SACCO's board of directors. For senior management roles, the bar is set higher, with PS Kilemi stating that candidates must have prior experience in a commercial bank. This initiative seeks to align SACCO managers with the professional standards of the formal banking sector, which operates under stricter consumer protection regulations. Furthermore, professionals such as Certified Public Accountants (CPAs) will be required to obtain clearance from the Institute of Certified Public Accountants of Kenya (ICPAK) before assuming any role. Other individuals in regulated fields will similarly need approval from their respective professional bodies.

Redefining Cooperative Classifications Beyond leadership, the government is also drafting new legislation to refine the classification and regulation of cooperatives. PS Kilemi revealed that a new law aims to define specific types of cooperatives, including transport and housing cooperatives. This reclassification addresses existing ambiguities, such as the operation of various transport collectives under the SACCOs law. Housing cooperatives hold particular significance, with President William Ruto's affordable housing agenda actively reshaping the sector. The cabinet has tasked PS Kilemi's office with developing a framework specifically for regulating housing and investment cooperatives. These comprehensive reforms are intended to restore confidence among individuals who have previously lost funds due to poorly managed SACCOs.
