The Federation of Kenya Employers (FKE) has opposed immediate calls for employee salary increases, advocating for a structured approach to address rising living costs. This stance follows the Central Organisation of Trade Unions (COTU)'s demand on April 16, 2026, for an urgent minimum wage hike due to recent fuel price increases.

FKE Rejects Immediate Wage Hike Demands

COTU Pushes for Minimum Wage Increase COTU Secretary General Francis Atwoli, speaking on Thursday, April 16, asserted that employees, especially within the public service, must demand pay rises to cope with inflation. Atwoli conveyed expectations for the President to announce a minimum wage increase on May 1, 2026, following ongoing negotiations with the government.

Employers Warn of Economic Repercussions FKE has cautioned that implementing wage increases without a comprehensive review of the economic landscape could lead to severe consequences. Employers highlight that businesses are already struggling with escalating operational costs, primarily driven by high fuel prices, which have created a ripple effect across numerous sectors. FKE Executive Director Jacqueline Mugo stressed that mandating wage increases would likely compel businesses to transfer these additional costs to consumers, thereby exacerbating the cost-of-living crisis. "If they have to pay, we will have to see how to pass on that cost to consumers," Mugo stated, adding, "In the end, you push up the entire cost. It is not the best solution, and it is not sustainable. This is a discussion that should be done in a structured way."

Call for Balanced Dialogue and Economic Sustainability Employers are now advocating for a balanced dialogue that thoroughly considers economic sustainability, productivity levels, and the continued survival of small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), which are vital to the economy. This discussion unfolds amidst reports indicating a shift from formal employment towards informal work such as freelancing and self-employment. A recent survey revealed an approximately 12 percent drop in employment, with hiring reductions predominantly observed in manufacturing, retail, hospitality, transport, and financial services, reflecting a broader context of deepening fiscal strain and eroding purchasing power.
