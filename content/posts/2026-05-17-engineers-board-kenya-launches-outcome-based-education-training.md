## Engineering Education Reform: Beyond the Classroom

The shift toward Outcome Based Education is sparking heated debate across Kenyan campuses. While the Engineers Board of Kenya pushes for global alignment by 2029, students and lecturers are questioning if the current infrastructure can truly bridge the gap between theory and international practice.

The Engineers Board of Kenya recently rolled out intensive Outcome Based Education (OBE) training for university staff across the Rift Valley, targeting institutions like Moi University, Egerton University, and the University of Eldoret. This move is part of a broader strategy to transition engineering degrees from rote, theory-heavy models to competency-based frameworks. The ultimate goal is to secure full signatory status under the Washington Accord by 2029, building on the provisional membership Kenya achieved in June 2025. By overhauling how lecturers assess student capabilities, the Board hopes to ensure that Kenyan engineering degrees become digital passports that are recognized and respected in the global job market, potentially ending the era where local graduates are forced to undertake redundant re-training to work abroad.

### From Theory to Competency: The Student Struggle

The conversation on the ground is starkly different from the boardroom projections. For many engineering students, the dream of international mobility often hits a wall in the laboratory. Students argue that while the shift to OBE sounds impressive, the reality of broken equipment and outdated workshops remains a persistent barrier. One final-year student at a prominent Rift Valley university noted that while they are being taught to think like global engineers, they lack the basic practical tools to perform like them. Another student pointed out that getting a degree is no longer a guarantee of competence; they feel the pressure to find expensive private internships just to prove they can do the work. Meanwhile, a veteran lecturer admitted that the faculty is struggling to balance the new, rigorous assessment standards with the reality of overcrowded lecture halls where personal, competency-based mentorship remains difficult to provide.

### The Global Price Tag of a Degree

The promise of the Washington Accord is essentially financial. Industry experts suggest that achieving full signatory status could significantly elevate the entry-level salary potential for Kenyan engineers working for multinational firms. Currently, a local degree is often viewed as a foundation that requires years of local experience before it is deemed equivalent to international benchmarks. Under an OBE model, the goal is to make the undergraduate qualification a direct equivalent to those in Europe or North America. By focusing on measurable outcomes rather than hours spent in a classroom, the Board hopes to push starting salaries higher, as employers will have more confidence in the immediate utility of fresh graduates. However, critics warn that until university labs receive the same attention as policy documents, the salary bump might only apply to the few who can access elite training facilities, widening the gap between top-tier institutions and the rest.

### Why It Matters Now

The move toward global standards comes at a time when Kenya is grappling with significant labor shifts, similar to how other sectors like health are addressing the impact of [recent salary disputes and CBA arrears for doctors](https://zandani.co.ke/article/doctors-receive-delayed-src-salary-increments-cba-arrears-budget-allocation). As engineering graduates look toward the global market, the value of their degree depends entirely on their ability to demonstrate practical skill. If the education system fails to evolve beyond paper-based credentials, Kenya risks producing a surplus of graduates who hold degrees but lack the specific competencies required by international projects. This reform is the government's attempt to keep local talent relevant in a competitive, mobile global economy.

### What to Watch Next

The coming months will reveal whether the Board can move beyond regional workshops and into tangible investment in technical infrastructure. Watch for the 2026/2027 university budget allocations to see if the government mirrors its policy changes with the funding required to modernize engineering labs. Additionally, observe the performance of the first cohorts to undergo the OBE-aligned curriculum as they enter the workforce. If these graduates struggle to secure roles with multinational firms, the 2029 target for Washington Accord membership may face significant scrutiny from a public that is increasingly wary of administrative promises that are not backed by physical improvements on the ground.

The mood among Kenyan students is one of cautious skepticism, waiting to see if these new standards will actually open doors or simply add more hurdles to an already difficult degree.
