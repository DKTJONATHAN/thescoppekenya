## The State Monopoly Racket Kenya and Rwanda Sign Government Fuel Supply Agreement is the central subject of the update.

The official narrative paints the new Kenya-Rwanda fuel pact as a triumph of logistical harmony. Behind the diplomatic handshakes, however, sits a calculated shift that effectively terminates market competition and shifts the burden of multi-billion shilling credit risks squarely onto the Kenyan public.

### Trading Competition for Bureaucracy

Cabinet Secretary Opiyo Wandayi and Rwanda’s trade representative Antoine-Marie Kajangwe have finalized a transition from commercial procurement to a government-to-government framework. Under this new regime, the 1.13 billion litres of fuel moving annually through the 1,342-kilometre pipeline corridor are no longer subject to the agile, price-sensitive negotiations of private independent fuel retailers. Instead, the process is now locked into bilateral agreements that prioritize political stability over retail margins.

Independent logistics operators who have spent years navigating the Mombasa-Kampala route report a creeping sense of displacement. These firms, which once competed to secure the most efficient transport and storage solutions, now find themselves sidelined by state-mandated logistics. When a government office manages procurement, the drive for efficiency dies. The retail margins currently seen in Kenya versus the heavily regulated, state-controlled pricing in Rwanda offer a preview of a market where consumer choice is replaced by fixed bureaucratic pricing. This transition mirrors the bureaucratic inertia seen elsewhere, such as the [NTSA's recent administrative stumbles with smart driving licences](https://zandani.co.ke/article/ntsa-begins-sending-notifications-processed-smart-driving-licences), where centralised control consistently fails to deliver on promises of speed and accessibility.

### The Hidden Taxpayer Bailout

The most contentious element of this deal is the extension of storage periods for Rwandan fuel cargoes from 35 days to 90 days within the Kenya Pipeline Company system. In a functioning market, storage costs are a private risk managed by banks and importers. By extending these terms, the Kenyan taxpayer effectively becomes the guarantor for Rwandan fuel stock. If global oil prices collapse or credit lines freeze, the liability for these extended storage windows rests with the Kenyan state.

This is not merely an infrastructure upgrade; it is a transfer of risk. While the government claims this will stabilize regional prices, it essentially uses Kenyan infrastructure as a buffer for Rwandan reserves without a clear compensation mechanism for the associated capital lock-up. Furthermore, Rwanda’s acquisition of a 20 percent stake in the Kenya Pipeline Company, formalized following the March IPO, complicates the balance sheet. When state actors own both the infrastructure and the procurement process, the incentive for infrastructure maintenance diminishes. The pressure to prioritize dividend distribution to minority state shareholders, like Kigali, will likely override the necessity for pipeline safety upgrades or repairs, shifting the cost of future failures back to the Kenyan consumer through higher tariffs.

### Who Owns the Flow?

The transition to a state-backed monopoly suggests that regional integration is being used as a shroud for rent-seeking activities. By consolidating the supply chain, the political elites in Nairobi and Kigali secure control over the most lucrative sector of the East African economy. Independent players who lack the political capital to influence state-level deals are being systematically pushed toward bankruptcy.

These developments echo a wider trend of state overreach where commercial disputes are increasingly handled via closed-door diplomatic deals rather than open market competition. While state officials toast to regional energy cooperation, the reality for the average motorist is a rigid, non-competitive market where prices are set by executive order rather than supply and demand. If the government is truly interested in lowering costs, why must the transition involve such a heavy hand of state intervention and the erasure of private sector competition? When the state becomes the merchant, the regulator, and the warehouse manager, who is left to watch the ledger?
