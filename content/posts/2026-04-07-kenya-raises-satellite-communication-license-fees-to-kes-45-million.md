## Kenya's Satellite Communication Licensing Overhaul Kenya's Communications Authority has revised its telecommunications market structure, significantly increasing satellite communication license fees for operators like Starlink. The new International Gateway Systems and Services license, costing up to KES 45 million for 25 years, replaces cheaper options, alongside an annual 0.4% gross turnover fee, impacting market dynamics.

New Licensing Framework Details The Communications Authority of Kenya's Revised Telecommunications Market Structure for 2026 introduces substantial changes to satellite communication licensing. The previous Satellite Landing Rights license, priced at $12,500 (approximately KES 1.6 million), has been replaced by more expensive categories. Companies now require an International Gateway Systems and Services (IGSS) license, which costs KES 15 million for a 15-year term or KES 45 million for a 25-year term. Additionally, an annual operating fee of 0.4% of gross turnover, with a minimum of KES 4 million, is mandated. A separate Landing Rights Authorization license, priced at $25,000 with a $500 application fee, is also introduced for entities transmitting signals into Kenya via satellite or submarine cables. Depending on the services offered, a company might need both licenses.

Starlink's Direct-to-Cell Expansion and Market Impact Starlink, operational in Kenya since 2023 and holding 0.8% of the local internet market as the ninth-largest ISP, is directly affected by these changes. The IGSS license is crucial for Starlink to launch its direct-to-cell service in Kenya, part of a 14-market Africa deal with Airtel. This service will initially offer internet-based texts and calls, with voice calls and SMS projected for 2028. The increased regulatory costs will influence its expansion strategy and competitive positioning against dominant players like Safaricom, especially as it seeks to scale its innovative offerings across the region.

Regulatory Evolution and Industry Shifts The regulatory adjustments stem from proposals initiated in December 2024, following Starlink's entry which disrupted Kenya's broadband market. Initially, Safaricom petitioned the CA to revoke Starlink's license due to concerns about illegal connections and network interference. However, Safaricom has since shifted its stance, now exploring collaboration with Starlink's network to extend its reach into remote areas. These policy reforms also extend to satellite broadcasters such as MultiChoice and regional mobile operators utilizing satellite infrastructure, indicating a comprehensive recalibration of the market. This regulatory overhaul echoes the need for adapting to evolving technological landscapes, much like the ongoing initiatives to modernize infrastructure access, as seen with Kenya Power's digital meter reading system.
