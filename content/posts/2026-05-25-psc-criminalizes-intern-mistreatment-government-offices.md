## PSC Declares Mistreatment of Government Interns a Criminal Offense

The Public Service Commission has officially classified the mistreatment of government interns as a criminal offense during a leadership summit in Naivasha, a move that strips supervisors of administrative immunity and exposes them to prosecution for workplace harassment. This directive signals a fundamental shift in how the state handles the welfare of young professionals, moving away from internal disciplinary memos toward active law enforcement intervention.

The Commission issued this stern warning on Monday, May 25, 2026, during a capacity building workshop for mentors and coaches involved in the Public Service Internship Programme (PSIP). PSC Chairperson Francis Meja told delegates that the era of treating intern abuse as a minor human resource grievance has ended. He emphasized that the 44,000 graduates currently deployed across various state departments are now protected by the same legal standards as permanent employees, with an added layer of criminal liability for those in positions of power.

This policy change follows a significant legal ruling involving the Teachers Service Commission (TSC), which established that government bodies and their individual supervisors hold a criminal duty of care toward junior staff. The PSC is now aligning its internal protocols with this precedent to ensure that physical, emotional, and sexual abuse results in immediate police reports rather than just internal transfers or warnings. This legal tightening mirrors recent government efforts to enforce accountability across all sectors, including the strict monitoring of public resources seen in the [2026-04-25-epra-recovers-800-litres-of-fuel-from-illegal-siphoning-site-in-nair](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-25-epra-recovers-800-litres-of-fuel-from-illegal-siphoning-sit) operations.

The Commission cited alarming internal data to justify the crackdown, revealing that 42 per cent of interns have reported various forms of harassment during their one-year placements. Additional statistics shared during the workshop indicated that 57 per cent of respondents in a recent survey highlighted verbal abuse as a primary concern, while 51 per cent pointed to the denial of professional duties or being assigned menial tasks unrelated to their training. Furthermore, 40 per cent reported emotional bullying, 21 per cent faced intimidation regarding their future employment prospects, and 10 per cent reported instances of sexual harassment.

### Ending the Culture of Administrative Immunity

PSC Secretary Paul Famba urged mentors and coaches to prioritize the career growth and safety of the youth under their care, noting that the internship program is a bridge to professional life, not a site for exploitation. He noted that the commission would no longer tolerate supervisors who use their seniority to coerce or mistreat interns, especially given the current economic pressures facing young graduates. These pressures have been compounded by broader fiscal policies, such as the adjustments discussed in the [2026-05-01-finance-bill-2026-car-tax](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-05-01-finance-bill-2026-car-tax) proposal, which make stable and safe employment even more critical for the youth.

The new directive places the burden of proof on the institutions to show they have created a safe working environment. If an intern reports abuse, the supervisor involved faces immediate suspension to allow for a criminal investigation. The PSC has clarified that "mistreatment" includes the withholding of stipends, the demand for personal favors in exchange for positive appraisals, and any form of physical aggression. By framing these actions as crimes, the PSC effectively flips the power dynamic, giving the 44,000 vulnerable interns a legal shield they previously lacked.

### Why it matters now

The criminalization of intern mistreatment represents a landmark redistribution of power within the Kenyan civil service. For decades, interns occupied a legal gray area where they were often viewed as transient help without full labor rights. By introducing criminal liability, the PSC is removing the "administrative shield" that rogue bosses used to hide behind, ensuring that workplace harassment is treated with the same severity as any other felony. This protects the integrity of the PSIP and ensures that the next generation of civil servants is not disillusioned by a culture of impunity.

### What to watch next

The focus now moves to the first set of prosecutions under these new guidelines to see how the Directorate of Criminal Investigations and the Judiciary handle cases referred by the PSC. Observers will also be looking for a potential update to the Public Service Commission Act to formally embed these criminal reporting requirements into permanent law. Impacted interns are expected to begin utilizing the new anonymous reporting channels established at the Naivasha summit to flag non-compliant supervisors.
