## Kenyan Economic Pressures Intensify on Fuel Hike

April 2026 witnessed a pronounced increase in Kenyan household expenditure, primarily driven by a significant surge in fuel prices. This directly translated into elevated transport fares across urban and inter-town routes, further fueling broader inflationary pressures on essential goods and services. The sustained impact on consumer purchasing power reflects ongoing economic adjustments.

### Transport Sector Repricing

The transport sector quickly adjusted to rising input costs, passing the burden to commuters. Petrol prices reached KSh 197.60 per litre, with diesel, vital for commercial transport, surging to KSh 196.63. Within Nairobi, peak-hour fares from Karen to the central business district increased to approximately KSh 120 from KSh 100. Long-distance travel also saw notable increments; the Nairobi-Kisumu route now averages KSh 1,900 per trip, up from KSh 1,750 in March. Short-distance mobility providers like boda bodas in towns such as Narok raised fares to KSh 120 from KSh 100. Overall, city fares climbed by 7.1 percent, inter-town travel by 9.7 percent, and motorcycle transport costs by over six percent, indicating a systemic response to higher fuel expenses.

### Mixed Food Basket Inflation

Household food expenses presented a varied picture, yet remained burdensome. Between March and April 2026, selected food items showed mixed price movements. Cabbages and oranges saw price declines of 3.3 percent and 1.9 percent, respectively. However, prices for Irish potatoes and salad cooking oil increased by 2.8 percent and 2.7 percent, respectively. Tomatoes experienced a significant price surge of 9.0 percent. Over the twelve months ending April 2026, the Food and Non-Alcoholic Beverages division index rose by 8.8 percent, as reported by the Kenya National Bureau of Statistics.

### Household Energy Bill Shifts

While electricity costs offered marginal relief, other energy expenses escalated. The average monthly electricity bill slightly eased to KSh 1,288.99 for 50 kWh and KSh 5,656.88 for 200 kWh. This minor reduction was overshadowed by a substantial increase in cooking gas prices, with a 13-kilogramme cylinder jumping to KSh 3,361.56 within the month.
