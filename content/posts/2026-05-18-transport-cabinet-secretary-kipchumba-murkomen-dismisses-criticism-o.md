Transport Cabinet Secretary Kipchumba Murkomen is currently facing widespread public disapproval regarding the state of the national transport sector and frequent power instability. Amid this mounting pressure, he has publicly attributed the ongoing criticism of his performance to ethnic bias against his community.

## Murkomen Faces Backlash Over Sector Mismanagement

### The Tribal Shield Strategy
When faced with intense scrutiny regarding his administrative performance, Cabinet Secretary Murkomen has opted to characterize the growing public frustration as a targeted attack on his Kalenjin heritage. By framing demands for accountability as a form of ethnic prejudice, the minister seeks to deflect attention from specific operational failures within his docket. This defensive posture suggests that criticism of the national road network and power grid management is motivated by regional bias rather than genuine concerns over service delivery. For many taxpayers, this narrative shift is viewed as a strategic distraction from the urgent economic realities affecting their daily lives.

### Examining the Hypocrisy of Accountability
Political observers have pointed to a significant contrast between Murkomen’s current rhetoric and his previous conduct as an opposition figure. During the administration of former President Uhuru Kenyatta, Murkomen was a vocal critic of the government, frequently challenging the leadership on fuel price hikes and economic mismanagement. At that time, his demands for accountability were presented as essential oversight. The fact that he now labels similar critiques of his own performance as politically or ethnically motivated has sparked accusations of hypocrisy, as he once utilized the same sharp rhetoric that he now condemns when directed at him.

### The Impact on Public Service Delivery
The focus on ethnic narratives has done little to alleviate the practical burdens facing the average citizen. As the ministry continues to grapple with its unpopularity, the transport sector remains plagued by infrastructure gaps, while the broader population struggles with the rising cost of living and persistent power outages. The public discourse has effectively stalled on political maneuvering rather than addressing the functional decline of critical services. Ultimately, the shift toward identity politics serves as a barrier to transparent governance, leaving the public to contend with an environment where administrative results are consistently sidelined in favor of defensive political theater.
