## The Panopticon Classroom: Is CCTV the Silver Bullet for School Security?

Following the tragic loss of 16 lives and 79 injuries at Utumishi Girls Service High School, the government is shifting its focus toward mass surveillance. CCTV footage showing students igniting dorm fires has prompted a national policy mandate that fundamentally changes the Kenyan boarding school experience.

The Interior Cabinet Secretary issued a directive on May 31 requiring all boarding institutions to install high-definition security cameras immediately. While the intent is to deter criminal activity, this response highlights a growing friction between state-mandated security measures and the pastoral responsibility of our education system. As the nation grapples with the fallout of the Utumishi incident, experts are questioning whether cameras can address the complex social and psychological pressures students face in rural and urban boarding schools alike.

### Why is the government choosing surveillance over other interventions?

The primary driver for this policy is the undeniable visibility provided by the recordings from the Marylyn Waithera dormitory. By identifying the eight students allegedly involved within minutes, the footage provided authorities with a clear path to action. However, relying on surveillance as a primary security feature is a reactive strategy. It functions as a deterrent that records history but does not necessarily prevent the underlying tensions that lead to school unrest.

There is also the significant issue of cost and technical feasibility. Installing robust, networked surveillance systems in schools situated in remote, grid-challenged areas requires more than just hardware. It requires consistent electricity and secure data storage. Just as [Kenya prioritizes grid modernization for renewable energy transition](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenya-prioritizes-grid-modernization-for-renewable-energy), the school security infrastructure must also be supported by a stable utility base. Without a reliable power supply, these expensive systems become nothing more than plastic decorations, offering a false sense of safety to parents and teachers.

### Can technology replace student-teacher mentorship?

Many child psychologists argue that a surveillance-first approach risks formalizing the relationship between students and staff into one of suspicion rather than support. In the wake of this disaster, the focus is shifting toward identifying the students responsible, yet there is less discussion regarding the internal cultures that allowed such deep-seated resentment to form in the first place.

When schools prioritize cameras over counselors, they miss the opportunity to foster the dialogue necessary to identify students who are struggling with mental health or academic pressures. CCTV can monitor movement, but it cannot read the signs of distress or the buildup of student conflict. If schools are treated as controlled environments managed by digital oversight, the personal rapport that teachers once used to de-escalate crises may eventually erode.

### What are the long-term implications for our schools?

The mandate effectively changes the definition of a school from a learning sanctuary to a secure facility under constant observation. For parents, this might offer temporary peace of mind, yet the institutionalization of classrooms brings new concerns regarding privacy and the psychological impact of being watched at all hours.

If the government focuses solely on the technical implementation of surveillance, it risks ignoring the root causes of student unrest, which often stem from institutional pressures, inadequate facilities, or systemic disciplinary failures. Implementing cameras is a straightforward administrative task, but improving school culture and student-teacher interaction remains a slow, complex process. The coming months will determine if this pivot to a digital eye will truly secure our institutions or merely mask deeper structural issues that technology is ill-equipped to solve.

True security in our boarding schools comes from fostering community connection, not just watching it through a lens.
