By Celestine Nzioka The Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA) has detailed the comprehensive process governing fuel price calculations, providing clarity amidst public discussions on petrol, diesel, and kerosene costs. The methodology integrates global crude oil expenses, shipping logistics, internal transport, government taxation, and regulated marketer margins to determine prices at the pump.

The Fuel Pricing Process Explained

Global Dynamics and Landed Cost The country primarily imports refined petroleum products, with the base price, termed the landed cost, heavily influenced by global crude oil and refined product prices, alongside shipping, insurance, and other logistical expenditures. The continuous fluctuation of the Kenyan shilling against the US dollar also directly impacts the local cost of imports, as oil transactions are denominated in US dollars. For the period March 15 to April 14, 2026, EPRA's gazetted prices included a landed cost of Ksh75.42 per litre for petrol, Ksh82.30 for diesel, and Ksh82.63 for kerosene.

Import Pathways: OTS and G2G The country historically acquired refined petroleum products through the Open Tender System (OTS), where oil marketing companies competed for import consignments. In recent years, the government has also established government-to-government (G2G) supply agreements with major Gulf suppliers, including Saudi Aramco and ADNOC. These G2G deals offer fuel on extended credit terms, which helps alleviate pressure on foreign exchange reserves and ensures a consistent supply. The Ministry of Petroleum coordinates these G2G deliveries and their distribution, integrating them into the existing regulated import process alongside the traditional OTS system.

Domestic Logistics and Distribution Expenses Upon arrival in the country, typically by sea, fuel is transported to various storage depots before distribution to retail stations nationwide. Essential costs such as pipeline transport, trucking, and storage are incorporated into the pricing formula, ensuring sustainable operations for oil marketers and consistent fuel delivery across the country. The regulator's review for the period of March 14 to April 14, 2026, indicated that the pipeline transportation cost from Mombasa to Nairobi was Ksh2.79 for petrol, diesel, and kerosene. Additionally, distribution and storage costs amounted to Ksh4.67 for petrol, Ksh4.40 for diesel, and Ksh4.36 for kerosene.

Government Levies and Marketer Margins Taxes and levies are significant determinants of the final pump price. These encompass excise duty, a 16 percent Value Added Tax (VAT), the petroleum development levy, the railway development levy, and various other statutory fees. Pricing fully incorporates the 16 percent VAT, in accordance with the Finance Act 2023, the Tax Laws (Amendment) Act 2024, and periodically adjusted excise duty rates to account for inflation. Oil companies are also allocated regulated margins to cover operational expenditures and generate profit. In its last review, EPRA set these limits at Ksh17.39 for petrol, Ksh17.31 for diesel, and Ksh17.24 for kerosene.

Stabilisation Measures and Market Realities The government occasionally implements stabilisation or intervention measures to prevent excessive price surges. However, these measures are limited and costly, necessitating their sparing application. Even when international oil prices decrease, local pump prices may remain elevated due to the cumulative impact of taxes, levies, domestic logistics, and exchange rate fluctuations.
