## Constitutional Deadlock Hits Laikipia Health Project

The Parliamentary Health Committee has summoned Health Cabinet Secretary Aden Duale to justify the legal framework behind a U.S.-funded Ebola quarantine center in Laikipia following a High Court order that halted construction activities on Friday.

The Milimani Law Courts issued conservatory orders today suspending the establishment of the facility, which was intended to provide a specialized response center for potential viral outbreaks. This judicial intervention stems from a constitutional petition filed by the Katiba Institute, which argues that the Ministry of Health bypassed mandatory civic engagement protocols. The project, initially designed to host up to 250 beds, has become a focal point for debates regarding the extent of ministerial authority when entering into bilateral health agreements with foreign entities.

At the heart of the legal dispute is the intersection between international donor obligations and the constitutional requirement for public participation. Legal experts argue that the government failed to integrate the project into existing national health policies, which generally mandate local consultation for infrastructure of this magnitude. By treating the agreement as an executive function rather than a public health mandate subject to legislative oversight, the Ministry triggered a procedural vacuum that left residents in the dark about the facility's long-term environmental and safety implications.

The friction highlights a significant policy gap in how the government balances foreign aid with territorial sovereignty. While the United States has pledged Ksh1.74 billion to bolster Kenya's Ebola preparedness, critics contend that the lack of transparency in the site selection process undermines the state's duty to protect its citizens. This incident mirrors recent public frustrations over infrastructure projects, such as the [Angry Residents Block Kisumu-Busia Highway, Demand Immediate Arrests Over Osotsi Attack](https://zandani.co.ke/article/angry-residents-block-kisumu-busia-highway-demand-immediate-arrests-), where failures in communication and local inclusion fueled civil unrest.

### Legal Challenges to Bilateral Agreements

The Katiba Institute petition asserts that the Ministry of Health violated Article 10 of the Constitution, which establishes public participation as a foundational national value. Counsel for the petitioners argued before the court that bilateral health deals cannot supersede local administrative laws or the right to information. This challenge marks a departure from traditional protests, as it seeks to set a judicial precedent for how foreign-funded health initiatives must align with Kenyan constitutional thresholds before breaking ground.

Government officials, led by the Ministry, have maintained that the facility is a critical component of national security and regional health preparedness. The Ministry holds that the urgent nature of managing potential hemorrhagic fever outbreaks necessitates a streamlined approach to project implementation. However, this defensive posture has failed to pacify lawmakers, who are now demanding a full disclosure of the Memorandum of Understanding signed between Kenya and American representatives.

### Oversight and Legislative Scrutiny

Parliamentary investigators are expected to focus their inquiry on the criteria used to select the Laikipia site and why local stakeholders were omitted from the planning phases. The summons issued to CS Duale requires him to present the specific policy frameworks that guided the site selection process. Lawmakers appear intent on determining whether the Ministry bypassed the Public Health Act and other sectoral regulations to satisfy international financing timelines.

The legislative branch is signaling a desire to reassert its role in monitoring executive agreements. By escalating the matter to a committee hearing, Parliament is moving to ensure that future international aid packages do not result in further legal challenges or local resistance. This push for accountability underscores a growing trend in the National Assembly to scrutinize ministerial actions that carry significant legal or territorial implications for the country.

The court has scheduled a hearing for the petition on June 15, which will provide a clearer picture of whether the government can proceed with the construction or must restart the planning process to incorporate constitutional requirements. Meanwhile, the Parliamentary Health Committee will continue its review of the ministry's internal procedures to see if any departmental guidelines were disregarded in the rush to secure the U.S. health grant.
