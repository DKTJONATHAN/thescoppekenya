## Kenyan Agritech Secures Critical Financing

SokoFresh, a Kenyan agritech enterprise, has successfully secured KSh 65 million ($500,000) in concessional debt financing. This capital aims to significantly reduce post-harvest losses in Kenya’s agricultural sector, reflecting a strategic pivot by development institutions towards blended finance models for supporting vulnerable agricultural markets.

### Tackling Post-Harvest Losses

SokoFresh’s financing directly addresses the critical issue of post-harvest losses across Sub-Saharan Africa. The region faces structurally high agricultural losses due to insufficient cold-chain infrastructure, underdeveloped aggregation systems, and pronounced price volatility. Kenya specifically experiences up to 40% loss of its harvests, primarily affecting staple crops like maize and potatoes, alongside high-value tropical fruits such as mangoes, avocados, and bananas, largely attributed to poor transit and storage issues. SokoFresh CEO Denis Karema emphasized the financing enables inclusive market access, prompt payments, and fair pricing for smallholder farmers, strengthening food systems.

### Evolution in Development Finance

The investment by UNCDF underscores a broader transformation within development finance, moving away from traditional grant-based support towards catalytic lending. This blended finance approach employs concessional terms designed to mitigate early-stage risks in markets where commercial lenders typically exhibit reluctance to extend credit at scale. This strategic shift is particularly relevant as global aid flows tighten, compelling development agencies to innovate with financial instruments capable of attracting private capital into sectors historically reliant on donor funding.

### Performance-Linked Financing

A notable aspect of SokoFresh’s financing package is the conditionality of repayment, which is directly tied to specific operational milestones. These include targets for expanding farmer reach and achieving efficiency in cold storage utilization. Such performance-linked structures are increasingly prevalent in blended finance transactions. They serve to align capital costs with impact delivery, although they concurrently introduce execution risk for recipients operating within the narrow margins characteristic of agricultural environments.
