## Beyond the Parade Ground: A Call for Hard Medical Truths

The tragic passing of Hassan Juma Moyo at Wajir Stadium has stripped away the veneer of ceremonial pageantry to reveal a stark, ignored reality. Kenyans are no longer whispering about the visible fatigue of their security officers; they are demanding a total overhaul of the occupational health policies governing those tasked with protecting the nation.

During the Madaraka Day celebrations on June 1, Hassan Juma Moyo collapsed while on duty at the parade. Despite the proximity of medical teams and his swift transfer to the Wajir County Referral Hospital, he was pronounced dead shortly after arrival, with medical reports confirming a myocardial infarction. This event follows a troubling pattern of health emergencies among security personnel, casting a harsh spotlight on the physical and mental pressures faced by officers working in high-stress, arid environments like Wajir. ### Voices from the Ground

The public response is centered on the disconnect between the demands placed on officers and the support they receive. One veteran security analyst noted that the current Kenya Police Service medical policy is far too reactive. He argued that waiting for a collapse to occur during a public event is a failure of leadership, suggesting that mandatory, rigorous health screenings should be Za Ndani for any officer stationed in the harsh climate of Northern Kenya, where extreme temperatures and long hours create a perfect storm for cardiovascular strain.

A family member of a serving officer expressed the anxiety shared by many, noting that they often wonder if their loved ones are being worked into the ground. She pointed out that when officers are deployed to areas with limited infrastructure, the lack of immediate, specialized medical support is a systemic vulnerability. She believes the government cannot continue to celebrate these officers as heroes during national holidays while treating their physical well-being as an afterthought during the rest of the year.

Meanwhile, a public health advocate emphasized that the issue is not just about isolated heart attacks but about the broader occupational health standards for officers in northern Kenya. He stressed that stress and environmental factors are not merely incidental but are occupational hazards that require dedicated policy interventions. He argued that the current approach is failing because it treats health as an individual responsibility rather than a structural requirement for the police force to remain functional and effective.

### Addressing the Policy Vacuum

The conversation has shifted toward the necessity of preventative medicine. There is a growing demand for the National Police Service to move beyond standard baseline assessments and implement continuous monitoring for officers operating in high-risk zones. Many are asking why the medical protocols in place since 2021 have not been sufficiently updated to account for the increasing frequency of these public health emergencies. The expectation is that the upcoming policy reviews will finally prioritize comprehensive cardiac screening and mandatory psychological health assessments.

Without a change in how the state manages the fitness of its frontline personnel, the risk of similar incidents during future state functions remains high. The public sentiment is clear: they are tired of seeing those who serve the country fall during the very moments they are supposed to be honored. The pressure is now on the administration to bridge the gap between rhetoric and the actual, daily survival of the men and women in uniform.

The mood remains somber across the country, marked by a growing impatience with institutional excuses and a collective insistence that the uniform should be a badge of support, not a weight that pushes officers to the breaking point.
