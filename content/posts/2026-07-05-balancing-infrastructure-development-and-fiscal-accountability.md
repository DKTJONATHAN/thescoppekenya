## Beyond Concrete: A Strategy for Sustainable Infrastructure Balancing Infrastructure Development and Fiscal Accountability in is the central subject of the update.

Last Friday’s gridlock on the Nairobi-Nakuru highway served as a grueling reminder that Kenya’s economic heartbeat is currently suffering from arterial blockage. While construction on the Mau Summit dual carriageway and Za Ndani Gauge Railway extension moves forward, the focus must shift from mere speed to the long-term fiscal viability of these expansive national projects.

To the Cabinet Secretary for Roads and Transport:

The Nairobi-Nakuru corridor functions as the primary artery for Western Kenya, moving people and goods that sustain our national output. When this route fails, the ripple effects touch every corner of our economy, from the delivery of fresh produce to the efficiency of logistics hubs supported by the KSh 10 billion recent influx from entities like Glovo. However, pouring concrete into the ground is not the same as building a functional transport system. We have seen too many projects turn into neglected white elephants because the excitement of a ribbon-cutting ceremony never factored in the quiet, unglamorous necessity of life-cycle maintenance.

### The Maintenance Deficit

Kenya has a historical habit of ignoring the depreciating value of its infrastructure. We build with borrowed billions, yet we fail to budget for the repair crews, road-side safety features, and structural integrity checks that ensure these assets serve us for decades rather than years. When infrastructure is viewed solely through the lens of construction completion, we invite early decay. 

If the current expansion of the Mau Summit route follows the path of past projects, it will eventually crumble under the weight of heavy traffic and seasonal rains. This necessitates another round of expensive, debt-funded rehabilitation. True institutional maturity lies in ensuring that every shilling spent today accounts for the maintenance requirements of the next fifteen years. We cannot afford to build our way into a debt trap, only to find the pavement failing before the loans are halfway serviced. The cost of neglect is always higher than the cost of consistent, proactive upkeep.

### Transparency in Procurement

The current lack of clarity surrounding procurement milestones creates an environment where inefficiency hides in plain sight. Citizens often watch these large-scale projects progress in a fog of uncertainty, unaware of the timelines, budgets, or specific obligations of the contractors involved. A move toward a centralized, public-facing digital dashboard would change this dynamic entirely. 

By providing real-time data on procurement phases, payment schedules, and projected completion targets, the Ministry would offer the public a genuine stake in the process. This level of oversight does more than prevent the misappropriation of funds; it builds the public trust required to support the tax burdens often associated with such massive undertakings. When the public can see exactly where the money goes, the conversation shifts from suspicion to collaboration. Accountability should be the default setting, not an optional feature of public administration.

### Debt and the Fiscal Horizon

Infrastructure is an investment, but only if it generates a return that exceeds the cost of financing. Kenya stands at a crossroads where our debt-servicing obligations are already competing with basic service delivery. As recent debates surrounding State House renovation costs demonstrate, the sensitivity surrounding government spending has never been higher. Any new infrastructure initiative must be scrutinized not just for its physical impact on traffic flow, but for its impact on our sovereign credit profile. 

We must ask whether these specific corridors are being optimized for maximum economic utility or if they are simply placeholders for political legacy. I urge the Ministry to move beyond the aesthetic satisfaction of finished roads. Commit to a transparent, publicly accessible tracking system for all major projects. Align your expansion plans with a rigorous, ring-fenced maintenance fund that prioritizes durability over speed. Ensure that the economic future of Western Kenya is not mortgaged for projects that lack a clear, sustainable fiscal strategy. Please, show us the blueprint for the next twenty years, not just the next twenty kilometers.
