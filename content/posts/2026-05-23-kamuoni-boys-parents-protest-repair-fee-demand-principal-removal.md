## Parents Demand Audit of Kamuoni Boys High School Property Damages

Guardians of students at Kamuoni Boys High School converged at the school gates this Saturday to protest a mandatory Ksh20,000 restoration fee, questioning the transparency behind a Ksh23 million damage claim linked to the May 12 campus riots.

The school, situated in Makueni County, has remained closed indefinitely following the student unrest that led to extensive property destruction. In response, the school administration issued a directive requiring every parent to pay a levy of Ksh20,000 to facilitate repairs. This financial requirement has triggered a backlash from parents who argue that the total assessment of Ksh23 million is not supported by the visible state of the facilities.

Parents are now demanding an independent audit of the damages before any payments are processed. They claim the school board failed to provide a detailed inventory or itemized valuation of the affected property. Many parents suspect that the Ksh23 million figure is a significant inflation of actual costs, leading to widespread calls for the immediate removal of the principal.

The unrest at Kamuoni Boys occurs amid a broader climate of institutional volatility in the region. Local leaders have recently raised concerns over the frequency of student strikes in Makueni County, warning that the lack of open communication between school management and stakeholders is deepening the crisis. Recent administrative shifts in national education policy have placed a renewed focus on school governance, much like the recent oversight measures applied by the Ministry of Education as seen in the appointment of committees to monitor national institutions.

### Questioning the Ksh23 Million Assessment

The primary point of contention remains the mathematical justification for the damage claim. A simple calculation indicates that if every student pays the Ksh20,000 levy, the collected amount would surpass the stated repair costs depending on the student population. Critics argue that this creates a financial burden that many families cannot sustain, especially without documented evidence of the damages.

Representatives of the parents gathered at the school entrance to present a list of grievances, specifically requesting a transparent accounting of the loss. They have argued that the school administration is attempting to pass the costs of general maintenance and potential systemic mismanagement onto the parents under the guise of repair fees.

### Demands for Leadership Accountability

Beyond the financial dispute, the parents are calling for a change in leadership. They contend that the principal has failed to maintain a stable environment, pointing to the May 12 incident as proof of administrative neglect. The crowd asserted that the school cannot return to normalcy under the current management.

"We are not willing to pay for figures pulled from thin air," said one parent during the protest. The group insists that an external, independent team must verify the property destruction. They are demanding that the Ministry of Education intervene to ensure that school funds are managed with the required level of transparency and accountability.

### Why It Matters Now

The situation at Kamuoni Boys highlights the growing tension between school administrations and parents regarding the financial responsibility for student unrest. As school fees continue to rise, the burden of paying for damages caused by student behavior is becoming a major point of contention. This case serves as a barometer for how schools account for public funds and whether boards of management can survive the scrutiny of parents who are increasingly vocal about the lack of administrative transparency. 

### What to Watch Next

The Ministry of Education is expected to address the mounting grievances in Makueni County as public pressure grows. Observers should monitor whether the regional education office mandates an independent property audit or if the principal remains in office despite the protests. Any decision from the education authorities will set a precedent for how other institutions handle similar damage claims during periods of unrest.
