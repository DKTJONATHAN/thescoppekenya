## Administrative Reform and the Ghost of 1937

On May 23, 1937, global administrators were obsessed with the efficiency of the bureaucratic machine, much like the modern obsession with fiscal rationalization. History reveals that when states layer new agencies over old ones, they rarely fix the original rot; they simply build a taller, more expensive tower of paper.

### The Echoes of Institutional Overlap

Parliament is currently occupied with a debate regarding the operational redundancies between the National Cohesion and Integration Commission and the Directorate of Cohesion and Values. Legislators are questioning why the taxpayer must fund two separate vehicles performing the same task of national reconciliation. This tension is not a byproduct of modern Kenyan politics but a persistent ghost in the halls of governance. In 1937, reform-minded thinkers, including those influenced by the managerial philosophies of men like John D. Rockefeller, argued that the proliferation of committees within the colonial apparatus was a symptom of avoidance. If a problem is too difficult to solve, the state creates a commission to study it. When that commission fails, the state creates a second, more focused office to manage the first. 

We see this same pattern in our current debate. The Directorate and the Commission share mandates that often result in circular reporting and delayed outcomes. The state is essentially funding two offices to chase the same fires that Cabinet Secretary Murkomen recently noted require urgent intervention, specifically the rising tide of political violence that has plagued parts of our country this year. We have seen how fragmented authority leads to slow responses, a weakness that echoes the disjointed administration of the mid-twentieth century.

### The Illusion of Structural Consolidation

The error in our current approach lies in believing that merging these offices is a purely fiscal exercise. History warns that structural reform without a change in mission is merely rebranding. In the late 1930s, efforts to trim the bureaucratic fat often resulted in the creation of super-agencies that carried the same inefficient staff and outdated ideologies into new buildings. 

If our legislators force a merger, the outcome will depend entirely on whether they are willing to strip away the performative layers of these institutions. We have seen how administrative bloating masks a lack of political will. When agencies are allowed to overlap, they become conduits for patronage rather than engines of cohesion. Much like the colonial era, where oversight bodies were often created as decorative shields against public scrutiny, our current agencies have struggled to move beyond reports and symposiums. The goal should not be to save a few coins by housing two offices under one roof. The goal must be to define what cohesion actually means in a country still recovering from localized shocks, such as the violence witnessed in Mwiki.

### Why it matters now

The current scrutiny in Parliament is a rare moment of clarity regarding the size of the state. If we continue to treat cohesion as an administrative problem to be managed by multiple committees, we will remain trapped in the cycle of building redundant infrastructure. The urgency of maintaining national peace makes this administrative bloat dangerous. We are paying for two agencies to produce the same reports while the actual work of de-escalating communal tension remains neglected.

### What to watch next

The debate will likely shift from the necessity of the agencies to the composition of a unified body. Watch for how the committees define the mandate of a merged entity. If the new structure retains the top-heavy management of its predecessors, it is a failure in the making. True reform will be measured by the reduction of bureaucratic layers, not the consolidation of budgets. 

Efficiency is rarely found in the reorganization of desks. It is found in the removal of the tasks that should not exist at all.
