## The Ledger of Our Inheritance

In the mid-1970s, as the initial optimism surrounding Kenya’s independence faltered against global oil shocks, the nation’s external debt sat at a modest US$ 1.3 billion. We were a young state still crafting our fiscal identity, yet the origins of our current confinement were already taking root in the financial centers of Washington and London.

Today, discourse regarding our public debt is often distilled into the petty squabbles of the current administration. However, the architecture of our financial dependency spans decades. We have transitioned from an era of post-colonial development loans to one dominated by commercial market interests. By recognizing this path dependency, we move past the simplistic act of blaming the navigator and begin a necessary interrogation of the vessel itself.

### The Erosion of Economic Sovereignty

Following the robust growth rates of the 1960s, the 1980s ushered in a period of harsh restructuring that fundamentally reshaped the Kenyan landscape. The introduction of Structural Adjustment Programs, championed by the IMF and World Bank, were presented as vehicles for efficiency. In practice, they served as blunt instruments that dismantled domestic manufacturing capacity. By mandating the privatization of state enterprises and the aggressive liberalization of markets, these programs ensured that Kenya could no longer utilize internal industrialization as a primary means to balance its books.

We exchanged our potential for self-sufficiency for a seat at the global credit table. This created a persistent cycle where borrowing stopped being a temporary bridge to growth and instead became a permanent requirement to satisfy the interest obligations of previous loans. When observing the shift from a 42 percent debt-to-GDP ratio in 2013 to current levels hovering near 70 percent, we see the culmination of a long-term strategy designed to tether the Kenyan economy to foreign liquidity. Much like the recent high court intervention regarding the proposed U.S. Ebola quarantine facility, our national policy spaces are frequently restricted by these lingering external arrangements.

### The Shift Toward Commercial Risk

Kenya’s graduation to lower-middle-income status in 2014 was a symbolic milestone, though it arrived with a hidden fiscal penalty. Access to affordable, concessional funding from multilateral lenders began to decline. This forced the country into the arms of the Eurobond market and high-interest commercial creditors. While China accounts for roughly 12 percent of our external debt, the majority of our obligations remain firmly anchored within Western-led financial systems. This has fundamentally altered the profile of our debt, moving it away from long-term development capital and toward short-term, high-cost liabilities.

The modern focus on digital infrastructure and aggressive tax measures in the recent budget highlights a desperate need to broaden the revenue base to service these debts. While the government struggles to keep basic services functional-a challenge as persistent as the battery life saga affecting devices like the Vivo Y31d-the structural reality is that a significant portion of every tax shilling is effectively spent before it reaches the treasury. We are not merely managing an annual budget; we are navigating a historical debt legacy that predates most of the current cabinet.

### A Legacy of Institutional Constraints

The institutional memory of our fiscal policy is defined by recurring cycles rather than progressive shifts. Every administration encounters identical constraints, whether they inherit a 1980s structural adjustment regime or a looming 2026 commercial debt maturity wall. A constant preoccupation with short-term liquidity masks a chronic absence of long-term planning, leaving us to react to external mandates or security concerns instead of setting a sovereign economic agenda.

History teaches that debt is rarely just about the math of interest rates. More importantly, it concerns the forfeiture of the ability to choose one's own path. If we continue to view our fiscal challenges through the narrow aperture of election cycles, we will remain trapped in the same patterns that began shortly after 1963. The past is not merely a record of what we borrowed; it is a blueprint of how our leverage was systematically depleted.
