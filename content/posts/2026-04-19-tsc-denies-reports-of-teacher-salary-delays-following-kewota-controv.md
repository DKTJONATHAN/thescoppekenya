The Teachers Service Commission (TSC) today, April 19, 2026, refuted widespread social media claims regarding delayed April salary payments for teachers nationwide. The Commission dismissed a fabricated public statement linking potential delays to the ongoing Kenya Women Teachers Association (KEWOTA) controversy, urging reliance on official communication channels for accurate information.

TSC Confirms Timely Teacher Salary Payments

Dispelling Falsehoods A false public statement, circulated on April 19, alleged that teachers across the nation should anticipate salary delays due to a High Court order involving KEWOTA. This order supposedly suspended decisions related to continued salary deductions. The initial suspension stemmed from a media exposé concerning unauthorized payroll deductions that implicated KEWOTA's Chief Executive Officer, Benta Opande, who was accused of overseeing illicit withdrawals and extensive nepotism. However, on April 17, 2026, the Employment and Labor Relations Court issued a stay order in favor of KEWOTA, allowing monthly deductions to resume pending further investigation. The misleading statement also claimed TSC had closed payroll on April 16 and teachers would receive salaries next week, contrary to an April 17 payment schedule. TSC has categorically dismissed these allegations as misleading, advising the public to only rely on official TSC communication channels for verified information.

Official Confirmations and Allocations “Teachers and the public are advised to only rely on official TSC communication channels for official and verified information,” emphasised TSC. The Commission also confirmed that Junior School interns and replacement teachers recruited in January have been included in the April payroll. These educators will receive their salaries along with any pending arrears. This development follows President William Ruto's approval of the Supplementary Appropriation Bill on Wednesday, April 8, allocating Ksh24.2 billion to the TSC. This allocation is specifically designated to cover salary shortfalls and health insurance for teachers. This move is expected to ease financial pressure on several teachers who have, for years, raised concerns over delayed benefits and inadequate remuneration, reflecting a broader strategic emphasis on robust foundational support, a principle also seen in high-stakes pursuits like securing top-tier talent for a new era in professional sports, as detailed in reports such as the [Man Utd Barella Chase: Italian Midfield Maestro to Lead New Era?](https://zandani.co.ke/article/man-utd-barella-chase-italian-midfield-maestro-new-era). Additionally, the government has set aside Ksh3 billion to settle pending medical bills for teachers, a measure aimed at improving welfare in the profession amidst consistent union advocacy for better healthcare coverage.
