## Finance Bill 2026: Tax Reclassification Threatens Kenya’s Green Mobility Sector

The Kenyan Treasury has introduced a pivotal tax amendment in the Finance Bill 2026 that could derail the nation’s transition toward sustainable transport. By proposing to reclassify electric vehicles, electric motorcycles, and lithium-ion batteries from zero-rated to exempt under the Value Added Tax Act, the government effectively terminates the fiscal environment that previously encouraged green technology adoption. This legislative pivot arrives only months after the executive branch ratified the National Electric Mobility Policy, creating a significant disconnect between revenue-seeking tax strategies and the state’s stated environmental goals.

### The Accounting Shift and Manufacturing Costs

The primary concern for industry observers involves the technical transition of green products from a zero-rated status to an exempt status. Under the current zero-rated framework, manufacturers and assemblers are permitted to reclaim VAT paid on production inputs, such as imported components, raw materials, and factory machinery. This system was designed to keep the final retail price of electric vehicles (EVs) competitive with traditional internal combustion engine vehicles by lowering the overall cost of production.

Under the new proposal, these goods would be designated as exempt. While this means no VAT is charged on the final sale to the consumer, it also prevents businesses from claiming any tax credits on their expenses. Consequently, the VAT paid on lithium-ion cells and specialized electronics becomes a non-recoverable cost. To maintain profit margins, manufacturers will likely be forced to incorporate these hidden taxes into the shelf price. For a nascent industry that has seen a 188 percent surge in capital investment since January, this change represents a significant barrier to maintaining growth momentum.

### Market Instability and Infrastructure Concerns

The timing of this fiscal adjustment is particularly challenging for utility providers and private investors. Kenya Power has recently invested in significant grid upgrades to accommodate a projected increase in charging demand from the e-mobility sector. However, industry analysts suggest that the new tax burden could increase the collective costs for solar storage and electric two-wheeler firms by more than KES 191 million this year alone. This financial pressure threatens to stall the expansion of charging networks and secondary battery markets.

The inconsistency between the National Electric Mobility Policy and the Finance Bill 2026 has introduced a layer of market uncertainty. Investors who entered the Kenyan market based on the February 2026 policy promises are now reassessing their inventory and long term expansion strategies. This legislative friction reflects broader administrative tensions within the government, similar to recent scrutiny over state agency leadership, including the reappointment of Brown Ondego to state boards. In both instances, the government is seen balancing the need for institutional continuity against the immediate demands of new political and fiscal mandates.

### Implications for Energy Independence

Beyond the immediate price of vehicles, the proposed tax change impacts Kenya’s broader economic strategy of reducing its dependence on imported petroleum. The government has previously framed electric mobility as a cornerstone of a climate-resilient economy. If the total cost of ownership for an electric motorcycle or solar-powered appliance exceeds that of a fossil-fuel alternative, the target demographic of middle-class commuters and small-business owners will likely revert to traditional fuels. Such a shift would negate the environmental progress achieved during the first quarter of 2026 and relegate green technology to a niche luxury market rather than a mass-market solution.

### Legislative Timeline and Next Steps

The Finance Bill 2026 is scheduled for public participation and hearings before the Parliamentary Committee on Finance and National Planning in the coming weeks. These sessions will be a critical venue for the Kenya Association of Manufacturers and other stakeholders to present data regarding the potential erosion of local assembly competitiveness.

Legislators will face pressure to reconcile the Treasury’s need for revenue mobilization with the executive's climate commitments. The final direction of the sector will likely remain unclear until the June 2026 budget reading. At that point, the government must decide whether to confirm the exempt status or reinstate the zero-rating mechanism to align with the February 2026 sustainability mandates. Market participants are watching closely for any amendments that might preserve the input VAT recovery system, which remains the most vital tool for the survival of the local green tech manufacturing hub.
