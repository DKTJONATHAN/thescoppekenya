## The Royal Decree on 'Unthinking' Audiences in Kenya

Indeed, the grand halls of the Ancien Régime buzzed with this unspoken truth: the common folk, the vast Third Estate, were deemed but a vast, unthinking populace, content with superficial diversions and entirely incapable of comprehending the intricate dance of statecraft or the nuanced beauty of the aristocratic world. They were to be seen, perhaps, in their distant toil, but certainly not heard with their tedious grievances. Their economic hardships, the crushing weight of taxes and feudal dues, were mere background noise to the tinkling harpsichords and the rustle of brocade. For the nobility, the masses existed solely to provide labor and, occasionally, a grateful audience for royal benevolence, never as arbiters of taste or intellect. Their preferences, it was assumed, leaned towards the most rudimentary of entertainments, a quick escape from their miserable realities, not complex narratives or challenging ideas. This profound disconnect, a chasm wider than any moat, only deepened as the financial woes of France worsened, and the court continued its elaborate charade.

### The Whispers from the Galleries: Kenyan Critics on Nollywood Quality

Outside the gilded cage, beyond the manicured gardens, whispers grew into shouts. Pamphlets, crudely printed but potent, circulated in taverns and marketplaces. They spoke of inequality, of hunger, of a ruling class that saw its people as little more than a collective of dim-witted serfs. The idea that these commoners might possess their own forms of intelligence, their own desires for meaning beyond mere subsistence, was utterly foreign to the powdered heads of Versailles. "Why," one Marquis scoffed at a salon, "would they bother with philosophy when there is a good harvest to pray for, or a festival to cheer?" The very notion of complex societal critique or artistic depth being appreciated by the general public was dismissed as absurd, a folly only the privileged few could truly grasp.

It was this entrenched conviction, this tragic irony, that paved the cobbled stones leading to the Bastille in July 1789. The aristocrats, blind in their opulent bubble, believed that the public's preference for simple fare indicated a fundamental lack of intellectual capacity. They failed to grasp that sometimes, the hunger for bread superseded the appreciation for a sonnet, and that what seemed like a preference for escapism was, in truth, a desperate need for respite from overwhelming burdens. The true cost of their disdain would be paid not in livres, but in blood and the irreversible loss of their dominion.

Centuries later, the scent of expensive perfume and the murmur of polite disinterest have given way to the hum of air conditioning and the sharp flash of camera lights, yet the echoes of a familiar hubris reverberate. From the grand, glittering film festivals where industry luminaries gather, a similar pronouncement has emerged, not from a French duke, but from a contemporary film producer. One might hear it in the hallowed halls where the future of African cinema is debated, a future that, ironically, hinges upon the very audience sometimes dismissed.
