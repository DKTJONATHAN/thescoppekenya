## Equity Bank and the Cybersecurity Mirage

Equity Bank Kenya is currently investigating the loss of KES 300,000 from a customer account, a scenario framing the bank's security infrastructure as a thin shield against the far more porous reality of personal email reliance. The incident exposes a growing structural failure.

### The Fiction of Banking Immunity

Equity Bank points to an email compromise as the vector for this breach, effectively washing its hands of the incident by placing the blame on a third party. While the bank operates within hardened, PCI-DSS compliant environments, it remains tethered to a legacy authentication method that ignores the vulnerability of the average user. When a bank permits password recovery or transaction verification through email, it is not just relying on the customer to be secure; it is essentially outsourcing its security integrity to the free-tier servers of Silicon Valley giants.

There is a profound disconnect between the high-level security mandates issued by the Central Bank of Kenya and the primitive reliance on consumer-grade email authentication. Banks demand biometric data and two-factor authentication for their own apps, yet they continue to allow email servers to act as the ultimate arbiter of account access. If your email is the master key to your password reset flow, then your bank is only as secure as the weakest spam filter in your inbox. This regulatory no-man's-land means that when an attacker gains entry, the bank treats the transaction as authorized because the credentials-the digital equivalent of a signed cheque-were processed correctly from the outside.

### The Burden of Proof Trap

In the current legal framework, the burden of proof rests heavily on the victim to prove that the bank failed in its duty of care. Banks capitalize on the fact that once a digital token is generated from a recognized email address, the transaction appears legitimate in their logs. The press release issued by the institution avoids the uncomfortable reality of who carries the risk when a third-party hack triggers a corporate transfer. 

We are witnessing a shift where the bank acts less as a guardian of funds and more as a passive pipeline. When [Google moves to implement automatic call recording for advertising data](https://zandani.co.ke/article/google-to-implement-automatic-call-recording-for-advertising-data), it shows how tech firms prioritize data flow over user privacy, a trend that mirrors the banking sector's reliance on unsecured email protocols. If Equity Bank continues to use email as a critical verification node, it effectively signals that customer account protection is a shared, yet ill-defined, responsibility between the bank and the user. The reality is that the bank maintains the protocol, but the customer carries the entire liability.

### Why it matters now

The financial sector in Kenya is pushing for mass digitization while clinging to authentication protocols that date back two decades. As cyber threats become more sophisticated, the gap between bank-grade encryption and standard email security is widening. This case serves as a warning that your account is not a fortress; it is a node in a network of vulnerable third-party services.

### What to watch next

Regulators need to address the systemic reliance on email-based password recovery. Look for whether the Central Bank of Kenya mandates a departure from email-integrated banking alerts. If institutional policy does not change, expect more victims to find themselves in the middle of a legal vacuum where the bank blames the email provider, and the email provider cites its terms of service to evade liability for financial loss.

### Verdict

Equity Bank is playing a dangerous game of outsourcing its risk. Until the bank mandates hardware-based security keys or moves entirely away from email-dependent recovery flows, customers are essentially banking on the security of a platform the bank does not control. Relying on an email account to protect a bank balance is not a security feature. It is a design flaw.
