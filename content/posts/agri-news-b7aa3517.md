Rice farmers in Kirinyaga County's Mwea region are grappling with a severe threat to paddy rice production posed by the invasive Golden Apple Snail (GAS). The mollusc, scientifically identified as *Pomacea canaliculata* and *Pomacea maculata*, has escalated into a major agricultural concern across the country, with its destructive capabilities capable of decimating one square meter of a field overnight and leading to over 50 percent yield loss. The snails were first observed in March 2020 within the Mwea-Tebere area, which remains the most affected part of the Mwea Irrigation Scheme. Initially mistaken for native snail species, farmers overlooked the distinctive strawberry-red eggs, unknowingly allowing the infestation to take root. Since its initial detection, the voracious snails have spread considerably, now affecting approximately 780 of the scheme's 26,000 acres. The Mwea Rice Irrigation Scheme, East and Central Africa's largest, annually produces about 114,000 metric tons of paddy rice valued at an estimated Ksh8.2 billion, with 80 percent consumed locally. The invasive apple snail has already reduced rice yield in Kenya by around 14 percent and net rice income by about 60 percent. The life cycle of the Golden Apple Snail is rapid; eggs hatch within seven to 14 days, grow into small snails in 15 to 25 days, and reach adulthood in 45 to 59 days. While warmer temperatures can shorten their life cycle to less than three months, they can survive for over two years in swampy and colder environments. In response to this growing crisis, the Mwea Irrigation Agricultural Development office has been actively equipping farmers with knowledge to differentiate between the invasive Golden Apple Snails and native species. Farmers are now trained to identify the Golden Apple Snail by its muddy brown shell, golden-pinkish or orange-yellow flesh, and larger size compared to native snails, as well as its bright pink eggs. Current efforts to manage the pest include various strategies. In September 2025, a significant biocontrol initiative was launched in Mwea by scientists from the Kenya Medical Research Institute (Kemri) in collaboration with Israel's Ministry of Agriculture. This project is exploring the use of freshwater prawns as natural predators to control snail populations and simultaneously reduce the prevalence of bilharzia, a parasitic disease for which snails act as vectors. This innovative approach aims to provide nature-based solutions to reduce reliance on chemical interventions. Earlier, in August 2023, the Ministry of Agriculture initiated a pilot program, procuring 20,000 liters of biograde pesticides and providing sprayers and personal protective equipment to farmers for targeted control during critical transplanting periods. Despite these interventions, farmers have often resorted to physical and cultural practices, alongside the use of illegal chemicals, which have largely proven ineffective, cumbersome, or expensive. Experts emphasize the critical need for strict quarantine measures to prevent the further spread of the pest within Kenya and to other susceptible regions in East Africa. Proposed management strategies also include the use of field screens to deter entry, diligent handpicking of adults, crushing of egg masses, synchronizing farm activities, adapting cropping systems and planting patterns, and effective water management in paddies. Raising awareness among rice growers in both affected and unaffected areas is considered paramount for effective, sustainable management. Globally, rice prices are expected to remain under pressure throughout 2026 due to surplus supplies from major exporting countries, which could impact the earnings of local farmers already battling pests like the Golden Apple Snail. This economic context further underscores the urgency of effective pest control measures to safeguard Kenya's food security and the livelihoods of its rice farming communities. The invasive apple snail poses not only an agricultural threat but also a public health risk, as it can carry parasites such as rat lungworm. Continued research and the implementation of integrated pest management strategies are crucial to mitigating the widespread impact of this destructive mollusc.
