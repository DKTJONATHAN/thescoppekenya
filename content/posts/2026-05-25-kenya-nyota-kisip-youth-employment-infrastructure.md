## The Quiet Dignity of Building a Future in Kenya's Settlements

The annual arrival of 800,000 young people into the Kenyan workforce is often framed as a daunting demographic hurdle. It is a number so large that it can feel impersonal, yet when you stand in a small workshop in Kilifi County, the scale of this challenge becomes deeply human. I find myself thinking about the sharp glare of a welder’s torch and what it represents for someone like Esther Karisa. As she works, she is not just joining metal; she is constructing a sense of agency that many in her position have long been denied. This individual effort is the pulse of a much larger movement, supported by the 165 million dollars flowing through the second phase of the Kenya Informal Settlements Improvement Project (KISIP 2) and the NYOTA program.

### From National Policy to Personal Agency

For a long time, the dialogue surrounding Kenya's development has been dominated by high-level fiscal news. We read about significant shifts in the energy sector, such as when [Uganda secures 21 percent stake in Kenya Pipeline Company](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-03-21-uganda-secures-21-percent-stake-in-kenya-pipeline-company), and we recognize these as necessary pillars of our economy. However, for the hundreds of thousands of youth entering the market each year, these boardroom deals can feel like they belong to a different world. The National Youth Opportunities Towards Advancement (NYOTA) program aims to bring that sense of progress closer to home. It operates on the considered belief that vocational training is a form of self-defense against poverty. When young people like Esther master a trade, they are no longer waiting for a formal job market that is often too slow to accommodate them. They are creating their own foundations.

The training provided in these programs is a thoughtful response to the reality of the Kenyan economy. It recognizes that the informal sector is not a temporary phase to be outgrown, but a vital ecosystem that needs support. By formalizing technical skills, the program gives youth the tools to participate in this economy with confidence. It is a shift from viewing the youth population as a potential crisis to seeing them as a reservoir of talent. In Kilifi, this transition is visible in every newly trained artisan who decides to open a shop or join a local project. They are moving away from the anxiety of unemployment toward the quiet pride of craftsmanship.

### The Foundation of Belonging and Infrastructure

Infrastructure is frequently discussed in terms of logistics and efficiency. We see this in the way [Kenya real estate shifts focus to logistics and data centers](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenya-real-estate-shifts-focus-to-logistics-and-data-centers), reflecting a modernizing nation. But in the context of KISIP 2, which reaches into 47 counties, infrastructure is about something more fundamental: the right to a stable life. When a settlement receives paved roads, functional drainage, and reliable water, the environment changes from a place of survival to a place of residence. For the families living there, these are not just engineering feats. They are signals that their community is valued.

Perhaps the most significant aspect of this project is the formalization of land tenure. To live without a title deed is to live with a constant, low-level hum of uncertainty. By providing legal ownership, KISIP 2 changes the psychology of these settlements. I believe that when a person knows the ground they stand on is truly theirs, they begin to look at the future differently. They invest in more permanent homes, they start small businesses, and they plant roots. This security transforms a collection of shelters into a neighborhood. It replaces the fear of being moved with the confidence of belonging.

### Why it matters now

Kenya is at a point where the energy of its youth must be met with real opportunity. The combination of physical infrastructure and technical skill-building creates an environment where local economies can finally take flight. By addressing the lack of land rights and the scarcity of technical training simultaneously, the government is tackling the structural barriers that keep people in poverty. This is about more than just short-term employment; it is about building the architecture of a society where every young person has a path toward a dignified life.

### What to watch next

The true test of these investments will be the durability of the systems put in place. It will be important to observe how the 47 counties handle the long-term maintenance of the new roads and water networks. We should also look for a surge in micro-investments within these settlements as land titles are distributed. As more graduates from the NYOTA program enter the market, the growth of small-scale service and manufacturing hubs will indicate if this funding has successfully seeded a new era of urban stability. The work in Kilifi is a beginning, and its success will be measured by how many other young people can eventually pick up a tool and build a life of their own.
