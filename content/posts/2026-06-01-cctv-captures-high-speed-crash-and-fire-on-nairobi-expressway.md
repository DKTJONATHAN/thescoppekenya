## Beyond the Lens: Nairobi Demands Accountability on the Expressway

Social media timelines are saturated with digital trauma this morning, as residents question why the Nairobi Expressway remains a high-speed theater of accidents caught on camera rather than a road designed to prevent them.

The CCTV footage from Sunday night, May 31, at 8:29pm, shows a collision involving two vehicles near the K14+896R marker by the Ole Sereni Interchange. The impact sent one vehicle slamming into the steel barriers, followed by a fire that quickly engulfed the frame. While authorities are still assessing the incident, the visual record circulating online has reignited a heated debate about whether the multi-billion shilling infrastructure serves as a modern transport corridor or a dangerous trap for those who fall victim to reckless driving and speed.

### A City Watching in Real-Time

For many commuters, the convenience of the Expressway has been overshadowed by a growing sense of anxiety. The digital footprint of this latest incident has triggered a wave of frustration, with many users noting that the cameras seem to capture the chaos perfectly while doing little to stop it before it happens. Online discourse is shifting rapidly from shock at the footage to a pointed critique of safety protocols.

One regular commuter who uses the route daily, Wanjiku Mwangi, expressed the sentiment shared by many: watching these clips feels like witnessing a tragedy in slow motion, yet there seems to be no change in how the road is monitored or managed. Others, like freelance tech analyst David Omondi, argue that the CCTV infrastructure is merely a diagnostic tool for post-mortem analysis rather than a proactive safety mechanism. He points out that while the sensors can detect a crash, they provide no warning system to help drivers avoid reckless counterparts on the road. A third voice in the conversation, local activist Samuel Otieno, questioned the lack of enforcement against high-speed weaving, noting that the cameras document violations but rarely seem to result in the immediate intervention required to save lives on such a restricted-access highway.

### The Myth of the Smart Highway

When the Expressway was launched, the narrative focused heavily on its smart features and integrated surveillance capabilities. Today, that promise is being scrutinized by a public that feels the road is failing in its core duty to provide a secure transit environment. While traffic safety experts have long highlighted the importance of barrier integrity and emergency response times, the recurring visual of burning vehicles near major interchanges has forced a conversation about the limits of technology.

If the cameras are the only line of defense, the public argues, then the system is fundamentally flawed. There is a palpable demand for more than just a recording of the events. Residents are calling for better enforcement, stricter speed monitoring that leads to real-time consequences, and a redesign of hazardous spots where merging traffic creates high-speed friction. Some have compared the current reliance on static monitoring to other technological shifts in the city, noting that even as the country advances in sectors like AI-powered computing or digital documentation, the basics of road safety seem to remain stuck in the past.

### The Path Forward

The focus now shifts to whether the authorities will prioritize active prevention over passive observation. Commuters are looking for evidence that the Expressway management is listening to these concerns. Proposals currently floating on social media include the installation of active speed-limit enforcement systems that alert drivers before a collision occurs, rather than waiting for the impact to trip a camera sensor.

Safety is not just about the quality of the tarmac or the efficiency of the toll collection booths. It is about creating an environment where a simple error in judgment does not lead to a catastrophic fire. As the city continues to process the images from the Ole Sereni interchange, the demand for tangible improvements to the highway's safety culture is louder than ever. Nairobians are no longer satisfied with being passive spectators to highway disasters. They want a road that protects them, not one that simply records their final moments. Until meaningful changes are implemented on the ground, the public mood will remain one of deep unease, watching every transition and merge on the Expressway with growing suspicion.
