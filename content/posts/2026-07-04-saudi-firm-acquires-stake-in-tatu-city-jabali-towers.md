## Whose City Is This Anyway? Saudi Firm Acquires Stake in Tatu City Jabali Towers Project is the central subject of the update.

A Saudi Arabian firm has snapped up a 49 percent stake in the Jabali Towers project, cementing a multibillion-shilling partnership that is turning the skyline of Tatu City into a playground for the global elite while leaving the surrounding communities in Ruiru questioning where their place is in this new Kenya.

This latest deal, which sees a massive international entity move into the heart of Kiambu, comes as the construction sector reports a 6.8 percent growth over the last year. Jabali Towers, a massive double-tower structure rising 25 and 36 storeys respectively, aims to anchor a residential and commercial hub that feels lightyears away from the reality of the average Kenyan worker. With China Road and Bridge Corporation tapped as the lead contractor, the project is moving fast toward its 2028 completion target. This influx of foreign capital is being hailed by government corridors as a win for bilateral trade, yet for those living just outside the Tatu City gates, the project represents a growing divide in access to housing and basic economic opportunities.

### The View From The Ground

Walk through the bustling streets of Ruiru, and the sentiment toward these glass towers shifts from pride to frustration. Many residents feel the rapid expansion of these special economic zones acts more like a barricade than a bridge.

Joseph, a local mechanic who has operated near the outskirts of Tatu City for over a decade, looks at the construction cranes and sees a world that does not want him. He points out that while the towers promise thousands of new homes and commercial spaces, they are priced in a currency he cannot touch. To him, the towers are not for the people building the roads; they are for an exclusive class of buyers who view the neighborhood as a commodity.

A few kilometers away, Sarah, a small-scale vendor, echoes this disconnect. She notes that while the government talks about jobs, the local businesses often feel squeezed by the rising land value and the gentrification that follows these projects. She explains that when foreign investment flows into luxury hubs, the cost of everything from rent to basic supplies in the surrounding areas spikes, making life harder for the very people who have lived in Kiambu for generations.

Then there is the perspective of Evans, a construction worker who has spent time on various sites across Nairobi and its satellite towns. He sees the efficiency of the Chinese contractors and the scale of the Saudi investment as a sign of progress but remains cynical about the long-term gains for local laborers. He notes that the technical demands of such high-end projects often mean that local workers are relegated to lower-tier roles, while the lucrative managerial or specialized positions go elsewhere. He wonders aloud why the massive construction boom is not translating into better-paying, sustainable work for the youth in his village.

### The Cost of Living Divide

The ambition of the Jabali Towers project is undeniable, but it highlights a massive gap between national economic statistics and the daily struggle of the common citizen. While the government celebrates billions in foreign direct investment, the average Kenyan is currently grappling with the weight of inflation and limited housing stock. As local banks protest taxes on repossessed collateral, reflecting the volatility in the property market, the shiny promises of Tatu City feel distant.

This project is not just about concrete and steel; it is about who holds the keys to the future of urban space in Kenya. The 49 percent stake from an international player is a standard move in modern real estate, but it emphasizes a trend where domestic land becomes a playground for external wealth. As the towers continue to climb toward the clouds, the pressure on surrounding infrastructure, water supply, and road networks remains a looming concern for the thousands who were here long before the investment firms arrived.

The project is moving steadily toward 2028, but the social cost of this development will be determined by whether these luxury towers remain islands of wealth or if they can eventually integrate with the vibrant, struggling, and resilient communities that surround them. For now, the skyline changes, but the distance between the boardroom at Tatu City and the reality on the Kiambu road remains vast.
