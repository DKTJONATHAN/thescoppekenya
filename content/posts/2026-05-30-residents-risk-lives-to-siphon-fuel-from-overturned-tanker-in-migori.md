On May 30, 2026, hundreds of local residents swarmed an overturned fuel tanker in Migori County. Ignoring the immediate threat of a catastrophic explosion, the crowd scrambled to siphon leaking petrol, prioritizing the acquisition of free fuel over their own physical safety.

## A Dangerous Gamble for Survival

### The irony of national service
Some observers have sarcastically suggested that the dangerous scramble represents a form of patriotic service aimed at curbing national fuel shortages. The logic implies that if current economic policies prevent affordable access to fuel, residents will simply redistribute the supply themselves. This grim outlook highlights a severe level of economic desperation, where the fear of a potential fire is overshadowed by the crushing cost of living. While some sectors of the economy celebrate large capital investments, this incident serves as a stark reminder that many citizens are left to hunt for resources on the roadside.

### A haunting case of collective amnesia
The scene in Migori mirrored the 2009 Sachang'wan tragedy, where a similar effort to siphon fuel from an overturned tanker resulted in a massive inferno and the loss of hundreds of lives. It appears that the historical lessons of such disasters are repeatedly ignored whenever an opportunity for free fuel arises. This pattern of memory loss is deeply concerning to onlookers. Whether fueled by a lack of awareness or the overwhelming pressure of poverty clouding basic survival instincts, the public continues to engage in life-threatening behavior, driven by a perceived lack of alternative options within the system.

### Betting on survival against the odds
The chaotic scene resembled a high-stakes competition as individuals rushed to fill buckets and jerrycans. Every second spent near the tanker carried the risk of a single spark turning the highway into a furnace, yet the crowd remained undeterred. For those involved, the immediate gain of obtaining a few liters of fuel clearly outweighed the catastrophic possibility of death. When economic conditions become sufficiently dire, survival instincts override common sense, forcing people to stop worrying about the long-term consequences and focus entirely on the immediate necessity of keeping their vehicles or businesses running for another kilometer.
