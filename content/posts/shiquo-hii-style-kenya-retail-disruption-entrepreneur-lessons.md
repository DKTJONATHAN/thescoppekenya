## The Young Woman Who Shook an Entire Industry

If you have spent any time on Kenyan TikTok or Instagram in the past two years, you already know the name. Shiquo Hii Style is the young Nairobi entrepreneur who walked into Chinese markets, started filming, and accidentally shook an entire retail industry. She sells air fryers at KES 2,000 when competitors charge KES 17,000. She sells flasks with six cups at KES 1,000 when other sellers charge that for the flask alone. And she does it all from her phone, her social media pages, and a bold refusal to follow the old rules of business.

This is not a celebrity profile. This is a business education. Whether you are a struggling retailer, a first-time entrepreneur, or someone with KES 10,000 and a dream, the Shiquo story has lessons that could change how you think about money, imports, and digital business entirely.

### Who Is Shiquo Hii Style?

Her real name is Sylvia Saru, born on May 21, 2002, in Nairobi, Kenya. She is Luhya, with family roots in the Butere area of Western Kenya. She is a wife, a mother of three children, and an importer who made her first million Kenya shillings at just 19 years old. She has been public about not coming from money. There was no wealthy family backing her. No inheritance. No shortcut.

Her husband, who she calls her "ride or die," is her business partner. He supported her even when his own venture was growing slowly, and she has publicly credited him as central to her success. The couple now travel to China together on sourcing trips, documenting every step for their audiences online.

Shiquo runs a physical store in Nairobi that regularly draws crowds, an active online sales operation, and a growing warehouse. By early 2026, she had expanded her physical footprint and continued sourcing new inventory categories.

### The Business Model That Terrified Retailers

To understand what Shiquo built, you first need to understand how traditional retail works in Kenya, and how many layers stand between a factory in Guangzhou and your hands.

In the conventional supply chain, a manufacturer in China sells to an exporter. The exporter sells to a Kenyan importer. The importer sells to a wholesaler. The wholesaler sells to a retailer. The retailer sells to you. Each of those five layers adds a margin. By the time a product reaches the consumer, the price can be two times, three times, or even seven times the original factory cost.

Shiquo collapsed all five layers into one.

She is the importer. She is the wholesaler. She is the retailer. She buys directly from Chinese factories, ships the goods to Kenya, and sells them directly to consumers at prices that leave traditional retailers unable to compete. Import business coach Rachael Maina, known online as Just Ray, described it clearly in an interview with journalist Lynn Ngugi: "What Shiquo is trying to do is to become a wholesaler who is also an importer and a retailer at the same time. She can sell in masses directly to the consumer, putting off the retailer who is the in-betweener."

This is not a new concept. The wholesalers of Eastleigh and Kamukunji have operated this way for decades. The difference is that they never showed up on social media. They stayed invisible. Shiquo showed up, camera in hand, standing in the middle of a Chinese market, and made the whole process transparent.

## Why Her Prices Are So Low: A Real Breakdown

A common misconception is that Shiquo is doing something illegal, selling counterfeit goods, or running at a loss. None of that is accurate. Her prices are low because she understands the true cost structure of importing, operates at volume with thin margins, and eliminates the middlemen that traditional retailers depend on.

Rachael Maina broke down the full cost of importing from China in her interview, and the numbers are eye-opening.

### The Actual Costs of Importing One Container to Kenya

**Freight Charges:** To ship one 20-foot container from China to Kenya, you pay the shipping line directly. The cost starts at approximately USD 4,000 and goes higher depending on the shipping company and the period. This is separate from the cost of the goods themselves.

**Bill of Lading:** This is a mandatory official document that certifies the contents of the container. Without it, you cannot clear your cargo at the Kenyan port. It comes with its own fee.

**Pre-Shipment Inspection and Certification:** Your goods must be inspected and certified in China before they leave. This process can cost up to USD 2,000, depending on how many different product types are in the container. A container with 100 different items will cost significantly more to certify than one with a single product type.

**China Handling and Loading Fee:** Before the container is loaded onto the ship, you pay a handling fee at the Chinese port. This is approximately USD 200.

**Import Declaration Form (IDF):** Once your goods arrive in Kenya, you pay the government a charge called the IDF. It is calculated at 2.5% of the total CIF value. CIF stands for Cost, Insurance, and Freight combined. This charge was reduced from 3.5% to 2.5% in 2023.

**VAT:** Standard value-added tax applies to imported goods upon arrival.

**Import Duty:** Every product imported into Kenya has a specific tariff code, known as an HS code. The duty rate attached to that code ranges from 0% for exempted goods to 100% for heavily protected categories. Each item in your container is calculated separately, multiplied by the quantity. There is no single flat rate.

**Railway Development Levy:** This is a mandatory government levy that all importers pay. It is not optional.

**Port Storage Fee:** The Kenya Ports Authority gives you four days to clear your cargo after it arrives. From day five, storage fees begin accumulating. Importers who run out of funds after shipping often find themselves unable to clear their goods, and abandoned containers at the port are eventually auctioned off.

The combined result of all these charges is dramatic. An importer who buys goods in China for KES 2 million can easily face a clearing bill of KES 4 million by the time everything is paid. For very cheap individual items, the shipping cost per unit can actually exceed the factory cost. A cup bought at KES 10 in China may cost KES 20 to ship.

Shiquo understands every one of these numbers. She accounts for them, communicates them to her customers, and still makes a profit because she sells at scale. One small margin multiplied by thousands of customers still adds up.

## What She Does Differently From Everyone Else

### She Became Her Own Brand

Rather than spend money paying influencers to promote her products, Shiquo became the influencer. She is the face, the voice, and the personality of her business. This was a calculated decision. Influencer marketing is expensive and unpredictable. Owning your own audience is permanent.

### She Made Importing Transparent

The biggest disruption Shiquo caused was not the prices. It was the transparency. She films inside Chinese markets. She shows the source, the product, the price, and the process in real time. Customers feel informed rather than exploited. That trust is worth more than any discount.

Rachael Maina made a powerful observation: "Whatever Shiquo is doing is what people in Eastleigh have been doing for years. Only that they were not on social media to be seen." The prices were always available. The knowledge was always there. Shiquo just made it visible.

### She Operates Outside Price Cartels

Traditional importers often gather in informal groups and agree on minimum selling prices for specific products. They collectively decide that a given item must be sold at a certain multiple of cost, and anyone who goes below that price faces social and economic pressure. These arrangements protect established players and keep prices artificially high. Shiquo operates entirely outside these networks. She sets her own prices based on her own cost structure and her own decisions about what margin she is comfortable with.

### She Uses a Demand-Driven Model

Rather than importing large quantities of untested products and hoping they sell, Shiquo largely takes orders first, sources in China based on demand, and ships accordingly. This reduces her inventory risk and ensures she is not sitting on unsold stock. It also gives her customers a sense of participation in the process.

### She Sells Volume Over Margin

Where traditional retailers might sell 50 items a month at KES 5,000 profit per item, Shiquo sells 5,000 items a month at KES 200 profit per item. The total profit can be similar or greater, but the consumer feels they are getting a deal. Customers who feel they got value will return, refer others, and defend the brand publicly.

## What Traditional Retailers Got Wrong

The retailers who are struggling in the face of Shiquo's rise are not victims of unfair competition. They are, in most cases, victims of their own refusal to adapt. Rachael Maina was direct about this: "I don't know why business people feel threatened by her. If you feel threatened, up your game."

**Refusing Social Media:** Retailers who do not have an active social media presence in 2026 are invisible. Their products exist, their prices may even be competitive, but no one knows about them. Shiquo's entire competitive advantage started with a phone and a TikTok account.

**Not Understanding Import Costs:** Many small retailers source goods from Eastleigh or Kamukunji without understanding the full import cost chain. They see a product on Shiquo's page and cannot explain the pricing difference. The answer is almost always volume. Someone importing 10 containers of an item pays a dramatically lower per-unit cost than someone importing 10 boxes. The more you buy, the cheaper China makes it.

**Selling at Inflated Multiples:** The culture of marking up goods by five, six, or seven times the cost is a relic of an era before price transparency. When consumers had no reference point, they accepted whatever price was on the shelf. Today, they have TikTok. Retailers who continue pricing at seven times cost will lose customers to anyone willing to work at three times cost.

## The Controversy: Where Shiquo Has Stumbled

Honest business reporting requires acknowledging the challenges alongside the success. By early 2025, a segment of Shiquo's customer base began organizing a boycott over specific grievances.

Customers reported undelivered orders, incorrect items shipped, unexpected shipping charges added after initial payment, and slow response times from customer service. A former model associated with her brand, Winnie Njenga, publicly accused her of failing to pay a salary owed since 2023, with the amount allegedly approaching KES 1 million.

Separately, Rachael Maina noted in the interview that Shiquo had publicly cited incorrect figures for import charges, specifically quoting a flat KES 2,000 for the IDF when the actual charge is 2.5% of total CIF value. At scale, this is not a minor error. It is a figure that could mislead thousands of followers who use her statements to plan their own businesses.

These controversies do not erase her achievement. But they are a clear signal that scaling a direct-to-consumer import business requires building systems for customer service, accurate public communication, and staff management that match the pace of growth.

## How to Build a Business Like Hers: A Practical Roadmap

You do not need to be Shiquo to apply her principles. The following steps are drawn directly from the advice given by Rachael Maina in the Lynn Ngugi interview, combined with the practical lessons of Shiquo's own journey.

**Step 1: Start With a Small Package, Not a Container.** Your first import should be a small consolidated shipment. You are learning the process, testing the product, and figuring out what customers want. Mistakes at small scale cost you KES 20,000. Mistakes at container scale cost you KES 4 million.

**Step 2: Calculate the Full Landed Cost Before You Buy Anything.** Contact a licensed clearing agent in Kenya. Give them a proforma invoice from your Chinese supplier. Ask them to calculate the full duty, VAT, IDF, levies, and freight for your specific product. Do not assume. Do not estimate. Get the number in writing before you commit a single shilling.

**Step 3: Check What the Same Product Sells for Locally Right Now.** Open Jiji. Walk through Eastleigh. Check the supermarkets. If the local selling price is lower than your projected landed cost, do not import that product. This happens more often than people expect, especially with goods that are manufactured locally and protected by government duty.

**Step 4: Build Your Social Media Presence Before Your Inventory Arrives.** Open a TikTok account. Open an Instagram page. Start filming your sourcing process, your products, your journey. Post consistently. The audience you build before your goods arrive is the audience that will buy when they land. Shiquo did not build her following after she became successful. The following was part of how she became successful.

**Step 5: Find a Product That Is Not Already Everywhere.** Search Alibaba or 1688. Look for items that solve a real problem Kenyan consumers have but cannot easily find locally. The moment you identify a product that is in high demand and low supply locally, you have a business. Importing the tenth person selling the same handbag type gets you nowhere.

**Step 6: Be Transparent About Every Cost to Your Customer.** Tell your customers the product price and the shipping cost separately. Tell them when their goods will be ready. Tell them what happens if there is a delay. Customers do not leave over problems. They leave over silence. Shiquo's biggest reputational damage came not from delays but from customers feeling blindsided by charges they were not told about upfront.

**Step 7: Sell on Multiple Platforms.** Beyond TikTok and Instagram, list your products on Jiji, WhatsApp catalogues, and eventually an e-commerce site of your own. Rachael Maina noted that 80% of sellers on Jiji Kenya are currently Chinese nationals. The market gap for Kenyan-run e-commerce stores is enormous.

**Step 8: If You Plan to Visit China, Prepare Properly.** When your business is ready to scale, a sourcing trip to China will change your operation. You will need a visa, a business invitation letter from a Chinese company, and a bank statement showing a minimum of USD 10,000. Return flights range from USD 1,500 to USD 2,000. On your first trip, do not buy anything. Spend the entire trip exploring markets, meeting suppliers, and understanding the geography.

**Step 9: Respect the Ecosystem Around You.** If you grow to the point where you are wholesaling to other small businesses, set a minimum order quantity. Do not sell single items to people who want to resell them. Let small retailers have their margin. An importer who puts every small retailer out of business eventually has no one left to buy from them in bulk.

**Step 10: Protect Quality as Aggressively as You Protect Price.** China has ten quality tiers for every product, all of which can look identical externally. The importer decides which tier reaches the Kenyan consumer. Choosing the cheapest tier to maximize margin is a short-term strategy. Quality is not charity. It is customer retention.

## The Bigger Picture

Shiquo Hii Style did not create a new business model. What she did was take a business model that had existed quietly in the back streets of Eastleigh and Kamukunji for decades and make it visible, personal, and digital. The prices were always there. The access was always there. The missing ingredient was a young woman with a camera and the confidence to show the world exactly how it worked.

That is the real lesson. Information asymmetry is the foundation of overpricing. When consumers do not know what something costs to produce and ship, they accept whatever price they are given. The moment that information becomes public, the market self-corrects.

For entrepreneurs, the message is not to copy Shiquo. It is to ask what information asymmetry exists in your own industry, and whether you have the courage and the digital presence to be the person who closes the gap.

For consumers, the message is simpler. You were never being sold to at fair prices. You were being sold to at whatever price the market could sustain before someone like Shiquo showed up.

She showed up.
