The Federation of Kenya Employers (FKE) today called for an immediate reduction in statutory deductions and other government-imposed taxes, citing a significant 12 percent drop in formal employment. FKE CEO Jacqueline Mugo stated on March 18, 2026, that high taxes and rising living costs are significantly reducing salaries, making formal jobs increasingly unattractive and pushing individuals toward informal work.

## Employers Decry Unsustainable Tax Burden

### Formal Employment Decline and Operational Costs
A recent survey by FKE indicates that employment has decreased by approximately 12 percent, with hiring reductions predominantly observed in the manufacturing, retail, hospitality, transport, and financial services sectors. Employers attribute this decline to escalating operational costs, including high energy prices, wage pressures, and complex regulatory requirements, such as those detailed in the KWS proposal on national park and reserve access fees. FKE Executive Director and CEO Jacqueline Mugo emphasized that the combined burden of taxation, statutory deductions, and compliance obligations is squeezing businesses, diminishing workers' take-home pay, and weakening competitiveness. The Federation warned that the current payslip structure is unsustainable, asserting that excessive deductions severely impact both employees and businesses.

### Calls for Comprehensive Payslip Review
Amidst these concerns, employers are advocating for a comprehensive payslip review aimed at alleviating the tax burden, simplifying deductions, and enhancing employees' disposable income. FKE is specifically calling for tax reductions, policy stability, improved access to finance, and a stronger alignment between education and industry to restore business confidence and stimulate job creation. Previously, FKE had submitted proposals to the National Treasury, recommending that deductions be pegged on basic pay instead of consolidated pay and pushing for a reduction of the housing levy from 1.5 percent to 0.5 percent of total earnings.

### Government's Proposed Tax Relief
These demands from FKE come weeks after Treasury Cabinet Secretary John Mbadi announced the government's intention to eliminate Pay As You Earn (PAYE) deductions for individuals earning below KSh30,000. Speaking on February 2, 2026, Mbadi confirmed that the Ministry would present the Tax Laws Amendment Bill before Parliament for approval of these reforms, ahead of the publication of the Finance Bill 2026. He stated that the government and President Ruto had agreed to provide this reprieve for low-income earners. Mbadi also added that the Bill would include a proposal for a 5 percent PAYE reduction for those earning between KSh30,000 and KSh50,000. The proposed changes are intended to increase the tax-free pay threshold from KSh24,000 to KSh30,000 and introduce a 25 percent tax rate for monthly income between KSh30,000 and KSh50,000. These proposed tax changes come alongside increased National Social Security Fund (NSSF) contributions, effective from February 2026, which will further impact payroll costs for both employers and employees.
