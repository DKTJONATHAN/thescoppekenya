## Nairobi’s Economy Grinds to a Halt

A citywide security lockdown has effectively paralyzed Nairobi today, June 25, as a dense network of police roadblocks forces thousands of commuters to abandon public transport and navigate the capital on foot to reach their workplaces.

The economic cost of today's security operation is already mounting as thousands of man-hours are lost to gridlock and transit stagnation. While the government had publicly signaled that the city would remain open for business, the reality on the ground contradicts these assurances. By restricting the flow of Public Service Vehicles (PSVs) into the Central Business District, state authorities have imposed a de facto tax on the informal economy. Small-scale traders, who rely on daily turnover, are witnessing a collapse in foot traffic that threatens their bottom line for the entire week.

### The Financial Toll of Gridlock

From the early hours of dawn, the impact of the shutdown became clear along the major arteries of Thika Road and Mombasa Road. In Mlolongo, operators of long distance matatus reported an almost total cessation of movement. Drivers who typically complete three to four trips between the satellite towns and the city center were stuck in stationary lines for hours. For the average matatu crew, this represents a loss of at least 70 percent of their projected daily revenue. 

The financial friction extends far beyond the transport sector. Market stalls in Githurai and Juja remained largely shuttered as supply chains for perishable goods were broken by checkpoints at Kasarani Stadium and Kahawa. Unlike the high-stakes drama often seen in [Kenya Airways New York snowstorm: Stranded passengers' mayhem!](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenya-airways-new-york-snowstorm-stranded-passengers-mayhem), there is no luxury lounge for these commuters. They are left standing in the sun, unable to reach their jobs, effectively neutralizing the productivity of Nairobi’s workforce for the day.

### Winners and Losers in the Transit Freeze

The city is currently divided into two distinct camps: the winners and the losers of this security-led disruption. The winners are the state control machinery, which has successfully asserted a visible, physical dominance over public movement. By consolidating control points at Uthiru-87 junction and along Ngong Road, authorities have ensured that the city operates strictly on their terms. The losers are the everyday citizens, particularly those in the informal sector, who shoulder the brunt of the lost wages and interrupted commerce.

This is not merely a matter of traffic congestion. It is an aggregate financial hit to the city's gross output. Every hour a worker spends standing at a checkpoint on Waiyaki Way rather than at their desk or workshop is an hour of lost economic activity. The efficiency of Nairobi as a commercial hub has been replaced by a rigid system of stops and starts that renders local business planning impossible. Looking ahead, the recovery of these lost earnings is unlikely. As the sun begins to set, the transport sector will face the daunting task of untangling the logistical nightmare created by the roadblocks. The immediate concern for businesses now shifts to Friday morning, when they must determine if the security footprint will persist or if the city will be allowed to return to its standard operational capacity. The economic performance of the coming weekend will depend entirely on how quickly the roads are cleared and how much confidence the commuting public retains in the city's ability to facilitate their movement.
