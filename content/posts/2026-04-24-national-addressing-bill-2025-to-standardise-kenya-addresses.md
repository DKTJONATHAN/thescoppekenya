By Celestine Nzioka

A proposed National Addressing Bill 2025, currently under parliamentary review, aims to introduce a uniform national addressing system across the country. This legislation is designed to standardise the identification of homes, businesses, and public facilities, promising significant improvements in daily movement, navigation, and access to essential services for residents.

## Standardising National Addresses

The National Addressing Bill 2025 seeks to establish a consistent format for addresses, intending to eliminate duplication and enhance the accuracy of location identification. Roads Principal Secretary Joseph Mbugua explained to the Committee on Communication, Information and Innovation on April 23 that the proposed system would assign each address a comprehensive format, including street names, locality, county, and postal identifiers. This structure is intended to provide a clear, verifiable identity for every location, aligning with global standards.

Mbugua underscored the broader impact of a reliable addressing system, stating its importance extends beyond mere navigation to saving lives, enabling commerce, and ensuring all residents have access to crucial services. Committee members acknowledged that the current absence of a consistent framework has long hindered service delivery and navigation, especially in areas lacking clearly named roads or defined addresses. A formal system is expected to streamline daily life, facilitating postal deliveries, emergency service access, and the efficient use of digital platforms.

The government has already initiated foundational work through a multi-sectoral team comprising 16 public and private institutions. This team has been developing a policy framework, legislative structure, and standards for the system, with the ICT ministry presently reviewing these documents. Under the proposed framework, the Communications Authority of Kenya would oversee implementation, collaborating with national and county governments to ensure accurate road naming and location coding. Enhanced property identification could also benefit real estate, particularly in areas like [Mombasa's Kizingo neighborhood, which attracts high-profile residents and high-value property investments](https://zandani.co.ke/article/mombasa-s-kizingo-neighborhood-attracts-high-profile-residents-and-h).

However, Members of Parliament raised concerns about delays in the system's rollout. Kisumu Central MP Shakir Shabir criticised officials, noting that many residents still depend on informal directions despite the availability of modern tools. Shabir asserted that identifying and documenting roads is an executive responsibility that should not await parliamentary initiation. The committee will continue to receive submissions before tabling its report on the Bill.
