## Is Nollywood's Cinema System Stifling Its Own Success?
Nollywood's cinema exhibition model is indeed hindering its potential economic growth by being too narrow, overly expensive, and predominantly focused on urban elites, despite the industry's high production volume and significant audience demand. This limited access means only a fraction of the thousands of films produced annually reach the big screen, and even fewer Nigerians can afford or access them.

## The Scoop
Well, mate, it turns out Nigeria's incredibly vibrant film industry, Nollywood, which pumps out thousands of flicks every year, is facing a right pickle when it comes to getting those films seen in cinemas. The new "FilmOne 2025 Box Office Year Book" has spilled all the beans, and it's not looking pretty. We're talking about a system that's just too posh, too pricey, and frankly, too stingy with its screens to truly make a dent in Nigeria's economy. Can you even imagine? It's not about talent, or brilliant stories, or even people wanting to watch - it's all about access, or the scandalous lack thereof!

## The Evidence
Honestly, the numbers are a bit of a shocker. Nigeria, with a whopping 250 million people and a massive youth population just itching for entertainment, only pulled in about ₦16 billion at the box office. Now, that might sound like a lot, but in a country with a trillion-naira economy, it's peanuts, isn't it? Less than one in a hundred Nigerians actually bothered to visit a cinema in a whole year. That's just wild! And get this, a single blockbuster can hoover up nearly 10% of the entire annual revenue, which just screams "trouble ahead" for diversified investment. It's a bit like putting all your eggs in one very expensive basket, isn't it?

Then there's the ticket price palaver. At about ₦7,000 a pop, or even a scandalous ₦15,000 in some places, cinema trips are becoming a rare treat rather than a regular bit of fun for most folks, especially the youth and middle-income families. No wonder repeat viewing, which is absolutely vital for cinemas to thrive, is plummeting.

And the biggest head-scratcher? Nollywood makes roughly 2,000 films a year, but only a measly 81 local titles actually made it to the big screen within the reporting period. Meanwhile, foreign films are practically taking over! It's a proper bottleneck, preventing local filmmakers from earning a decent crust and stopping audiences from seeing a wider variety of Nigerian stories. With only about 122 cinemas across Nigeria, Ghana, and Liberia combined, and most of those tucked away in fancy urban malls, it’s clear the infrastructure is just not there for the masses. Interestingly, while some reports suggest overall box office growth and Lagos dominating earnings, the core issue of accessibility for the wider population remains a glaring problem. There have even been whispers, and not-so-whispers, of filmmakers accusing cinemas of "sabotage" with unfair showtimes and promotion of certain films over others, particularly during big holiday seasons. It certainly makes you wonder who's really running the show, eh?

## The Verdict
Honestly, it's a bit of a shambles, isn't it? Nollywood's got the creative genius, the production power, and a massive, enthusiastic audience, but it's all locked behind a velvet rope! The industry needs to ditch this "urban luxury" model and embrace community and campus cinemas, maybe even mobile ones. Imagine the ripple effect - more jobs, a boost to the cultural economy, and Nigeria's soft power soaring globally! The future isn't about how many films are made; it's about how many eyes get to see them!
