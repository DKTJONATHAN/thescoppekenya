## Fiscal discipline or an early electoral lock-down?

The National Treasury has fast-tracked the 2027/28 budget preparation cycle, officially imposing a moratorium on the creation of new pending bills. While the directive cites improved fiscal oversight, the timing suggests a desperate attempt to throttle the pre-election spending surge that historically drains the public coffers.

John Mbadi’s office is betting that an early start will insulate the Treasury from the pressures of the 2027 General Election. By locking the budget framework in now, the government aims to close the loopholes that usually see ministries and state departments ramp up procurement as political survival becomes the primary objective. This is not mere administrative diligence. It is a strategic attempt to preempt the fiscal recklessness that turns every election cycle into an economic disaster.

### The arithmetic of political survival

History shows a reliable correlation between Kenya’s election cycles and an uncontrollable spike in pending bills. As the countdown to the polls begins, contractors with political connections often secure lucrative government tenders, many of which remain unpaid until the new administration takes over. By mandating a zero-tolerance policy on new bills now, the Treasury is effectively trying to prevent the next administration from inheriting a poisoned chalice of debt.

The government claims that by settling historical road sector arrears dating back to 2005, they are clearing the deck. Yet, the skepticism is palpable. Clearing ancient debt while demanding total austerity on new projects creates an uneven playing field. It smells like a cleanup operation designed to sanitize the books before the real political lobbying begins. While [Nairobi County fights to cut its own massive tax bill](https://zandani.co.ke/article/tax-appeals-tribunal-cuts-nairobi-county-tax-bill) in the courts, the national government is demanding a level of fiscal precision that has never been successfully enforced in the heat of a campaign season.

### Can the taps truly be turned off?

The technical enforcement of a zero pending bills policy is a fantasy when political campaigns require deep pockets. In reality, the budget is not just a spreadsheet; it is a tool for patronage. As officials prepare the 2027/28 cycle, they must reconcile the need for economic growth figures like 5.3 percent with the reality that campaign cash often flows through diverted procurement channels. 

The pressure is mounting. With [protests over economic grievances becoming a fixture](https://zandani.co.ke/article/murkomen-orders-police-protection-nairobi-protests-declared-illegal) in the central business district, the Treasury knows that any sign of fiscal instability will provide fuel for the opposition. If they can prevent the usual election-year bloating, they might survive the transition. However, the disconnect between official circulars and the behavior of junior officials in procurement departments remains wide. You can ban pending bills on paper, but you cannot easily ban the influence of political donors who expect their pound of flesh from government contracts.

The Treasury is essentially asking its own departments to stop the very behavior that makes political careers possible. They are demanding that officials look at the 3.2 percent to 6.7 percent growth targets and choose long-term stability over the immediate requirement to bankroll a campaign. As the 2026/27 cycle grinds on and the transition to the 2029/30 horizon nears, the math simply does not add up for the political class. If the Treasury manages to hold this line, it will be the first time in modern Kenyan history that fiscal policy triumphs over election-year greed. Can Mbadi truly keep the door locked when his own colleagues hold the key?
