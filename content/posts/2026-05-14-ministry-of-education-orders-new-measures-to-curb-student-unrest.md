## Curbing Student Unrest in the Kenyan School System

As the Ministry of Education issues new directives to stem a wave of school fires, observers recognize a familiar rhythm. These administrative reforms attempt to force systemic order onto a recurring cycle of student frustration that predates the current academic calendar.

The Ministry of Education, led by Principal Secretary Julius Bitok, met with school administrators on May 14 to formalize a new mandate. Schools are now required to establish early warning systems and open direct channels of communication between students and management. This latest push arrives as secondary institutions from Embu to Kericho grapple with sudden, destructive disruptions. While officials frame these measures as a tactical response to recent property damage, the timing suggests an uncomfortable alignment with past trends. When school communities face high-pressure academic periods, the history of Kenyan education suggests that administrative circulars often arrive too late to alter the underlying social temperature of the classroom.

### A History of Circulars and Consequences

The pattern of the Kenyan "arson season" typically follows a rigid timeline, appearing with the same predictability as the midterm lull.

* July 2021: A massive surge in school fires led the Ministry to advocate for increased surveillance and police presence, shifts that momentarily suppressed incidents but failed to address the root causes of the student pushback.
* June 2023: Another spike in campus instability resulted in a series of circulars demanding stricter gatekeeping protocols, which were largely ignored once the academic cycle moved into the exam preparation phase.
* May 2026: The current directive prioritizes early warning systems over punitive measures, shifting the focus toward grievance management rather than simply increasing security patrols or student arrests.

These cyclical responses demonstrate a reliance on top-down mandates. History shows that when the Ministry releases a flurry of directives, it is often reacting to a contagion effect where student frustrations in one region ignite similar displays elsewhere. Previous disciplinary mandates, such as the 2023 surveillance push, provided short-term security but struggled to create genuine dialogue within the school walls. The current push for "early warning" is a departure in tone, yet it assumes that students possess an institutional avenue they trust enough to use before resorting to fire or violence.
