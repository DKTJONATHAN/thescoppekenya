## KeNHA Progress on the Ksh 100 Billion Isiolo-Mandera Highway

As construction milestones for the 740-kilometre Isiolo-Mandera Highway come into focus, Kenyans are looking beyond the tarmac to the economic reality of the 2028 completion date. This project represents a massive shift in how Northern Kenya connects to the national economy.

### Why is the 2028 completion date significant for the national budget?

The Kenya National Highways Authority (KeNHA) has set January 31, 2028, as the firm target for completing this massive infrastructure project. While previous government estimates suggested an earlier finish in 2027, the current timeline reflects the reality of building in a complex terrain. This project is not just about moving dirt; it is a Ksh 100 billion investment that impacts the national debt. A significant portion of this funding comes from a Ksh 37.6 billion World Bank loan specifically allocated for 518 kilometres of the route.

When project timelines shift, the cost of financing often moves with them. Every month of delay can result in increased interest payments or rising costs for materials. For the average taxpayer, this means the efficiency of KeNHA in Wajir County is directly linked to how much fiscal space the government has for other services. While the state manages these billion-shilling debts, it must still maintain social safety nets. For instance, the [Government disburses Inua Jamii funds to over 430,000 vulnerable households](https://zandani.co.ke/article/government-disburses-inua-jamii-funds-to-over-430-000-vulnerable-hou) to ensure that large scale infrastructure spending does not come at the expense of the most at-risk citizens.

### How much of the highway is actually paved today?

Progress is currently measured in specific segments, particularly within Wajir County. Recent reports show that the 57-kilometre section between Wajir and Tarbaj has reached a 17 per cent completion rate. Further along, the 64-kilometre stretch from Tarbaj to Kotulo is more advanced, with 30 per cent of the work finalized. These numbers are the pulse of the project.

Beyond the road itself, the plan includes a 2-kilometre spur road and a commitment to environmental restoration. KeNHA intends to plant 6,000 trees along the corridor, a move aimed at hitting national targets for forest cover by 2050. This focus on environmental standards is becoming a requirement for major projects, mirroring the strictness seen when [NEMA shuts down Mlolongo factory over Nairobi River pollution](https://zandani.co.ke/article/nema-shuts-down-mlolongo-factory-nairobi-river-pollution) to protect local ecosystems. For the residents of Isiolo and Mandera, these percentages translate into how soon they can expect a reduction in the time it takes to move livestock and produce to market.

### What does this road mean for the cost of living in Northern Kenya?

Currently, transporting goods to the northern frontier is expensive due to the wear and tear on vehicles and the long hours spent on broken roads. When the highway is finished, the cost of essential commodities is expected to drop as transport logistics become more predictable. This is a long-term play for regional economic stability. By linking Isiolo to Mandera, the government is opening up a 1,270-kilometre corridor that connects Kenya to its neighbors, potentially turning Wajir into a major transit hub.

The financial scale of this project also highlights the growing role of major investments in the Kenyan economy. Just as [Fintrust Securities Licensed as Authorized Securities Dealer](https://zandani.co.ke/article/fintrust-securities-licensed-as-authorized-securities-dealer) signals a more robust financial sector, the completion of a Ksh 100 billion road signals to international investors that Northern Kenya is open for large-scale business. For the local shopkeeper in Mandera, the 2028 date is the point at which the "northern frontier tax" on their inventory finally begins to vanish.

### Why it matters now
The confirmation of the 2028 date provides a realistic baseline for businesses to plan their expansion into Northern Kenya. As KeNHA balances the Ksh 37.6 billion World Bank funding with local construction challenges, the project serves as a test of Kenya's ability to deliver high-value infrastructure without further straining the national purse.

### What to watch next
Keep a close eye on the quarterly progress reports for the Wajir-Tarbaj section. If the 17 per cent completion rate does not double by mid-2027, the January 2028 target may come under pressure. Additionally, look for updates on the procurement of the remaining segments that have yet to see significant boots on the ground.

A road is only as valuable as the trade it facilitates and the debt it justifies.
