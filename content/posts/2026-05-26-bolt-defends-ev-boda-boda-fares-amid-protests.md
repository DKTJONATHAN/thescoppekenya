## Bolt's Green Dream, Rider's Debt Reality

Bolt's recent clarification on electric motorcycle fares rings hollow for operators grappling with a deepening financial paradox. While the platform touts lower operational costs for EVs, it conspicuously overlooks the substantial asset financing burden placed squarely on riders, creating a stark imbalance in Kenya's rapidly evolving gig economy.

Gig economy operators powering electric boda bodas across Kenya staged protests on Monday, vocalising a growing frustration over what they term unsustainable earnings and opaque fare structures. Bolt, in response, quickly moved to justify its pricing for electric two-wheelers, insisting that these fares are naturally set lower than their petrol counterparts due to reduced running expenses. Curiously, this defense arrives just weeks after the same platform initiated a six per cent fare increase for its petrol-powered cab services, a move attributed to rising fuel costs, a concession apparently not extended in spirit to the electric sector's unique financial pressures.

### The Unseen Cost of Electrification

The platform vehemently denied any increase in its commission rates, yet this sidesteps the core grievance. For operators, the equation isn't just about fuel or electricity costs; it's a multi-layered financial entanglement. Many electric motorcycle riders are locked into daily lease-to-own agreements or battery-swap schemes, costs that often dwarf the savings gained from cheaper electricity. These daily outgoings - sometimes as high as KES 500-700 - are the elephant in the room, ignored by Bolt’s narrative of 'reduced operational costs'. While a petrol boda boda operator faces daily fuel expenses, their initial asset purchase might have been lower, or fully owned, allowing more flexibility. The EV rider, conversely, faces a perpetual daily debt, often for bikes that remain the property of third-party financiers until years of payments are complete.

The disparity creates a chasm in net daily take-home pay. A petrol operator, despite higher fuel costs, often retains a larger proportion of their earnings after deducting a one-off purchase cost. The EV rider, even with 'lower operational costs' as defined by Bolt, finds their gross earnings siphoned off by relentless asset financing, leaving less disposable income. The "green transition" championed by ride-hailing platforms and government initiatives becomes a financial squeeze for those on the ground, struggling to navigate the transition while simultaneously financing the new infrastructure. This situation contrasts sharply with the ease of capital flow observed in other sectors, such as when Asahi Group secured regulatory approval for its EABL stake acquisition, highlighting a different standard of financial scrutiny for large corporations versus individual gig workers.

### A Cycle of Debt, Not Savings

Bolt’s messaging implies that lower fares for electric bikes are a direct benefit passed on to consumers, enabled by cheaper 'fuel'. What it does not address is whether this benefit is simultaneously being extracted from the operator through a heavy debt burden, effectively making the rider a silent financier of the platform's competitive pricing. The absence of a transparent breakdown of how Bolt’s fare algorithms factor in the operators' daily lease payments or battery subscriptions is glaring. The "tens of riders" who protested on Monday were not demanding a return to petrol, but a fair and sustainable economic model for the future.

While the rhetoric centres on environmental benefits, the practical outcome for many EV boda boda riders is a precarious existence, trading one set of overheads for another that might be even more restrictive and debt-laden. It is a system where the risk and upfront capital outlay for the "green" infrastructure falls largely on the individual, while the platform reaps the rewards of competitive pricing and a cleaner image.

Is Bolt's "green" initiative genuinely about sustainable livelihoods, or is it a clever financial maneuver designed to shift capital costs onto the most vulnerable actors in the gig economy?
