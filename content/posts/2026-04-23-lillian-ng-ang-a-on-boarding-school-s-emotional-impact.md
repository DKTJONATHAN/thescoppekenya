Kenyan public figure Lillian Ng'ang'a recently shared her personal reflections on boarding schools, sparking considerable online conversation regarding their emotional impact on young children. She questioned the practice of sending children under 13 to such institutions, advocating for home-based growth.

## Lillian Ng'ang'a's Take on Boarding School Dynamics

Nganga, known for her candidness, ignited discussions through a series of Instagram reels. She critically described boarding school for younger children as a “mzungu concept,” suggesting it could foster emotional distance, loneliness, and even mental health challenges for developing individuals. Her perspective highlights a growing conversation around traditional upbringing versus modern psychological well-being.

### Emphasising Holistic Nurturing

The public figure underscored the importance of a holistic upbringing, arguing that crucial learning extends far beyond formal classrooms into everyday family and community life. She maintained that institutional settings often restrict this broader developmental scope, placing children in environments where peer influence can sometimes overshadow direct parental guidance. This emphasis on local community support and upbringing resonates with ongoing public conversations about civic amenities and local influence, such as the recent community discussions surrounding the proposed [closure of Capitol Hill Police Station](https://zandani.co.ke/article/residents-oppose-planned-closure-of-capitol-hill-police-station).

Nganga further elaborated that educators in such environments primarily focus on academic achievement and discipline. This dynamic, she argued, often leaves children to be shaped largely by their peers, potentially weakening emotional bonding and real-life grounding during their crucial developmental years.

### A Personal Revelation

Adding a personal touch to her narrative, Nganga recounted her own experience attending boarding school during her primary education. While she acknowledged the support from Catholic sisters, she openly admitted that the separation from her parents and community created an emotional void she later had to address.

She also shared a challenging period when she took her firstborn child to boarding school after experiencing the loss of her husband and relocating abroad for work. Although she observed that the experience fostered discipline and responsibility in her child, Nganga confessed to later regretting the emotional distance it inadvertently created.

Her forthright remarks have generated varied reactions across online platforms. Some users have expressed agreement with her views on parenting and child development, while others have staunchly defended the merits of boarding schools in cultivating independence and discipline among young Kenyans. The dialogue continues to unfold as many weigh in on this deeply personal and culturally significant topic.
