The Kenya Meteorological Department has issued a flood alert for Nairobi, Murang'a, Kisii, and several other counties, forecasting heavy rains from April 7 to 13, 2026. This warning, detailed in the Biometeorological Weekly Bulletin, highlights a moderate flood risk across multiple regions of the country.

Elevated Flood Risk Across Key Regions

Rainfall Intensifies Across Highlands and Western Regions The latest bulletin from Kenya Met indicates that Nairobi, Murang'a, Nyeri, Embu, Kirinyaga, Meru, and Tharaka Nithi are projected to receive cumulative rainfall exceeding 50mm this week, an amount capable of triggering widespread flooding. Similar downpours are anticipated in Kisii, Bomet, Migori, Homabay, Kericho, Narok, Vihiga, Busia, and Kakamega counties, where low-lying areas face a real possibility of inundation. Up north, Moyale in Marsabit County is flagged as a high-concern zone, with over 50mm of cumulative rainfall expected and a moderate flood risk identified through April 13. These areas had been experiencing significant drought conditions for much of this year and late last year, as detailed in previous Za Ndani reports such as [Stranded Up North: Our Journalist's Secret Struggles in Drought-Stricken Northern Kenya!](https://zandani.co.ke/article/stranded-up-north-journalist-secret-struggles-drought-stricken-northern-kenya). Kitui and Taita Taveta counties are also expected to record above-normal rainfall, with isolated areas experiencing levels beyond typical averages during this period.

Public Health Officials Sound Alarm on Disease Outbreaks The expected heavy rainfall carries significant public health implications. Stagnant floodwater is raising alarm bells among health officials, with Kenya Met pointing to a heightened risk of mosquito-borne diseases including malaria, dengue fever, Rift Valley Fever, and chikungunya across all affected counties this week. Waterborne diseases pose an equally urgent concern. Cholera, diarrhoea, and typhoid are all likely to surge wherever floodwaters contaminate water sources, making access to clean drinking water critically important right now. The department explicitly stressed an "Increased risk of mosquito-borne diseases (Malaria, Dengue, Rift Valley Fever, Chikungunya) and Waterborne diseases (Cholera, Diarrhoea, Typhoid) due to rainfall and stagnant waters."

Cold Snap Poses Health Threats in Highland Areas Compounding the dangers, parts of Nyandarua, Nyeri, and Meru counties are forecast to record minimum temperatures of 10 degrees Celsius or below. This range is well outside normal for this time of year. Nyahururu in Nyandarua County faces the sharpest cold-weather health threat, with the bulletin warning of moderate risks of pneumonia, flu, bronchitis, influenza, and asthma for residents in the coming days. Children, the elderly, and those living with chronic illnesses are identified as most vulnerable to cardiovascular complications from cold snaps.

Urgent Precautions Issued for Public Safety Kenya Met is urging all residents to drain stagnant water, sleep under mosquito nets, and avoid flooded and landslide-prone zones as the country enters what forecasters describe as a high-risk weather week. Residents in colder highland areas are further advised to dress warmly and stay updated with weather information from the department, as dangerous weather conditions are expected to persist throughout the week.
