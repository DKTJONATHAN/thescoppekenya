## Treasury Bills and the Hidden Cost of Your Loan

The Central Bank of Kenya has halted its cycle of benchmark rate cuts, triggering a sharp reversal in Treasury bill yields. This shift is not just a technical market adjustment; it is an immediate signal that credit for Kenyan households and small businesses is set to become more expensive and harder to secure.

The current climate marks a departure from the recent trend of easing monetary conditions. For months, the market benefited from a decline in yields that encouraged cheaper borrowing. Today, that momentum has stalled. With the 91-day rate climbing toward 16.73 percent and similar pressures felt across the 182-day and 364-day papers, the domestic debt market is signaling a return to high-interest territory.

### The Crowding Out Effect on Your Wallet

When the government sets a domestic borrowing target of KSh 1.1 trillion, it essentially enters the local market as the biggest competitor for available cash. Banks, facing less risk by lending to the government through Treasury bills, naturally scale back on loans to private individuals and SMEs. This process, known as crowding out, means that even if you have a solid business plan, your local lender is often more interested in buying government paper than funding your expansion.

This dynamic directly inflates the cost of credit. Because banks can achieve double-digit returns by lending to the state, they demand higher interest rates from private borrowers to make the risk worthwhile. For a small shop owner or an individual looking for a personal loan, this translates to steeper monthly repayments. The irony is that while the government finances its deficit, the average Kenyan pays the price through restricted access to capital and higher borrowing costs.

### Inflationary Pressures and the Budget Squeeze

The broader economic environment is compounding this stress. Headline inflation has climbed to 5.6 percent, but the reality for most families is harsher. With transportation inflation sitting at 10 percent, the cost of moving goods and people is eroding disposable income. When you combine high transport costs with the rising interest rates on loans, household budgets are being squeezed from two sides. 

Just as we have seen digital innovation reshape how students prepare for exams, such as the upcoming [KNEC transition to digital platforms](https://zandani.co.ke/article/knec-to-transition-senior-school-exams-to-digital-platforms), the financial sector is also undergoing a transition, though one that currently favors debt servicing over investment. When credit is expensive, families put off large purchases, and businesses postpone upgrades. The local economy slows because the money that should be circulating in production is instead locked into government debt instruments.

### Why it Matters Now

The decision by the Central Bank to maintain the benchmark rate at 8.75 percent represents a strategic pivot. By keeping rates steady despite economic pressure, the regulator is choosing to prioritize currency stability and inflation control over the immediate need for cheaper credit. However, this creates a vacuum where the government's massive borrowing requirement dictates the price of money for everyone else. Understanding this link is essential for anyone currently managing a household budget or running a business.

### What to Watch Next

Keep an eye on the upcoming fiscal policy announcements and the state of talks regarding international financing. If the government secures external funding, the pressure on domestic borrowing might ease, potentially allowing T-bill yields to stabilize. Conversely, if the 1.1 trillion target remains the primary focus of the Treasury, expect banks to remain selective with their lending. High government demand for cash is the primary anchor keeping your personal loan rates elevated.

History shows that periods of heavy government borrowing rarely coexist with affordable private credit.
