## Corporate Resilience Amidst Kenya's Political Realities Britam Holdings achieved a significant Ksh7.9 billion pre-tax profit in 2025, marking an 8 percent increase despite a challenging operating environment characterized by increased insurance claims from political violence and floods. This performance underscores corporate agility and strategic execution amidst Kenya's complex political and economic landscape.

Economic Performance Under Duress Britam's 2025 financial results demonstrate a robust performance, with pre-tax profit rising to Ksh7.9 billion from Ksh7.3 billion in 2024, and net profit growing 10 percent to Ksh5.5 billion. This growth occurred despite a reported surge in insurance claims attributed to both natural disasters like floods and internal political violence. The company's insurance revenue increased by 11 percent to Ksh41.7 billion, driven by strong performance in its Life and General Insurance businesses. Net investment income also saw a 4 percent rise to Ksh31.9 billion, supported by consistent portfolio returns. These figures suggest that while the political climate presented hurdles, Britam's strategic allocation of assets and disciplined management contributed significantly to its financial resilience.

Political Unrest and Business Environment The mention of "political violence" as a factor increasing insurance claims highlights a persistent challenge for businesses operating in Kenya. Throughout 2025, Kenya experienced cycles of protests, particularly between June and July, which often turned violent, leading to property damage and business disruptions. These demonstrations, driven by public frustration over the cost of living, taxation policies, and governance concerns, posed a significant test for investor confidence and overall economic stability. Such unrest creates an unpredictable operational environment, increasing risks for insurance providers and other sectors. The government's response to these protests, including the use of force and the application of anti-terrorism laws against some protestors, further shaped the business climate by influencing perceptions of rule of law and political stability. This period also saw ongoing public debate regarding government policies and their impact on citizens and businesses, a dynamic seen in legislative developments such as the NTSA instant fines update, which reflects evolving regulatory frameworks affecting various industries.

Governance, Strategy, and Future Outlook Britam's ability to navigate increased claims and a challenging environment speaks to effective internal strategies, including customer-centric innovation and investments in technology and talent. The closing of their 2021-2025 EPIC² Strategy, which aimed to restore profitability and accelerate digital adoption, coincided with this strong performance. However, the broader economic outlook for Kenya in 2025 indicated mixed sentiments, with some businesses expressing less optimism due to high taxation, unfavorable government policies, and limited access to capital. Concerns over public debt, which absorbs a significant portion of government revenue, and policy uncertainty also played a role in shaping investor sentiment. Discussions surrounding the transparency and implications of state asset management, as evidenced by legal challenges like those related to the Kenya Pipeline sale, also contribute to the broader perception of governance stability affecting investor confidence. Despite these challenges, sectors such as agriculture and financial services showed resilience, and the country's GDP growth projections indicated steady expansion. Britam's decision not to recommend a dividend for 2025, despite strong profits, indicates a prudent approach to balance sheet management amid the complex macroeconomic and political conditions.
