## Why your daily commute remains expensive despite falling fuel costs EPRA Announces Fuel Price Reductions for June to July Cycle is the central subject of the update.

The Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority has published its latest fuel schedule for mid-June to mid-July. This update brings a welcome Ksh10.00 reduction for diesel and a slight downward adjustment for super petrol. Even with these savings at the pump, passengers across the country have yet to see any change in the fares charged for their daily travel.

The regulatory authority confirmed that a litre of diesel in Nairobi now retails at Ksh191.38, while super petrol is priced at Ksh214.03. In a correction to previous figures, kerosene is now priced at Ksh190.94, reflecting the government's ongoing effort to manage costs for essential household energy. These adjustments mirror a broader stabilization in global oil markets, yet the public transport sector continues to operate as if these price cuts never occurred.

### Do pump price drops actually lower transport costs?

The immediate impact on the average commuter is virtually non-existent. Public Service Vehicle operators have kept their fare schedules unchanged, meaning passengers continue to pay rates that were set during periods of significantly higher fuel costs. Kenya's transport market operates on a sticky pricing model. When fuel prices climb, operators quickly raise fares to protect their margins. Conversely, when fuel costs retreat, there is no automatic mechanism in place to ensure those savings are passed on to the public. Consequently, the relief intended by government interventions and fuel subsidies is often absorbed by industry intermediaries instead of providing financial breathing room for the average worker.

### Why government interventions struggle to reach the household level

The Petroleum Development Levy Fund was designed to shield consumers from the volatility of global energy markets. However, the current framework is incomplete because it lacks a regulatory connection to secondary transport costs. Financial experts note that without specific policy mandates linking bus fares to fuel price fluctuations, subsidies only serve to stabilize pump prices rather than easing the overall cost of living.

While the state utilizes billions of shillings to ensure the fuel supply chain remains functional, these funds fail to reach the ordinary citizen who allocates a large portion of their monthly income to transport. Commuters find themselves in a systemic trap where they pay for operational costs based on historic peaks, despite evidence that the underlying price of fuel has declined. This reflects a broader economic frustration where official price adjustments exist on paper but fail to translate into tangible relief for those navigating the city.

### The missing regulatory link

The core issue lies in the lack of an enforceable policy trigger. While regulatory agencies oversee the transition of fuel from importers to petrol stations, their authority over the fare-setting practices of matatu associations and Saccos is minimal. These groups often dictate prices based on past highs and face little incentive to lower them when fuel becomes more affordable. Because the matatu industry remains largely fragmented and informal, institutions like the National Transport and Safety Authority find it difficult to implement and enforce unified fare structures.

Until the government introduces a formal, automated policy that ties mandatory fare reviews to fuel price shifts, transport costs will remain artificially rigid. The current policy focus prioritizes macro-level stability for fuel imports while largely ignoring the micro-level behavior of those operating on the ground. Much like the noise surrounding viral digital trends or the influence of local celebrity gossip, the daily financial burden on the workforce is often overshadowed by peripheral discussions. The recent reduction in diesel prices serves as a technical correction on a ledger, but until those savings reflect in the fare paid by a person boarding a bus, the policy remains an incomplete economic measure.

Lowering pump prices is only one half of the equation. True economic relief for the commuter will only arrive when transport providers are held accountable for passing those savings down the line.
