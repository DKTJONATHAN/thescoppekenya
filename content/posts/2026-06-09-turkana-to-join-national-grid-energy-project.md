## The Billion-Shilling Mirage: Why Plugging Turkana into the Grid Won't Ignite Industry

The government suggests that connecting Lodwar to the national electricity grid by September 2026 will transform Turkana into an industrial powerhouse. This narrative is a dangerous oversimplification that confuses the presence of power lines with the creation of economic capacity.

### The Diesel Trap and the Industrial Vacuum

For years, the state has poured 900 million Kenyan Shillings annually into diesel generation to keep Lodwar’s lights flickering. Officials argue that replacing this volatile expense with a 1.0 billion Shilling transmission line will slash operational costs for local businesses, triggering a manufacturing boom. Yet, this logic ignores the reality on the ground. A survey of local small and medium enterprises reveals that electricity costs rarely rank among their top three existential threats. Entrepreneurs in Turkana consistently cite the lack of reliable transport links, the absence of specialized cold chain storage, and the scarcity of skilled labor as the primary bottlenecks to growth. Power is merely an input; it does not replace the necessity of supply chain stability or a localized industrial base.

History offers a sobering corrective. We have seen this experiment play out in other peripheral regions where grid connectivity arrived with fanfare, only to result in a stagnant landscape of residential consumption. Without a deliberate, state-supported industrial strategy to process local produce or tap into the trade corridor between Juba and Mombasa, the substation in Lodwar will likely serve the same purpose as the previous diesel generators: keeping streetlights on and powering mobile phone chargers.

### The Myth of Build It and They Will Come

The prevailing optimism assumes that private capital will spontaneously flow into a region simply because the voltage is stable. This ignores the geography of risk. Turkana is subject to extreme climate volatility and profound structural isolation that no amount of 66kV cabling can mitigate. If the government’s recent maneuvers, such as the [tactical shifts observed in Nyanza politics](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-21-wanga-s-wobble-ruto-s-nyanza-takeover-leaves-odm-leaders-sweating), have taught us anything, it is that infrastructure projects are often repurposed as tools for political signaling rather than economic engines. 

The focus on the transmission line from Turkwel suggests a top-down bureaucratic mindset that prioritizes electrification milestones over long-term economic integration. If the goal were genuinely to ignite industry, the investment would be coupled with aggressive tax incentives for manufacturing startups or the creation of dedicated agro-processing zones. Instead, we are presented with a technical fix for a structural deficiency.

### The Reality of Modern Economic Development

We should stop viewing this project as an inevitable spark for commerce. A grid connection is a baseline utility, not a catalyst for industrialization. By framing it as a shift that will naturally lead to prosperity, planners are setting the region up for a failure of expectations. True development in the North requires more than plugging into the grid; it requires the hardening of the transit route to Juba, the formalization of cross-border trade mechanisms, and the development of a workforce that can handle more than just retail services.

While urban centers in the heartland might leverage grid expansion to power machinery, in Turkana, the grid will likely remain a subsidised luxury for residential use. The irony is that the 900 million Shilling annual subsidy we currently waste on diesel is merely being swapped for a massive capital expenditure that ignores the real market hurdles. Until the government addresses why businesses in Lodwar are fundamentally isolated from the broader Kenyan economy, this billion-shilling investment is just another monument to poor planning. Electricity is a prerequisite for progress, but it is not the progress itself. If we confuse the two, we will be back here in five years, wondering why the factories were never built.
