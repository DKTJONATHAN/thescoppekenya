## Safaricom Performance as a Template for Public Sector Reform

Safaricom has hit the Sh400 billion revenue mark, offering a stark lesson to our struggling state agencies. While public institutions rely on bailouts, this corporate success proves that operational discipline and market innovation are the only paths to sustainable institutional survival.

To the Chiefs of Kenya's Parastatals:

The latest financial results from Safaricom are not merely a corporate milestone; they are an indictment of the way many of our state agencies are managed. While you draft memoranda for government bailouts, the private sector has just crossed the Sh400 billion revenue threshold. This is not a matter of luck. It is a matter of institutional accountability. When a state agency fails, the taxpayer absorbs the cost. When a private firm fails, it loses its market share. This asymmetry is the rot at the heart of our public service.

### The Myth of Public Sector Unprofitability

We are frequently told that parastatals cannot be expected to turn a profit because they provide essential services. This is a convenient falsehood used to excuse incompetence. Safaricom’s growth across its connectivity and M-Pesa segments-with mobile data rising by 14.4 percent and M-Pesa revenue climbing by 13.4 percent-proves that essential service delivery is, in fact, the most lucrative sector if managed with precision. 

Consider the culture of apathy currently festering in our county assemblies, such as the recent [EACC probe into Embu MCAs over a Ksh95 million tender scandal](https://zandani.co.ke/article/eacc-probes-embu-mcas-over-alleged-ksh95-million-tender-scandal). While public funds are diverted into the pockets of a few through opaque procurement, market-driven entities are using technology to refine their service delivery. You treat your mandates as fiefdoms of patronage, while the private sector treats every client as a stakeholder. If you applied the same rigorous audit standards to your ledgers that shareholders demand of private firms, we would not need to discuss the recurring bailouts that keep your doors open.

### Lessons from the Ethiopian Turnaround

The most damning contrast lies in Safaricom’s expansion into Ethiopia. While many of our local state agencies have never left their home districts and still fail to operate effectively, this firm entered a complex foreign market and managed to slash its losses by 50 percent in record time. They did not do this by lobbying the state for subsidies. They did it through aggressive efficiency and a clear strategy for growth. 

John Stuart Mill, in his observations on the progress of society, understood that individual and corporate development require the presence of challenge. Our parastatals have removed the challenge of competition by leaning on the state for protection. You are shielded from the consequences of your own lethargy. As long as you know that the treasury will write a check to cover your operational deficits, you will have no incentive to innovate.

### The Ethical Obligation of Leadership

True leadership is not about maintaining the status quo or securing the next budget allocation. It is about the ethical responsibility to provide value for every shilling received. When you oversee a government agency, you are the steward of public trust. When that agency loses money year after year while its leadership remains unchanged, you are not just failing at your job; you are actively undermining the economic stability of the nation. 

Stop viewing your positions as career appointments and start viewing them as service contracts. If you cannot produce results, you do not deserve the office. The private sector is showing you exactly what is possible when you prioritize data, technology, and customer-centric growth over bureaucratic bloat.

### Why it matters now

The Kenyan economy is under immense strain. Every shilling lost to the mismanagement of a parastatal is a shilling that could have been used for infrastructure or debt relief. We can no longer afford to subsidize the incompetence of state-owned entities that refuse to adopt modern operational standards.

### What to watch next

Observe how many of these agencies attempt to deflect from this benchmark by citing regulatory hurdles or "unique" circumstances. Look for a shift in how the Auditor General scrutinizes these institutions. If there is no political will to fire the leadership of failing agencies, the Sh400 billion benchmark will remain a distant dream for the public sector.

I ask you today: what specific policy, process, or personnel change are you implementing this week to move your agency toward self-sustainability? Stop waiting for the next budget cycle and start cleaning your own house.
