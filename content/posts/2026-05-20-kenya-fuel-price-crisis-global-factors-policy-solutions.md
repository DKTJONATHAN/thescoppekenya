Fuel Prices and the Limits of Public Protest

To every citizen currently waiting in line at a petrol station or raising their voice in protest over the surging cost of fuel, I address you not to dismiss your frustration, but to redirect it. While your indignation is entirely justified, targeting local leadership ignores the fundamental mechanics of the global energy market. It is time we confront the reality that our current economic hardship is a byproduct of international volatility rather than a failure of domestic administration.

## The Global Origins of Local Hardship

The anger displayed in our streets often stems from a misunderstanding of how fuel is priced. When the cost of crude oil surpasses 100 dollars per barrel due to geopolitical instability in the Strait of Hormuz, the impact on local consumers is inevitable. You are reacting to the symptoms of a crisis, yet the root cause remains located thousands of miles away in the Middle East. When global supply chains fracture, the price of a liter of fuel at a pump in Mombasa becomes a hostage to international maneuvers that no amount of local demonstration can alter.

The state has not been passive in the face of these headwinds. Authorities have already disbursed 11.5 billion shillings in subsidies during the most recent quarter to act as a buffer against the full weight of market forces. Even with this significant intervention, the base cost of importing refined products remains strictly tethered to an unstable global index. It is important to recognize that burning tires or disrupting traffic does not decrease the international price of crude oil, nor does it compel OPEC+ members to adjust production quotas for our benefit.

## Shifting Focus from the Streets to the Statehouse

If we genuinely desire a change in our cost of living, we must move our attention from the chaos of public protests to the strategic precision of our legislature. Parliament has already demonstrated its ability to influence domestic prices by reducing the VAT on fuel from 16 per cent to 8 per cent. This fiscal adjustment is a functional tool intended to lessen the burden on the average household. 

Meaningful progress occurs within the halls of Parliament, not on public roads where civil agitation often leads to disorder. We must look no further than recent reports of passengers being harmed during transport disruptions to see that street-level unrest undermines our daily stability. Instead of reactive protests that hinder the very economy we aim to protect, we should demand a rigorous audit of local petroleum margins and advocate for a transition toward long-term energy independence. We need sustained, organized pressure on our legislators to refine taxation models and negotiate better procurement terms.

## The Necessity of Targeted Advocacy

The current crisis is steadily eroding the purchasing power of every Kenyan, driving transportation costs to levels that many can no longer sustain. If we fail to distinguish between issues dictated by global markets and those manageable through domestic policy, we will continue to waste our social capital on demonstrations that cannot physically reduce the cost of a barrel of oil. Recognizing this distinction is essential to avoiding national fatigue and ensuring that our policy advocacy remains productive rather than merely performative.

## Critical Areas for Ongoing Oversight

As we navigate the coming months, keep your attention fixed on the parliamentary debates regarding the potential extension of the 8 per cent VAT adjustment. It is vital to observe whether the government pursues bilateral agreements to secure more stable fuel sources or if further tax concessions will be considered to mitigate prices that have reached the 260 shilling per liter mark. Furthermore, monitor the fiscal consequences of the 11.5 billion shilling subsidy payout to determine if the Treasury maintains the capacity to sustain such measures should the 100 dollar per barrel threshold hold firm through the third quarter.

Direct your energy toward your parliamentary representatives. Demand absolute transparency regarding the management of the fuel levy and insist on a policy framework that prioritizes economic efficiency over short-term political theater. While the price at the pump is determined by a global tally, your vote remains the most powerful currency available to influence the future of our nation.
