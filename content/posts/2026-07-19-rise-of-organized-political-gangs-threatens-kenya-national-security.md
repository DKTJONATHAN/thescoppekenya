## The Myth of Lawless Streets

Political violence in Kenya is widely dismissed as the work of rogue, unemployed youth operating outside the reach of the state. This assessment is convenient but entirely wrong. The truth is far more calculated: these gangs are not a failure of order, but a functional, budget-optimized tool of the political establishment.

### The Economics of Outsourced Security

For many politicians, the math of campaigning is cold and precise. Formal, state-sanctioned security details come with public scrutiny and rigid bureaucratic protocols that restrict movement and access. Conversely, a private squad of local enforcers functions as a flexible, off-the-books security asset. By hiring street-level groups to manage political rallies, intimidate opponents, or secure territory, candidates reduce their electoral overheads significantly. 

Our current campaign financing laws essentially incentivize this behavior. Because formal expenditure is heavily monitored and subject to strict reporting caps, politicians prefer to channel resources into unmonitored shadow budgets. Funding a gang is effectively a "security retainer" that costs a fraction of the price of official, accountable protection. When we look at the financial landscape, it becomes clear that political violence is not a sign of state collapse; it is a direct result of state-sanctioned cost-cutting measures.

### Policing the Hand that Feeds

Critics often point to the inability of the police to dismantle these groups, yet this ignores the structural reality of the relationship. Police forces cannot be expected to dismantle an economy they are fundamentally bound to protect. These gangs are integrated into the political patronage networks that influence police appointments, resource allocation, and career trajectories. 

When a gang leader is arrested, the intervention often comes from the highest levels of local leadership, ensuring the street-level operative remains untouchable. This creates a feedback loop where the police act less as law enforcement and more as a mediation layer, ensuring that gang violence remains within the boundaries of what is acceptable for the political patron. Much like the strategic deal-making seen at the [Africa CEO Forum](https://zandani.co.ke/article/africa-ceo-forum-shifts-focus-toward-strategic-deal-making), political campaigns operate on a system of high-stakes transactional trust. In this environment, the gang is the primary service provider.

### The Normalization of Intimidation

The presence of organized gangs has become a normalized feature of our political life because it serves as a constant reminder of power. Intimidation is not a side effect of the campaign trail; it is the infrastructure through which influence is projected. Every time a crowd is dispersed or an opponent is silenced, the cost of entering the political arena rises for anyone without the budget to maintain their own private security apparatus.

While some look at cultural shifts or artistic expressions like [Tiwa Savage's Afrobeats Music Foundation](https://zandani.co.ke/article/tiwa-savage-afrobeats-music-foundation-africa-berklee-dream) as signs of a modernizing Kenya, the basement of our democratic process remains stuck in a brutal, archaic cycle. We continue to treat these incidents as isolated disturbances that require more community policing. This perspective refuses to acknowledge that the violence is designed to be there. 

We must stop viewing political gangs as an accidental byproduct of a broken system and recognize them as a deliberate policy choice by those who profit from our instability. As long as the state offers no cheaper alternative to the security provided by these syndicates, and as long as campaign financing remains a shadow game, the gangs will continue to flourish. They are not the antagonists in this story; they are the private contractors of the political elite. Solving this requires more than just calling for arrests. It requires ending the financial incentives that make it profitable for our leaders to keep an army of thugs on the payroll. We are not suffering from a lack of policing; we are suffering from an excess of political patronage.
