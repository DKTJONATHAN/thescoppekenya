**CGIAR SAAF Program Poised to Transform Kenya's Livestock and Aquaculture Sectors, Aligns with National Blue Economy Ambitions**

**By Timothy Muli, Agricultural News Desk**

**Nairobi, Kenya - March 1, 2026** - Kenya's agricultural landscape is set for a significant boost with the Consultative Group on International Agricultural Research (CGIAR)'s Sustainable Animal and Aquatic Foods (SAAF) Science Program initiating its ambitious plans to reach 1.7 million people across 17 countries, including Kenya. The program, a cornerstone of CGIAR's 2025-2030 research portfolio, aims to drive a profound transformation towards more nutritious, climate-smart, and inclusive animal and aquatic food systems, directly supporting Kenya's food security and economic development goals.

The SAAF Program’s strategic focus on improving productivity through genetics and animal health, advancing regenerative aquaculture, and fostering low-emission livestock systems directly complements Kenya's ongoing efforts to modernise its agricultural sector. Dr. Rodrigue Yossa, Interim Director of the SAAF Program, underscored the urgency of these innovations, noting that demand for animal-source foods is projected to rise by 30% by 2050, with low and middle-income countries like Kenya driving this growth.

In Kenya, the SAAF Program is particularly relevant given the nation's pronounced commitment to enhancing its blue economy and livestock sectors. Just days ago, on February 26, 2026, Kenya officially launched its National Blue Economy Strategy 2025-2030, an ambitious plan to elevate the sector's annual earnings from KES 40 billion to KES 350 billion by 2030 and increase fish production to over 450,000 metric tonnes. This national strategy prioritises sustainable resource utilisation, technology promotion, and the development of a skilled workforce.

Lucy Obungu, Director of Fisheries in Kenya, has enthusiastically backed SAAF's focus on aquaponics, digital tools, and gender equity, predicting these will "revolutionise Kenya's blue economy." This sentiment aligns with current government initiatives, including partnerships with organisations like WorldFish, which signed a Memorandum of Understanding in October 2025 to strengthen Kenya's blue economy through a learning hub, modern technologies, and joint research. Earlier efforts under the EU-funded Go Blue Kenya Programme also saw the introduction of digital tools like the "Soko Samaki" app for fish traceability and the provision of equipped boats and solar-powered cold chain systems to small-scale fishing cooperatives.

Kenya's small-scale fishers, who contribute significantly to the total fish catch, face persistent challenges such as overfishing, inadequate infrastructure leading to substantial post-harvest losses, climate change impacts, and high input costs. The SAAF program's emphasis on digital innovations, such as AI and blockchain, is expected to drive smarter decisions and enable inclusive, data-driven markets that can alleviate some of these burdens for smallholder producers.

Furthermore, the SAAF program directly supports Kenya's broader agricultural policy framework. The Kenya Climate Smart Agriculture Strategy (KCSAS) 2017-2026, which is in its final year of implementation, aims to adapt agriculture to climate change, build resilience, and minimise greenhouse gas emissions. The Ministry of Agriculture and Livestock Development also unveiled the National Livestock Agenda (2025-2035) in February 2026, prioritising animal genetics, disease control, and value addition to position livestock as a driver of food security and exports.

The Kenyan government's commitment to the agricultural sector is evident in recent policy discussions. Cabinet Secretary for Agriculture and Livestock Development, Sen. Mutahi Kagwe, is advocating for a significant increase in the Ministry's budget allocation to at least five percent of the national budget, approximately KSh 140 billion, for the 2026 fiscal year, up from the current 2.7 percent. This push aims to bolster investments in climate-smart agriculture, livestock genetics, and access to quality feed and water resources. Additionally, the Finance Bill 2026 proposes substantial tax relief for agricultural exporters, including halving input VAT and streamlining refunds, which is expected to boost liquidity and investment across livestock and other value chains.

With its farmer-first approach and strong commitment to partnerships with national governments, universities, NGOs, and the private sector, the SAAF Program is well-positioned to enhance national capacity through technical support, extension training, and innovation hubs. This collaborative model, combined with Kenya's proactive policy environment, promises a sustainable future for animal and aquatic food systems, improving livelihoods and safeguarding the environment across the nation.
