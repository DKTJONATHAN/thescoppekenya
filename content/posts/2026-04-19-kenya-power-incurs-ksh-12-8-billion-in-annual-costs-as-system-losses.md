## Kenya Power's Mounting System Loss Burden

Kenya Power confronts a KSh 12.8 billion annual financial burden from system losses, consistently missing regulatory targets. Despite grid investments and recent profit recovery driven by currency stabilization, losses remain significantly above benchmarks, suppressing unrecovered revenue and impeding corporate performance.

### Financial and Regulatory Pressures

Kenya Power and Lighting Company (KPLC) continues to absorb substantial financial costs due to system losses exceeding regulatory benchmarks. The estimated unrecovered revenue currently stands at approximately KSh 12.8 billion annually, a direct consequence of a 5.57 percentage point gap between actual losses and permitted thresholds. The Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA) prevents KPLC from passing losses above the allowed benchmark to consumers, forcing the utility to bear the full financial impact. Each percentage point of unrecovered losses translates to roughly KSh 2.3 billion in annual unbilled revenue. In FY2025, KPLC reported system losses of 21.21%, which, despite being the company's best full-year figure on record following a KSh 29.4 billion grid investment, still exceeds EPRA's 16.5% benchmark by 4.71 percentage points and the government's 14.4% target by 6.81 percentage points. The utility also incurred direct losses exceeding KSh 328 million in 2024 alone due to 365 vandalized transformers.

### Regional Performance Disparity

Kenya's electricity system loss performance lags significantly behind its East African Community peers and the global average. At 21.21%, Kenya's losses are the highest in the region. Comparatively, Tanzania operates at 14.57%, Rwanda at 16.90%, Burundi at 18.30%, and Uganda at 20.60%. The global average for system losses typically ranges between 8 and 10%, highlighting that Kenya's current rate is more than double this global standard. Reducing these losses to the government's 14.4% target is projected to save approximately KSh 10 billion annually.

### Broader Corporate Challenges and Recovery Drivers

While KPLC has invested KSh 29.4 billion in its grid infrastructure, the company's recent financial recovery has primarily been driven by external factors rather than a reduction in system losses. The utility swung from a KSh 3.2 billion net loss in FY2023/24 to a KSh 30.08 billion profit before declining to KSh 24.47 billion in FY2025, largely due to shilling stabilization reducing dollar-denominated power purchase costs. This indicates that the system loss problem remains a primary impediment to sustained profitability once currency conditions normalize. KPLC also faces a broader pattern of deferred obligations, with government institutions owing over KSh 26 billion in unpaid bills. Additionally, a 2019 Cabinet directive to refund KSh 19.4 billion remains unimplemented, and over 21,000 connection projects worth KSh 12 billion are incomplete, some dating back over a decade. These long-standing financial and operational challenges underscore the complex environment in which companies, including those benefiting from partnerships such as UBA UK and British International Investment expanding trade finance for African businesses, operate.
