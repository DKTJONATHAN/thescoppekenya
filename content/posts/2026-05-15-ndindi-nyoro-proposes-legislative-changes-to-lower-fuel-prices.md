## The Ksh190 Promise: Can Ndindi Nyoro’s Tax Revolt Bypass the Treasury Wall?

Kenyans are greeting the latest fuel price hike with a mix of weary skepticism and cautious hope as Kiharu MP Ndindi Nyoro launches a bold legislative challenge to slash pump prices to below Ksh190 per litre through drastic tax restructuring.

The current economic landscape remains unforgiving, with EPRA recently pushing super petrol to Ksh214.25 and diesel to a stinging Ksh242.92. At the heart of the frustration is a pricing structure heavy on state-mandated levies, including a Road Maintenance Levy of Ksh18, a petroleum development levy of Ksh5.40, and an 8 percent VAT, which together form a significant portion of the final bill. Nyoro’s proposal seeks to dismantle parts of this architecture by axing the 2024 Road Maintenance Levy and significantly trimming VAT. His plan also targets the government-to-government oil importation deal, arguing that systemic inefficiencies and profiteering are keeping costs artificially high. However, the Treasury remains bound by the existing G2G agreements, which provide the state with extended credit terms for oil imports, complicating any unilateral attempt to pivot away from the current system without risking a supply shock or currency volatility.

### Voices from the Pavement

For matatu operators in Nairobi, the math behind the pump price is a daily struggle that makes long-term policy promises feel like a distant dream. Peter Maina, a driver operating the Rongai route, argues that while talk of a Ksh190 price point sounds great on television, he has heard too many promises that never translate to lower operating costs. He remains unconvinced that the legislature can actually override the deep-seated economic agreements that dictate how much the country pays for fuel.

Small business owners see the proposal as a necessary, if overdue, confrontation with the status quo. Sarah Wambui, who runs a bakery in Nakuru, notes that every time the price of diesel climbs, her transport costs eat directly into her profit margins. She believes that even if the Ksh190 target is ambitious, the push for transparency in the G2G deal is the right fight because the current system feels like a closed loop that leaves consumers carrying the burden of inefficiency.

Economists and policy watchers, meanwhile, are more cautious about the mechanics of the shift. Analyst James Otieno points out that the Fuel Stabilisation Fund is already under immense pressure, and simply cutting taxes without a corresponding plan to fill the revenue hole could destabilize the entire budget. He suggests that while Nyoro’s rhetoric resonates with a public battered by inflation, the real hurdle is not just political willpower, but the rigid fiscal obligations the government has already locked itself into with international oil suppliers.

### Why it Matters Now

The intersection of legislative action and pump prices is hitting a boiling point. The fiscal reality is stark: with taxes and levies accounting for a massive chunk of what a motorist pays, any meaningful reduction requires more than just a vote in parliament; it requires a structural overhaul of how Kenya sources its energy. If the legislature forces a tax cut, the government will face a massive hole in its infrastructure budget, which is currently tied to those same road levies. It is a classic policy trap where lowering the cost of living could inadvertently stall the funding for the very roads the fuel tax is meant to maintain.
