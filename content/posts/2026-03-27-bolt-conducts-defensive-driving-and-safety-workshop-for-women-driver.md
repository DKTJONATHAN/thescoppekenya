Bolt hosted a comprehensive Defensive Driving and Flood Awareness Workshop on Friday, March 27, 2026, specifically targeting its women drivers. The initiative aimed to enhance road safety skills and bolster driver and passenger security, reflecting a tangible commitment to empowerment within the transport sector.

## Empowering Women in Professional Driving

The workshop, held in recognition of International Women’s Month, moved beyond symbolic gestures to provide practical, impactful training. Bolt emphasized ensuring women drivers gained competence in critical road safety skills, fostering their inclusion and empowerment in a traditionally male-dominated space.

### Workshop Content and Expert Guidance

Sessions featured practical training guided by professionals and were highly interactive, allowing drivers to engage facilitators on real-life situations. Key areas covered included defensive driving techniques, general road safety protocols, flood awareness, and basic vehicle maintenance. Hazel May Omune, Bolt’s Safety Program Manager, stated that "Training combats stereotypes that women are viewed as ill-equipped to handle road safety emergencies." The workshop was predominantly led by women experts and attended by women Bolt drivers, who constitute approximately 4-5 per cent of the company's driver base.

Flood awareness was a critical component, addressing the challenges drivers face with heavy rains in Nairobi. Bolt provided an emergency tool equipped with an alarm, flashlight, and window-breaking mechanism. Joan Nyambura, an automotive technician and founder of 4RealKE, advised drivers against taking unnecessary risks, emphasizing, “You can make that money another day. Don’t put yourself at risk.”

Lydia Mwaniki, a defensive driving expert, underscored the necessity for drivers to be responsive to their environment, understand various road user behaviors, and interact safely based on speed and road type. Mwaniki also addressed personal safety, noting that risks like being followed affect women drivers more frequently and that defensive driving training remains largely male-dominated despite the increasing number of women drivers.

### Vehicle Maintenance and Driver Experiences

Nyambura highlighted the importance of routine vehicle maintenance, including regular checks of engine oil, coolant, and battery condition. She also advised monitoring brake fluid levels for signs of leaks or worn pads. Nyambura stressed that women drivers, often underestimated by mechanics, should actively participate in vehicle servicing by asking questions, verifying part quality, and building relationships with trusted technicians to make informed decisions and enhance safety.

Drivers shared experiences of overcoming stereotypes and supporting each other. Bethsheba Obiero, a boda boda rider, recounted facing discouragement when starting, with men suggesting the work was not for women. She affirmed her resolve, stating, “But I didn’t give up. I know what I want, and I have some goals to achieve.” Joyce Karuri mentioned being part of a WhatsApp group of 434 women drivers who share daily experiences and advice, underscoring the collective empowerment. She concluded, “You can be anything that you want to be. Don’t let society limit you, saying that this is not a woman’s job. We can do what men can do. And actually, some of us can do it better.”
