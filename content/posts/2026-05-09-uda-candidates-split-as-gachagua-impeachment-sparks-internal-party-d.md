The United Democratic Alliance is currently grappling with internal turmoil as the ongoing impeachment proceedings against Deputy President Rigathi Gachagua force party candidates across Kenya to reconsider their public loyalties, resulting in a stark divide between those distancing themselves and those doubling down.

## A Fractured Alliance Within the Ruling Party

### The scramble for political survival
As the impeachment process against Deputy President Rigathi Gachagua gains momentum, the United Democratic Alliance is experiencing an internal exodus of political imagery. Across numerous regions, aspirants are proactively removing the faces of top-tier party leadership from their campaign materials. This sudden rebranding effort highlights a pragmatic approach to political survival, where candidates prioritize individual electoral prospects over party association. For many aspirants, the current climate has rendered previous displays of party unity a liability, prompting them to quietly scrub their campaign assets to avoid being tethered to the political fallout.

### Defiance in Emurua Dikirr
While much of the country sees a retreat from the Deputy President’s image, certain areas are moving in the opposite direction. In constituencies such as Emurua Dikirr, local leaders have adopted a stance of open defiance against the executive-led purge. Rather than removing the Deputy President’s face from their posters, these candidates are prominently displaying them as a show of solidarity. This localized resistance serves as a direct challenge to the national party structure, framing the regional political landscape as a bastion of opposition against the broader efforts to remove the Deputy President from office.

### The patterns of political convenience
The divergent reactions among party members expose a long-standing trend of convenience in Kenyan politics. The contrast between candidates who are sanitizing their campaign materials and those who are leaning into the controversy illustrates the performative nature of party loyalty. While those fleeing the fallout demonstrate a calculation rooted in self-preservation, the bold defiance seen in regions like Emurua Dikirr reveals a different, yet equally calculated, use of political optics. This divide proves that for many within the party, regional interests and personal survival remain far more influential than the established directives of the national party leadership.
