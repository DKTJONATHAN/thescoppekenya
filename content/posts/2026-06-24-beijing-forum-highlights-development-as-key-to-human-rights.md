## The False Promise of Managed Progress Beijing Forum Highlights Development as Key to Human Rights is the central subject of the update.

Beijing’s recent push to redefine human rights as a product of economic achievement presents a tempting shortcut for leaders tired of democratic friction. By framing liberty as a reward for prosperity rather than an inherent entitlement, this model asks nations like Kenya to trade civic accountability for the promise of rapid industrialization. Yet, beneath the veneer of this pragmatic bargain lies a structural danger that risks dismantling the very institutions designed to protect the Kenyan public.

The Forum on Global Human Rights Governance highlighted the 2026-2030 Human Rights Action Plan, an initiative that essentially treats GDP as the prerequisite for personal freedom. While the appeal of this top-down efficiency is clear for any administration facing intense scrutiny, history offers a starkly different lesson. When development is uncoupled from the mechanisms of public oversight, prosperity rarely trickles down. Instead, power tends to calcify around a small circle of elites who decide which fundamental rights are convenient to grant and which can be indefinitely sidelined.

### Deconstructing the Development Trade-off

The Beijing framework relies on the shaky premise that civil liberties are a luxury reserved only for the wealthy. In truth, the absence of institutional oversight during rapid development creates a vacuum where corruption thrives. This is not a theoretical concern; it is mirrored in the ongoing instability and systemic failures currently plaguing the Social Health Authority. Prioritizing infrastructure projects over the right to interrogate state action does not lead to streamlined progress. Rather, it facilitates the rise of cartels. Without the protections of free speech, investigative journalism, and the right to peaceful protest, economic gains are often captured by the powerful long before they reach the average citizen.

Emerging economies that have pursued this rights-second strategy frequently produce impressive macroeconomic statistics. However, these figures often mask a fractured social contract. By suppressing dissent in the name of development, a state deliberately severs the feedback loop necessary to prevent economic mismanagement. A government shielded from criticism will invariably conceal its errors, allowing them to metastasize into the type of crises currently haunting our utility sectors. When the state functions as the sole arbiter of truth, it creates a fragile ecosystem where small mistakes eventually become cataclysmic failures.

### The Constitutional Mandate

The philosophy radiating from Beijing stands in direct opposition to the 2010 Kenyan Constitution. Our legal framework identifies rights as inherent, non-negotiable principles, not as performance-based incentives distributed by a state once it achieves a specific income threshold. Some politicians may suggest that Western human rights norms are incompatible with our current developmental trajectory, but the Beijing model is equally alien. It effectively asks Kenyans to surrender their agency in exchange for concrete and steel. This is not a binary choice between starvation and rights; it is a choice between a democracy that empowers its people and an authoritarian structure that views them merely as subjects for developmental experimentation.

The allure of this model is understandable in light of Kenya's persistent infrastructure deficit. Nevertheless, there is a profound distinction between attracting foreign investment and sacrificing core political values. The human cost of ignoring accountability is already visible, highlighted by the tragic circumstances surrounding Kenyans lured into the Russia-Ukraine conflict. That scandal remains a haunting reminder that when a nation treats the rights of its people as expendable, the most vulnerable citizens are the first to pay the price.

We must reject the false dichotomy that pits development against liberty. Authentic, sustainable growth flourishes only when citizens possess the authority to demand transparency, the right to challenge corruption, and the tools to hold public officials accountable. If we internalize the belief that our rights must remain on hold until we reach a certain level of national wealth, we will only find ourselves poorer and more subjugated. Prosperity is not the prize for staying silent. True national success is forged in a society that insists on accountability as a condition of growth at every single level.
