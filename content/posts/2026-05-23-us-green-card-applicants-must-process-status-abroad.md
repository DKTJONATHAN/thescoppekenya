## The Diaspora U-Turn: How Washington’s New Green Card Policy Hits Kenyan Households

The United States has shifted its immigration stance, forcing green card applicants currently on American soil to return home for processing. For thousands of Kenyan families, this bureaucratic pivot transforms long-term residency dreams into an immediate, high-stakes financial and logistical nightmare.

### The End of Domestic Adjustment

For decades, many Kenyans who moved to the United States on temporary visas sought to transition to permanent residency without leaving the country. This adjustment of status allowed families to remain together, maintain their employment, and avoid the risks associated with reapplying from abroad. As of yesterday, May 22, the United States Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) has effectively shuttered this path. Under the new directive, the government now mandates that applicants must exit the U.S. and complete their consular processing through embassies in their home countries.

This policy adjustment, pushed by the current administration, aims to reduce the number of people who remain in the U.S. while their residency applications are pending. While Washington presents this as a matter of enforcement, for the Kenyan diaspora, it is an abrupt disruption of stability. Nairobi-based immigration consultants are already reporting a surge in panicked inquiries from Kenyans in the U.S. who fear that leaving the country to comply with this mandate could trigger years of separation, as consular processing backlogs often stretch far longer than the timelines for domestic adjustments.

### A Timeline of Growing Uncertainty

*   Early 2026: The U.S. began signaled a hardening stance on visa overstays, quietly increasing pressure on USCIS to clear backlogs through strict adherence to consular processing protocols.
*   April 2026: Discussions emerged in D.C. regarding the economic costs of domestic visa adjustments, with officials arguing that permanent residency should be earned through verified, out-of-country vetting.
*   May 22, 2026: The official mandate was issued, removing the flexibility previously enjoyed by applicants already living and working in the U.S., including many Kenyan professionals such as those highlighted in our previous reporting on [international nurses](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-05-07-kenya-marks-international-nurses-week-with-focus-on-specialized-care).

### The Financial and Emotional Toll

The immediate impact is financial. Families who spent years saving for legal fees and life in the U.S. are now faced with sudden, exorbitant costs. The mandate requires applicants to fund international travel, pay new sets of processing fees in Kenya, and manage the expenses of maintaining a household in two countries simultaneously. There is also the profound anxiety of visa insecurity; an applicant who leaves the U.S. has no guarantee of re-entry should a minor clerical error or a shifting diplomatic priority lead to a visa denial at the embassy level.

Consultants observe that this policy disproportionately affects those mid-application, leaving them trapped in a cycle of debt and uncertainty. It forces a choice between abandoning a nearly complete legal process or risking total separation from their families and livelihoods. While some may qualify for exceptions under extraordinary circumstances, the bar for such relief is set exceptionally high, offering little comfort to the average Kenyan resident trying to build a stable life abroad.

### Why it matters now
The sudden shift threatens the financial stability of countless Kenyan households who rely on remittances and the professional success of their relatives in the U.S. This is not merely a legal change but a potential drain on the resources families have spent years accumulating to secure a foothold in a new country.

### What to watch next
Observers should monitor the potential for litigation against the USCIS. If legal challenges arise regarding the retroactive nature of these rules, the administrative burden on U.S. embassies in Nairobi will likely spike, leading to further delays.
