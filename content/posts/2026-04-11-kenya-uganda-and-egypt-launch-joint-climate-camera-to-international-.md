Kenya, Uganda, and Egypt's joint Climate Camera (ClimCam) mission successfully launched on Saturday, April 11, 2026, aboard the Cygnus NG-24 resupply mission to the International Space Station, marking a historic moment for regional space collaboration.

East Africa Launches Climate Camera to ISS The long-awaited Climate Camera (ClimCam) mission successfully took off on Saturday, April 11, from Cape Canaveral Space Force Station in Florida. The payload, developed jointly by Kenya, Uganda, and Egypt, was launched aboard a SpaceX Falcon 9 rocket at approximately 2:41 AM, with the Cygnus spacecraft serving as the delivery vehicle to transport it into orbit. Images from the launch sequence captured the smooth ascent of the spacecraft. ClimCam is now en route to the International Space Station (ISS) and is expected to arrive on Monday, April 13, at around 7:50 PM, according to NASA.

Regional Space Collaboration Reaches New Heights This tri-national climate monitoring system represents a significant advancement in regional space collaboration, bringing together the Kenya Space Agency (KSA), the Egyptian Space Agency, and the Uganda National Space Programme. The mission, initially deferred, proceeded smoothly, with the separation phase clearly visible as the spacecraft detached and continued its journey toward the ISS. Once at its destination, ClimCam will be mounted on the Airbus Bartolomeo platform, attached to the European Columbus module, offering a continuous observation point for Earth’s surface.

Advancing Climate Resilience Through AI The ClimCam system is engineered to leverage artificial intelligence to generate near-real-time weather and climate data. This capability is expected to significantly enhance how countries predict and respond to environmental threats. Experts anticipate the data collected will be instrumental in monitoring critical climate-related hazards prevalent in the East African region, such as floods and droughts. These advancements in space technology underscore a broader national commitment to strategic infrastructure and energy independence, complementing projects like the planned Ksh 375 billion gas power plant at Dongo Kundu. The data is poised to transform climate resilience strategies across agriculture, water management, and disaster preparedness, directly impacting sectors like food security and environmental monitoring. Kenya's Growing Satellite Presence Apart from the newly launched ClimCam mission, Kenya currently operates one active satellite in orbit, TAIFA-1. This satellite was launched in April 2023 aboard a SpaceX Falcon 9 rocket and is dedicated to Earth observation to support agriculture, food security, and environmental monitoring efforts. Kenya has launched two satellites in total, including the earlier 1KUNS-PF nanosatellite, which was sent to space in 2018 from the International Space Station but has since completed its mission and is no longer operational.
