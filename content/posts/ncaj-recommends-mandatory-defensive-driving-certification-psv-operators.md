<h2>NCAJ Recommends Mandatory Defensive Driving Certification: Beyond the Headlines for PSV Operators</h2>
The National Council on the Administration of Justice (NCAJ) has recommended mandatory defensive driving certification for Public Service Vehicle (PSV) operators, a directive President William Ruto announced on Monday, March 2, 2026, as part of a national push to enhance road safety and drastically reduce fatalities. This sweeping reform aims to instill a safety culture and professionalise a sector grappling with incessant accidents.

While the government champions these measures as a definitive solution to Kenya's escalating road carnage, critical questions arise regarding the immediate and long-term implications for the thousands of PSV operators and the sector's pervasive systemic challenges. President Ruto's pronouncement, made during a State House briefing where he received a road safety status report, highlights a deeply entrenched crisis: over 5,000 deaths were reported on Kenyan roads in 2025 alone, costing the economy an alarming five percent of its Gross Domestic Product annually.

<h3>The Burdens of Mandatory Defensive Driving Certification for PSV Operators</h3>
The introduction of mandatory defensive driving certification, while ostensibly a step towards greater safety, places a significant financial and logistical burden squarely on the shoulders of individual PSV operators. Defensive driving courses in Kenya typically range from KSh 10,000 for a two-day basic course to KSh 12,000 for a five-day advanced program, excluding accommodation costs. For many operators already navigating thin margins, this additional cost, coupled with potential income loss during training days, presents a substantial hurdle. The absence of clear government subsidies or support mechanisms raises concerns that this financial strain could inadvertently fuel corruption, as operators may seek shortcuts to obtain certification, thereby undermining the very essence of the safety initiative. Without robust oversight, the certification process itself could become another avenue for rent-seeking, rather than a genuine improvement in driver competency.

The emphasis on driver behaviour, while crucial, risks overshadowing deeper, systemic failures that continue to plague Kenya's transport sector. Issues such as inadequate road infrastructure, particularly in high-accident blackspots, and the alarming prevalence of unroadworthy vehicles often escape stringent scrutiny, as evidenced in past tragedies that laid bare [the unspoken truths of public transport in Kenya](https://zandani.co.ke/article/miss-trudy-mothers-tragic-death-matatu-tragedy). These factors, compounded by fragmented institutional structures across various government bodies responsible for transport, present a complex web of challenges that cannot be resolved by driver training alone.

<h3>Automated Enforcement and PSV Operators: A Promise or a Problem?</h3>
In conjunction with driver certification, President Ruto has ordered the immediate rollout of smart traffic cameras and instant fines, an initiative intended to strengthen enforcement and minimise corruption in traffic management. The National Transport and Safety Authority (NTSA) is reportedly deploying 1,000 high-definition smart cameras across major highways, linked to an automated system for issuing digital fines via SMS and integrating with smart driving licenses for demerit points. While this technological leap promises to reduce direct interaction between motorists and traffic police-a known breeding ground for bribery-its implementation carries inherent risks.

The effectiveness of such systems hinges on meticulous calibration and transparency. Any perception of arbitrary fines or technical glitches could erode public trust, potentially leading to widespread resistance. Furthermore, the focus on 'instant fines' for minor offences, while intended to streamline justice, might inadvertently divert attention from more egregious violations by PSV operators and other road users that often lead to fatal accidents. The underlying issue of bribery, a significant contributor to weak enforcement, remains a formidable adversary, and merely shifting the mode of enforcement without addressing its root causes may prove to be a cosmetic adjustment rather than a transformative reform.

<h3>Formalisation of Boda Boda Operations and PSV Operators: A Path to Inclusion or Exclusion?</h3>
The NCAJ recommendations also extend to the formalisation of boda boda operations through Sacco structures, a move aimed at bringing order to a largely informal yet economically vital sector. While formalisation offers potential benefits such as improved safety standards, access to financial services, and better social protection for riders, it simultaneously presents a precarious tightrope walk. The boda boda industry provides livelihoods for millions of Kenyan youth, often serving as a last resort in the face of high unemployment.

Imposing stringent formalisation requirements, including potential costs associated with Sacco registration and compliance, could inadvertently marginalise a significant portion of these operators, pushing them further into the shadows of the informal economy. Many boda boda riders already face challenges such as police harassment and poor health conditions due to road infrastructure. A nuanced approach is required, one that balances regulation with socio-economic realities, ensuring that formalisation acts as a ladder to inclusion rather than a barrier to survival for these essential PSV operators and their counterparts. Without careful consideration, what appears to be a solution for road safety could become a catalyst for economic displacement and social unrest.
