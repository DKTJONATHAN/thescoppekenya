## The Ghost of 1937: Why Kenya’s Industrialization Must Avoid the Colonial Commodity Trap

The Africa Forward Summit has concluded, leaving behind the customary echoes of ambitious handshakes and transient pledges. Yet, beneath the polished veneer of modern diplomacy lies an economic pattern that has persisted for generations, quietly eroding national sovereignty. History teaches a sobering lesson: nations that forgo value addition remain perpetually relegated to the role of primary extractors for wealthier powers.

### A Mirror to the Past

On May 15, 1937, a sharp contraction in global commodity markets crippled the colonial economies of East Africa. At that time, the economic order was governed by a rigid, exploitative formula. The metropole commanded a steady stream of raw tea, coffee, and minerals, while the colonies provided these commodities at prices unilaterally determined in London or Paris. 

Nearly ninety years later, the Africa Forward Summit featured leaders such as Emmanuel Macron standing shoulder to shoulder with African heads of state. Despite the passage of time, the underlying architecture of global trade remains stubbornly archaic. We continue to ship the raw potential of our soil overseas, only to purchase the finished goods that our own domestic labor could have produced.

This ongoing reliance on raw material exportation is far from a neutral economic choice. It is a structural inheritance. Current trade agreements mirror the imbalances that defined the mid-thirties, where wealth flows outward while infrastructure is designed exclusively to ferry resources to the coast rather than to cultivate internal connectivity. While media narratives often focus on the spectacle of external cultural exports, such as the glamour surrounding Nigerian socialite weddings, there exists a sheen of progress that obscures a deeply precarious reality. Our efforts at industrialization are frequently suffocated by trade barriers constructed by those who profit from our raw goods while keeping the technology for refinement safely behind their own borders.

### The Ruto Stance and the Reality of Value Addition

President William Ruto has spent the summit advocating for a departure from this vintage colonial model. His insistence on value-added production is technically sound. If Kenya takes the initiative to process its own leather, refine its petroleum, and package its own coffee, the economic multipliers will circulate within our own borders. This represents a clear shift from the passive trade precedents of the last century. 

However, political rhetoric is a poor substitute for structural transformation. Genuine industrialization demands far more than declarations at a summit. It requires a radical reconfiguration of how African nations trade with one another. Looking at the conditions of domestic infrastructure, including local rehabilitation programs, serves as a grim reminder that institutional strength and internal stability are the prerequisites for any enduring economic shift. If we fail to manage our own internal systems, we remain indefinitely vulnerable to the external pressures that turn minor commodity price fluctuations into catastrophic national crises. The theater of diplomacy often ignores a fundamental truth: true independence requires the domestic machinery to transform our own natural resources.

### Why It Matters Now

The global economy is presently drifting toward protectionism. If Africa persists in relying on the whims of external demand for raw commodities, we will be the first casualties when global supply chains eventually tighten. To ensure survival in an increasingly volatile international market, we must pivot from being a mere supplier of raw materials to becoming a hub for finished manufacturing.

### What to Watch Next

The immediate future hinges on the trade policy negotiations emerging from this summit. It is vital to look past the superficial details and monitor for actual investments in localized manufacturing plants and power infrastructure. We must be wary of simple loans intended to accelerate the transport of raw exports to foreign ports. If these new agreements fail to mandate significant technology transfer or require local processing quotas, the Ghost of 1937 will continue to linger over our economic trajectory. 

History is clear on this point. Nations only achieve lasting prosperity when they maintain control over the final stages of their own production. The time for passive reliance has long since passed.
