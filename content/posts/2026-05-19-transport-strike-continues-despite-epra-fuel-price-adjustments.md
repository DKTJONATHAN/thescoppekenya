## The Silent Commute: How Nairobi’s Stranded Passengers are Navigating a Market Without Matatus

For the commuters of Nairobi, the latest fuel price adjustments from the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA) have brought zero relief, only deepening the frustration of a 25 day urban paralysis. With matatu operators remaining steadfast in their strike, the streets have become a grueling gauntlet for the working class.

The standoff began after EPRA held a marathon six hour session with transport stakeholders earlier today, resulting in a modest Ksh10.06 drop in diesel prices and a significant Ksh38.60 hike in kerosene costs. While the regulator claims these adjustments are meant to stabilize the market and mitigate fuel adulteration, the matatu cartels have rejected the move entirely. They argue that the marginal savings do not account for the rising operational costs that have crippled their business model for weeks. As a result, the cycle running from May 19 to June 14 remains a period of uncertainty, with thousands of Kenyans still stranded at bus stops from Nairobi to Mombasa, forced to contend with predatory motorcycle taxi rates that have more than tripled since the industrial action began.

### The View from the Bus Stop

In the heart of Nairobi, the mood is one of exhausted defiance. Benson, a construction worker who typically spends Ksh100 on his daily commute to industrial area, says his transport budget has ballooned to Ksh400 for a boda boda ride, eating into his meager food budget. He describes the current situation as a form of house arrest for the poor, noting that while the high offices argue over fuel margins, the ordinary person is the one walking miles in the morning sun.

Down in Mombasa, the sentiment is equally sharp. Fatuma, a small scale trader, explains that she has stopped traveling to the market to purchase her goods because the cost of hiring a motorbike is more than her expected daily profit. She pointed out that it does not matter if diesel drops by ten shillings when there is no matatu running; the real cost is the complete breakdown of movement for those who cannot afford private vehicles.

In Nakuru, the frustration is directed at both sides. A student named Kevin noted that the government and the operators seem to be playing a game of chess while the board is on fire. He mentioned that passengers are being caught in the middle, forced to pay whatever extortionate rates are demanded by opportunistic riders, effectively turning a simple journey into a financial crisis.

### Why it Matters Now

This strike is no longer just about fuel pump prices; it is a full blown crisis of accessibility. When the informal public transport sector stops, the economy stops. Many Kenyans, already reeling from the ripple effects of [new tax proposals on essential items like mobile devices](https://zandani.co.ke/article/mbadi-proposes-new-tax-rules-for-mitumba-and-mobile-devices), now find their basic mobility treated as a luxury. The refusal of the transport unions to accept the regulator’s offer suggests that the disconnect between policy decisions and the reality of crew earnings is wider than ever. Until there is a compromise that addresses the cost of living rather than just the cost of fuel, the urban commute will remain a dangerous and expensive gamble for the average citizen.

### What to Watch Next

The coming days will determine if the government is willing to offer further concessions or if they will attempt to break the strike through administrative pressure. Passengers should watch for any signs of a split within the transport unions, as smaller operators may find it impossible to stay off the road for much longer. If the gridlock persists, expect more calls for alternative transit solutions, though these remain largely theoretical for most of the population. For now, the streets remain dominated by motorbikes and the quiet desperation of those who have no choice but to keep walking.

The city breathes in the rhythm of tired feet and empty terminals, waiting for a resolution that remains frustratingly out of reach.
