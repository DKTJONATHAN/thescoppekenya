## NPSC Dismisses Fake Car Auction Claims

The National Police Service Commission (NPSC) on Saturday, May 2, dismissed a widely circulated fake notice about a supposed car auction in Nairobi. The Commission warned the public against fraudsters attempting to solicit funds for vehicles allegedly priced as low as Ksh250,000, confirming the claims were false.

### Deceptive Tactics Exposed

The fraudulent notice, which gained traction on social media platforms, claimed the NPSC was organizing a public auction of approximately 47 vehicles at remarkably low prices. To lend an air of authenticity, the advertisement utilized the Commission’s name and branding, even listing popular car models like the Toyota Vitz, Mazda Demio, Toyota Corolla Axio, and Subaru Forester alongside their purported prices. Such common vehicles are frequently encountered on Kenyan roads, sometimes unfortunately involved in incidents such as a recent [Trailer and Toyota Voxy collision along Narok-Mai Mahiu road leaves seven people dead](https://zandani.co.ke/article/trailer-and-toyota-voxy-collision-along-narok-mai-mahiu-road-leaves-).

The NPSC, however, identified several inconsistencies within the document. These included the use of unofficial communication channels and suspicious payment procedures. Notably, the advert directed interested buyers to register through WhatsApp and pay a participation fee, a method that diverges significantly from the Commission’s established official vehicle disposal processes.

### Public Caution Urged Amid Rising Scams

The NPSC urged Kenyans to exercise extreme caution and verify information rigorously before engaging in such activities. The Commission further advised the public to report any suspicious notices to relevant law enforcement agencies.

This clarification regarding the fake NPSC car auction comes amidst a growing trend of online scams targeting unsuspecting Kenyans with seemingly genuine deals. Just two days prior, the Kenya National Highways Authority (KeNHA) issued a public warning about fake recruitment messages. These messages targeted job seekers for projects like the Rironi-Gilgil and Rironi-Naivasha road developments, instructing applicants to submit personal documents through unofficial channels and, in some instances, demanding payments for application processing.

KeNHA had similarly advised the public to remain vigilant and verify any recruitment information before sharing personal documents or making payments. The prevalence of such scams underscores the need for constant public awareness and verification of official communications.
