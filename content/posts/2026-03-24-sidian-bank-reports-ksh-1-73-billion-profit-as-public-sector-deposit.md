## Sidian Bank's Transformative Growth and Public Sector Mandates Sidian Bank reported a KSh 1.73 billion profit after tax for the year ended December 31, 2025, a sixfold increase from KSh 287.35 million, marking its strongest financial result in 40 years. This performance was significantly driven by a surge in public sector deposits and a strategic shift towards government securities.

Financial Performance Overview The bank's net interest income increased by 54.6% to KSh 4.43 billion, while non-interest income more than doubled to KSh 3.80 billion from KSh 1.73 billion. A substantial elevenfold increase in "other income," from KSh 188.76 million to KSh 2.09 billion, constituted 55.0% of the total non-interest income. Other fees and commissions grew 35.4% to KSh 1.09 billion, although foreign exchange trading income decreased by 40.3% to KSh 192.04 million. Operating income rose 79.2% to KSh 8.24 billion, and the cost-to-income ratio improved to a historic 73.2% from 89.9%.

Public Sector Deposit Expansion Sidian Bank's deposit base nearly tripled from KSh 27.62 billion at the end of 2023, primarily fueled by new public sector deposit mandates. Nairobi County Government designated Sidian as its principal banker in October 2025, transferring accounts from Co-operative Bank and Equity Bank. The bank now manages the county's health facilities, revenue collection, donor funds, and Facility Improvement Fund. Sidian also acts as a receiving agent for the Social Health Authority and the housing levy. Additionally, the bank secured deposits from state corporations such as Kenya Railways, where it is a primary banker, and KEMSA, where it ranks among the top deposit holders. In March 2026, Nairobi MCAs approved a KSh 1.7 billion monthly payroll overdraft facility with Sidian Bank.

Asset Allocation Shift The bank channeled a significant portion of its new deposits into Treasury bills and bonds. Its holdings in government securities more than doubled to KSh 49.05 billion from KSh 23.72 billion, now representing 54.0% of total assets, compared to 12.1% a decade prior. Interest income from government paper increased by 115.0% to KSh 4.51 billion, surpassing loans as the bank's largest income source for the first time. In contrast, loans constitute just 30.3% of total assets currently.

Ownership Transition and Capital Plans The financial transformation aligns with a complete change in ownership. Centum Investment Company completed its full exit in March 2026, ending a 25-year involvement. A planned KSh 4.3 billion sale to Nigeria's Access Bank collapsed in January 2023. The current shareholder structure is led by Solomon Muriithi Maina's Wizpro Enterprises (24.95%), alongside Afram Limited (24.36%) and Pioneer General Insurance (16.89%). Former Ugandan attorney-general William Byaruhanga holds 14.63% through Kenbe Investments. CEO Chege Thumbi plans to raise an additional KSh 3 billion in fresh capital, building on a recently completed KSh 3 billion rights issue.

Asset Quality and Loan Book Gross non-performing loans (NPLs) increased by 6.8% to KSh 8.23 billion, a more modest rise compared to the 71.9% jump in the previous year. However, the NPL stock has grown tenfold since 2014, while net loans increased 2.6 times over the same period. Loan loss provisions against NPLs rose to KSh 3.10 billion from KSh 2.65 billion, improving the coverage ratio to 37.7% from 34.4%.

Growth Dynamics and Scrutiny Sidian's rapid growth is unprecedented in Kenyan banking, with deposits growing 162% in two years and the bank moving from Tier 3 to Tier 2. However, this expansion relies on a narrow foundation, with the Nairobi County relationship, in particular, facing political and legal scrutiny. Senator Edwin Sifuna questioned the shift from a Tier 1 bank, and civil rights group Bunge La Mwananchi has petitioned the High Court, citing concerns about public participation and transparency. Kenyan laws define everyday actions that could result in arrest, and these legal frameworks are often invoked in public sector compliance matters. The fee structure for the proposed revenue collection partnership has not been disclosed, drawing comparisons to previous arrangements that faced compliance disputes.

Future Diversification Strategies The bank's deposit growth, while substantial, is largely policy-driven and could be redirected with changes in administration or procurement cycles. To mitigate this, Sidian is pursuing diversification through SACCO partnerships, with over 120 across Kenya, and a planned branch expansion from 30 to over 100 locations. The success of these initiatives in attracting sticky retail and SME deposits will be crucial for establishing a durable franchise beyond its current institutional book.
