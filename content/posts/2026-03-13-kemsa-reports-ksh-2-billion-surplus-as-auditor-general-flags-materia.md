By Grace Mkamburi, Za Ndani

## KEMSA Financial Turnaround Amidst Audit Scrutiny

KEMSA reported a KSh 2 billion surplus, reversing a previous deficit, driven by a 9% revenue increase and 20% expense reduction. Despite this, the Auditor-General issued a qualified opinion, highlighting major financial record inaccuracies, undisclosed donor inventory, and unaddressed liabilities, signaling underlying operational and governance challenges.

### Financial Performance Overview

The Kenya Medical Supplies Authority (KEMSA) recorded a KSh 2 billion surplus, a significant reversal from the KSh 779.1 million deficit in the prior year. This improved financial position resulted from a 9% increase in revenue, reaching KSh 9.49 billion, coupled with a 20% reduction in total expenses to KSh 7.43 billion.

### Auditor General's Material Concerns

Despite the reported surplus, the Auditor-General issued a qualified opinion on KEMSA's financial statements, flagging four critical concerns. These included inaccuracies in receivables and property, plant, and equipment records. The Auditor also cited inappropriate disclosure of KSh 7.61 billion in donor-owned inventory, which KEMSA does not legally own or control. Additionally, KEMSA failed to recognize a KSh 21.4 million liability for damaged third-party stocks.

### Cash and Banking Relations

KEMSA's cash and cash equivalents grew by 78%, reaching KSh 3.079 billion at year-end from KSh 1.730 billion previously. National Bank of Kenya (NBK) remained the primary custodian with KSh 1.057 billion across two accounts, followed by Equity Bank holding KSh 951.6 million across four accounts, and Co-operative Bank with KSh 774.4 million. Notably, Sidian Bank emerged as a new custodian, holding KSh 341.0 million, representing approximately 11% of total holdings by June 30, 2025.

### Receivables and Supplier Obligations

Receivables from exchange transactions stood at KSh 6.28 billion at year-end. County governments accounted for KSh 2.7 billion of this balance; however, debt confirmations from 43 counties showed only KSh 1.15 billion outstanding, leaving an unexplained KSh 1.54 billion variance. A further KSh 2.99 billion had been outstanding for over one year without an agreed payment plan or provision for bad debts. The average collection period reached an extended 487 days. On the other side, KEMSA owed suppliers KSh 5.71 billion, with KSh 1.73 billion outstanding for over 90 days without an approved payment plan.

### Operational Inefficiencies and Inventory Management

KEMSA distributed health products valued at KSh 24.7 billion to more than 11,500 public health facilities. However, core delivery metrics remained significantly below target. The capital order fill rate for essential medicines reached only 41% against a 90% target, while donor-programme commodities achieved a 79% fill rate, also below the threshold. Order turnaround times deteriorated to 19.5 days for hospitals (7-day target) and 24.2 days for primary health facilities (10-day target). Six of KEMSA's seven regional warehouses lacked distribution capacity, routing all orders through Nairobi. Expired commodity losses totaled KSh 547.1 million for the year. Effective inventory management and adherence to regulatory frameworks are critical for public health agencies, much like Nigeria's efforts to enhance cosmetics safety.

### Project Delays and Governance Lapses

Key projects faced substantial delays. A KSh 499.7 million SAP ERP system, contracted in November 2023 for completion by May 2024, was only 70% complete at the audit time. A KSh 3.08 billion warehouse contract from 2018, due in September 2021, remains unfinished, with a subcontractor abandoning mechanical works over seven years ago. KEMSA's strategic plan expired in December 2024 without replacement, breaching a government circular requiring new plans by June 30, 2024. No governance audit was conducted during the year, and the credit policy had not been reviewed in ten years.
