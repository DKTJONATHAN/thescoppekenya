## The Surveillance Dividend: Why East Africa’s Digital Economy is Being Rebuilt as a Tax Net East African Governments Prioritize Tax Systems Over Digital Grow is the central subject of the update.

Government budget estimates across East Africa for the 2026/27 fiscal year reveal a sobering pivot. Digital transformation funds are being diverted to automate revenue collection instead of expanding the foundational infrastructure required for private sector innovation.

The narrative of a thriving East African digital economy is hitting a structural wall as state treasuries tighten their belts. In Kenya, the most prominent casualty is the budget for digital economy and creative industries, which has been slashed from KES 12.7 billion to a mere KES 8 billion. This reduction of KES 4.7 billion is a deliberate fiscal choice rather than an accidental oversight. While projects like the Digital Economy Acceleration Program remain on the books with a KES 4.3 billion allocation, the broader ecosystem is seeing capital flow toward high-frequency revenue monitoring tools like the eTIMS platform. When the government speaks of digitizing the economy, the current budget suggests it is prioritizing the footprint of a taxpayer over the growth of a startup.

### Capital Allocation Priorities

The shift in capital allocation paints a clear picture of state intentions. While the Fourth Industrial Revolution is frequently invoked in public policy speeches, regional budget data from CIO Africa indicates that the reality on the ground is far more utilitarian. Tanzania is a prime example; the country has prioritized significant investment in high-capacity digital infrastructure to modernize its national utility systems. Meanwhile, Rwanda is funneling $200 million from a World Bank-backed project into digital identity systems. While these tools offer administrative efficiency, they serve as the bedrock for more aggressive tax compliance across both formal and informal sectors.

In Uganda, the science, technology, and innovation budget of UGX 1.14 trillion is increasingly tied to project-based monitoring that allows the state to track commercial activity with granular precision. For tech startups, this creates a lopsided landscape. Companies that align their business models with state-led compliance infrastructure find themselves favored, while those aiming for regional scaling through open-access digital platforms find public funding for connectivity and foundational cloud services drying up. As seen with recent shifts like KCB Bank scrapping fees for small transfers, the private sector is attempting to lower friction for users. However, they are doing so against a backdrop of state infrastructure designed primarily for fiscal observation.

### The Long-Term Cost of Extraction

This reliance on technology as a tax-collection utility threatens to dampen the very innovation required to sustain the regional economy. When a government prioritizes the installation of tax-tracking software over the expansion of reliable, high-speed broadband in underserved counties, it caps the growth potential of local businesses. Startups that once looked toward state-backed initiatives for their growth are now finding that the only government-backed tech projects moving forward are those designed to integrate businesses into the national tax net.

The digital economy is being rebuilt as a tax net. By transforming the digital ecosystem into a vehicle for revenue extraction, governments are creating a paradox: they are digitizing commerce to increase the tax yield today at the cost of expanding the digital marketplace for tomorrow. The regional obsession with compliance-based digital infrastructure creates a high barrier to entry for early-stage founders who cannot afford the overhead of integrating into complex state revenue reporting systems from day one. Investors should be wary of the assumption that government ICT spending signals growth. In East Africa, these numbers are increasingly indicative of how the state intends to monitor, track, and ultimately tax the digital evolution of the citizen.

Fiscal appetites will continue to override innovation. This trend suggests that for the foreseeable future, the digital ledger will serve the interests of the tax collector far more effectively than it serves the entrepreneur. Founders, developers, and investors must recognize that the digital frontier in East Africa is currently defined by regulatory reach rather than technological liberation. As the state refines its ability to extract value from every transaction, the capacity for organic, startup-led growth becomes secondary to the efficiency of the treasury.
