## Shareholders Initiate Board Ouster at WPP Scangroup

A collective of minority shareholders holding a 13.59 percent stake in WPP Scangroup has officially requisitioned the removal of the company’s nine-member board. This action marks a significant escalation in tensions surrounding the firm’s governance, fueled by a total of KSh 3.1 billion in trading losses recorded over the past four years. The move is currently reshaping discourse on the Nairobi Securities Exchange regarding executive accountability and the limits of investor tolerance.

### A Formal Challenge to Governance

The petition, submitted under the provisions of the Companies Act of 2015, demonstrates a tactical application of minority rights. By exceeding the 5 percent shareholding threshold required to call an Extraordinary General Meeting, these investors are challenging the status quo of corporate inertia common in the local market. For years, observers have noted that many boards at the Nairobi bourse remain shielded from the consequences of poor financial performance. This requisition serves as a practical test of whether existing legal mechanisms can effectively disrupt that insulation.

The catalysts for this rebellion include a sharp downturn in the firm’s financial trajectory. Between 2021 and 2025, WPP Scangroup saw revenues collapse from KSh 7 billion to KSh 2.04 billion. This period of contraction saw the loss of major clients including KCB, Equity Bank, NCBA, and Airtel Africa. Beyond these operational failures, shareholders have cited specific fiscal management concerns as evidence of a leadership vacuum. A KSh 1.2 billion loan granted to WPP Group Services SNC has become a point of contention, while a KSh 1.95 million fine for data protection failures further damaged the board’s standing. These issues, compounded by the lingering complications from the 2021 leadership transition, have spurred shareholders to seek a total change in the company's oversight team.

### Shifting Dynamics on the Nairobi Securities Exchange

This push to oust the board reflects a broader evolution in the Kenyan capital market. Historically, investors have shown a tendency toward passive observation, often deferring to the decisions of incumbent boards. The WPP Scangroup case breaks this pattern, signaling that investors are becoming increasingly assertive in demanding professional stewardship. By moving beyond traditional internal resolutions, these shareholders are asserting their influence as primary stakeholders rather than mere bystanders.

The implications for other listed companies are clear. Boards operating under poor performance metrics can no longer assume that investor patience is indefinite. The 5 percent threshold for requisitioning meetings has shifted from a theoretical legal provision to an actionable tool for oversight. Market participants are increasingly emphasizing corporate governance as a key performance indicator. This transition indicates a growing maturity in the investment landscape, where the quality of stewardship is scrutinized with the same intensity as financial disclosures. This heightened level of scrutiny requires investors to remain aware of all risks, whether they arise from internal corporate failures or broader issues, such as the persistent need for vigilance against fraudulent schemes often highlighted by regulatory warnings.

### The Path Toward Accountability

The dispute at WPP Scangroup stands as a notable instance of direct shareholder intervention. It highlights the utility of the Companies Act of 2015 in fostering transparency and executive responsibility. If this effort succeeds, it could set a powerful precedent for the Nairobi bourse, potentially forcing boards across the market to prioritize shareholder value over legacy management structures. The transition from passive ownership to active stewardship is a defining trend of the current fiscal year.

### The Regulatory Roadmap

All eyes are now on the board’s formal response to the requisition. The company is legally obligated to convene an Extraordinary General Meeting within a specified timeframe. If the board fails to act, the law allows the requisitioning shareholders to initiate the meeting process independently. Investors should look for upcoming regulatory filings with the Capital Markets Authority for updates on the meeting date and the formal agenda. The ensuing vote will be the decisive factor in the firm's future governance, serving as a bellwether for the level of institutional appetite for activist pressure. As this situation unfolds, the market remains on high alert, watching to see if this trend of accountability will permeate other sectors, including the fast-evolving digital and creative industries.
