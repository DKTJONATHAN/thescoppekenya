## Demi Moore and the Artificial Intelligence Shift at Cannes

Demi Moore recently sparked a lively debate at the Cannes Film Festival. She encouraged filmmakers to stop resisting technological progress and instead find ways to integrate new tools into their workflows. Her call for collaboration provides a useful roadmap for Kenyan creators who are looking to scale their projects and reach global audiences.

The entertainment industry is moving through a period of intense change. In France, Moore argued that viewing software as an existential threat is counterproductive. She proposed that the future of storytelling depends on a partnership between human imagination and machine assistance. This conversation is not limited to Hollywood boardrooms. It is a vital signal for creators in Nairobi, Kisumu, and Mombasa who want to compete effectively on the international stage.

### Why the resistance narrative is fading

For years, many feared that automation would eventually replace actors and writers. Moore suggests this perspective is shortsighted. By choosing to work alongside these systems, artists can potentially boost their output and reach. Think back to the industry transition from physical film reels to digital editing. That move was a significant hurdle, yet it eventually lowered the barrier to entry, allowing independent storytellers to flourish.

The industry is already adjusting its rules to match this reality. Major institutions like the Academy and the Golden Globes have updated their eligibility requirements to account for the use of artificial intelligence. These policies are designed to build a framework where human-led work remains at the center of the creative process. For Kenyan producers who frequently manage tight budgets, these digital tools could be the key to achieving high-end production value without the need for massive studio support. Much like the broader economic landscape-where the Central Bank of Kenya continues to license new digital lenders to create stability-the creative sector must also find ways to regulate and leverage modern tech to ensure a sustainable future.

### What this means for Kenyan talent

The local film industry is overflowing with talent, but creators often lack the resources found in massive international post-production suites. Moore’s perspective offers a path forward for local filmmakers. Strategic integration of these tools could help bridge the gap in visual effects, sound design, and script refinement. 

Stars such as Reese Witherspoon and Sandra Bullock have echoed similar sentiments, noting that while many artists remain cautious, others are eager to explore the capabilities of these systems. The goal for a Kenyan creator should not be to outsource the entire script to an algorithm. Instead, treat the software as a brainstorming partner. If you can save time on pre-production logistics, you have more bandwidth to focus on the authentic, raw storytelling that makes Kenyan cinema resonate with global audiences. We are seeing a push for higher professional standards across all sectors, including the Media Council of Kenya’s ongoing advocacy for press freedom. The creative arts should follow that same spirit of evolution. Adaptability will be our greatest asset in the coming years.

### Why it matters now

The global film community is watching how Cannes balances tradition with innovation. By setting a precedent for collaboration, the festival is signaling that the future of the industry is hybrid. For Kenyan filmmakers, this means the international market will soon expect a baseline level of digital fluency. If we ignore these tools, we risk falling behind. If we embrace them responsibly, we can export our narratives more efficiently than ever before.

### What to watch next

Keep a close eye on the submission guidelines for local film festivals over the next year. As international bodies refine their policies, local organizations will likely adopt similar frameworks to ensure our entries remain eligible for global competitions. Seek out workshops focused on digital literacy for writers and editors. These practical skills will be the most effective way to stay relevant in an increasingly competitive market. 

Real innovation in Kenyan film will come from those who use technology to refine their stories rather than those who allow the software to dictate the plot. Focus on the tools that enhance your vision and keep your unique perspective at the heart of every frame.
