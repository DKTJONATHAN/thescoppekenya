## The AfCFTA Disconnect: Evaluating the Cost of Regional Integration

HACO Industries frames its recent shift toward an intra-African supply chain as a triumph of regional integration. By replacing international licensing with materials sourced from Egypt, Tunisia, and Ghana, the firm aims to navigate a new trade landscape. However, for the average Kenyan shopper, this strategic pivot remains invisible. Despite the supposed advantages of continental trade preferences, supermarket prices continue to climb, leaving many to question if the promised economic relief will ever materialize.

### The Disparity Between Policy and Price

The African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) is frequently presented as a catalyst for manufacturing growth. Under the framework, regional raw materials face a 5 percent import duty, theoretically lowering production overheads. Yet, this optimistic math ignores the harsh reality of energy costs. Industrial entities in Egypt benefit from electricity rates near 0.03 US cents per kilowatt hour, whereas Kenyan manufacturers contend with a rate of 0.18 US cents. 

When HACO relocates its sourcing to North Africa, it effectively exploits this massive discrepancy in production input costs. These savings are being funneled into margin protection rather than being passed down to the retail buyer. Under the leadership of Marianne Musangi, the company is prioritizing corporate solvency and bottom-line stability. While this is a rational move for a business in a volatile market, it exposes a gap between trade policy goals and consumer experience. The expected 5 percent tariff benefit is failing to bridge the gap between production efficiency and the final shelf price in Nairobi.

### Infrastructure and the Cost of Inefficiency

While proponents of the AfCFTA look to policy as the solution, the physical reality of African transit remains the primary obstacle. Industry leaders, including Tobias Alando of the Kenya Association of Manufacturers, have proposed centralized hubs like a warehouse in Zambia to streamline logistics. Such plans, however, fail to account for the systemic dysfunction at bottlenecks like the Nakonde border. 

The financial burden of moving goods across the continent is immense. A 20-foot container shipment carries freight costs between 3,500 and 7,000 dollars. On top of this, bureaucratic documentation hurdles add between 15,000 and 30,000 shillings per shipment. The delay is equally punishing. When transit times stretch to 30 days or trucks linger at borders for 2.5 days, that downtime transforms into a hidden tax. 

The consumer ultimately pays this surcharge at the checkout counter. Although regional manufacturing is designed to shorten supply chains, the persistent failure of transport infrastructure cancels out the fiscal gains of trade agreements. Instead of fostering genuine price competition, the current system merely shifts where capital is deployed without addressing the underlying logistical waste. While national governments tout new financial models intended to reduce reliance on foreign aid, local industries remain shackled to the same inefficient transit networks that existed decades ago.

### The Reality of Corporate Strategy

The AfCFTA is marketed as a boon for the common citizen, but the restructuring of companies like HACO confirms that manufacturing localization is a survival tactic rather than a humanitarian initiative. Until the continent addresses the massive disparity in energy costs and clears the logistical choke points that inflate transit times, the Kenyan consumer will continue to bear the cost of regional inefficiency. 

Moving forward, stakeholders must watch for tangible retail adjustments. If the migration of supply chains to lower-cost markets like Tunisia and Egypt does not eventually translate to lower prices for the buyer, the AfCFTA risks being viewed as a mechanism for corporate profit optimization rather than broad-based economic development. If the proposed distribution hubs in places like Lusaka fail to meaningfully reduce the 30-day transit bottleneck, we should expect manufacturing firms to abandon local operations in favor of further consolidation in more stable jurisdictions. The question remains: who actually profits from regional trade if the consumer is left with the same stagnant price tag?
