## Counter-terrorism on the Garissa-Dadaab road: A reactive charade

The arrest of a lone suspect with an IED near Haji Aden today is being framed as a tactical win, yet it masks a deeper, systemic failure. Security forces are chasing shadows on a corridor where intelligence gaps remain the real threat.

### The myth of the tactical victory

Security agencies are currently celebrating the interception of a suspect carrying an improvised explosive device on the Garissa-Dadaab road. A multi-agency patrol followed a pursuit in the Haji Aden area, resulting in the recovery of the device, military-grade attire, and two mobile phones. While the public is invited to breathe a sigh of relief because the device did not detonate, the reality is far colder. An intercepted suspect at the point of near-detonation is not evidence of a robust security apparatus; it is evidence of a failure in proactive threat neutralization.

This incident marks yet another instance where our border region security relies on reactive chases rather than the disruption of terror networks during the planning phase. If an individual can transport volatile materials to within striking distance of a major thoroughfare without being flagged until a physical chase occurs, the intelligence perimeter is effectively non-existent. We are watching a recurring pattern: checkpoints acting as the last line of defense rather than the final filter in a web of surveillance that should have caught these actors weeks ago.

### Holes in the perimeter

The Garissa-Dadaab route remains a sieve. Despite consistent budgetary allocations for high-tech surveillance, the dependence on manual stops and impulsive pursuits highlights a chronic lack of integrated intelligence. In this region, physical roadblocks are frequently the only barrier between a militant cell and a catastrophe. There is no evidence of a cohesive digital surveillance network that identifies movement patterns or logistical supply chains long before an IED enters a vehicle. 

Security planners often point to the difficult terrain of Northern Kenya, but terrain is a constant, not an excuse for the lack of actionable, forward-looking intelligence. When security relies on the luck of a patrol stumbling upon a threat, it leaves the civilian population-and the officers themselves-in a state of perpetual, avoidable vulnerability. While urban infrastructure issues, like the recent [Nairobi Water supply interruption following a pipeline burst](https://zandani.co.ke/article/nairobi-water-reports-supply-interruption-following-kabete-kilimani-), cause significant public irritation, the gaps in national security infrastructure represent an existential peril that remains unaddressed by standard police briefings.

### Why it matters now

The normalization of these near-misses is dangerous. By focusing the narrative on the arrest, the authorities avoid answering for why the suspect was able to acquire materials and reach the road in the first place. This state-led narrative discourages an honest audit of why intelligence networks in Garissa County are consistently outpaced by the individuals they are sworn to track.

### What to watch next

The suspect is currently held at Garissa Police Station, but the focus must shift to the content of those two mobile phones. If the investigation merely processes this individual as a lone actor, the policy gaps will persist. Watch for whether the DCI provides evidence of a wider network disruption or if this is filed away as an isolated win while the next device is already being assembled elsewhere. 

How many more high-stakes chases must occur before the intelligence-sharing failure that allows these threats to reach the roadside is treated with the same urgency as the pursuit itself?
