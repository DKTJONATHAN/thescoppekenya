## The Judiciary Keeps the Status Quo Under Lock and Key

Justice Bahati Mwamuye has effectively kicked the constitutional can down the road, choosing to prioritize the rigid framework of the Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act over a substantive debate on the limits of religious freedom in Kenya.

The High Court dismissed the petition brought by the Rastafari Society of Kenya, citing a lack of sufficient evidentiary burden regarding the infringement of rights. While the ruling appears to be a procedural administrative decision, it functions as a strategic pivot. By fixating on the failure to provide data, the court bypassed the existential question lurking beneath the case: at what point does a constitutional mandate under Article 32 collide with the state's historical obsession with criminalizing specific cultural behaviors?

### The Evidentiary Wall

The court did not dismantle the argument that the Rastafarian faith centers on the use of bhang as a sacrament. Instead, Justice Mwamuye focused his critique on the petitioners' failure to demonstrate that the current statutory prohibition constitutes a disproportionate restriction that violates their specific, protected rights. It is a classic judicial tactic. When faced with the friction between entrenched penal laws and evolving human rights claims, the judiciary often retreats into the safety of technical deficiencies rather than issuing a definitive ruling on the conflict itself.

This approach ensures that the state avoids the messy business of creating legal precedent. If the court had engaged with the constitutional merit, it would have been forced to define the boundaries between secular governance and personal conscience. By maintaining the evidentiary gap, the bench preserves a convenient silence. This mirrors the pattern seen in the [Microsoft and G42 Data Center project](https://zandani.co.ke/article/microsoft-g42-data-center-project-stalls-kenya), where systemic administrative bottlenecks-whether they involve infrastructure capacity or constitutional interpretation-serve to maintain the status quo while delaying inevitable, and perhaps uncomfortable, policy reckoning.

### A Global Vacuum and the Kenyan Reality

Other jurisdictions have struggled with similar tensions. In various countries, courts have been compelled to distinguish between public health concerns and the preservation of indigenous or religious cultural identities. South Africa, for instance, saw its courts move toward a more nuanced acknowledgment of personal use rights, forcing the legislature to catch up. In Kenya, however, the judiciary remains firmly anchored in a restrictive interpretation that treats narcotics legislation as untouchable, regardless of the competing constitutional claims.

Legal experts point to a growing constitutional vacuum. If the courts refuse to define whether religious practice provides an exemption to statutory law, the government retains unchecked authority to police the private habits of its citizens under the guise of public safety. This outcome leaves a clear message for future litigants: unless one can provide empirical, state-approved data to satisfy a court’s narrow evidentiary demands, fundamental constitutional rights remain subservient to existing statutes.

The rejection of this petition is not just a loss for the Rastafari community. It is a signal that the Kenyan judiciary is unwilling to arbitrate the most pressing contradictions of the modern era. When the court sidesteps the interpretation of religious freedom, it effectively outsources the drafting of moral policy to the police and the state’s narcotics agencies.

If the judiciary is tasked with protecting the Constitution, but chooses to let state statutes hold it hostage whenever a conflict arises, what exactly remains of the promise that our laws are supreme?
