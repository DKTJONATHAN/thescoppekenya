President Ruto Calms South Rift Dissatisfaction Over Stalled is central to this update for Kenyan readers.

President William Ruto recently toured his South Rift political stronghold, Kericho and Bomet counties, addressing widespread local discontent. He acknowledged concerns over stalled projects and slow progress since his inauguration, appealing for patience and pledging prioritization of development and appointments in his second term.

## President Ruto Addresses South Rift Discontent

President William Ruto’s recent visit to the South Rift region was more than a routine political engagement; it was an effort to address deep-seated dissatisfaction within his political base. Local leaders and residents openly voiced their frustrations, highlighting numerous development projects that have either stalled or failed to materialize, leaving many feeling underserved. The President acknowledged these grievances, appealing to the Kalenjin community for understanding and assuring them that their "share" of development and government appointments would be prioritized in his second term. This appeal for patience was widely interpreted as an attempt to calm growing political unrest.

### The Bitter Taste of Neglect

A profound sense of disillusionment permeates the region. Many residents lament that despite having one of their own in the highest office, tangible development has been painfully slow. A widely circulated critique highlights a bitter irony, asserting that previous administrations, including those led by "outsider" Presidents Mwai Kibaki and Uhuru Kenyatta, delivered more significant infrastructural projects and economic benefits-such as improved tea prices for farmers-to Kalenjin land than what has been observed under their "own" Kalenjin presidents combined. This sharp comparison underscores the depth of current frustration.

### Beyond Prayers and Clapping

The perceived lack of progress has fostered a cynical view of the community's engagement with the national government. Many residents express a sentiment that their primary role since 2022 has been to "join the president in prayer services, shouting 'Tutam!' (a campaign slogan meaning 'we will win/support'), and clapping," while other regions appear to benefit more substantially from government initiatives. This feeling of being sidelined, despite their unwavering support, is palpable. Critics contend that while national attention focuses on advancements elsewhere, tea farmers in the South Rift, whose produce is a core economic pillar, continue to grapple with inconsistent prices and inadequate support.

### A Second Term Promise: Too Little, Too Late?

President Ruto's assurance that development will be prioritized in a hypothetical second term has done little to soothe jaded nerves across the region. For many, this sounds like a familiar plea for continued loyalty without immediate, tangible returns. A scathing commentary questions why the President would "beg for mercy" and promise future rewards when the present is marked by stalled projects and unmet expectations. As national conversations unfold, the people of the South Rift are left questioning when their share of national progress will materialize, seeking tangible action beyond mere promises.

From Kericho to Bomet, a clear message resonates: for the Kalenjin community, patience is wearing thin, and the "maendeleo" (development) promised for tomorrow is urgently expected today.
