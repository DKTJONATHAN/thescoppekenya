## Accountability at the Ministry of Education

The Consumers Federation of Kenya has formally requested the Public Service Commission to initiate the immediate removal of Basic Education Principal Secretary Julius Bitok, citing systemic negligence and the failure to uphold safety standards in national learning institutions.

This petition arrives as the Ministry faces mounting scrutiny following the May 28 tragedy at Utumishi Girls Academy, where a dormitory fire resulted in 16 deaths and left 70 students hospitalized. The consumer lobby argues that the incident is a direct consequence of institutional inertia rather than an isolated accident. By filing this formal grievance, the organization is testing the limits of civil society influence over senior ministerial appointments.

The core of the legal challenge rests on Article 155(4) of the Constitution, which mandates that the appointment and removal of a Principal Secretary must adhere to established standards of integrity, efficiency, and professional conduct. The federation asserts that Bitok has breached these requirements by allegedly failing to enforce safety protocols across public and private boarding schools. The petition outlines six specific grounds for removal, ranging from gross misconduct to administrative incompetence in oversight functions.

This effort to challenge a high-ranking government official mirrors past legal battles, such as the [High Court petition regarding Noordin Haji](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-03-12-high-court-dismisses-petition-challenging-appointment-of-no), which sought to address perceived failures in public integrity. While the courts have historically demonstrated reluctance to interfere with presidential appointments, this specific petition targets the administrative accountability mechanism provided by the Public Service Commission. The Commission is now required to conduct a preliminary review to determine if the allegations warrant a formal tribunal.

### Legal Hurdles for Institutional Reform

The process for removing a Principal Secretary is notoriously complex, requiring clear evidence of a breach of the constitution or gross professional failure. The Public Service Commission maintains a strict procedural framework for these petitions, which involves verifying the merits of the complaint before recommending further action to the President. Historically, such petitions face a steep climb, as the executive branch retains significant latitude in how it manages its core appointments.

Critics of the current administration have long argued that institutional change in the education sector requires more than policy updates; it demands a shift in personal accountability for those overseeing student welfare. Unlike previous protests that focused on policy debates, this petition forces a direct legal confrontation between the state and a consumer interest group. Success would set a significant precedent for how future citizens can hold appointees accountable for operational failures that lead to preventable loss of life.

### Perspectives on the Petition

Legal analysts suggest the petition serves as a litmus test for the independence of the Public Service Commission. If the Commission ignores the request, it risks appearing as an extension of the executive rather than a neutral oversight body. Conversely, by entertaining the claim, the Commission would signal a new phase of public scrutiny for state officials tasked with managing essential services.

Government spokespeople have remained largely silent on the petition, opting instead to focus on the ongoing investigations into the Utumishi Girls Academy fire. However, the pressure on the Education Ministry is palpable as stakeholders demand immediate reforms to school boarding standards. With the school safety crisis gaining national attention, the government faces the dual challenge of addressing the structural flaws in fire safety while managing the reputational damage caused by calls for the resignation of its senior leadership.

The Public Service Commission is expected to signal its next steps by the end of the week. Observers are watching to see whether the body dismisses the petition on technical grounds or allows the evidence to be debated in a public hearing, which would significantly raise the political stakes for the Ministry of Education.
