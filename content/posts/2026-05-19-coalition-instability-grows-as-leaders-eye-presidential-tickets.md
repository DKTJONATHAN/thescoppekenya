## The 2027 Pre-emptive Post-mortem: Why Coalition Collapses are Already Costing Kenyans

**Political elites have abandoned their mandate, hijacking public office for a self-serving scramble toward the 2027 ballot. This premature obsession has effectively turned every county and legislative seat into a theater of private war, stalling the nation’s progress at the taxpayer's expense.**

The current Broad-Based Government was sold as a necessary scaffold for national stability. Instead, it has become a mechanism for institutional decay. While Nairobi’s political class huddles in secret to carve up future nomination slots, the machinery of state is grinding to a halt. This is not mere political theater; it is a direct levy on every citizen who continues to bankroll a governing class that has traded its duty for personal ambition.

### Devolved Stagnation
The consequences of this transition are most toxic in the counties, where the race for gubernatorial tickets has crippled the delivery of essential services. In regions ranging from Homa Bay and Siaya to Nyandarua, development agendas are being held hostage by factional warfare. Rather than prioritizing healthcare systems or critical infrastructure, local leaders are focused on cannibalizing political rivals to solidify their 2027 standing.

We are witnessing a return to the most cynical patterns of our political history. Budgetary allocations are now being weaponized as tools of patronage, funneling resources to loyalists while starving dissenting constituencies of funding. This internal sabotage directly stalls road repairs and water projects that were promised to the electorate. When a Governor prioritizes the mechanics of party primaries over the health of the local market economy, the voter suffers. This behavior serves as a stark reminder that resource control remains the primary motivation for our elected officials, regardless of the official platform they campaigned on.

### Legislative Gridlock as Policy
The National Assembly has been reduced to a hollow chamber, its legislative output sacrificed at the altar of factional dominance. Vital discourse regarding the crushing cost-of-living crisis is routinely sidelined to make space for petty brinkmanship. When legislators spend their working hours lobbying for alliances instead of rigorously scrutinizing finance bills or resolving public sector disputes, it is the average Kenyan who foots the bill.

Even when the government attempts to address pressing crises, such as the mediation of the teachers’ medical scheme, the process is invariably tainted by competing political brokers. When the house becomes a staging ground for future presidential campaigns, productivity collapses. The electorate is effectively paying the salaries of representatives who are no longer working for their districts, but for shadow campaigns that possess no interest in the daily struggle for survival.

### The Illusion of Competition
Some apologists characterize this internal friction as the hallmark of a healthy democracy. They claim that competition within a coalition keeps leaders accountable. This is a dangerous fallacy. True competition is born from robust policy debate and measurable track records. What we are witnessing is not a contest of ideas, but a fight for survival predicated on tribal and regional zoning.

This fixation on the 2027 calendar prevents the current administration from achieving meaningful economic progress. By shifting the internal focus to dominance, coalition leaders have ensured that accountability is diluted by design. When no single leader is responsible for policy failure, the entire system becomes immune to criticism.

### Why it matters now
The national economy is fragile, and public patience has hit a breaking point. When political parties focus exclusively on their internal hierarchy, they broadcast a clear message: the immediate needs of the people-affordable food, quality healthcare, and functional infrastructure-are secondary to the pursuit of power. The ongoing infighting confirms that the next election cycle is being prioritized over the preservation of the household budget.

### What to watch next
Observe the shifts in the legislative agenda over the coming months. If the National Assembly continues to prioritize motions that serve party posturing over public interest, it will be clear that the political elite have checked out of the current governance cycle. Watch for further fractures in regional power bases, where local officials will likely defect to alternative brokers as the primary season nears.

A political class that consumes itself will eventually starve the public. Unless the electorate demands a return to the mandates of governance, we are effectively watching the slow-motion collapse of the institutions designed to protect us. The 2027 ticket is a distant, selfish prize; the welfare of the Kenyan people is a present obligation that is currently being ignored.
