Nyeri town MP Duncan Maina on Saturday, April 18, 2026, urged education stakeholders to urgently reassess university and TVET courses. He warned that many current programs fail to meet the demands of a job market increasingly shaped by technology and artificial intelligence, leaving graduates unprepared for emerging opportunities.

Curriculum Alignment Imperative MP Maina's call emphasizes the urgent need for a strategic overhaul of higher education curricula to better equip young people for the evolving economy. His concerns highlight a growing disparity between academic offerings and the practical demands of employers who prioritize digital literacy and practical experience over theoretical knowledge. This comes as the Kenya Universities and Colleges Central Placement Service (KUCCPS) is currently processing applications for 2025 KCSE students, with the application window closing on May 6.

Regional Disparity in IT Integration Maina specifically compared academic programmes across institutions in the Mount Kenya and Lake regions, noting a significant difference in the integration of information technology. He cited Dedan Kimathi University of Technology, Karatina University, and Kirinyaga University in the Mount Kenya region for offering numerous standalone courses lacking robust digital skill integration. In contrast, institutions such as Maseno University and Tom Mboya University in the Lake region embed IT across diverse disciplines, including education, engineering, and medicine, making their graduates more competitive in the labour market.

Political Focus Overshadows Education Needs The legislator warned that political priorities within the region have overshadowed critical discussions about education's role in positioning young people for employment in a rapidly changing economy. He stressed that this focus on politics has led to a neglect of fundamental aspects concerning the preparedness of the workforce. This broader issue of critical sector oversight is echoed in other areas, such as when senior medical consultants recently sought presidential intervention over Nairobi Hospital mismanagement, highlighting the need for focused leadership across various vital institutions.

Graduate Unemployment and Skills Gap Maina's concerns are timely, given the persistent issue of graduate unemployment. Reports from the Ministry of Education indicate that out of approximately 50,000 university graduates each year, fewer than 40 percent secure employment, leaving over 60 percent struggling to find meaningful work. This significant unemployment rate is compounded by a substantial gap between the skills taught in universities and those desired by employers, who increasingly value practical experience, digital proficiency, and essential soft skills.
