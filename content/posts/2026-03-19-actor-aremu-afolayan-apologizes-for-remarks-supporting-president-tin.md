Nigerian actor Aremu Afolayan, brother to filmmaker Kunle Afolayan, publicly apologized on March 19, 2026, for controversial remarks supporting President Bola Tinubu during a March 15 appearance on JUJU TV. His comments, which dismissed widespread hardship and insecurity in Nigeria, sparked significant online outrage, leading to his swift retraction.

Afolayan Faces Backlash Over Political Remarks

Initial Comments Spark Fury Aremu Afolayan's appearance on JUJU TV on March 15, 2026, generated significant buzz across social media platforms. The actor declared his intention to vote for President Tinubu in the 2027 elections, commending what he described as the president's efforts to unite Nigerians and his intervention in lifting the UAE's travel ban on Nigerian citizens. Afolayan's most contentious statements, however, concerned the nation's economic realities and security situation. He candidly dismissed widespread hardship, stating, "When people say the country is hard, I don't really care because I'm doing very well." On insecurity, he provocatively added, "When you watch the news about insurgency, don't mind them, it's all scripted."

Public Outcry and Calls for Accountability These remarks landed harshly in a country grappling with significant challenges, including soaring fuel costs, inconsistent electricity supply, and a security crisis that has displaced communities and claimed lives across various regions. Nigerians swiftly responded, flooding Afolayan's social media with expressions of frustration and condemnation. The sentiment online highlighted a perception that his statements were out of touch with the struggles faced by millions. The public discourse often links such issues to broader government fiscal policies, with ongoing discussions around national financial transparency.

A Dramatic Apology Just four days later, on March 19, 2026, Afolayan posted a video offering a public apology. Literally on his knees, he addressed Nigerians directly, pleading for forgiveness and acknowledging the intensity of the negative reactions. "To all Nigerians, please forgive me. I'm very sorry. The curses you guys are raining on me are too much," he stated. He also clarified that he had not received any financial inducement for his initial comments, seeking to counter accusations of being a "sellout." Afolayan attempted to reframe his controversial insecurity remarks, explaining that he meant anyone dismissing insecurity was "reading a script," not those experiencing it. As evidence of his sincerity, he revealed that his best friend's mother had recently been kidnapped, with a ransom subsequently paid. He concluded by announcing his withdrawal from all political engagement, using the Yoruba phrase "Epe yi po," signifying the overwhelming nature of the curses.

Skepticism Greets Afolayan's Reversal The online community has met Afolayan's apology with a predictable wave of skepticism. The prevailing sentiment among commenters suggests that the actor's reversal was primarily a response to the sheer volume of public backlash rather than a genuine change of heart. Many believe the retraction was a pragmatic move to mitigate further damage to his public image. The lasting impact of the incident on his career and public standing remains to be seen.
