## Should Kenya Worry About a Single Contractor Controlling Our Skyline? CRBC Lands Tatu City Jabali Towers Deal After JKIA Contract Win is the central subject of the update.

China Road and Bridge Corporation just locked in the contract for Jabali Towers, a massive mixed-use development at Tatu City, just six days after securing a massive state project at Jomo Kenyatta International Airport. This double win raises sharp questions about market concentration.

### What are the actual project footprints?

The Jabali Towers project represents a significant shift for Tatu City, the 5,000-acre special economic zone. This development spans a 25-storey tower featuring a 150-room hotel alongside 35 commercial units, designed to serve the growing residential and business hub of Tatu Central. It is a commercial venture backed by private capital.

Contrast this with the Jomo Kenyatta International Airport modernisation deal, signed by the government on June 23. That project is valued at approximately 375 billion shillings. It involves comprehensive upgrades to airside and terminal facilities, intended to position Nairobi as a regional aviation powerhouse. While the Jabali Towers deal is smaller in scale than the state-funded 154 billion shilling investment in regional airport expansion projects, the cumulative weight of both contracts places an immense volume of Kenya's most critical infrastructure in the hands of a single entity.

### Does one-sided procurement hurt the local economy?

When a single foreign contractor dominates both public and private bidding, the ripple effects are felt by Kenyan firms. Usually, large-scale construction requires local sub-contractors for services like site preparation, material supply, and civil works. However, when a lead firm holds a monopoly, the procurement process becomes top-down. Local Kenyan companies often find themselves competing for small crumbs rather than playing a significant role in the value chain of major infrastructure.

This pattern limits the growth of local construction capacity. For Kenyan firms to mature, they need experience on multi-billion shilling projects, not just as minor suppliers of raw materials, but as lead partners or tier-one sub-contractors. If the market continues to favor a single giant, the institutional knowledge and the capital generated from these massive builds will consistently flow out of the country rather than remaining within the local ecosystem to build indigenous capacity.

### What are the risks of relying on a single infrastructure giant?

Beyond the lack of diversity in the sector, there is a clear economic risk in over-reliance on one entity. When a contractor is involved in both private real estate and public infrastructure, they gain an unparalleled view of the nation's construction cost indices and supply chain bottlenecks. This visibility can distort competition. If a private developer sees the same entity working on the national airport, they often feel pressured to hire that same firm to ensure project completion, believing it provides better access to state resources or materials.

This concentration of power reduces the bargaining strength of Kenyan clients. Without vibrant local competition, costs for infrastructure projects are less likely to stay competitive. While efficiency is often cited as the reason for awarding such massive deals to established international firms, the long-term cost to the Kenyan taxpayer and the private sector is a hollowed-out construction industry that cannot support itself without foreign intervention.

Just as in other sectors where success stories like the [TikTok Sensation Dennis Wawira Transforms Maize Business Into Success Story](https://zandani.co.ke/article/tiktok-sensation-dennis-wawira-transforms-maize-business-into-succes) demonstrate the power of local entrepreneurship, the construction sector should ideally reflect a diverse range of players. Without a deliberate effort to diversify, the future of the Kenyan landscape remains tethered to the priorities and business models of one foreign partner.

When one company builds the airport and the high-rise offices next to our homes, we lose the diversity that keeps our infrastructure affordable and our local experts employed.
