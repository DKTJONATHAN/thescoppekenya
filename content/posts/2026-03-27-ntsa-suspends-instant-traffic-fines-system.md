The National Transport and Safety Authority (NTSA) has suspended its instant traffic fines system with immediate effect on Friday, March 27, 2026. Operational since March 9 on major highways, particularly in Nairobi, the system attracted lawsuits and significant public outcry, prompting the NTSA's reversal.

## Instant Fines System Halted for Public Education

NTSA Director General Nashon Kondiwa explained the sudden withdrawal is necessary to allow for further civic education and public awareness. This education will focus on proper procedures for handling minor traffic offences under Section 117 of the Traffic Act. The public notice announcing the system's March 9, 2026, launch has been withdrawn. NTSA is currently developing new standard procedures, which will be communicated after stakeholder engagement is complete.

### Legal Challenges and Court Proceedings

The suspension follows High Court orders issued two weeks prior, barring NTSA from implementing the instant fines system. Justice Bahati Mwamuye granted a conservatory order based on a petition filed by lawyer Shadrack Wambui, who challenged the directive. The matter is currently pending in court and is scheduled for mention on April 9, 2026, to confirm compliance and determine directions for the case's hearing.

### Review of Enforcement and Penalties

In response to motorist complaints, NTSA had previously revealed it was reviewing speed limits across major roads nationwide in coordination with other government agencies. This ongoing review is also expected to examine the overall fine system for greater clarity and efficiency. Transport Cabinet Secretary Davis Chirchir stated that fines would eventually be divided into two categories: major offences requiring court appearances and minor offences allowing for instant payment. To facilitate this new framework, NTSA is engaging with the Judiciary, Kenya Bureau of Statistics, Kenya National Highways Authority (KeNHA), Kenya Urban Roads Authority (KURA), and the Kenya Law Reform Commission, among other crucial agencies. The instant fines system, originally introduced under the Traffic, Minor Offences, and Rules of 2016, aimed to reduce court congestion, enhance enforcement efficiency, and foster responsible road use.
