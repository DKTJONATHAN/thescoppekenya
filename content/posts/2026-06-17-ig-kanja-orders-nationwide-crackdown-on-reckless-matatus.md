## Cycle of Enforcement Fails to Stem Road Carnage IG Douglas Kanja orders nationwide crackdown on reckless matatus is the central subject of the update.

Inspector General of Police Douglas Kanja has directed a nationwide crackdown on matatu operators, aiming to curb an 11 per cent surge in road fatalities that has intensified public demands for accountability across the Kenyan transport sector.

The directive follows a series of lethal incidents, including the tragic death of student Eugene Mutuku and a separate fatality involving a Detat matatu on Enterprise Road. While these events have sparked immediate public indignation, the police response follows a familiar pattern of reactive enforcement. Official data confirms that road fatalities have climbed by 11 per cent over the last few months, highlighting a widening gap between administrative mandates and the daily reality on major corridors like Kamiti Road.

Kanja, speaking during the commissioning of the Kiamumbi Police Station administration block, emphasized that officers will maintain a zero-tolerance approach toward reckless driving. This push seeks to restore order in the Industrial Area and other high-traffic zones where disregard for traffic laws remains rampant. Despite the heightened police presence, the move arrives as the transport industry continues to operate under systemic pressures that often bypass existing regulations.

### The Policy Void in Driver Oversight

A critical factor undermining these enforcement surges is the stalled implementation of the mandatory driver retesting policy by the National Transport and Safety Authority. This technical disconnect means that police officers are tasked with policing the symptoms of reckless behavior on the ground while the regulatory framework intended to ensure driver competency remains largely unutilized. History suggests that cyclical crackdowns provide only temporary relief, as they fail to address why unqualified drivers remain behind the wheel in the first place.

Transport stakeholders have frequently criticized this quick-fix model, noting that it shifts the burden of systemic failure onto individual officers and bus crews. Without a fully functioning retesting pipeline, the current crackdown functions as a stop-gap measure rather than a sustainable safety solution. This skepticism is shared by families of victims who view the current surge in enforcement as a reactionary performance rather than a commitment to reform.
