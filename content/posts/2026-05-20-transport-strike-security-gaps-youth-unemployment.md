The acrid scent of burning tires hung in the air long after the smoke drifted away from the Thika Superhighway. Watching young men with soot-stained hands pull apart road barriers this morning, it struck me that this scene was not merely about transit costs or government regulations. It felt like a raw, wordless cry from a generation feeling sidelined by an economy that provides few opportunities for belonging.

This transport strike was not just a logistical failure or a breakdown in policing. It signaled a deeper fraying of the social contract. When thousands of young people find themselves in a permanent state of waiting, the logic of civil order begins to unravel. We are observing a volatile mix of stagnant labor policies and a lack of youth agency, where public infrastructure becomes a stage for airing deep-seated grievances.

### The Problem with Public Order

Philosophers have long argued that the state should only exercise power over its citizens to prevent harm. However, our current governance model seems to overlook the other side of that idea. Liberty is not simply the absence of state interference; it is the presence of genuine opportunity.

When the state fails to provide the basic mechanisms for economic participation, it loses its moral standing to regulate public conduct. Yesterday, we saw law enforcement standing by, seemingly overwhelmed by the scale of the protest. The police did not fail because they lacked equipment or manpower. They failed because they were attempting to manage a symptom while the underlying disease of exclusion continued to spread. Without a clear path to dignity through work, many young people no longer view public order as a system that protects them. Instead, they see it as an obstacle to be moved.

### Labor Policy as a Foundation for Stability

When we look at our national systems, we often see a preference for top-down bureaucratic solutions, such as the recent grassroots tax awareness campaigns launched by the Kenya Revenue Authority. While fiscal literacy is important, we often ignore the underlying productivity of our workforce. We treat labor as a spreadsheet item rather than a foundational pillar of national stability.

A job provides more than just a paycheck. It serves as a psychological tether that binds a person to their community and its shared norms. When that tether is severed, the individual becomes detached from the collective. We are witnessing the direct consequences of a labor policy that ignores the potential for youth engagement, preferring instead to rely on reactive policing. If we do not shift our focus toward policies that incentivize real, scalable youth employment, we will continue to see these roadblocks. A protest on the highway is often just an idle hour turned into a weapon of frustration.

### Why This Matters Now

We are reaching a tipping point where economic anxiety is channeling itself into performative destruction. If the state continues to frame these strikes as mere policing issues rather than existential crises of identity, the frequency of such disruptions will inevitably rise. We must stop asking why the police could not clear the road and start asking why so many young people feel they have no other way to be heard.

### What to Watch Next

Pay close attention to the policy rhetoric in the coming weeks. Are we hearing serious discussions about job creation and private sector incentives? Or are we hearing only calls for increased surveillance and harsher sentencing? If the former, there is hope for a recalibration of our social contract. If the latter, we should prepare for a difficult, restless period ahead. The youth are not just protesting transport fares; they are demanding a legitimate stake in our future. Until that core need is addressed, the road will remain a battleground.

The silence that follows the smoke is not peace. It is merely a pause, waiting for the next catalyst to reveal the deep fissures we refuse to mend. We must decide if we will continue to address the symptoms or finally begin the hard work of addressing the cause.
