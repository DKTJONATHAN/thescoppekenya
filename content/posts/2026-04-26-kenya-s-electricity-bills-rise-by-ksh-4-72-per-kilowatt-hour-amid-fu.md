## Kenya's Electricity Bills Surge Amid Fuel and Forex Pressures

Kenya's electricity consumers are now facing substantially higher power bills this April, with adjustments gazetted by the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA) adding KSh 4.72 per kilowatt-hour. This increase, driven by escalating fuel costs and currency depreciation, compounds the cost-of-living burden for households and businesses already grappling with rising pump prices.

### Escalating Fuel Energy Costs

The significant rise in electricity costs stems primarily from the Fuel Energy Cost Charge, which reflects the higher diesel and heavy fuel oil expenses incurred by thermal power stations in March 2026. This KSh 4.72 per kWh increase disproportionately affects consumers based on their usage; a low-consumption household using 50 units will pay an extra KSh 236, while an average household consuming 150 units faces an additional KSh 708. Small businesses utilizing 1,000 units will see their bills climb by approximately KSh 4,720. Remote areas, heavily dependent on diesel for electricity, experience even higher fuel costs, with EPRA data showing prices between KSh 193 and KSh 295 per kilogram in March. Kiunga registered KSh 284.86, and Faza Island KSh 251.58, highlighting the vulnerability of Kenya's energy mix compared to cheaper alternatives like Olkaria geothermal steam at KSh 2.59 per kWh.

### Foreign Exchange Impact on Power Purchase

Further contributing to the increased power tariffs is the Foreign Exchange Fluctuation Adjustment, set at KSh 1.2341 per kWh. This charge accounts for KSh 1.342 billion in currency losses sustained by KenGen, Kenya Power, and independent power producers during March 2026. These losses arose from dollar-denominated power purchase agreements. Spread across 1.302 billion kWh generated and purchased that month, this directly translates into higher April and May bills. Annually, currency losses of this magnitude would exceed KSh 16 billion, directly passed on to consumers.

### Broader Economic Implications

These electricity adjustments follow a period of significant fuel price volatility. Just two weeks prior, EPRA announced a record gross diesel increase of KSh 40.30 and a petrol rise of KSh 28.69. While a subsequent VAT cut from 16 percent to 8 percent partially cushioned these shocks, net increases of KSh 30.09 on diesel and KSh 19.32 on petrol remained. The gazette notices confirming these adjustments were signed by Dr. (Eng.) Joseph Oketch, Acting Director General, amidst ongoing scrutiny within the petroleum supply sector following the arrest of former DG Daniel Kiptoo Bargoria. The cumulative effect of these price hikes places considerable strain on disposable incomes and operational costs for businesses across Kenya.
