## Scotland establishes Charlotte base for 2026 World Cup Scotland sets up camp in US ahead of first World Cup in decades is the central subject of the update.

The Scottish national team has settled into the Atrium Health Performance Park in North Carolina, signaling the end of a 28-year wait to return to the FIFA World Cup. This relocation is a pragmatic necessity for manager Steve Clarke, who must navigate the logistical challenges of a tournament hosted across North America. While the squad adapts to the climate of the Carolinas, the United Kingdom has undergone its own legislative shifts to ensure the population can follow the action despite the significant time zone differences.

### Licensing laws amended for tournament viewing

The UK government has moved to adjust licensing regulations to accommodate the unusual broadcast schedule. Because many matches are slated for the early morning hours in British time, existing restrictions on hospitality venues threatened to stifle public viewing. Ministers have authorized an extension that allows bars and pubs to serve alcohol until 01:00 or 02:00 throughout the group stage. 

This policy change highlights the government's recognition of the tournament as a major national event. While fans in other regions often manage around local infrastructure challenges, such as periodic power grid maintenance, the UK’s hurdle was purely bureaucratic. By granting this reprieve to the hospitality sector, the state has effectively prioritized the consumption of the tournament over strict adherence to standard nightlife statutes. It is a pragmatic move intended to avoid the political friction that would inevitably follow if supporters were barred from public venues during high-profile matches.

### Tactical preparation in a new environment

From a sporting perspective, the choice of Charlotte as a base camp is calculated. The facility provides a controlled, professional environment designed to mitigate the travel fatigue associated with games in cities like Boston and Miami. For the squad, led by captain John McGinn, this isolation offers a reprieve from the intense domestic media scrutiny that currently dominates the conversation back in Scotland.

The team faces a difficult path in Group C, where they are drawn against Brazil, Morocco, and Haiti. Clarke is expected to lean into a strategy of defensive discipline, knowing that his side is viewed as the underdog against established powers like Brazil. While the Scottish media is fixated on the logistics of pub closing times, the coaching staff remains focused on technical preparation. The sterile, high-end environment of the North Carolina facility is intended to keep the players insulated from the noise, allowing them to prepare for the specific physical demands of a tournament that has been two decades in the making.

### The broader impact of the campaign

The government’s decision to relax licensing laws illustrates the sheer scale of the World Cup. Just as major media corporations engage in intense competition for viewer attention, the state has realized that it must facilitate public access to the tournament to maintain social stability during the group stages. The extension of licensing hours serves as a functional subsidy for the hospitality industry, ensuring that businesses can capitalize on the late-night footfall generated by the matches.

Scotland’s participation has become the catalyst for this legislative adjustment. The nation is currently preparing for a schedule that will force viewers to adjust their sleep patterns to accommodate the North American kickoff times. Whether the team performs well or suffers an early exit, the legacy of this tournament in the UK may be defined as much by these policy shifts as by the results on the pitch. As the tournament begins, the focus turns to Clarke and his players. They are tasked with justifying the significant logistical and legislative effort taken to bring this campaign to the fans. The country is ready to stay awake for the outcome, even if the late hours place a strain on the average supporter. The team enters the tournament knowing that the nation will be watching, waiting to see if a quarter-century of anticipation will result in a competitive showing on the global stage.
