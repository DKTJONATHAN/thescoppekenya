## Energy security through a Caspian lens

President Ruto returned from Baku with grand promises of an oil refinery partnership with Azerbaijan, aiming to stabilize the pump price of Ksh242.92. Yet, the communique ignores the local regulatory wall that ensures fuel prices remain pegged to the whims of international markets regardless of supply origins.

### The phantom relief of foreign refineries

The optics in Baku were designed for domestic consumption: handshakes at the Olympic Stadium and the promise of a new refinery. But for the Kenyan taxpayer, the geography of the oil supply chain is secondary to the mathematics of the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA). EPRA currently operates under a mandate that ties the landed cost of fuel to international benchmarks and the strength of the shilling. Even if Azerbaijan provides crude at a discount, the current pricing formula remains dominated by tax levies and the high cost of distribution.

Bringing a new refinery into the Kenyan landscape creates an immediate, silent conflict with existing, multi-billion shilling private storage infrastructure. These facilities are owned by deep-pocketed local interests who have spent years building a monopoly on how fuel moves from the coast to the interior. Any move to re-calibrate the supply chain via Baku will face massive pushback from these players, who have far more influence over current pricing than any foreign energy minister.

The reality remains stark: Azerbaijan operates a state-subsidized model where the government controls the entire energy value chain, from extraction to the pump. Kenya, by contrast, relies on a liberalized, tax-heavy market where the government’s primary role is to extract revenue rather than subsidize consumption. Importing a foreign supply model without systemic legislative reform is like trying to install a luxury engine into a chassis that is missing its axles. The engine might be high-tech, but the vehicle will remain stuck in the same bureaucratic mud that has been trapping [the nation during recent security crises](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-05-01-umulkheir-harun-addresses-the-escalating-border-conflict-an) and fiscal policy shifts.

### The regulatory bottleneck

While the President speaks of 10,000MW targets and refinery dreams, the legislative limitations of EPRA remain the elephant in the room. The regulator is not empowered to force price drops; it merely calculates the math defined by the Finance Act. Unless the government amends the tax structure on fuel, even a free supply of oil from the Caspian Sea would be swallowed by the layers of excise duty, road maintenance levies, and VAT that inflate the price at the pump.

Recent appointments, such as [those of Paul Russo and Rita Kavashe](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-03-president-ruto-appoints-paul-russo-rita-kavashe-and-kevit-desai-as-u), suggest a focus on corporate efficiency, yet the energy sector remains a closed loop of protected interests. A refinery project is a decade-long endeavor, requiring massive capital expenditure that Kenya currently struggles to fund. Attempting to bypass the Middle East supply chain in favor of Azerbaijan does nothing to address the structural reliance on import-based financing that keeps the local economy vulnerable to every fluctuation in the global market.

### Why it matters now
Fuel costs are the single largest driver of inflation in the country, impacting everything from the cost of basic goods to the operational expenses of [the latest technological consumer products](https://zandani.co.ke/article/infinix-smart-20-launched-in-kenya). Without a fundamental change in the local regulatory framework, bilateral energy deals function as political theatre rather than economic relief.

### What to watch next
Observe the upcoming legislative sessions for any proposed amendments to the Petroleum Act. If the government does not move to decentralize the storage and distribution sectors, any "partnership" with Baku will likely end up as another stalled project, leaving citizens waiting for a price drop that cannot occur under the existing, tax-heavy regulatory regime.

If the government genuinely seeks to lower costs, why does the Baku communique remain silent on the specific domestic tax reforms necessary to make any imported oil cheaper at the pump?
