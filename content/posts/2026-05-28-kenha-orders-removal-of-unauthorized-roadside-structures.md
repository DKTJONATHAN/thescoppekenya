## The Perpetual Clearing of the Roadside Commons

The Kenya National Highways Authority has issued a 30-day mandate for the clearing of illegal structures along the Chebarbar-Lessos-Nabkoi, Mayoni-Bungoma, and Chepsonoi-Kapsabet corridors. This notice follows a familiar pattern of administrative assertion against the persistent growth of informal roadside commerce.

### A History of Circular Enforcement

This latest directive from KeNHA relies on the established powers of the Roads Act, 2007, specifically the provisions that empower authorities to clear land within designated road reserves. However, the operational reality of these orders often mimics a recurring cycle rather than a definitive resolution. Historically, efforts to clear corridors like the Thika Superhighway encountered significant friction at segments such as Kihunguro, Allsops, and Delview. In those instances, notice periods were frequently superseded by cycles of litigation and political negotiation, resulting in a fractured implementation where only select structures were removed while others remained undisturbed for years.

The pattern of enforcement typically hinges on the visibility of the infrastructure. Major urban arteries and high-traffic superhighways often see sporadic, high-profile clearance drives meant to signal order. Conversely, rural stretches like those currently targeted in the Western and Rift Valley regions often face a slower, more localized form of encroachment. These areas become hotbeds for informal trade because urban planning has consistently failed to provide designated, formal zones for small-scale merchants. When traders lack access to structured market spaces, the road reserve becomes the only viable location for economic survival, effectively institutionalizing the encroachment long before the bulldozers are ever summoned.

### The Litigious Limbo of Land Use

When the June 26 deadline arrives, the transition from paper notice to physical demolition is rarely seamless. Affected traders frequently utilize the court system to file for injunctions, arguing that the authority failed to follow due process or that the structures were permitted under previous local government arrangements. These legal loopholes serve to pause enforcement indefinitely, creating a state of limbo that satisfies neither the planners looking to secure the reserve for future expansion nor the traders looking to secure their livelihoods.

This bureaucratic friction is not merely a matter of defiance but a symptom of a deeper mismatch between infrastructure policy and economic reality. Like the confusion observed in the [procedure for recovering misdirected teacher salaries](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-25-tsc-outlines-procedure-for-recovering-misdirected-teacher-s), the struggle to clear road reserves is hampered by a lack of coherent, localized institutional tracking. Without a sustainable integration of these traders into formal hubs, the act of clearing a road reserve is often little more than a temporary pause in a permanent settlement process.

### Prospects Beyond the Notice

Looking toward the June deadline, the trajectory of this directive suggests a high probability of institutional fatigue. Without a clear plan to relocate affected businesses, the authority is likely to encounter the same bottlenecks seen in previous years, where the cost of enforcement-both in terms of legal fees and public relations-often exceeds the immediate urgency of the road safety concerns cited in the notice.

The reliance on Clause 49 of the Roads Act represents a strictly legalistic approach to a complex socio-economic issue. As seen in the recent public disputes surrounding [various public figures dealing with their own private, legal, or professional dramas](https://zandani.co.ke/article/videographer-broke-down-emurua-dikirr), the public sphere in Kenya often prioritizes high-visibility drama over the slow, unglamorous work of urban zoning. Until the government pivots from reactive demolition to proactive land-use planning, these periodic notices will remain a standard administrative ritual.

Infrastructure policy that ignores the necessity of local markets is destined to repeat its own history.
