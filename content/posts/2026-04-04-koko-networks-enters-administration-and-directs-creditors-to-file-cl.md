Multinational bioethanol company KOKO Networks has entered administration, prompting creditors to file unpaid claims by Wednesday, April 8, 2026, following its withdrawal from the market. A virtual creditors' meeting is scheduled for Friday, April 10, 2026, to discuss the company's future after financial challenges.

## KOKO Networks Faces Administration Amid Carbon Credit Dispute

### Creditors Called to Submit Claims and Attend Meeting

The notice, dated Thursday, April 4, 2026, and filed at the High Court, confirms KOKO Networks Limited has formally entered administration. Creditors have until Wednesday, April 8, 2026, to report any unpaid claims against the company. A first meeting of creditors has been requested by the Joint Administrators and will convene virtually on Friday, April 10, 2026, at 10 am. Administrators working with PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC) are currently overseeing the insolvency process.

To participate in the vote on proposed next steps, including any restructuring or recovery plans, creditors must submit their claims with appropriate supporting documentation to the Joint Administrators. Attendance confirmation by email is required, after which registered creditors will receive relevant access and login details for the virtual meeting. Creditors unable to attend are directed to communicate their decisions on proposals in writing to ke_knk_administrators@pwc.com.

### Financial Strain Linked to Carbon Credit Approvals

KOKO Networks' decision to cease operations and appoint administrators follows months of significant financial strain. The company, valued at Ksh22 billion, primarily focused on supplying bioethanol fuel and smart cookstoves, aiming to facilitate a transition away from charcoal and kerosene use. This model generated carbon credits intended for sale in international markets, with revenue earmarked to subsidise fuel prices and enhance clean cooking affordability for low-income households globally.

However, the company encountered a substantial setback when President William Ruto's administration allegedly declined to issue a letter of authorisation, a crucial requirement for the international sale of these carbon credits. This alleged denial reportedly blocked the company's access to its primary income stream, rendering it unable to monetise carbon credits. Consequently, despite its presence across multiple African and Asian markets, KOKO Networks could not sustain operations, leading to an abrupt halt of activities and the layoff of hundreds of workers.
