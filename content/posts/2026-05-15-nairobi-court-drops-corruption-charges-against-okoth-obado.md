## The Section 87(a) Debate: Kenyans Question the Price of Legal Settlements in High-Stakes Graft Cases

"If I swipe a chicken, I end up at Kamiti, but if the loot is big enough, we just call it a settlement and walk away?" This biting question from an X user perfectly captures the widespread frustration vibrating through Kenyan social media this Friday. Following the Nairobi court’s decision to drop corruption charges against former Migori Governor Okoth Obado via a Section 87(a) withdrawal, the online atmosphere is far from celebratory. Instead, it is a space defined by a deep, palpable demand for transparency regarding how the law applies to the political elite versus the average person.

The judiciary’s formal endorsement of this settlement has transformed a routine courtroom exit into a lightning rod for national grievances. While the withdrawal may be procedurally airtight under the law, the court of public opinion is rejecting the optics of concluding massive graft allegations with a polite handshake rather than a rigorous trial.

### The Digital Verdict on Legal Loopholes

On platforms like X and Facebook, the conversation has moved rapidly from the specifics of the Obado case to a broader critique of the Section 87(a) route. Many users are arguing that this is not an outlier, but rather a worrying pattern in high-level corruption litigation.

To the public, the distance between a formal conviction and a quiet settlement feels like a canyon where accountability goes to die. One Facebook user from Migori noted that the public deserved to see the evidence tested in a courtroom. Instead, they were left with a closed file and a lingering sense of mystery. The prevailing sentiment is that such outcomes diminish the deterrent power of the law. If the only consequence for being caught is a clean exit strategy, critics argue, corruption will remain an attractive business model rather than a criminal risk.

Others are pointing to a profound sense of fatigue. A well-known political analyst recently commented that the most painful part of these trials is their predictability. After years of watching lawyers shuffle files and navigate procedural delays, the public finds the eventual quiet departure to be a bitter pill. For many Kenyans, the script feels written before the judge even enters the room.

### Why it Matters Now

This wave of frustration surfaces during a period of intense skepticism toward the Kenyan justice system. Because these cases often involve the alleged misuse of significant public funds, the use of Section 87(a) is being interpreted by many as an institutional bypass. Citizens are openly questioning whether these settlements serve the public good or simply function as a soft landing for those in power. 

There is a widening disconnect between the letter of the law and the public’s expectation of moral integrity. Much like the intense scrutiny surrounding celebrity narratives-such as the recent public fascination with Size 8 and her claims regarding witchcraft-Kenyans are increasingly determined to peek behind the curtain of performative events. They are no longer willing to accept legal conclusions that lack a clear, transparent justification. 

### What to Watch Next

The fallout from this decision is likely to trigger a new wave of pressure from civil society and legislative bodies. We should expect renewed demands for a total review of how settlement agreements are applied to corruption-related matters, particularly those involving public assets. Activists are already signaling a push to limit the scope of Section 87(a) when the integrity of public resources is on the line.

Furthermore, political candidates will likely have to address this sentiment in their upcoming campaigns. It is a potent issue that could force a pivot toward stronger advocacy for judicial reform. While national attention may occasionally drift toward lighter fare-such as the chatter surrounding Willy Paul’s recent summit performance snub-the hunger for genuine transparency in the courts is not going away. The conversation has evolved beyond the guilt or innocence of a single individual. It has become a critique of a structural framework that many Kenyans believe is failing to keep pace with their daily reality. 

As the weekend approaches, the legal system remains in the spotlight, facing questions that a simple court withdrawal cannot resolve. The quiet closure of a file is doing very little to silence a public that is tired of the status quo.
