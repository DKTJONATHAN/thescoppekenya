As you walk through the streets of Nairobi this Thursday, March 19, 2026, you might be carrying a heavier weight than your laptop bag. You’re about to become a first-time father, and your entire world is currently shifting beneath your feet.

<h2>The Highs You Didn't See Coming</h2>
<p>There is a specific kind of pride that hits a man when he first sees that crib he spent three hours assembling standing in the corner of the room. I have spent over a decade covering the glitz and glamour of the Kenyan entertainment scene, but no red carpet moment compares to the quiet realization that you are about to be someone’s entire world. This is the "good" that everyone talks about, but few truly describe. It is the moment you realize your role has shifted from being just a guy who shows up at the base to being a provider and a protector.</p>

<p>The joy isn't just in the birth itself; it’s in the small, quiet moments that happen before the chaos peaks. Bonding starts early. I tell young men all the time that you don't have to wait until the child can kick a ball to be a dad. You can start by talking to the bump, singing those songs you usually only belt out in the shower, and being the one who fields the endless phone calls and messages from relatives while your partner catches twenty minutes of sleep. That connection is the anchor that will keep you steady when the "ugly" parts of parenting start to surface.</p>

<h2>The Brutal Reality of the No-Sleep Club</h2>
<p>Let’s talk about the "bad," and I’m going to be very direct here: your sleep schedule is about to be murdered. We often hear that newborns sleep for 12 to 16 hours a day, which sounds like a dream. The reality is that these hours are served in tiny, agonizing portions. Sometimes a baby will sleep for only twenty minutes before waking up to demand a feed or a change. They don't care that you have a 9:00 AM meeting in Upper Hill or that you’re exhausted from a long day in the office.</p>

<p>This sleep deprivation is the biggest challenge you will face in the first three months. It turns minor disagreements with your partner into major blowouts because neither of you is functioning on a full brain. You will find yourself arguing over who didn’t buy the right brand of nappies or why the dishes haven't been done. My advice is to approach this as a tag team. If she’s breastfeeding, you’re the one doing the diaper changes and the burping. If she’s sleeping, you’re the gatekeeper. This isn't just about being "helpful"—it’s about survival.</p>

<h2>The Ugly Truth About Men’s Mental Health</h2>
<p>Then there is the "ugly" side that we rarely discuss at the local pub over a cold drink. Many men believe that because they aren't the ones physically giving birth, they are immune to the emotional crash that follows. That is a lie. Men can and do get a version of the "baby blues." It’s a mix of the overwhelming pressure to provide, the sudden loss of your old identity, and the feeling that you are now second place in your own home.</p>

<p>You might find yourself feeling constantly irritable, losing interest in the things you used to love, or feeling a strange sense of detachment from the baby. These are symptoms of perinatal anxiety and depression. It’s not a sign of weakness; it’s a physiological response to a massive life shift and extreme exhaustion. If you find yourself feeling like you’re drowning, you have to speak up. Whether it’s talking to a trusted friend who has been through it or seeing a doctor, don't let the "strong man" persona keep you from being the father your child needs.</p>

<h2>Redefining Your Relationship</h2>
<p>Your relationship with your partner is going to change, and for a while, it might feel like you’re just roommates running a very loud, very messy small business. The intimacy you’re used to will take a backseat. The focus will be entirely on the new human in the room. This is where many first-time dads feel left out. You might feel like your partner has a deeper bond with the baby because of the physical connection of pregnancy and nursing.</p>

<p>The fix is simple but hard: get your hands dirty. Be the one who handles the bath time. Be the one who does the skin-to-skin contact. The more involved you are in the "ugly" tasks—the blow-out diapers and the 3:00 AM rocking sessions—the more you will feel like a core part of the family unit rather than an observer. Take care of yourself too. Go for a walk, keep your gym routine if you can, and don't let your own health slide. You can’t pour from an empty cup.</p>

<p>Parenting doesn't come with a manual, and honestly, even if it did, every child is different. Some will learn the difference between day and night by three months, and others will keep you guessing for a year. The goal isn't to be a perfect father; it’s to be an engaged one. It gets easier as the routines set in and the baby starts to recognize your voice. Until then, take it one nap at a time and remember that this version of you—the dad version—is the most important role you’ll ever play.</p>
