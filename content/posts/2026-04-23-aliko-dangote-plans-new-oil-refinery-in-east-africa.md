Nigerian billionaire Aliko Dangote committed to building an oil refinery in East Africa on April 23, 2026, during the Africa We Build Summit in Nairobi. The proposed facility, a replica of his Lagos plant, aims to provide the region with reliable fuel, contingent on serious presidential support from Uganda's President Yoweri Museveni and President William Ruto.

## Regional Fuel Security Initiative

Dangote's pledge to construct an identical refinery to his Nigerian facility comes with a direct condition: robust support from the respective heads of state. This initiative is designed to alleviate East Africa's reliance on costly fuel imports, which currently impact household finances and business operational costs. If built, the refinery would locally produce diesel, petrol, and jet fuel.

### Replication of Lagos Success

The proposed East African facility would mirror the Dangote Oil Refinery located in Nigeria's Ibeju-Lekki Free Zone. This $20 billion plant, commissioned in May 2023 and fully operational by February 2026, processes over 650,000 barrels of crude oil daily.

### Expanding Global Footprint

The Dangote Group has outlined a USD40 billion investment plan, approximately Ksh5.17 trillion, across various sectors including petrochemicals, fertilizers, and industrial raw materials, slated for completion by 2030. Dangote also stated intentions to expand his Nigerian refinery to 1.4 million barrels per day, which would establish it as the world's largest refinery. This expansion aims to achieve 10 percent of the United States of America's refining capacity.

### Championing African Self-Sufficiency

During the summit, Dangote criticized Africa's economic model, which he argued prioritizes exporting raw materials over finished goods. He highlighted this pattern as a contributor to brain drain. In a broader strategic move to counter this issue, the East Africa facility intends to contribute to full African fertilizer self-sufficiency by 2028. This plan includes 20 blending plants, 12 million tonnes of urea, and the sourcing of potash and phosphate from Congo and Brazil.

### Parallel Regional Energy Investments

This development coincides with the Kenyan government's decision to invest in Uganda's oil refinery industry. President Ruto announced this move as part of a strategic regional energy play following the successful privatization and partial regional uptake of the Kenya Pipeline Company (KPC). The Ugandan refinery project, to be based in Hoima City, is estimated to cost about USD4 billion, roughly Ksh500 billion, marking it as a significant industrial energy investment in East Africa.
