## KNEC Launches TrueCert System: The Price of Proving Your Past

The Kenya National Examinations Council has unveiled TrueCert, a digital platform designed to verify KCSE certificates instantly. While officials hail it as a solution to widespread forgery, the system effectively mandates a financial barrier for every job seeker in Kenya.

### The Monetization of Academic Integrity

The Kenya National Examinations Council, under the leadership of David Njengere, has finally unveiled TrueCert. The platform claims to solve the systemic rot of academic fraud that has plagued public service hiring for decades. By embedding student data into encrypted QR codes, KNEC promises that document forgery will become a relic of the past. Yet, the official narrative conveniently sidesteps the most glaring detail: the shift from a passive public service to a pay-to-play gatekeeping mechanism.

In the past, verifying a certificate was a procedural expectation handled by employers or institutions. Today, the process is integrated directly into the eCitizen platform. This is not merely a technical upgrade. It is a calculated move to extract revenue from the very demographic most desperate for employment. Whether the fee is offloaded onto the applicant or forced upon the employer, the reality remains that an academic credential, which has already been paid for through years of school fees and examination levies, now requires a recurring tax just to exist in a professional context.

The state argues that the cost is a necessary expense to uphold the sanctity of our education system. But one must ask why the burden of proof rests entirely on the shoulders of the citizen. The youth, already struggling to find entry-level roles while grappling with the harsh realities of our roads where lives are lost to negligence, now face another hurdle. They are expected to navigate the digital bureaucracy of eCitizen and part with their hard-earned money to prove they are who they say they are. If the government truly believes that academic forgery is a threat to the nation, why is the cost of validation not borne by the state, rather than the job seeker?

### Shifting the Burden to the Jobless

The implementation of TrueCert creates a permanent revenue stream for the state under the guise of security. By standardizing the verification process through eCitizen, the council has ensured that every single job application becomes a taxable event. The logic of accountability is being used to mask a predatory fiscal policy. When a graduate applies for a role, they are no longer just competing with thousands of others; they are now footing the bill for the internal failure of the state to secure its own records for years.

This digital tax is particularly insidious because it ignores the existing socio-economic climate. As public trust in government institutions continues to erode-mirrored by the hollow political posturing surrounding the former president or the empty promises of zero tolerance toward political violence-this new system adds a layer of cynicism to the job market. It treats the certificate holder as a suspect who must pay for their own clearance.

### Why It Matters Now

The launch of TrueCert arrives at a time when the youth unemployment rate remains a ticking time bomb. By turning verification into a paid digital service, the state has institutionalized the idea that even basic citizenship and the right to present one's academic history comes with a price tag. It is a tax on the desperate, masquerading as a fix for the corrupt.

### What to Watch Next

Monitor the uptake by private sector employers. If corporate Kenya decides to push the TrueCert verification cost onto applicants as a standard pre-interview requirement, the barrier to entry for the average Kenyan will rise significantly. Watch for how many certificates are flagged as fraudulent in the first ninety days; if the number is low, the system will serve as little more than a cash cow for the state, extracting fees while providing a solution to a problem that may have been overstated to justify the digital rollout.

If the government possesses the technological capacity to secure our academic records, why is the citizen expected to pay a fee to access what is rightfully theirs?
