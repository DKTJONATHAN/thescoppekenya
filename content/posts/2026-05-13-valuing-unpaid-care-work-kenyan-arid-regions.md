## Integrating Unpaid Care into the Frontier’s Fiscal Future

As the Frontier Counties Development Council (FCDC) formalizes its 2025-2029 development agenda, a critical economic driver remains absent from the conversation. The immense volume of unpaid care work, largely shouldered by women and girls, functions as an essential, yet invisible, pillar of our regional economy. Treating this labor as a foundational infrastructure investment is no longer merely a social goal; it is a prerequisite for the sustained economic liberation of our pastoralist communities.

To the Governors of the Frontier Counties Development Council,

Your recent discussions at the 2024 Pastoralist Leadership Summit rightly prioritized the expansion of livestock value chains and the strengthening of cross-border security. These pillars are vital for the growth of Garissa, Isiolo, Lamu, and Mandera. However, these sectors rely on a massive labor subsidy that is currently missing from every official balance sheet. The domestic labor performed by women and girls is not a private family matter. It is, in fact, the primary economic infrastructure of the north. It is time to treat it as such.

### Acknowledging the Hidden Economic Subsidy

The Aga Khan University’s Care for All Project has begun to illuminate the reality long ignored in our arid and semi-arid lands. Activities like gathering fuel, fetching water, and providing multi-generational care are not lifestyle choices. They are full-time, demanding occupations that keep the broader workforce functional. When a mother in Mandera spends her day ensuring family health and nutrition, she provides a service that would otherwise require significant state funding.

International efforts, such as the trade finance partnerships involving UBA UK and British International Investment, are working to inject capital into our markets. Yet, at the same time, we are draining the local capital of time. This represents a significant fiscal oversight. Your upcoming budgets are currently built upon a foundation of uncompensated work. Should this labor cease for even a single day, the formal pastoralist economy would immediately grind to a halt.

### Converting Time Poverty into Policy Action

The defining obstacle for our region is time poverty. In the frontier counties, the absence of basic services functions as a regressive tax on women, paid not in currency, but in hours of their lives. When a county government fails to prioritize water reticulation or community-based childcare, it essentially mandates that half of the population remain excluded from the formal market. A thriving livestock trade cannot exist if a vast portion of the workforce is trapped in a cycle of survival-based labor.

We must approach this with the same rigor applied to other sectors. Similar to how independent voices like Teslah Kenya are pushing for structural changes to rectify inequality in the arts, our counties must implement systemic changes to grant women true economic power. This requires decentralized early childhood development (ECD) centers and localized water solutions that return precious hours of productivity to the household.

### Investing in the Infrastructure of Care

The FCDC ECD Strategic Plan for 2025-2029 provides a necessary framework, but it demands more than political rhetoric. It requires the creation of multi-sectoral ECD Technical Working Groups with the mandate to influence treasury allocations. The Care for All Project has already demonstrated in Isiolo and Lamu that care is a public good, not a private social program. 

By strategically locating childcare facilities near markets and grazing corridors, you enable women to pivot from unpaid labor to income-generating activities. This is how you expand your tax base and transition from a subsistence-based model to a resilient, modern pastoralist state. I urge you to integrate the findings of the Care for All Project into your 2025 county integrated development plans. By treating the care economy as a formal budget line item, you will grant it the same urgency as road construction or veterinary services.

### The Path Forward

The FCDC is currently finalizing its 2025-2029 strategic cycle. With empirical data now available, governors possess the evidence needed to elevate care from a domestic burden to a public policy priority. Failing to act now will ensure another decade of uneven growth. As we approach the county budget readings in June 2026, observers will be watching for specific fiscal commitments toward the ECD Technical Working Groups in Mandera and Garissa. The efficacy of these groups will ultimately determine if the FCDC Strategic Plan becomes a transformative reality or remains a collection of good intentions.

For a more productive and equitable frontier, the time to invest in our caregivers is now.
