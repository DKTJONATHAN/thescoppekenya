## The Biology Trap: Why Kiganjo’s 'Medical' Exit Strategy Targets Female Careers

The National Police Service claims its training protocols are non-negotiable, yet the dismissal of 18 pregnant recruits from Kiganjo suggests a rigid adherence to archaic doctrine. This policy shift prioritizes institutional inflexibility over constitutional equity, effectively punishing female biology under the guise of physical readiness.

The numbers are tidy on a spreadsheet. Of the 54 recruits axed from the National Police College in Kiganjo, 18 were categorized as medical exits due to pregnancy. The remaining 36 were weeded out for forged academic credentials, undisclosed criminal convictions, or terminal health conditions. To NPS spokesperson Muchiri Nyaga, the logic is binary: police training is physically grueling, and any deviation from Za Ndani curriculum constitutes a failure to meet the requirements of the service. 

But when the state treats reproductive health as a disqualifying medical condition rather than a temporary state, it reveals a structural refusal to modernize. While the service focuses on the intensity of the program, it ignores the broader implications of a recruitment pipeline that effectively bans women of reproductive age from pursuing a career in law enforcement. 

### Constitutional Friction and the Policy Gap

The Constitution of Kenya is explicit regarding non-discrimination. Article 27 guarantees that every person is equal before the law and protected from discrimination on the basis of sex, pregnancy, or marital status. Yet, the NPS maintains an administrative wall that turns a constitutional right into a career-ending obstacle.

Legislators Millie Odhiambo and Gathoni Wamuchomba have led the charge against this practice, arguing that modern security training should accommodate the realities of the workforce rather than enforcing a standard built for a different century. If a recruit meets the intellectual and physical requirements of the service, the emergence of pregnancy during a training cycle should trigger a deferment, not an immediate termination of a hard-won professional opportunity. Instead, the current protocol functions as an automatic filter that disproportionately targets women, regardless of their capability or commitment to the uniform.

The state’s insistence on a one-size-fits-all training intensity ignores the fact that modern policing requires a diverse range of cognitive and soft skills that are not negated by the early stages of gestation. By forcing a choice between the career and the child, the institution reinforces a patriarchal barrier that keeps women out of the security sector, ensuring that those who make it to the field are those who can hide their reproductive lives until the doors are locked behind them.

### The Myth of Administrative Neutrality

Muchiri Nyaga defends these dismissals as a matter of rigorous institutional standards, yet this rigidity stands in stark contrast to the state's supposed commitment to gender mainstreaming. While the NPS celebrates inclusive recruitment figures in public, the internal reality at Kiganjo remains hostile to the female life cycle.

This institutional posture is particularly telling when contrasted with other sectors where maternal support is codified. The National Police Service operates as a state within a state, where internal regulations effectively supersede the spirit of constitutional protections. When the government simultaneously promotes gender equality in public offices and fires recruits for being pregnant, it creates a toxic dissonance that discourages talent from joining the service in the first place. Much like the current friction regarding [Energy Cabinet Secretary Opiyo Wandayi's fuel policy decisions](https://zandani.co.ke/article/energy-cabinet-secretary-opiyo-wandayi-rules-out-resignation-as-fuel) or the ongoing struggles with [Finance Bill tax measures criticized by Faith Odhiambo](https://zandani.co.ke/article/faith-odhiambo-critiques-treasury-finance-bill-proposals), the common thread is a government that favors administrative convenience over the long-term well-being of the citizens it serves.

### Why it matters now
The Kiganjo dismissals highlight a widening gap between constitutional human rights and state practice. If the National Police Service cannot adapt its training cycles to accommodate the basic biological realities of its workforce, it will continue to face legal challenges that undermine its authority and alienate a significant portion of potential recruits.

### What to watch next
Expect this debate to migrate from the halls of the police college to the floor of Parliament. With MPs like Odhiambo and Wamuchomba demanding policy modernization, the pressure will mount for a judicial review of whether the NPS training bylaws are legally compliant with current constitutional mandates. 

If the state refuses to pivot, how can the National Police Service claim to protect the rights of all Kenyans when it systematically discards those who do not fit its narrow definition of a recruit?
