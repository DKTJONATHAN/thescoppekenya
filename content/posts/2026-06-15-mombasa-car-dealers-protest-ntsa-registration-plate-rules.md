## Administrative Gridlock at the Coast

The NTSA promises seamless digital integration, yet the reality on the ground in Mombasa is a grinding halt of automotive logistics. While the regulator champions modernization, the latest enforcement of registration protocols is transforming the Port of Mombasa into a bottleneck for the entire national vehicle supply chain.

### The Procedural Clash

Today, June 15, the streets of Mombasa echoed with the frustrations of car dealers who find themselves caught in a regulatory vice. At the heart of the standoff is the rigid application of Section 24 of the Traffic Act. The National Transport and Safety Authority is currently demanding full adherence to new registration plate requirements for all vehicles in transit. For the dealers, this is not merely a bureaucratic checkbox. It is an operational wall.

The disconnect lies in the speed of the port versus the pace of the NTSA portal. Vehicle throughput requires constant, fluid movement from the ship to the showroom. By mandating a digital registration verification that often lags or experiences systematic downtime, the regulator has effectively created a non-tariff barrier to trade. Just as the [cancellation of major tenders](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-28-what-1-tender-cancellation-means-for-kenya-railways) previously paralyzed logistics, this current administrative friction is bleeding resources from the automotive sector. Dealers are now forced to sit on idle inventory, unable to clear vehicles because of an digital interface that fails to account for the physical velocity of port operations.

### The Hidden Tax of Compliance

Mathew Katili, representing the Independent Car Dealers Association, points to a reality that the state refuses to quantify. Every hour a vehicle sits in a holding yard because of plate-related clearance delays, the cost of doing business inflates. These are not absorbed costs. They are passed directly to the Kenyan consumer, who remains the ultimate payer for the government's inefficiency.

This is the hidden tax of modernization. When enforcement precedes the infrastructure required to handle the volume, the result is predictable chaos. The current standoff demonstrates that the regulator is prioritizing procedural uniformity over the economic health of the sector. For a country attempting to manage an automotive supply chain tied to a national budget that expects massive fiscal output, such friction is expensive. It mirrors the struggle seen in other sectors, such as the [ongoing management of bad loans](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-06-faulu-microfinance-bank-narrows-annual-loss-as-bad-loans-approach-30) across the financial industry, where rigid mandates often fail to align with the precarious nature of market operations.

The NTSA views this as a matter of simple legal compliance. The dealers view it as the strangulation of their livelihood. If the government truly intends to foster a trade environment conducive to growth, it cannot simultaneously implement rules that ignore the physical realities of the port. A digital system is only as effective as the throughput it enables. When that system becomes a barrier to the movement of goods, it ceases to be a tool for regulation and becomes a mechanism for economic decay.

As the government prepares its fiscal roadmap for the 2026/2027 period, the silent costs of these administrative bottlenecks will surely surface in the final retail prices of every car sold in the country. Is the rigid adherence to a specific section of the Traffic Act worth the systematic destabilization of a key national economic hub, or is this merely a failure of the regulator to recognize that a policy is only as good as its implementation in the real world?
