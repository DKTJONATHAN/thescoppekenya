The International Monetary Fund has lowered its 2026 growth forecast for Sub-Saharan Africa, including Kenya, following recent fuel price increases that underscore escalating economic pressures. Regional growth, projected at 4.3 per cent for 2026, reflects a slowdown directly linked to the intensifying conflict in the Middle East.

## IMF Forecasts Economic Headwinds

The IMF's Regional Economic Outlook for Sub-Saharan Africa indicates a deceleration from an estimated 4.5 per cent regional growth in 2025, which marked the fastest pace in a decade. The 2026 projection of 4.3 per cent is 0.3 percentage points below pre-war forecasts, with significant variations observed across individual countries.

### Deteriorating Trade Balances and Higher Living Costs

As an oil-importing nation, Kenya is expected to experience a substantial burden from these global shifts. The IMF warns that oil-importing, non-resource-rich countries will face a deteriorating trade balance and an elevated cost of living through the remainder of 2026. Inflation, which had decreased to 3.4 per cent by the close of 2025, is now anticipated to rebound to 5.0 per cent by December 2026, further straining household budgets still recovering from the 2020 pandemic's impact on incomes and savings.

### Middle East Conflict Disrupts Key Sectors

The conflict in the Middle East continues to destabilize global markets, leading to sharp surges in oil, gas, and fertilizer prices. Shipping costs have increased, and established trade routes with Gulf partners, vital for East Africa, are disrupted, impacting supply chains. This disruption extends to tourism, a crucial foreign exchange earner for the country, with the IMF noting a decline in tourist arrivals due to the conflict. Remittances, a lifeline for millions of families, are also at risk, as money sent from Gulf-based workers is likely to diminish amid a deteriorating regional economic environment and tightening job markets. These developments, including the pathways some individuals take to join foreign militaries, highlight the broad impact of regional instability.

### Addressing Food Insecurity and Fiscal Pressures

The IMF estimates that a 20 per cent rise in international food prices could push over 20 million people in Sub-Saharan Africa into moderate or severe food insecurity. In a downside scenario, regional output could contract by 0.6 per cent, with inflation increasing by an additional 2.4 percentage points above pre-war projections. The global lender urges governments to implement swift, targeted support to shield vulnerable households from rising living costs and advises oil-importing nations to protect social and development spending, avoiding cuts to essential services under fiscal pressure.
