Scientists have identified a new bat-borne coronavirus in East Africa, including Kenya, with the ability to infect human cells, raising alarms about a potential resurgence of dangerous strains. The research, a collaborative effort between UK scientists and regional researchers, highlights public health concerns stemming from close human-wildlife interactions in the region.

## New Virus Identification and Infection Potential

The newly identified virus, designated CcCoV-KY43, was detected in heart-nosed bats across Kenya, northern Tanzania, and parts of eastern Sudan. This discovery was part of a broader study focusing on coronaviruses circulating in wildlife populations throughout East Africa. Dr. James Nyagwange of the Kenya Medical Research Institute (KeMRI) was among the researchers involved in the study, which was published in the science journal Nature.

### Laboratory Findings on Human Cell Infection

Laboratory tests confirmed that CcCoV-KY43 can bind to receptors found in human lung cells, a critical step that enables coronaviruses to infect people if a spillover event occurs. Researchers meticulously analyzed spike proteins from numerous alphacoronaviruses, which function as molecular "keys" allowing viruses to enter cells. These proteins were tested against a variety of human receptors in controlled experiments to assess their infectivity.

### Expert Insights on Viral Mechanisms

Dr. Dalan Bailey, a molecular biology expert at The Pirbright Institute, indicated that previous assumptions about these viruses were too limited. He noted, "Before our study, it was assumed most alphacoronaviruses used just one or two possible receptors. We now know they might use a whole variety of additional receptors to get into cells." These findings suggest that bat coronaviruses may possess a greater capacity to interact with human biology than previously understood. Professor Stephen Graham of the University of Cambridge emphasized the ongoing challenge, stating, "Viral spike proteins are keys that fit into locks. The challenge now is to find the others. Now that we're aware there is a potential risk, a risk that has always existed, we can start to prepare for it."

### Current Status and Research Safety

Importantly, researchers have confirmed there is no current evidence of CcCoV-KY43 having infected humans. Testing conducted in Kenya and the broader East African region indicates that no spillover has yet occurred. Dr. Giulia Gallo of the Pirbright Institute clarified that the research was conducted safely by utilizing only viral spike proteins instead of the full virus, thereby minimizing laboratory risks while still allowing for detailed analysis of the virus's infection potential.
