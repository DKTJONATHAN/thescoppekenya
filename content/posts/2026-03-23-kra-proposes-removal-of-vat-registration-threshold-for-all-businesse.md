The Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA) is proposing to remove the Value-Added Tax (VAT) registration threshold for all businesses. This amendment to Section 34 (1a) of the VAT Act would require every enterprise, regardless of turnover, to register for VAT and remit the 16 percent tax.

Broadening the Tax Base

Direct Impact on Traders and Services The proposed changes by the KRA are set to redefine tax obligations for numerous small businesses that have historically operated outside the VAT framework due to their lower annual sales. Under the contemplated amendments, micro-enterprises will be obligated to charge 16 percent VAT on taxable goods and services, remitting these collections to the KRA monthly. This shift will directly impact traders of everyday commodities such as mobile phones, soft drinks, bottled water, cosmetics, snacks, cooking gas, and petroleum products. Price increases are anticipated as traders are likely to pass the additional tax burden onto consumers. Service providers, including consultants, will also need to incorporate the 16 percent VAT charge into their fees. While KRA aims to widen the tax base, there are concerns that the added compliance requirements and higher taxation could stifle business growth and potentially force some enterprises out of operation, compounding existing operational challenges for many businesses, including those in commercial transport, who are already navigating complex issues like ensuring adequate visibility through crucial rear mirrors [cite: https://zandani.co.ke/article/rear-mirrors-crucial-driving-commercial-vans-kenya-visibility-crisis].

KRA's Revenue Goals and Policy Discrepancies KRA argues that eliminating the VAT threshold will address an estimated 38 percent VAT collection gap, projecting a boost in collections from Ksh 653 billion to over Ksh1 trillion. This move, however, contrasts sharply with previous legislative discussions. The Finance Bill 2024 and subsequent debates in 2025/2026 had proposed increasing the VAT registration threshold to Ksh 8 million, aiming to ease the compliance burden on smaller firms. The KRA's current stance to scrap the threshold entirely represents a significant departure from this medium-term revenue strategy.
