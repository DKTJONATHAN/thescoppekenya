## Wilson Airport Emergency Protocols Challenged After Aircraft Crash

A Dornier 228-202 aircraft crash-landed at Wilson Airport on Friday morning, forcing a total suspension of operations and exposing critical gaps in emergency evacuation protocols amid rising runway congestion.

The incident involving the 5Y-CES registered aircraft occurred at approximately 10:00 AM, paralyzing the hub. The Dornier 228, a workhorse for UNHAS operations, veered during landing and came to a rest in a position that effectively blocked the primary runway. This immobilization prevented secondary traffic from landing or taking off, highlighting the vulnerabilities inherent in the airport's single-runway design.

Wilson Airport serves as the busiest regional aviation hub in East Africa, handling an increasing volume of UNHAS and commercial shuttle traffic. The operational strain on the facility has reached a tipping point, where a single disabled aircraft like the 5Y-CES can halt the movement of dozens of other regional flights. The current infrastructure lacks the necessary bypass taxiways to mitigate total gridlock during such events.

Kenya Civil Aviation Authority (KCAA) protocols dictate strict closure timelines when a runway is obstructed. These procedures mandate that all incoming aircraft be diverted to Jomo Kenyatta International Airport or held in a holding pattern until the debris is cleared. However, the proximity of the airport to residential neighborhoods like Langata complicates the implementation of these emergency directives when ground congestion is already at its daily peak.

### Infrastructure Limitations and Response Times

Analysis of current airport emergency vehicle access reveals a significant disparity between regulatory expectations and actual performance during periods of heavy air traffic. While emergency teams are stationed on-site, the physical layout of the tarmac-often cluttered with parked aircraft awaiting maintenance or short-haul turnarounds-forces ground units to navigate restricted corridors. This bottleneck increases response times, potentially jeopardizing the safety of passengers and crew during a crash.

The KCAA currently maintains rigorous safety standards, yet the policy regarding the density of light aircraft traffic at Wilson remains under intense scrutiny. Stakeholders argue that the current volume of flights exceeds the facility's capacity for rapid emergency intervention. If an emergency vehicle cannot reach the mid-point of the runway within the mandated three-minute window due to parked aircraft blocking access routes, the entire emergency response framework effectively fails.
