## Miwani Sugar Land Dispute Signals Governance Challenges A parliamentary inquiry into the Miwani Sugar land auction has uncovered deep-seated issues of alleged forgery, untraceable payments, and contradictory positions within government structures, raising significant concerns for corporate governance and investor confidence in Kenya's land sector. Lawmakers suggest this could be one of Kenya's most troubling land cases.

Allegations of Forgery and Irregular Auction The core of the dispute revolves around a 1993 case initiated by Nagendra Saxena, who sought to recover a KSh 114 million debt from the sugar mill. Evidence presented to Parliament indicates that the court order used to legitimize the land auction may have been forged, and the original case file is reportedly missing from court records. These revelations led to criminal charges in 2010 against both court officials and private individuals for alleged conspiracy to defraud and illegal acquisition of public property. The investigation highlights potential vulnerabilities within the judicial system regarding land transactions, where such irregularities can remain unresolved for extended periods.

Contradictory Judicial Outcomes The ownership of the Miwani Sugar land remains ambiguous due to conflicting judicial rulings. The Court of Appeal of Kenya, in 2011, nullified the auction, affirming that the land rightfully belongs to Miwani Sugar Company. However, an earlier High Court ruling had favored Crossley Holdings, contributing to the prolonged ambiguity. This judicial discord creates an environment of uncertainty, which can deter investment and undermine the predictability essential for market operations.

Fragmented Government Stance The inconsistency observed in judicial decisions is mirrored by a fragmented government response. The Ministry of Agriculture, acting on advice from the Office of the Attorney General, at one point moved to facilitate the land's transfer to Crossley. Yet, in recent testimony before Members of Parliament, the Agriculture Principal Secretary reversed this stance, asserting that the land belongs to the Government of Kenya. This shifting position underscores a lack of unified strategy among state agencies in defending public assets. Lawmakers, including Nyamoko, emphasized the expectation of a coherent government approach, especially concerning strategic national assets. The ongoing probe, initiated by a petition from Suba South MP Caroli Omondi, seeks clarity on Crossley Holdings' identity and accountability from public officials, a critical step towards resolving the long-standing dispute and potentially influencing future measures to prevent similar issues, which could involve scrutiny akin to how the [United States Government Adds 24 Kenyans to Priority Deportation List](https://zandani.co.ke/article/united-states-government-adds-24-kenyans-to-priority-deportation-lis) for individuals implicated in various legal issues.
