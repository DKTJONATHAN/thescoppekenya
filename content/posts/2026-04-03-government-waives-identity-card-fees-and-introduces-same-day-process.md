By Celestine Nzioka, Za Ndani The government has abolished all fees for national identity card applications and replacements, concurrently launching same-day processing for applicants in Nairobi. This significant overhaul, announced by Immigration Principal Secretary Belio Kipsang on Friday, April 3, 2026, aims to enhance service delivery and eliminate delays nationwide.

Streamlined ID Processing and Fee Waivers

Enhanced Efficiency Through Technology Immigration Principal Secretary Belio Kipsang confirmed that substantial investment in modern technology has streamlined the national identity card application and production process across the country. As a direct result of these advancements, applicants within Nairobi can now receive their identification documents on the same day they apply. For those residing outside the capital, the processing timeline has been considerably reduced, with documents expected within three to seven days. "Our cards are now delivered between three and seven days, and if you are in Nairobi, you can even get them the same day. The level of technology we have invested in has made the process very fast and efficient," Kipsang stated on Friday, April 3, 2026.

Abolished Fees and Presidential Directive Further enhancing accessibility, the government has scrapped all fees associated with national identity card applications and replacements. This move follows a presidential directive to waive the previous charges of Ksh300 for new applications and Ksh1,000 for replacements.

Dispelling Misinformation on Foreigner IDs Kipsang also addressed and dismissed claims that the government is issuing national IDs to foreigners to influence the 2027 General Election, terming the allegations unfounded. He clarified that foreigners, including refugees and asylum seekers residing in the country, are issued alien cards through a distinct legal process that does not grant them voting rights. "For foreigners, there is a clear process they must follow. Refugees are issued with alien cards, not national IDs, and without a Kenyan ID, one cannot vote," he explained. The Electoral Commission recently opened temporary positions for a voter registration exercise, reinforcing the distinct legal frameworks surrounding citizenship and electoral participation. [cite: internal link] This clarification comes amid ongoing political debate regarding the removal of vetting requirements for new ID applicants, with the government maintaining that the previous system fostered discrimination and unnecessary delays for many individuals.

Unclaimed Documents and Service Delivery Concurrently, Huduma Kenya has urged individuals to collect their unclaimed identification documents, including national IDs, smart driving licences, and birth certificates, from various service centres nationwide. On Wednesday, April 1, 2026, Huduma Kenya revealed that thousands of critical personal documents remain uncollected. According to the agency, at least 152,398 national IDs, 85,550 smart driving licences, and 28,550 birth certificates are currently awaiting collection in government offices across the country.
