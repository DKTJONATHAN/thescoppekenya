## The Silence of State Security: Why High-Level Orders Fail to Stop the Cycle of Political Arson

Interior Cabinet Secretary Kipchumba Murkomen and Inspector General Douglas Kanja have demanded a high-priority investigation into the horrific assault on gospel singer Rachel Wandeto. Yet, the public remains trapped in a familiar cycle of reactive directives that arrive only after the damage is done.

The arson attack against Rachel Wandeto in Mwiki is not merely a crime scene; it is a declaration of impunity. When unidentified individuals douse a citizen in petrol and set them ablaze, they do so with the chilling confidence that the state is either unable or unwilling to intervene before the match is struck. Following the news that Wandeto is fighting for her life at Kenyatta National Hospital, the response from the executive has been predictably swift in tone but historically hollow in substance. Cabinet Secretary Kipchumba Murkomen and Inspector General Douglas Kanja have issued urgent directives for the Directorate of Criminal Investigations to close in on the perpetrators. It is a script we have read before, following the same tired rhythm of outrage followed by bureaucratic silence.

### The Illusion of Urgent Orders

Government directives carry the weight of authority, but they often lack the teeth of enforcement. If the state is serious about ending political violence, why do conviction rates for such assaults remain at an abysmal low? The directive to investigate an attack in a high-density area like Mwiki, which is theoretically monitored and densely populated, reveals a systemic gap rather than a logistical challenge. When the DCI pleads for public assistance to identify attackers in a neighborhood brimming with eyes and ears, it is an admission of institutional collapse. The request for help is a polite way of saying the state has no eyes on the ground until it is far too late.

There is a blatant disparity in how the Kenyan state values security. While high-ranking officials and elite political figures are protected by layers of tactical police and armored details, grassroots mobilizers and public figures who lean into the political fray are left exposed to the elements. This is not a matter of budget or capacity. It is a matter of priority. Just as we saw with the [Kakuzi security tactics that faced backlash over property disputes](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kakuzi-security-tactics-face-backlash-after-viral-videos-of-macadami), the state frequently reserves its most robust interventions for the protection of capital or the preservation of political image, while the physical safety of citizens is treated as an afterthought.

### Predictability Over Protection

We are living through a period where political mobilization is increasingly shadowed by the threat of physical liquidation. The violence against Wandeto is not an anomaly; it is a structural byproduct of a political climate where the state remains passive until a high-profile victim enters the ICU. If the government were truly interested in safety, it would be regulating the precursors to such violence, much like the [EPRA intervention on illegal gas storage facilities](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-03-11-epra-shuts-down-illegal-lpg-storage-facility-in-kilifi-over-safety-c) sought to manage high-risk environments. Instead, the security apparatus waits for the smoke to rise before drafting a press release. Performative urgency is not a security strategy. It is a public relations exercise designed to quell public outcry while the underlying mechanisms of political arson continue to operate without friction.

### Why it matters now

The 2027 election cycle is approaching, and the use of extreme physical violence as a tool of political intimidation is becoming normalized. When the state fails to prosecute these crimes, it signals to political foot soldiers that they can escalate their tactics without facing the full weight of the law.

### What to watch next

Keep a close eye on the DCI's progress reports. If the investigation remains stalled at the request for public help without resulting in tangible arrests or clear revelations about the motive, it will confirm that the high-level orders from Murkomen and Kanja were never intended to stop the violence, but merely to manage the political fallout.

How many more directives will be issued before the state decides to protect its citizens before they are set on fire, rather than merely ordering investigations into their remains?
