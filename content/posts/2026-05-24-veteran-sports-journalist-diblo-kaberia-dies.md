## The Unsung Toll: Why Kenyan Sports Icons Struggle in Silence

The mourning of veteran broadcaster Diblo Kaberia has transformed into a raw, national debate regarding the dignity afforded to our media legends. While the country celebrates his iconic voice, Kenyans are asking why the heroes who defined our sports culture often face their final days in desperate financial isolation.

Diblo Kaberia, a voice that became the soundtrack to Kenyan football for generations, passed away on Sunday, May 24, following a protracted illness. His death was officially confirmed by longtime colleague Fred Arocho, who had been instrumental in mobilizing public support for Kaberia during his final months. The news has reignited frustrations over the lack of a structured welfare system for retired journalists, even as the Football Kenya Federation president, Hussein Mohammed, issued a formal tribute highlighting the depth of the loss to the nation. This quiet departure follows a recent period of community-led medical fundraising, a recurring and often insufficient lifeline for many who once held the microphone for this country.

### A Cycle of Crisis and Crowdfunding

The exhaustion in the voices of those who led the charge to save Kaberia is palpable. Fred Arocho has been vocal about the grueling reality of these grassroots fundraising efforts. He points out that the burden of care consistently falls on friends and former colleagues rather than institutions. Arocho expressed that waiting for a public crisis to trigger a GoFundMe-style response is a systemic failure that strips veterans of their dignity. He argues that the industry treats these personalities as disposable once their peak years of broadcasting are over, leaving them to rely on the erratic goodwill of the public when health emergencies strike.

For many Kenyans, this tragedy feels like a mirror held up to our own failures in valuing labor. Conversations across digital platforms reflect a growing anger that the same voices who provided entertainment and analysis for decades are left to battle terminal illness without institutional backing. Observers note that while the country is currently occupied with political tremors, such as the [High Court orders for a partial recount in Mbeere North](https://zandani.co.ke/article/high-court-orders-partial-recount-in-mbeere-north-by-election), the slow decay of our social safety nets for media workers remains a persistent, unaddressed sore.

### The Institutional Silence

Hussein Mohammed, speaking on behalf of the Football Kenya Federation, acknowledged the magnitude of the loss, describing Kaberia as a foundational pillar of sports commentary. However, many are looking past the condolences to demand policy changes. There is a sharp contrast between the accolades offered in death and the silence of sports bodies during a veteran's period of decline. Critics are calling for the FKF and major media houses to collaborate on a permanent welfare fund. Without such a mechanism, the industry will continue to mourn its icons while ignoring the financial precarity that plagued their final years.

Some voices on the ground suggest that the focus on modernization-such as recent shifts toward [local data infrastructure for digital growth](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenya-prioritizes-local-data-infrastructure-for-digital-growth)-is meaningless if the human capital that built our cultural memory is abandoned. The public sentiment is clear: we are tired of being the only security net for people who gave their lives to inform and inspire us.

### Why It Matters Now

The passing of Kaberia is not merely a loss of talent; it is a catalyst for institutional accountability. For too long, the Kenyan public has stepped in where corporate and institutional responsibility has failed. The current discourse signals a shift from passive sympathy to a demand for structural reform in how we treat retired media professionals.

### What to Watch Next

All eyes are now on the leadership of the Media Owners Association and sports federations. The public is waiting to see if there will be a concrete proposal for an endowment or health insurance scheme for aging sports personalities. Without a visible change in policy, the next time a veteran falls ill, the public will likely continue the exhausting cycle of digital fundraising, but the appetite for that cycle is clearly waning.

The mood across the nation remains somber, defined by a lingering sense of collective guilt over how we allow our legends to fade away.
