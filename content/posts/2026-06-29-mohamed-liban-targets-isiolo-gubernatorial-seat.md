## From Procurement Scandal to Ballot Box Former Petroleum PS Mohamed Liban Targets Isiolo Governor Seat is the central subject of the update.

Mohamed Liban recently secured the formal blessing of Warsu community elders in Isiolo to launch his 2027 gubernatorial bid. This pivot into local elective politics arrives just months after he exited the Petroleum Ministry under the dark cloud of a multi-billion shilling procurement controversy.

### The Mathematics of Political Rehabilitation

The transition from a high-stakes government office to the campaign trail in Isiolo relies on a familiar political alchemy: replacing institutional scrutiny with the protection of clan patronage. Liban, who once served as the Petroleum Principal Secretary, finds himself at the center of a Ksh4.8 billion fuel procurement investigation that rattled the foundations of the energy sector. Alongside figures like Daniel Kiptoo and Joe Sang, Liban faced arrest in early April 2026, marking a sharp exit from a tenure that was supposed to focus on energy stability.

While the public record remains burdened by the allegations surrounding the fuel deal, the local narrative in Isiolo is being carefully steered toward ancestral legitimacy. The elders who vetted Liban did not ask for a forensic audit of the Ksh4.8 billion; they asked for a leader who understands the geography of local loyalty. By moving his battleground from the corridors of power in Nairobi to the grassroots of Isiolo, Liban is effectively attempting to trade his administrative baggage for the communal validation that only regional politics can provide. This strategy suggests that in the current political climate, a local endorsement is often treated as a more potent currency than fiscal clearance from ethics authorities.

### Auditing the Cost of Silence

The official story avoids the uncomfortable intersection between the fuel procurement fallout and the upcoming 2027 election cycle. When Liban resigned, the conversation centered on executive accountability and the management of strategic petroleum reserves. Now, the focus is restricted to the dynamics of the gubernatorial race. There is a deafening silence from the political establishment regarding whether the pending legal inquiries into the fuel deal should disqualify a candidate from seeking the highest office in a county.

This pattern of political movement mirrors broader national trends, where officials facing ongoing investigations into [Finance Bill Proposals](https://zandani.co.ke/article/finance-bill-proposals-threaten-green-tech-pricing-in-kenya) or other systemic economic failings simply shift their sphere of influence. Just as we have seen with [Ruto Condemns Strikes on UAE, Qatar and Saudi Arabia, Calls for Urgent De-escalation: Kenya's Hidden Stakes Amidst Gulf Conflict](https://zandani.co.ke/article/ruto-condemns-strikes-uae-qatar-saudi-arabia-urgent-de-escalation), the complexities of external policy are often used to distract from internal governance gaps. In Isiolo, the strategy is more direct: wrap a contentious record in the flag of community representation to make it untouchable by critics.

The elders, by design, possess no mandate to review procurement logs or internal ministry memos. Their role is to provide a shield of tradition against the scrutiny of the state. Consequently, the Ksh4.8 billion question is being buried beneath layers of political campaigning. The state machinery, currently preoccupied with its own stability, appears content to let candidates with questionable financial histories occupy themselves with local power struggles. It is a convenient arrangement for all involved, provided the electorate does not look too closely at the paper trail left behind in Nairobi.

If the vetting process for the next Isiolo governor ignores the fiscal integrity of the candidate in favor of clan-based endorsements, what remains of Za Ndani for public service?
