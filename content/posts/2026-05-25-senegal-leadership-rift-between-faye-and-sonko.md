## The Dakar Paradox: Why the Constitution Makes Two Lions in One Cage Impossible

**The unfolding rift between President Bassirou Diomaye Faye and Prime Minister Ousmane Sonko is not a failure of character, but a systemic inevitable clash between two political giants operating under a constitution designed for a solitary head.**

The headlines coming out of Dakar are obsessed with personality politics. They paint the growing distance between President Bassirou Diomaye Faye and Prime Minister Ousmane Sonko as a messy, interpersonal falling out. This is a distraction. If we look past the optics of the presidential palace, we see a structural crisis. Senegal is currently experiencing the friction of a constitutional architecture that centralizes absolute power in the presidency while simultaneously demanding a secondary executive who functions with the weight of a co-leader. In a system built for one lion, two apex predators cannot share the cage without tearing at the bars.

### The Constitutional Culprit
At the heart of the matter lies the Senegalese executive structure. The constitution mandates that the President is the sole holder of executive prerogative. The Prime Minister is a delegated entity, a creature of the President's pleasure. Unlike systems with clearer delineations of power, the Senegalese framework creates a bottleneck. When the leader of a populist movement assumes the role of Prime Minister under a President who must, by law, assert his singular authority, the result is administrative gridlock. 

We see this elsewhere. In Kenya, our executive model is different. We have a clear separation between the presidency and the roles of Cabinet Secretaries, where the office of the President is legally defined to eclipse other executive functions. This avoids the ambiguity currently paralyzing Dakar. In Senegal, however, the Prime Minister position carries significant political capital, especially for a figure like Sonko, whose rise was built on a promise of radical reform. When the President invokes his constitutional mandate to override the Prime Minister, he is not just acting as an individual; he is defending the integrity of a system that refuses to allow a dual-executive model to exist.

### The Failure of Flexibility
Surviving this dynamic requires immense legislative flexibility. History shows that power-sharing arrangements only endure when there is a mechanism for compromise. In Dakar, that mechanism is absent. The tension between parliamentary dominance and presidential prerogative has reached a breaking point because the legal framework does not allow for a power-sharing, consultative presidency. 

While some might suggest this is merely a political honeymoon ending, it is more profound. It is a governance mismatch. The legislature is currently caught between the President's unilateral decrees and the Prime Minister's political agenda. Even as we debate the future of public policy-much like students currently weighing their own future paths via [university guidance](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-17-kuccps-to-hold-webinar-to-guide-students-on-university-and-college-a)-Senegal’s leaders are trapped in a constitutional tug-of-war. The system demands a subordinate, but the political reality has produced an equal.

### A Lesson in Structural Design
Critics argue that Faye and Sonko simply need to communicate better. This ignores the reality of executive law. If the constitution provides for a singular authority, then any deviation from that creates an instability that threatens the functioning of the state. Personality matters little when the blueprints of government are flawed. 

**Why it matters now**
The Senegalese standoff serves as a warning for emerging democracies. When political movements focus on winning elections without amending the constitutional structures that concentrate power, they often find themselves at war with their own governing documents.

**What to watch next**
Observe the upcoming legislative sessions. If the rift deepens, expect a move toward constitutional reform that either strips the Prime Minister of political leverage or forces a formal rebalancing of the presidency. Either way, the era of the dual-leader experiment in Dakar is rapidly closing.

Politics remains a game of institutional logic, not just popularity. Just as we have seen in our own local governance, from the [management of infrastructure funds](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-05-01-nairobi-expressway-toll-waiver-floods) to the shifting of political tides, success depends on whether the laws accommodate the players or bind them until they break. One cannot govern a nation if the chair of the President is built only for one.
