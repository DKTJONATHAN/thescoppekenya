## Shadows of Sovereignty: Decoding the Grassroots Backlash Against French Geopolitics in Nairobi

The streets of Nairobi are echoing with a visceral rejection of the Africa Forward Summit. As protesters clashed with police outside the Kenyatta International Conference Centre, the message was clear: Kenyans are demanding a fundamental re-evaluation of who holds power in our diplomatic halls.

Today, the KICC became a flashpoint as security forces moved to contain a spirited demonstration aimed at the summit's French-led agenda. Activists, led by figures including Booker Ngesa Omole, attempted to bypass security cordons to voice their opposition to what they label as the neo-colonial shadow cast by French influence in East Africa. The situation escalated quickly, resulting in the detention of approximately thirty individuals. Among those taken into custody were both local activists and a cohort of foreign nationals who had joined the picket line, highlighting the increasingly internationalized nature of anti-establishment dissent in Kenya.

### The Face of the Resistance

The mood on the ground suggests that this is not merely a momentary flare-up, but a deepening ideological chasm. Many observers see the presence of international protesters as a sign of solidarity against a shared struggle, yet others remain skeptical of foreign involvement in local political grievances.

Booker Ngesa Omole, who remained defiant even as police tightened the perimeter, argued that the summit is a performative exercise that ignores the economic realities of the average Kenyan. He insisted that true development cannot be imported through high-level bilateral summits if the terms of engagement remain fundamentally skewed against local sovereignty.

A university student from the nearby central business district expressed a different, though equally firm, concern. While acknowledging the frustration with French policy, she questioned whether the focus on foreign interference distracts from the pressing local issues that leaders should be solving, such as the rising cost of living or the ongoing focus on [KCB Bank scrapping fees for Pesalink transfers](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kcb-bank-scraps-fees-for-pesalink-transfers-below-1000) to help struggling families.

A long-time civil servant who watched the commotion from a nearby cafe offered a weary perspective. He noted that while the protesters have a right to be heard, the optics of foreign nationals marching alongside locals in the heart of Nairobi invites unnecessary friction. To him, the protest felt like a misplaced theater production where the script is written in Paris, leaving Kenyans to pick up the pieces of the fallout.

### Why it Matters Now

This unrest marks a significant shift in how Kenyans perceive international summits hosted on home soil. For years, such gatherings were seen as opportunities for investment and growth. Today, they are increasingly viewed through the lens of accountability. The public is asking hard questions about whether the proceeds from deals negotiated in these boardrooms actually benefit the economy, or if they just mirror the [Ksh 103B proceeds from the Kenya Pipeline IPO](https://zandani.co.ke/article/treasury-receives-ksh-103b-proceeds-from-kenya-pipeline-ipo) that have yet to translate into tangible relief for the average household. The protest is a symptom of a public that is tired of policy frameworks that seem detached from their daily survival.

### What to Watch Next

The immediate focus will be on the legal fate of the thirty individuals currently in custody. Legal experts expect a swift move to charge them, which will likely trigger a secondary round of protests focused on civil liberties and the right to peaceful assembly. Furthermore, the diplomatic fallout will be telling. Watch to see if French officials or the local government issue a statement to defuse the tension or if they double down on the security-first approach. The government faces a delicate balancing act: maintaining the security required for international dignitaries while avoiding the appearance of suppressing legitimate political dissent. As the summit continues, the KICC will remain a pressure cooker of conflicting ideas about what a forward-looking Africa should actually look like.

The air in Nairobi feels heavy with the scent of tear gas and the unspoken question of whether the era of unquestioned diplomatic consensus has officially come to an end.
