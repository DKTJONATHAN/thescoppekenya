## Shri Krishana Overseas and the NSE Debt Dilemma

If you are a small business owner looking at the Nairobi Securities Exchange, the recent performance of Shri Krishana Overseas is a head-scratcher. While profits have plummeted by 59 percent, the stock price keeps climbing, leaving traders wondering if they are looking at a smart long-term bet or just a market mirage.

For the average SME trader, the news coming out of Shri Krishana Overseas is a stark reminder that what happens on the balance sheet and what happens on the ticker tape are often two different conversations. The company reported a 59 percent drop in annual earnings, with gross profit sliding to KSh 87.82 million. Despite this, the share price sits at KSh 8.94. This disconnect highlights a high-stakes gamble on the future rather than a reflection of current cash in the bank.

### The Kisaju Bet and the Debt Ceiling

The centerpiece of this investor optimism is the new manufacturing facility in Kisaju, Kajiado County. Market sentiment suggests that investors are ignoring the current operational slowdown because they expect this plant to unlock massive industrial potential once it comes online in Q3 2026. However, for those of us running smaller operations, debt is a cold reality that cannot be ignored for the sake of future promises. 

Shri Krishana is currently carrying a 2.12x net debt-to-equity ratio. This means for every shilling of equity the company has, it owes more than double in debt. When you add in the KSh 14.83 million paid out in finance costs, it becomes clear why the company is struggling. Their interest coverage ratio sits at 1.57x, which is a thin margin of safety. If your own business had to pay out such a large portion of its operating profit just to satisfy lenders, you would likely be feeling the pressure of every single delay in your supply chain. 

### Why the Market is Acting Up

The Kisaju plant was supposed to be running last year. The delay to the third quarter of 2026 has clearly eaten into the bottom line, driving operating profits down by nearly 43 percent. Yet, the stock market continues to price the company as if the plant is already a success. This is a classic case of speculative inflation. 

For the everyday investor or small trader, this creates a dangerous trap. It is easy to be swayed by a rising share price, but in this case, the price is not reflecting the company's ability to generate cash today. It is reflecting a hope that the Kajiado industrial corridor will eventually turn this ship around. While waiting for the market to correct itself, some of us might prefer to focus on current stability, much like fans keeping an eye on the latest [FIFA Series 2026 updates](https://zandani.co.ke/article/fifa-series-2026-explained-harambee-stars-matches) to see where real, tangible progress is being made on the pitch. Even in entertainment, like the long wait for the [Davido and Chris Brown album](https://zandani.co.ke/article/davido-confirms-2027-release-for-joint-album-with-chris-brown), patience is a requirement, but it does not always pay the bills for a small business.

### Why it Matters Now

The situation at Shri Krishana Overseas serves as a warning for SMEs seeking external financing. When your operational costs and debt servicing exceed your output, growth becomes a liability. The company is currently pinning its survival on the Kisaju plant's future production. If that facility does not hit the ground running by Q3, the current share price could face a sharp, painful adjustment.

### What to Watch Next

Keep a close eye on the Q3 production reports from the Kisaju site. If the plant misses the new commissioning deadline, the current interest coverage ratio of 1.57x may become unsustainable. For those invested in the stock, look for signs of debt restructuring. If the company continues to rely on borrowing to fund these delays, the gap between their share price and their actual cash flow will only widen, making the stock significantly riskier for anyone holding it with the hope of short-term dividends. Prioritize cash flow health in your own ventures while the market works through this speculative phase.
