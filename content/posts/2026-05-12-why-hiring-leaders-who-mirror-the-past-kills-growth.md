## The Mirror Trap: Why Institutional Succession Is Stagnating

The boardroom at a major Nairobi bank fell silent last week. As the search committee finalized their choice for the incoming CEO, the decision felt wearyingly predictable. They opted against a disruptor, choosing instead a carbon copy of the outgoing leader. They prioritized a comfortable, familiar pedigree over the messy, unpredictable requirements of a digital future.

Our legacy institutions are currently trapped in a cycle of self-replication. This tendency threatens our collective economic vitality. By valuing historical pedigree above adaptive agility, we are designing organizations optimized for the battles of the past, leaving them defenseless against the disruptions now reshaping our markets.

### The Illusion of Proven Success

When hiring committees meet, they frequently succumb to the temptation of measuring value through awards, citations, and linear career paths. It feels secure to appoint someone who has navigated the exact same trajectory. Yet, the research of Boris Groysberg at Harvard Business School provides a sobering counterpoint. His findings suggest that professional performance is rarely portable. When a star performer shifts from one firm to another, their success often vanishes because it was tethered to a specific institutional context rather than an inherent, universal brilliance.

In Kenya, this reality is stark within our telecom and banking sectors. Companies recruit executives who have spent decades climbing a singular vertical ladder, assuming these individuals will navigate a digital-first future with the same ease they navigated the analog past. When these leaders struggle to innovate, boards are genuinely baffled. They mistakenly view experience as an insurance policy against failure, forgetting that in a shifting climate, deep-rooted habits often function as anchors rather than sails. This reliance on pedigree over potential is a silent, systemic engine of failure.

### Moving Beyond the Mirror

To survive, an institution must seek out "complementary successors." Instead of appointing a deputy who simply mirrors the current leader's strengths, boards need to identify the holes in their own armor. If the current leadership excels at regulatory compliance but falters in consumer-centric product design, the successor must be an outlier in those specific domains. 

This requires a fundamental departure from traditional recruitment. It necessitates looking for individuals who have failed in unconventional ways, or who have built ventures in entirely different industries. We must shift our focus from hiring a personality to assembling a mosaic of capabilities. The operational challenges faced by companies like KOKO Networks remind us that structural stability is never a permanent state. Organizations that prioritize internal institutional memory over external adaptability will inevitably find themselves sidelined by nimbler, non-traditional players.

### Why it matters now

The Kenyan economy sits at a precarious turning point, where global digital shifts collide with local market constraints. Institutions that persist in treating leadership as a closed-loop system of patronage and credentialism are squandering their resources. We are failing the next generation of professionals by rewarding the mimicry of past leaders instead of the pursuit of future-ready skills.

### What to watch next

Observe the upcoming executive rotations within our Tier 1 financial institutions closely. If the pattern of appointing internal, legacy-aligned candidates continues, expect those institutions to see a gradual erosion of market share to regional fintech challengers. The true indicator of health will not be the prestige of a new CEO's previous employer, but their demonstrated ability to dismantle the very processes that led to their appointment.

Real growth demands the humility to hire people who challenge your own professional identity. True leadership is not about finding someone who can walk in your footsteps, but finding someone who possesses the vision to recognize when it is time to build a new road entirely.
