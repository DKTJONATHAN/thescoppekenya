While the trophy is the tangible prize, the match serves as a high-stakes meeting between two men deeply entwined in the history of Scottish football. For Dunfermline, this final is the culmination of a campaign that saw them knock out three Premiership opponents, defying expectations to reach the national stadium.

### A Coaching Lineage Defined

Martin O’Neill remains Za Ndani-bearer for a specific brand of Scottish football: pragmatic, physical, and uncompromising. His Celtic side arrives at Hampden banking on structural integrity and the expectation that big games are settled by discipline rather than improvisation. With Callum McGregor and James Forrest at the heart of the setup, the intent is clear. Celtic will seek to stretch the pitch, using their experience to manage the tempo and force the opposition into errors.

Neil Lennon, meanwhile, approaches this fixture from a different vantage point. While his organizational roots are tied to the principles O'Neill instilled in him, his time in the Kingdom of Fife has fostered a more fluid tactical identity. By placing his faith in the technical proficiency of Charlie Gilmour to manage the midfield, while utilizing the veteran presence of Scott Brown and James McFadden, Lennon has constructed a side capable of more than just defensive resistance. This is not merely a nostalgia act for the two men; it is a competitive collision where Lennon aims to prove his own methodology can overcome his predecessor's blueprint.

### Tactical Battles and Institutional Weight

The dynamics of this final are influenced by the starkly different pressures resting on each bench. Celtic enters the match hunting their 43rd Scottish Cup victory, carrying the weight of a club where domestic dominance is an obligation. O’Neill relies on this institutional momentum to guide his players through the pressure of a potential double. His staff, including long-serving coach Stevie Woods, look toward the trophy as a standard validation of their long-running success.

Dunfermline operates with a different energy. Backed by 14,000 supporters who have waited since 1968 for a major trophy, Lennon’s squad plays with the relative freedom of a side that has already exceeded its seasonal mandate. The tactical focal point will undoubtedly be the midfield battle. Lennon’s challenge is to isolate Celtic’s pivots, thereby stifling the supply lines to the wide areas. Should he succeed, he will force O'Neill to abandon his preferred control-based game and play under conditions that favor a more chaotic, reactive match. If O’Neill’s defensive discipline remains intact, however, Celtic will likely suffocate Dunfermline’s ambitions, turning the match into a war of attrition that plays into their hands.
