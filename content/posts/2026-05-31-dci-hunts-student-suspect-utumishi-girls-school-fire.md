## The Vanished Suspect and the Protocol Gap

The Directorate of Criminal Investigations has confirmed that a primary student suspect linked to the tragic blaze at Utumishi Girls Senior Secondary School has absconded after being released to her family, exposing a deep fracture in juvenile justice oversight within school settings. While investigators work to identify the 16 victims through DNA analysis at the Naivasha Sub-County Referral Hospital, the focus has shifted toward why a minor suspected of such a grave offense was not held under tighter state supervision.

### Why was a juvenile suspect released to guardians?

Current legal protocols for handling minors in criminal investigations prioritize the presence of parents or guardians. When a student becomes a person of interest in a school-based incident, police are often directed to release the child into the custody of parents during the initial phases of the inquiry to protect the minor from the psychological trauma of incarceration. However, this policy relies heavily on the assumption that guardians can maintain control over the child and ensure their availability for further questioning. In the Utumishi case, this expectation failed. When the suspect vanished, the mechanism for holding a minor accountable while simultaneously upholding their constitutional rights proved dangerously inadequate. There is no middle ground between a school environment and a jail cell, leaving a vacuum where supervision is supposed to reside.

### Where does the accountability lie between school and state?

The tragedy at the Gilgil-based school has ignited a debate about the shared responsibility between administrative authorities and law enforcement. School boards are expected to act as the primary supervisors of student conduct, yet they lack the forensic capacity to manage suspects in arson investigations. When the DCI steps in, the transition of custody from school administration to police is often informal. This lack of a structured transfer process means that once a student leaves school premises, the school loses its legal authority to detain, and the police often lack the resources to monitor the student at home. The systemic failure is clear: there is no formal framework requiring schools to report the whereabouts of high-risk students to both local law enforcement and the Ministry of Education in real-time. This oversight allows children facing immense pressure to slip through the cracks of the justice system entirely.

### How should school safety policy evolve?

Legal experts are now pointing to the urgent need for a legislative review regarding how juvenile suspects are managed in cases of institutional fire incidents. Reforms must prioritize a specialized supervision model that avoids the harsh environment of an adult detention center but also prevents the current reliance on parental monitoring, which clearly failed in this instance. Such a framework would involve court-mandated counseling and secure observation homes where the state, rather than the parent, assumes the burden of care and security. Without such changes, the legal system remains ill-equipped to handle the unique intersection of child welfare and criminal liability.
