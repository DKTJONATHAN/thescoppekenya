## The Senate of Kenya and the Quorum Illusion

The prevailing narrative suggests our Senators are simply lazy, neglecting their constitutional duties while the country burns. This moral outrage misses the mark entirely. We are not watching a lack of work ethic; we are watching a broken economic incentive structure.

### The Profitability of Absence

To label these men and women as lazy is to misunderstand the logic of their employment. We treat the Senate as a deliberative assembly, but for many, it functions as a high-stakes enterprise where physical presence is treated as a premium commodity rather than a baseline requirement. When 48 committee meetings fail to launch in a single cycle, we aren't seeing personal failure; we are seeing a system that lacks a fiscal penalty for inaction.

Consider the economics of our legislative inaction. These public servants draw lucrative salaries, allowances, and per diems tied to their status as elected officials, not to their output or the success of their proceedings. When a plenary session is delayed by 30 minutes, or a sitting is adjourned for the ninth time since February due to a thin floor, the taxpayer loses twice: once in the wasted administrative overhead of keeping the doors open, and again in the opportunity cost of stalled legislation. If we ran any other sector with such a blatant disregard for attendance, the payroll department would have ceased issuing checks months ago. The problem is not a lack of interest in the job; the problem is that the job pays exactly the same whether the meeting happens or not.

### The Case for Fiscal Clawbacks

We need to pivot from shaming individuals to rewriting the rules of their compensation. If a corporation faced 48 cancelled meetings in a quarter, it would be bankrupt. Why does the Kenyan taxpayer subsidize this lack of productivity? We require a performance-linked compensation model. This should include mandatory salary clawback mechanisms for every session adjourned due to lack of quorum. If a Senator is not present to vote on the bills they were elected to pass, they have effectively resigned from their duty for that day. Why should the public subsidize a vacancy?

Our legislative framework currently lacks the teeth to force accountability. We should advocate for automated docking of pay for any member absent during a quorum call. This is not about being punitive; it is about recognizing that a legislative seat is a contract of service. If the service is not rendered, the invoice should not be paid. While the national discourse recently shifted toward [Kenya and Mozambique signing a security agreement](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenya-and-mozambique-sign-security-and-counter-terrorism-agreement), our internal legislative security remains fragile because our own houses are failing to meet. Even as sectors like entertainment see professionals like [Kalondu Musyimi recognized for excellence](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kalondu-musyimi-showbiz-reporter), our lawmakers seem content to retreat into a standard of mediocrity that would be laughed out of any other industry.

### Why it matters now

The 2027 general election is looming, and the public is already being coached to view the quorum crisis as a reason to vote incumbents out. However, voting out individuals without fixing the underlying pay-for-participation model will only rotate a new group of people into the same flawed incentives. We are funding a system that does not prioritize the completion of its own agenda.

### What to watch next

Keep a close eye on the proposed legislative triggers currently being discussed in policy circles. Specifically, look for motions aimed at tying sitting allowances strictly to quorum-verified attendance registers. If these proposals are buried or watered down, it will be the clearest indicator that the Senate has no interest in self-correction. True accountability will arrive not when voters get angry, but when the Senators realize that every empty chair is coming directly out of their own bank accounts. 

Stop asking for more diligence. Start demanding a paycheck that reflects the actual cost of their absence.
