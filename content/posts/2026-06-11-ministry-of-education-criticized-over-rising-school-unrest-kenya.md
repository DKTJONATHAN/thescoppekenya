## A Crisis of Stewardship: An Open Letter to the Ministry of Education

Seventy educational institutions have shuttered their gates in a mere fortnight. As dormitory roofs smolder and thousands of students find themselves stranded, this unfolding disaster serves as a grim reflection of a profound leadership void within our national education sector.

To the Ministry of Education,

The volatility observed since the tragedy at Utumishi School on May 28 cannot be dismissed as mere adolescent defiance. These incidents are a visceral, fire-lit warning that our schools, meant to be sanctuaries of learning, have evolved into environments of administrative neglect. We are confronted with a systemic collapse that consistently favors bureaucratic optics over the fundamental safety and stability of the classroom.

### The Consequences of Administrative Instability

Swapping personnel at the executive level without addressing the underlying culture of an institution is a hollow gesture. It solves nothing; it merely displaces the burden. The recent reassignment of Prof. Julius Bitok, moving him from the State Department for Basic Education to the tourism docket, is widely perceived by the public as an attempt to evade accountability. By displacing the official primary figurehead during such a period of volatility, the government has signaled that political maneuvering takes precedence over the continuity of our children's education.

This is an inappropriate time for boardrooms to prioritize musical chairs while school corridors are in peril. The public perception of instability, underscored by the PS transition, has rippled down to our institutions. Principals and school boards now report feeling isolated and without direction. When leadership appears desperate to abandon their posts, it is inevitable that the systems they oversee will succumb to instability. Governance is not a relay race where one leaves the track the moment the burden becomes excessive.

### The Erosion of Dialogue

The silence emanating from Jogoo House has been profound. Rather than engaging in proactive discourse, the Ministry has relied solely on the reactive closure of facilities. When students utilize arson to manifest their frustrations, it is a definitive indicator that all legitimate avenues of communication have been severed. Our youth feel that their feedback concerning management styles, academic stressors, and school conditions is viewed as a nuisance rather than an essential component of school life.

Instead of deploying heavy-handed security tactics or resorting to closing gates, the Ministry must facilitate independent, reliable mediation channels. We have watched public debates regarding national leadership and prosperity dominate the news, yet we have failed to foster that same spirit of inquiry and open dialogue within our schools. It is a grave policy error to assume that students will remain compliant while their daily environment deteriorates. The arrogance behind such an assumption is the catalyst for the present unrest.

### A Demand for Radical Transparency

The status quo is both dangerous and unsustainable. We are trapped in a predictable, tragic cycle: a school is set ablaze, students are dismissed, the Ministry issues standard platitudes, and the next school prepares for a similar outburst. This pattern must be broken immediately.

The Ministry of Education owes the public a comprehensive audit regarding the grievances that prompted these seventy closures. We require more than press releases focused on discipline; we demand a transparent assessment of how administrative failures have fueled this turmoil. Will the Ministry commit to an independent, rigorous review of student-administration communication channels before another dormitory is reduced to ash?

The remnants of these buildings are not merely damaged property. They are remnants of your own authority. It is time to step out from behind your desks and confront the reality of the crisis you are currently overseeing. If you are unable to bridge the widening divide between the Ministry and the student body, you must yield to those who can.

The question remains for you to answer: are you genuinely governing our schools, or are you merely passive observers of their collapse?

Respectfully,

An observer of the fire.
