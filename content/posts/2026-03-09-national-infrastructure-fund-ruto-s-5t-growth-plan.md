## National Infrastructure Fund to Mobilize KSh 5 Trillion for Infrastructure Development President William Ruto has formally established the National Infrastructure Fund (NIF) through the assent of the NIF Bill on Monday, March 9. This landmark legislation aims to mobilize over KSh 5 trillion within the next decade for significant infrastructure projects, marking a pivotal shift in Kenya's economic financing strategy.

Establishment of the Fund President William Ruto assented to the National Infrastructure Fund (NIF) Bill at State House, Nairobi, on Monday, March 9, 2026. This act officially establishes the NIF, a mechanism designed to secure more than KSh 5 trillion over the next ten years for large-scale infrastructure development. The presidential assent followed the bill's successful passage by the National Assembly on March 5, 2026, after its third reading.

A Shift in Economic Policy The new law signifies a fundamental change in Kenya's approach to economic policy. It moves the nation away from a heavy reliance on debt-driven financing models towards a more sustainable, investment-led strategy for funding essential projects. President Ruto emphasized that the fund will revolutionize how Kenya finances major development initiatives, indicating that it will be managed by competitive individuals rather than politicians.

Operational Structure and Governance The National Infrastructure Fund is structured as a body corporate, granting it the legal capacity to own property, enter into contracts, and invest in projects. Critically, the fund is explicitly prohibited from borrowing or taking credit against its balance sheet, reinforcing its independent, investment-focused mandate. Treasury Cabinet Secretary John Mbadi announced that a seven-member Board of Directors will oversee the fund, chaired by an independent director, with the Treasury CS also holding a position on the board. The board is required to report to the Treasury ministry four times annually. The board's composition includes four independent directors and two development banking experts. Strict regulations are in place to ensure independence, barring members with recent government employment or political affiliations. Directors are also prevented from having conflicts of interest linked to suppliers, consultants, or auditors associated with the NIF. Board members will serve three-year terms, renewable only once, and can be removed for reasons such as bankruptcy, conviction, absenteeism, or entry into political office.

Funding and Capital Mobilization The government will initiate the NIF with KSh 106 billion, raised through the partial privatization of the Kenya Pipeline Company. President Ruto also stated that several government agencies, including the Kenya Airports Authority (KAA), Kenya Ports Authority (KPA), Kenya Railways, and the National Cereals and Produce Board (NCPB), will now operate under the NIF's framework. These entities will access capital markets over time, with the generated proceeds earmarked for further infrastructure development. President Ruto had previously indicated that Kenya anticipates raising KSh 2.5 trillion, half of the total target, by April 2026, with the remaining KSh 2.5 trillion to be secured over the subsequent decade.
