Nairobi 44 Police Stations Lack Title Deeds Risk Grabbing is central to this update for Kenyan readers.

Inspector General Douglas Kanja has revealed that 44 out of 64 police stations in Nairobi lack title deeds. The report, presented to the Senate Committee on National Security, warns that iconic stations face potential land grabbing and closure due to missing documentation.

## Security Installations Without Land Ownership
The report highlights a precarious situation where key law enforcement facilities sit on land with untraceable records or informal registration. Several stations occupy donated land that was never legally transferred to the National Police Service. According to the report seen by Za Ndani, the problem cuts across multiple sub-counties, leaving critical security assets vulnerable to private interests.

### Starehe and Kilimani Stations Affected
Starehe is currently the most affected sub-county in the capital. Vital installations including Ngara, Pangani, Eastleigh North, Kariobangi, Korogocho, and Muthaiga police stations have no proper documentation. This lack of legal protection leaves these premises vulnerable to encroachment.

In the Kilimani area, Kileleshwa Police Station, Kibra Police Station, and Sarangombe Police Post are also operating without title deeds. This mirrors the precarious nature of public property and systemic challenges in coordinating national initiatives, much like the logistical hurdles seen during [Eric Omondi’s 466km Busia charity walk](https://zandani.co.ke/article/omanyala-samidoh-azziad-eric-omondi-busia-charity-walk) earlier this year.

### Widespread Ownership Gaps in Nairobi
The ownership crisis extends to Lang'ata Police Station, alongside the Hardy and Akila stations and the Bomas Police Post. In the Westlands and Gigiri regions, Runda, Gigiri, Loresho, and Evergreen posts have also been flagged for lacking ownership documents. Parklands Police Station and Luthuli Police Post face similar legal uncertainties.

In the Kabete area, Muthangari Police Station and the Kawangware and Gatina posts are also listed as lacking documentation. These revelations follow a broader trend of unsecured public assets. Over 26,000 public schools in Kenya still lack title deeds, leaving them open to land grabbing and stalled infrastructure development. The current situation suggests that critical security infrastructure remains at risk despite ongoing government efforts to secure public land.
