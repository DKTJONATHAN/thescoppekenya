Civic education activist Morara Kebaso recently ignited online buzz, stating on Obinna Show Live that KSh50 million is too small to influence his political principles. His bold 2026 declaration, emphasizing his unwavering integrity and independent thinking, confirms he simply "cannot be bought" by any sum.

Morara Kebaso's Unwavering Stance Morara Kebaso has sent ripples through both entertainment and political spheres with his firm declaration that KSh50 million simply isn't enough to compromise his integrity. Speaking candidly during the popular Obinna Show Live, Kebaso emphasized his commitment to independent thinking, asserting that the amount is too small to buy his loyalty. Clips of the interview, where he stated, "50 million is a small amount to me-not in a bad way-but it’s not the kind of money that can influence me," have quickly become a major talking point across online platforms.

Integrity Over Influence Kebaso has long cultivated a reputation as a fierce advocate for civic education and political accountability within Kenya. Through his consistent appearances on various platforms, including Obinna Show Live, he dedicates himself to enlightening citizens on critical aspects of governance, financial discipline, and leadership responsibility. He stresses that his core mission involves empowering Kenyans with knowledge, fostering informed decision-making, and encouraging active participation in democratic processes. This commitment to principle aligns with ongoing national conversations about integrity, especially as institutions like the IEBC continue to ensure fair representation through scheduled by-elections for vacant seats across the nation.

Sparking National Dialogue Amidst 2026's ongoing discussions surrounding integrity, transparency, and accountability in leadership, Morara Kebaso's remarks resonate deeply. He consistently challenges both leaders and citizens alike to prioritize ethical values over immediate financial gain, arguing that genuine, sustainable progress is rooted in principled choices. His reiterated belief that activism and leadership must be driven by purpose and service, rather than monetary incentives, continues to fuel lively online debates regarding the intricate role of money in Kenyan politics and public life. Kebaso's growing influence, particularly among younger audiences on digital platforms, positions him as a notable voice shaping discussions around leadership and accountability in Kenya.
