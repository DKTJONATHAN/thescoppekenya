## Is God Is: What Kenyan Creators Can Learn from Harris’s Masterclass in Genre-Bending

Everyone is talking about Vivica A. Fox leading a fierce new revenge saga, but the true brilliance is happening behind the camera. As Aleshea Harris brings her celebrated stage play to the big screen, Kenyan filmmakers have a front-row seat to an excellent case study in creator-led production.

When a playwright moves into the director’s chair, the results are often unpredictable. However, the film adaptation of Is God Is showcases a major victory for narrative ownership. By keeping the creative vision firmly with the original storyteller, the project avoids the common pitfalls that occur when studios dilute a play’s unique voice during the transition to film.

### How Playwright-led Adaptation Changes the Stakes

Kenya has an abundance of incredible theatrical talent. All too often, those stories lose their sharp, biting edge when outsiders translate them to the screen without understanding the script's cultural rhythm. Harris provides a perfect blueprint for local writers to follow. By directing the adaptation herself, she ensures the visceral and deeply emotional core of her play remains completely intact.

The story follows twin sisters, played by Kara Young and Mallori Johnson, who are tasked by their severely burned mother, Ruby, to hunt down and kill their father. While a revenge plot might sound like standard thriller material, the execution is entirely unique. This is a story about the messy, painful inheritance of intergenerational trauma. It forces viewers to face how the sins of parents ripple through their children’s lives-a theme that hits home in our own storytelling traditions, where family secrets drive our most compelling dramas.

### Vivica A. Fox as the Anchor of Narrative Agency

The decision to cast Vivica A. Fox as Ruby is a smart move that elevates the film from a typical genre piece to a character-driven study. Ruby is far from a passive victim. She is the catalyst who ignites the narrative, and Fox delivers a performance that demands the audience respect her agency. It is a powerful reminder that when iconic actors choose roles focusing on the complexity of Black womanhood instead of shallow tropes, the entire production gains significant gravitas.

For local production houses aiming to create content that stands up on an international scale, the lesson is straightforward. Success is not merely about having a large budget or flashy camera work. It is about honoring the source material and ensuring the creative team is united by a singular, bold vision. When directors like Harris collaborate with seasoned veterans like Fox, they effectively bypass the traditional gatekeeping of Hollywood. It serves as proof that independent voices can successfully command their own narratives.

### Why It Matters Now

We are at a turning point regarding how we produce and consume local content. While many are still distracted by the viral shifts in music culture, such as the latest rumors surrounding Afrobeats stars, the real growth for our creative sector will come from mastering these technical adaptations. Seeing a story move from the stage to the screen without losing its soul is a goal our local playwrights should pursue as they look to expand their global reach.

### What to Watch Next

Keep a close eye on the film's theatrical rollout. The true measure of its success will be whether it sparks a broader trend of playwright-directors securing the funding needed to adapt their own stage work. If this model proves to be a profitable venture, it could encourage Kenyan producers to invest in formal stage-to-screen pipelines, effectively bridging the gap between our theater halls and major streaming platforms. 

Observe closely to see if the film's brutal, stylized aesthetic resonates with mainstream audiences or if it settles into a cult following. The public's reaction will tell us a great deal about the current appetite for challenging, genre-defying stories. Ultimately, true artistic power in cinema comes from keeping the pen and the megaphone in the very same hands.
