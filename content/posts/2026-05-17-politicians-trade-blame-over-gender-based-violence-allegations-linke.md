A brutal assault involving a woman and an alleged extramarital affair has ignited a fierce political confrontation between the executive branch and factions loyal to Deputy President Rigathi Gachagua, as both sides exploit the violent incident to discredit their opponents across Kenya.

## The Politicization of Gender-Based Violence

### Weaponizing Tragedy for Political Clout
The assault has prompted a scramble for moral authority, with state-allied actors actively attempting to link the violence to the Deputy President’s political circle. Rather than confronting the broader surge of Gender-Based Violence cases affecting the country, officials appear to be treating the physical attack as a strategic tool for political maneuvering. The safety of the victim has been sidelined in favor of scoring points against rivals, reinforcing public frustration with a political class that prioritizes optics over the urgent systemic issue of personal security.

### A Pattern of Selective Outrage
The political reaction to this incident has drawn sharp criticism regarding its inconsistency. Observers point out that when previous violent confrontations involved figures such as Senator Godfrey Osotsi, the response from senior government officials was notably muted. This selective outrage suggests that the condemnation of violence is contingent upon the political affiliation of those involved. Critics argue that this double standard exposes a cynical approach to law and order, where the outrage machinery is only activated when it serves the interest of high-ranking state actors.

### The Shift Toward Victim-Blaming
Amid the escalating rhetoric, the public discourse has shifted toward regressive narratives that blame the victim for her own assault. Some commentators have suggested that women should avoid relationships with married men to ensure their personal safety, effectively shifting the burden of violence from the perpetrators to the targets. This trend serves as a dangerous distraction from the government’s failure to provide adequate protections for women. By focusing on the personal choices of the victim rather than the criminal act of assault, political factions are successfully diverting attention from the lack of substantive reform in addressing violence in Kenya.
