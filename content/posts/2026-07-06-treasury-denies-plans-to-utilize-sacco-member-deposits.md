## Are Your SACCO Savings Really at Risk?

Rumors circulating on social media claim the government is eyeing private deposits held in savings and credit societies to fund the National Infrastructure Fund. With millions of Kenyans relying on these accounts for financial security, Treasury officials have stepped in to clarify the status of these assets.

The National Treasury has formally rejected claims that it intends to tap into the roughly Ksh1 trillion held in member-owned SACCOs. Cabinet Secretary John Mbadi described these circulating reports as fabricated misinformation, noting that such a move would contradict the existing financial architecture of the country. For the 14 million Kenyans who participate in these societies, the primary concern remains the safety of their hard-earned capital and the legal boundaries protecting their money from state interference.

### What Prevents the Government from Accessing SACCO Deposits?

The primary legal shield for members is the Cooperative Societies Act. Unlike pension funds or other state-managed pools of capital, SACCOs are registered as private, member-owned entities. Their mandates are strictly limited to providing credit and savings facilities to their specific membership base. Because these funds are essentially private property held in trust by the society for its members, there is no legislative mechanism within the current financial statutes that allows the National Treasury to requisition these deposits for national development projects or deficit financing.

The National Infrastructure Fund operates under different fiscal mandates, primarily focused on public-private partnerships, taxation, and international debt instruments. Using private SACCO savings would require a fundamental dismantling of existing property rights and the Cooperative Societies Act, which would lead to a massive legal and financial crisis. The government currently lacks any legal standing to override the internal bylaws of these societies, which are governed by elected boards accountable to their members, not to the state.

### How Does the New Cooperatives Bill Affect Member Autonomy?

Legislative efforts currently underway, such as the Kenya Cooperatives Bill, have been misinterpreted by some as a backdoor to state control. In reality, the Bill is primarily designed to improve transparency and enhance the oversight role of the Commissioner for Cooperatives to prevent the mismanagement seen in past sector scandals. While the state is increasing its regulatory gaze to protect against fraud, this is distinct from accessing the funds for infrastructure.

The goal of this oversight is to align SACCO operations with broader financial stability benchmarks, not to turn them into secondary government treasuries. By creating a more standardized regulatory environment, the Bill seeks to ensure that SACCOs remain stable enough to handle their own liquidity needs. For instance, the reforms are aimed at preventing the kind of systemic failure that might force a SACCO to seek government bailouts in the first place, rather than creating a pipeline for the government to drain those accounts for public works.

The structural difference between a sovereign infrastructure fund and a private cooperative is significant. The government manages public infrastructure through tax collection and the issuance of sovereign bonds, which are public debt instruments. SACCO deposits, by contrast, are governed by contractual agreements between the individual member and the society. Moving capital from a private member account to a public infrastructure project would violate the core contract of the cooperative model. Members should view these deposits as protected by these clear, distinct regulatory walls.

Your SACCO savings remain legally shielded by private ownership laws that the government cannot bypass for public infrastructure projects.
