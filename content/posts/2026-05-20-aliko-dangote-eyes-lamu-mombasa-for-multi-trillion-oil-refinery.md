## Competition Intensifies for Multi-Trillion Shilling Oil Refinery Investment

Billionaire industrialist Aliko Dangote has officially included Lamu as a potential site for a proposed 650,000-barrel-a-day oil refinery, forcing a direct competition between the coast region and Mombasa for the massive infrastructure project. 

The refinery, projected to cost between Ksh1.95 trillion and Ksh2.21 trillion, aims to supply petroleum products to landlocked nations including Uganda, South Sudan, Ethiopia, and the Democratic Republic of Congo. This development follows high-level consultations held on May 16 between Mr. Dangote and Tanzanian President Samia Suluhu, signaling a regional scramble for energy dominance. While the project is intended to serve East Africa, the final site selection rests with President William Ruto, who must weigh the strategic logistics of existing infrastructure against long-term industrial potential.

### The Logistics Battle: LAPSSET Versus Mombasa 

The selection process has essentially become a beauty contest between two distinct coastal assets. Mombasa currently offers the advantage of an established industrial ecosystem and existing refinery support infrastructure. It serves as the primary gateway for regional imports, meaning the integration of a massive refinery would encounter fewer logistical hurdles in terms of existing road, rail, and energy grid connectivity. However, space constraints and urban congestion in Mombasa present significant challenges for an operation of this magnitude.

Lamu, anchored by the LAPSSET corridor project, offers a blank slate. The Lamu Port infrastructure is designed for massive bulk handling and large-scale industrial expansion that Mombasa struggles to accommodate. If chosen, Lamu would benefit from the LAPSSET connectivity, potentially turning the region into a global petroleum hub. Proponents argue that building in Lamu would stimulate the northern corridor economy, whereas skeptics point to the immense cost of establishing supporting infrastructure from scratch in a region currently lacking heavy industrial readiness.

### Economic Stakes and Regional Priorities

The scale of this project promises to reshape local economies. An investment of this size is expected to create thousands of direct and indirect employment opportunities for local residents in either region. Beyond the jobs, the refinery would fundamentally alter Kenya's status in the regional energy market, moving the country from a consumer of imported refined products to a primary supplier.

The government continues to prioritize regional industrial development to curb the high cost of fuel and petroleum imports. President Ruto has previously indicated that state agencies are reviewing the logistical viability of these sites. While stakeholders in Mombasa fear losing potential revenue to a new hub, Lamu residents view the prospective refinery as a vital catalyst for regional development, much like the scrutiny seen in other [state-led initiatives](https://zandani.co.ke/article/president-william-ruto-re-appoints-chairpersons-of-four-state-agenci).

### Why it matters now

Kenya is currently facing volatile fuel prices and logistical constraints that hamper regional trade. By anchoring a 650,000-barrel-a-day facility within its borders, the country could stabilize supply chains and lower costs for the entire East African community. The decision between Lamu and Mombasa is not merely about location; it is about choosing whether to optimize existing capacity or invest in the future of the northern frontier. As the state balances these interests, the project remains a pivotal test of Kenya's industrial policy.

### What to watch next

Observers are waiting for the formation of a technical committee to finalize the environmental and logistical impact assessments for both sites. The timeline for these assessments remains unconfirmed, but market analysts anticipate a definitive site announcement before the end of the third quarter.
