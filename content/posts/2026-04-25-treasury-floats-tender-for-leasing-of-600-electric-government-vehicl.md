By Celestine Nzioka

The Treasury has issued multimillion-shilling tenders this month for the leasing of 600 electric and hybrid vehicles, alongside approximately 70 DC fast chargers, for government use. This initiative marks a significant public-sector transition towards clean transport technology across various ministries, departments, and agencies.

## Government Accelerates E-Mobility Transition

The open national tender seeks a minimum of 600 units to be deployed under the Government Motor Vehicle Leasing Programme, benefiting entities such as the National Police Service (NPS), Kenya Prisons Service (KPS), National Government Administrative Services, and other ministries, departments, and agencies (MDAs). Procurement will be conducted through an open tendering method under a specially permitted procedure, allowing manufacturers, dealers, assemblers, and leasing firms to participate. Submissions are being accepted via the newly launched eGP system, outlining a diverse fleet mix including electric 4x2 and 4x4 passenger utility vehicles, double-cab electric pickups, and a smaller allocation of hybrid units.

### Coordinated Infrastructure Development

In a parallel move, the Treasury has issued a separate tender for the leasing of roughly 70 DC fast chargers, underscoring a coordinated strategy to ensure the vehicle rollout is supported by adequate charging infrastructure across government installations. This approach aims to facilitate a seamless transition for the incoming electric and hybrid fleet. The tender for the fast chargers closed on April 21, 2026.

### Strategic Leasing Model and Financial Requirements

The Government Motor Vehicle Leasing Programme, initiated in 2013, adopts a leasing model instead of outright purchase. This decision is projected to reduce upfront costs for the government while transferring maintenance and lifecycle risks to private sector providers. Tender documents stipulate that all bids must be accompanied by a Bid Security of Kenya Shillings Five Million Only (Ksh5,000,000.00) from a reputable bank regulated by the Central Bank of Kenya, valid for 30 days beyond the tender validity period. The tender for the vehicles closed on April 14, 2026.

### E-Mobility Push and Rising Consumption

This initiative aligns with a broader government push towards e-mobility, spearheaded by the Ministry of Transport. Transport Cabinet Secretary Davis Chirchir unveiled green number plates for all electric vehicles in February this year, signaling the country's commitment to reducing carbon emissions and accelerating cleaner transport adoption. This follows significant policy developments, including the launch of the National E-Mobility Policy in February 2026. While Mr. Chirchir's ministry has championed advancements, scrutiny has also been directed towards related policies, such as those highlighted in "Chirchir's Folly: NTSA Vehicle Inspection Hidden Costs Exposed."

Kenya Power reported a 188 per cent rise in electricity consumption linked to electric vehicles, with usage nearly tripling in 2025 as uptake grew among private and commercial users. Electricity consumption by electric vehicles rose to 8,433,437 kilowatt-hours (kWh) in 2025, up from 2,922,692 kWh recorded in 2024. This consumption surge generated Ksh125.9 million in revenue from EV charging in 2025, compared to Ksh64.8 million in the previous year. By the end of 2025, Kenya had registered over 35,000 electric vehicles.
