## The Tiered Tax Shift: How the eCitizen Fee Overhaul Changes Your Service Cost

For many months, Kenyans have questioned how the government would reform digital service costs following the judicial invalidation of the previous flat convenience fee. The National Treasury has finally introduced a new framework, transitioning from a uniform charge to a tiered structure that recalibrates how citizens pay for online government services.

The government is moving away from the former KES 50 fee, which applied to every digital transaction regardless of the service type. That model was struck down by the courts last year due to concerns regarding transparency. The proposed system ties convenience fees directly to the scale and complexity of the service provided, moving away from a blanket approach.

### Comparing the Old Flat Rate to the New Tiered Reality

Previously, every interaction with the eCitizen portal carried a flat KES 50 charge. Whether a user was renewing a simple permit or requesting a high-value land search, the cost remained identical. Critics frequently labeled this system as regressive. Under the new draft regulations, fees will range from as low as KES 5 for micro-services to a maximum of KES 100 for more intensive transactions. 

| Service Volume | Old Fee (Flat) | Proposed Fee (Tiered) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Low Value/Micro Service | KES 50 | KES 5 - 20 |
| Standard Government Service | KES 50 | KES 40 - 60 |
| High Value/Complex Processing | KES 50 | KES 80 - 100 |

This change has a clear impact on the average user. Those who frequently access basic digital services, such as checking NHIF status or downloading standard government forms, should see a decrease in costs. Conversely, users who rely on the platform for high-volume or complex processing tasks will likely experience a cost increase compared to the previous flat rate.

### Who Gains and Who Loses?

The new structure effectively segments users based on their digital activity. Casual users benefit from lower entry points, as the system subsidizes basic services. Meanwhile, higher costs are allocated to the complex and time-intensive applications that require more significant digital infrastructure.

This represents a departure from the one size fits all strategy that triggered significant public pushback. By implementing these tiers, the Treasury aims to stabilize revenue collection. Previously, private partners were estimated to collect between KES 100 million and KES 200 million monthly through the portal. Bringing these collections under state control is intended to remove the legal friction that resulted in the 2025 court ruling.

### Why It Matters Now

The move toward a formal regulatory framework is the Treasury's primary method for establishing a legal basis for digital tax collection. Since the court nullified the earlier arrangement, the government cannot legally impose convenience fees without these new regulations. 

For the general public, this signifies a shift toward a more structured and granular cost environment. If your daily business involves frequent government transactions, you should review which tier your common tasks fall into to ensure your budget is prepared for these adjusted fees.

### What to Watch Next

The transition into law requires a period of public participation. The Kenya School of Government has scheduled stakeholder sessions for May 22 and May 25, 2026. These meetings will allow for the scrutiny of specific service categorizations. If you have concerns regarding the classification of certain services as high volume or complex, these sessions serve as the appropriate venue to provide input.

The deadline for submitting written feedback to the Treasury is May 29, 2026. While the government focuses on these digital policy shifts, remember that regulatory adjustments are occurring across various sectors. For instance, authorities are also tightening rules regarding school vehicle safety, specifically concerning the NTSA crackdown on drug use by drivers. You should monitor the official Treasury website for the final gazetted list of fees, as the figures provided in the draft may be modified based on the feedback received during the late May consultations.
