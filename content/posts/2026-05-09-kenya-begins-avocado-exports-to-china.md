## Kenya's Avocado Exports to China and Your Wallet

As Kenya officially begins sending avocado shipments to China under a new zero-tariff agreement, many local shoppers are asking one question: will this rush for international profit make the fruit on our own tables more expensive?

The first batch of Kenyan avocados reached Chinese markets on May 8, signaling the start of a new era for local farmers. Under this arrangement, which went into effect on May 1, Kenya aims to bridge the trade deficit by moving large volumes of produce into the world's most populous market. While the government celebrates the influx of foreign exchange, the average Kenyan consumer must consider how this shift toward global markets affects the price of a simple avocado at the neighborhood kiosk.

### Will the price of avocados in Nairobi go up?

Economic logic suggests that when supply is diverted to high-paying international buyers, local availability often shrinks. When avocados become a premium export, traders naturally prioritize the Chinese market, where demand is high and profit margins are significant. This creates a supply squeeze here at home. If you have noticed higher prices for fruits at your local market recently, it is not merely a seasonal trend. It is a direct response to the global demand for Kenya's green gold. As long as the export market remains lucrative, local vendors may continue to hike prices to compete with what the international buyers are willing to pay.

### How does this change the lives of small-scale farmers?

For the person tending a small shamba in Murang'a or Kisii, this zero-tariff deal is a double-edged sword. On one hand, the removal of duties makes Kenyan produce highly competitive, offering a chance for farmers to earn significantly more than they would selling to local middlemen. If these farmers can meet the strict quality standards required for the Chinese market, their household income could rise substantially. However, the requirement for high-quality, export-grade fruit means many small farmers must invest more in fertilizer, pesticides, and post-harvest handling to qualify. The risk is that only the largest, most established farming operations will reap the rewards, leaving the small-scale grower stuck with lower quality fruit that nobody wants to buy.

### Why this trade-off matters for our economy

The national government is betting on these exports to bring in billions of shillings, helping to strengthen the value of the shilling and pay down national debt. This is essential for macro-economic stability. Yet, the trade-off is domestic inflation on food staples. We saw a similar dynamic in the local entertainment sector recently, where the push for global streaming dominance left many wondering if creators are actually gaining or if the platform owners take the lion's share, as discussed in [Kenya's Spotify Amapiano Powerhouse: Are Artists Really Cashing In, or is it All Just a Grand Illusion?](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenya-spotify-amapiano-powerhouse-artist-cash-illusion). Just as artists weigh the value of global reach against local recognition, the nation must decide how much of our own harvest to sell abroad. Prioritizing exports is excellent for the national treasury, but it can make the cost of living bite harder for the person whose salary does not scale with export profits.

### Why it matters now

The implementation of this deal is not just a diplomatic win; it is a shift in the local supply chain. With the first 6.9 tonnes already abroad, the precedent for moving large quantities of local staples like tea and coffee to China is set. Households should prepare for a period where export-oriented crops become more volatile in local price.

### What to watch next

Keep an eye on the market prices for other export-bound commodities like coffee and tea over the next quarter. If the government follows through on its plan to aggressively push these items into the Chinese market, expect further upward pressure on your household shopping basket.

When our farmers start selling to the world, the price of our daily breakfast is never the same again.
