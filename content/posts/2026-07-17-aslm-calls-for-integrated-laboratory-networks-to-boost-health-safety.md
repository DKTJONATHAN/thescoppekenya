## Closing the Diagnostic Gap: A Plea for One Health Integration

The 2026 mandate from the Africa Society for Laboratory Medicine serves as a critical catalyst for Kenya to harmonize its disconnected health surveillance protocols. By unifying diagnostic efforts across human, animal, and environmental sectors, the nation can transition from reactive crisis management toward a proactive framework for national health security.

Dear Minister,

Recent infectious disease patterns demonstrate that our current laboratory framework operates under severe self-imposed limitations. We maintain clinical diagnostic systems for human health that function entirely apart from the veterinary and wildlife surveillance networks. This systemic fragmentation is no longer merely an administrative inconvenience; it represents a primary vulnerability in our national defense.

### The Financial and Operational Cost of Siloed Diagnostics

The tendency to isolate data streams results in a hazardous delay in identifying emerging outbreaks. As mpox and cholera cases intensify in neighboring territories, our laboratory response frequently misses the broader epidemiological context because human clinicians and veterinary experts operate in disconnected bureaucratic spheres. Such isolation hinders our ability to identify zoonotic shifts before they escalate into widespread public health emergencies. While recent investments in specialized facilities have improved our physical capacity to handle high-risk pathogens, these individual projects remain insufficient if they do not contribute to a cohesive, national flow of real-time intelligence.

The ongoing crisis of antimicrobial resistance further illustrates the deficiencies inherent in our compartmentalized approach. Pathogens move fluidly between livestock operations and clinical settings. When veterinary antibiotic usage is monitored without reference to human clinical resistance patterns, we essentially allow resistant bacterial strains to propagate without oversight. Our current spending on reactive containment measures is disproportionately high; this expenditure could be significantly lowered if we synchronized diagnostic outputs to build a comprehensive, national map of microbial risks.

### Bridging the Infrastructure Divide

The 2026 ASLM mandate emphasizes that excellence in laboratory science is determined not only by the precision of a test result but by the velocity at which that data informs ministerial policy. Kenya possesses a wealth of scientific expertise, yet our researchers lack the unified platforms required to cross-reference findings between sectors. This represents a tangible failure in infrastructure planning. We require a modernized digital architecture that enables the rapid, secure exchange of information between the Kenya Medical Research Institute and our veterinary diagnostic counterparts.

This proposal does not advocate for the administrative merger of government departments. Rather, it demands the operational integration of diagnostic outputs. We need comprehensive, cross-disciplinary training programs that empower field technicians to recognize how a pathogen discovered in animal populations may serve as a precursor to human transmission. By centralizing our laboratory intelligence, we can move away from the current model of constant crisis response and toward a strategic system that treats the environment as our first line of defense.

### A Call for Decisive Investment

Minister, the era of limited pilot programs has reached its natural conclusion. The safety of the public relies on our capacity to identify the intricate links between environmental health and human wellness. I urge your office to prioritize the capital investment necessary for an integrated laboratory network that requires data interoperability as a fundamental condition for laboratory accreditation.

Will you commit to establishing a formal, cross-sector working group before the end of this year? Such a move would effectively dismantle these diagnostic barriers, ensuring that our health surveillance systems are as comprehensive and unified as the biological threats we are destined to face.

Respectfully,

Jonathan Mwaniki
