The Digital Dungeon: Cyberbullying Kenyan University Students Face Daily Our students face a digital battlefield, not a sanctuary. A recent study, published in February 2026, by the Collaborative Centre for Gender and Development in partnership with the University of Nairobi Women's Economic Empowerment Hub, ripped the lid off this ugly truth. It revealed a shocking 64.4% of female students in Kenyan universities have endured online harassment, with male students not far behind at 35.5%. This isn't idle banter; this is `technology-facilitated violence kenyan universities study` confirming a pervasive threat that often spills into real-world brutality. Facebook, the supposed hub of connection, has become a viper's nest. Research from 2020 by Ndiege et al. and Kwanya et al. confirmed the high prevalence of `cyberbullying kenyan university students facebook` face, with 76% of respondents experiencing victimisation. Shaming, impersonation, blackmail, exposure, and cyberstalking are the weapons of choice. And what have our esteemed university authorities done? Token gestures and empty awareness campaigns, if anything at all. They preach vigilance while the digital wolves feast on their flock. It is a dereliction of duty, plain and simple.

Kenyatta University's Policy: A Paper Tiger for Sexual Harassment Take Kenyatta University, for instance. They boast a "Sexual and Gender Based Violence Policy," updated in July 2021. It proclaims a "zero-tolerance" stance, a noble sentiment printed on paper, yet divorced from reality. The policy acknowledges "cases of SGBV are thus not uncommon" and that complaints against lecturers and staff have been received. So, what good is a policy if the harassment persists and victims still live in fear? It's a performative act, a bureaucratic shield against genuine accountability. The administration pretends to care, while the rot continues beneath the surface. This `sexual harassment kenyatta university policy` is clearly a paper tiger, toothless against the powerful perpetrators who roam free.

Maasai Mara University Student Safety: A Mirage? And what of `maasai mara university student safety`? While their Student Welfare Policy proudly declares a "key strategic priority" to provide a safe environment, the grim reality often paints a different picture. Past incidents, such as the 2021 police firing teargas at students protesting a road accident, or the closure of the campus in 2015 due to student clashes, highlight a volatile environment where "safety" often equates to crowd control rather than genuine protection from insidious threats like harassment. Their security policy talks of alarms and CCTV, but what good are gadgets when the threat lurks in the lecture halls and cyber spaces? It's a superficial layer of protection, a mirage of security, failing to address the deeper, more personal threats that erode student well-being. The focus on property and physical altercations often overshadows the silent torment of harassment. The excuses are as tired as they are pathetic. "Culture of silence," they wail. No, it is a culture of complicity, enforced by spineless administrators who prioritize institutional image over student welfare. The truth is, many universities operate like feudal fiefdoms, where powerful individuals are shielded, and victims are left to suffer in isolation. Jomo Kenyatta University of Agriculture and Technology (JKUAT) collaborated with Badili Africa in October 2023 to review its sexual harassment policy. This is a minuscule step, a band-aid on a gaping wound. Real change demands heads must roll, policies must be enforced with an iron fist, and perpetrators, regardless of their position, must face the full wrath of the law. Until then, our universities will remain breeding grounds for predators, and our students, mere prey.
