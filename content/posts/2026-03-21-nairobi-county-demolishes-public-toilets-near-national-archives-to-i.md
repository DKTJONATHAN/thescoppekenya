Nairobi County demolished public lavatories near the National Archives in the Central Business District on the night of Friday, March 20, 2026. The action, part of a campaign to improve drainage and restructure the capital, has resulted in immediate losses for traders and widespread public discontent over lost essential facilities.

## Redevelopment Impact on CBD Facilities

### Economic Repercussions for Traders

The demolition has severely impacted traders and shoe shiners operating from stalls within and around the now-destroyed lavatories. Many businesses are unable to function, resulting in significant financial losses. The structure, located between the Archives building and Mr Price, served as a vital hub for informal commerce, and its partial removal has left many without their livelihoods. Some parts of the structure remain standing, creating an uncertain operational environment for remaining businesses.

### County's Rationale for Demolition

Nairobi County reportedly carried out the demolition to restructure the capital and enhance drainage systems. This initiative follows a campaign launched on Wednesday, March 18, 2026, by Governor Johnson Sakaja, aimed at increasing drainage capacity after catastrophic flooding claimed lives on March 6. The county has been implementing broader structural changes in the area, including paving around the Archives building with cabro and relocating PSV bus stations previously situated between the National Archives and the Ambassador building. The section between the two buildings is now complete, leaving the area near Mr Price and the Archives pending until this recent demolition.

### Unanswered Questions and Public Concerns

Uncertainty surrounds the demolition, with no official word from Nairobi County regarding whether the removal is final or if the structure will be rebuilt with a new design. Efforts by Za Ndani to contact county officials for clarification proved unsuccessful. A primary concern for residents is the lack of public lavatories in a busy meeting area, forcing individuals to seek facilities at Agha Khan Walk or nearby private businesses. Existing lavatories directly opposite the Archives and in front of the former Hilton Hotel are now expected to manage increased foot traffic.

### Speculation on Underlying Motives

Curiously, a building barely five meters from the demolished toilets remains untouched, fueling viral speculation of personal feuds influencing the county's actions. The lavatories operated as private businesses, paying a fee to the county government for their operation. One Nairobi resident commented that "Cartels had made it their personal property, not the county government's." Such allegations highlight concerns about transparency and fairness in public service conduct, particularly in light of ongoing discussions about accountability in local governance.

### Public Toilet Management and Revenue

Nairobi County lists approximately 68 public toilets across the county, with about 18 concentrated in the immediate Central Business District. These facilities, once managed by the Nairobi Metropolitan Services (NMS), have since been transferred to youth and women groups. Toilets in prime locations, such as the Archives area, can generate substantial income, with estimates suggesting approximately KSh30,000 daily and up to KSh1.5 million monthly. Typical public toilets in the CBD generally yield KSh10,000 to KSh15,000 daily after covering basic operational costs.
