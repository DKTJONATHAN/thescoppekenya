## Kenya Revenue Authority Posts Record Collections Amid Narrow Base Risks

The Kenya Revenue Authority reported a record-breaking KES 2.844 trillion in tax revenue for the 2026 financial year, representing a 10.6 percent increase over the previous period. Despite this growth, the figures highlight a precarious fiscal structure, as the national treasury relies heavily on a small group of formal sectors to fund public expenditure.

This performance reveals a deepening structural imbalance within the economy. Only five sectors contribute 62 percent of the total tax haul, even though these industries represent just 27.4 percent of the national nominal GDP. This heavy concentration indicates that the formal economy is under immense pressure to sustain government spending while a vast informal sector remains largely untaxed. Although the state recently simplified administrative procedures, such as introducing same-day processing for identity cards in Nairobi to bolster civic services, the fundamental fiscal framework remains tied to a narrow cohort of established taxpayers.

### Digital Compliance and the Concentration of Fiscal Weight

The KRA has intensified its digital integration efforts, with eTIMS onboarding reaching 750,915 taxpayers by the end of June. This shift was a primary driver for an 8.5 percent rise in domestic VAT collections. However, the requirement to maintain complex technical infrastructure creates a significant compliance burden for small and medium-sized enterprises. As the government adjusts public sector wage bills, including recent salary hikes and promotion plans for teachers under the TSC, the necessity of expanding the tax base becomes more urgent. Relying on a formal labor market that accounted for only 15.3 percent of total employment in 2025 creates long-term sustainability risks for the national budget.

The Significant Economic Presence Tax has offered a new avenue for revenue, particularly regarding cross-border digital operations. Following the removal of the KES 5 million threshold, collections in this category doubled to KES 1.609 billion, allowing the state to capture value from foreign digital service providers that previously operated outside the local tax net. Similarly, the gaming and betting industry has emerged as a vital fiscal pillar. With 143 firms now fully integrated into the tax system, excise duty from this segment climbed by 24.9 percent. This trend underscores a deliberate strategy to utilize high-frequency consumer sectors to offset the relative stagnation seen in traditional wage-based revenue streams.

### Economic Sustainability and the Tax Gap

The divergence between corporation tax growth of 14.0 percent and the 6.7 percent growth in Pay-As-You-Earn (PAYE) highlights a cooling labor market. While businesses have bolstered their tax contributions through increased operational efficiency and stricter digital enforcement, income tax growth from individual employees has failed to keep pace. This gap suggests that high unemployment rates and stagnant wages in the formal sector are hindering the government's ability to extract further value from personal income. Unless the tax net is widened to incorporate the informal economy, the treasury will likely struggle to maintain its current revenue trajectory without imposing unsustainable burdens on already compliant businesses.

Looking toward the next fiscal cycle, the authority plans to prioritize the expansion of the eTIMS platform into the informal retail landscape. Officials intend to leverage advanced data analytics to pinpoint sectoral compliance gaps, with the ultimate objective of redistributing the tax burden away from the current narrow base. Future policy interventions are expected to modify thresholds for micro-enterprises, a move designed to reduce the reliance on the existing 62 percent sectoral concentration by the start of the 2027 financial year. By pivoting toward these untapped segments, the government hopes to stabilize its revenue growth while moderating the pressures placed on established industry leaders.
