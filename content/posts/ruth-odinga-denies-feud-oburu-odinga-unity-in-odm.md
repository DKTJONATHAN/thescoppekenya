Ruth Odinga Denies Feud with Oburu Odinga Amidst Deepening ODM Divisions Kisumu Woman Representative Ruth Odinga has publicly dismissed claims of a personal feud with Orange Democratic Movement (ODM) Acting Party Leader Oburu Odinga, reiterating her calls for internal party unity. Her statements underscore the profound internal struggles currently plaguing ODM as it navigates a critical post-Raila Odinga era. Ruth Odinga, who also serves as the party's Deputy Organising Secretary, clarified her recent critiques of internal party operations should not be misinterpreted as disrespect towards her elder brother. The assertions came on Monday, March 2, as the ODM grapples with factionalism and a contentious debate surrounding its Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) with the ruling United Democratic Alliance (UDA).

The Intricate Web of ODM Leadership and the Question of Unity in ODM The very foundation of ODM's current leadership structure is a subject of intense scrutiny, casting a long shadow over calls for **Unity in ODM**. Following the passing of long-time party leader Raila Odinga on October 16, 2025, Siaya Senator Oburu Odinga was appointed as the Acting Party Leader. His formal swearing-in on November 13, 2025, after National Governing Council approval, solidified his temporary hold on the party's helm. This swift transition, however, has not been without its detractors, with some members, including Embakasi East MP Babu Owino, questioning the legitimacy and transparency of the process. The upcoming National Delegates Conference (NDC) on March 27, 2026, is poised to be a battleground where Oburu Odinga has openly invited critics to challenge his leadership.

Ruth Odinga Denies Feud: Unmasking the Factions While **Ruth Odinga Denies Feud** with Oburu, her advocacy for unity starkly highlights the deep ideological fissures within the party. Two prominent factions, "Linda Ground" and "Linda Wananchi," have emerged, representing divergent visions for ODM's future. The "Linda Ground" faction, reportedly aligned with Oburu Odinga, advocates for continued cooperation with President William Ruto's UDA, framing it as a pragmatic approach to development. Conversely, the "Linda Wananchi" group, spearheaded by Nairobi Senator Edwin Sifuna, positions itself as the true opposition, focused on citizens' issues and rejecting closer ties with the ruling party. This ideological schism is not merely a contest of personalities but a fundamental dispute over the party's core identity and strategic direction in the national political landscape.

The Contentious UDA-ODM MoU and its Implications for Unity in ODM A critical flashpoint in the ongoing internal wrangles is the Memorandum of Understanding signed between UDA and ODM on March 7, 2025. This MoU, anchored on a 10-point agenda linked to the National Dialogue Committee (NADCO) recommendations, is set to reach its due date on March 7, 2026. Senator Sifuna has vehemently opposed any extension, asserting the agreement was deliberately structured without an extension clause and would require the signature of the late Raila Odinga to be prolonged. This position is in direct conflict with Oburu Odinga's faction, which views March 7, 2026, as an anniversary and a review point, rather than an automatic termination of cooperation. The debate over the MoU's future extends beyond its immediate political implications, touching on the very essence of governance and public accountability. While the government has reaffirmed its commitment to implement the NADCO report, the discord within ODM risks diverting attention from these crucial national reforms. Such internal political instability can have far-reaching consequences, echoing challenges seen in other critical sectors. Similarly, a fractured ODM could hinder effective engagement on national issues, leaving Kenyans vulnerable to policy inconsistencies and stalled progress.

The Path Forward for a United ODM The calls for **Unity in ODM** by figures like Ruth Odinga, who also publicly shielded Edwin Sifuna from an ouster bid as Secretary-General, highlight the precarious state of affairs within the party. Sifuna's position, temporarily secured by a Political Parties Disputes Tribunal (PPDT) order until a March 12, 2026, hearing, further complicates the leadership landscape. The resolution of these internal power struggles and the clarification of the party's stance on critical national alliances will be pivotal for ODM's ability to remain a formidable political force ahead of the 2027 general elections. The party's ability to consolidate its ranks and present a cohesive front will determine its leverage in future political negotiations and its capacity to genuinely serve the interests of its constituents.
