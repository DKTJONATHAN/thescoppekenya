The Kenyan shilling strengthened against the U.S. dollar this week, reaching 129.53 per dollar on April 9, according to the Central Bank of Kenya. This modest gain occurred despite the CBK's warning that inflation could peak at 6.2 per cent by July 2026 due to escalating crude oil prices.

Economic Stability Amidst Global Pressures

Currency Resilience and Reserves The shilling exchanged at 129.53 per U.S. dollar on April 9, demonstrating a slight strengthening from 129.99 recorded on April 2, as reported in the Central Bank's weekly bulletin. This modest movement signifies a measure of stability that policymakers strive to maintain amidst global commodity price volatility. The Central Bank of Kenya (CBK) attributes this currency resilience to "diversified foreign exchange inflows, increased confidence in the economy and adequate foreign exchange reserves". As of April 9, foreign exchange reserves stood at USD 13,316 million, equivalent to 5.7 months of import cover. This level is well above the statutory minimum of four months that the central bank is required to maintain, providing a critical buffer against external shocks.

Rising Oil Prices and Inflationary Pressures Despite the shilling's recent gains, the nation's economic outlook is being tested by surging crude oil prices. These increases are linked to the escalating U.S.-Israel-Iran war, with the Strait of Hormuz closure disrupting shipping and curtailing supply from major oil-producing nations. Murban crude, a key benchmark, rose to USD 90.33 per barrel on April 9, up from USD 89.45 just a week earlier on April 1. This marks a dramatic increase from the USD 60.6 per barrel low recorded in late 2025. On Tuesday, the public will learn how much they will have to contend with at the pump, with many expecting fuel prices to rise. Such adjustments can have broader implications for transport costs, a subject often reviewed by bodies like the National Transport and Safety Authority, which recently published insights regarding [NTSA Reviews Traffic Offences and Speed Limits Following Court Ruling](https://zandani.co.ke/article/ntsa-reviews-traffic-offences-and-speed-limits-following-court-rulin). The CBK's Monetary Policy Committee (MPC), which convened on April 8, warned that international oil prices have "risen sharply and remained volatile due to supply disruptions and elevated uncertainties attributed to the war in the Middle East." Damage to oil infrastructure in the region has further compounded the supply crunch. The central bank's projections indicate that without the oil price shock, overall inflation would remain comfortably between 4.5 and 5.0 per cent through the coming year. However, with the current trajectory, the CBK projects inflation peaking at 6.2 per cent by July 2026. While still within the upper limit of its 5±2.5 per cent target band, this would represent the highest level projected since the country's inflationary spike of 2023. Kenya's headline inflation stood at 4.4 per cent in March 2026, a marginal increase from 4.3 per cent in February, remaining below the midpoint of the target range. The MPC anticipates inflation to remain anchored, supported by appropriate monetary policy actions, expected stability in food prices attributed to favourable weather conditions, and a broadly stable exchange rate. Nevertheless, the committee acknowledged the real and growing risk, emphasizing "a need to monitor any second-round effects of the recent increase in international oil prices on overall inflation." This signals the bank's readiness to intervene if rising fuel costs begin to translate into broader price pressures across the economy.
