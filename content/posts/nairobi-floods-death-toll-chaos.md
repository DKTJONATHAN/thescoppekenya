<h2>Nairobi Drowns: The Predictable Tragedy of Heavy Rain</h2>
Nairobi has been plunged into utter chaos and despair as relentless heavy rainfall has caused widespread flooding, resulting in a staggering death toll of at least 23 people and leaving the capital utterly gridlocked. The deluge has submerged major roads, halted transport, and exposed the city's perennial unpreparedness for the long rains.

The city's notorious traffic, already a nightmare on a good day, became a full-blown crisis, stranding commuters for hours as public service vehicles struggled to navigate the impassable, waterlogged routes. Residents were left wading through knee-deep water or forming endless queues at bus stops, drenched and desperate for any escape from the deluge. The Kenya Red Cross Society and Kenya Defense Forces units have been deployed, but even emergency responders are battling the "situation on what used to be roads," as Secretary-General Ahmed Idris starkly put it.

<h3>A Hero Lost, Warnings Ignored</h3>
Among the heartbreaking fatalities reported amidst this chaos is Samuel Wanyonyi Wangila, a veteran aqua rescue volunteer who tragically lost his life on March 3, 2026, during a mission along the Mbagathi River. Wangila, celebrated for over 15 years of selfless service and a recipient of a Special Recognition Award in 2025, died while responding to a distress call involving a drowning. His sacrifice highlights the sheer danger faced by those on the front lines, battling a disaster that many argue could have been mitigated.

The Kenya Meteorological Department had, predictably, issued warnings of heavy rainfall across Nairobi and surrounding counties between March 3 and March 9, 2026, with some areas forecast to receive up to 50 millimeters of rain within 24 hours. Yet, despite these advance warnings, the city's drainage systems were quickly overwhelmed, causing water to accumulate rapidly and turning roads into rivers. This recurring cycle of warnings followed by widespread devastation echoes similar failures, such as when [Hundreds Affected as Night Fire Engulfs Building in Kangemi](https://zandani.co.ke/article/hundreds-affected-night-fire-engulfs-building-kangemi), where investigations were always underway, but solutions remained elusive.

<h3>The Annual Monsoon Melodrama</h3>
Flooding during seasonal rainfall is not some unforeseen calamity; it's an annual melodrama in Kenya, consistently leading to displacement, infrastructure damage, and widespread transport disruptions. The authorities’ repeated calls for residents to "remain vigilant" near flood-prone areas ring hollow when the basic infrastructure to prevent such catastrophes remains woefully inadequate. It's the same old story, year in, year out: the rains come, the city drowns, lives are lost, and we pretend to be shocked. When will Nairobi move past this predictable, tragic script?
