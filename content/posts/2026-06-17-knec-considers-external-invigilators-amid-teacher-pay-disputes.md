## The Gig Economy Invades the Examination Hall KNEC considers external invigilators as teacher pay disputes moun is the central subject of the update.

Teachers across the country are expressing fury at the suggestion that their role in national examinations is becoming obsolete, sparking fears that the sanctity of the KCSE could be sacrificed for a temporary labor fix. As educators weigh a potential boycott, the shift toward outsourced supervision threatens to dismantle the long standing professional bond between the classroom and the examination board.

The Kenya National Examinations Council is currently considering a radical overhaul of its invigilation model, moving away from its traditional reliance on Teachers Service Commission staff. This proposal emerges as a direct response to a looming crisis, with KUPPET ordering its members to stop all examination related duties until arrears from the 2025 cycle are fully cleared. While Treasury Cabinet Secretary John Mbadi has promised to release 1.5 billion shillings to settle these debts, the trust has already fractured. The proposed pivot mimics the recruitment style used by the IEBC, viewing examination management as a task that can be fulfilled by any temporary worker, rather than a pedagogical duty requiring professional oversight.

### A Professional Decoupling

The transition from a professional teacher-led system to a gig-style staffing model introduces significant risks to the security of national tests. Seasoned educators argue that the rigor required for a multi-week examination period cannot be replicated by casual laborers hired on a contract basis. Unlike professional teachers who are bound by professional codes of conduct and institutional loyalty, external invigilators would likely operate without the same stakes in the integrity of the results. Security analysts point out that replacing a vetted teaching workforce with a transient pool of workers makes it harder to track accountability, especially when high-pressure exam integrity is at risk. Much like the frustrations seen recently with fake job advertisements circulating at Kenya Railways, the public is wary of mass recruitment drives that lack transparency and professional vetting protocols.

### Voices from the Frontlines

The discourse on the ground suggests that the proposed change is seen as a devaluation of the teaching profession.

Samuel, a secondary school teacher in Nairobi, argues that teaching is not just about showing up to monitor desks. He believes that removing teachers from the exam process ignores the rapport built with students, which is essential for maintaining order and discipline during the most stressful weeks of a student's academic life. To him, the exam is an extension of the learning process, not a logistical chore that can be outsourced to the lowest bidder.

On the administrative side, a retired school principal expressed deep concern regarding the vetting process for these proposed non-teaching invigilators. She highlighted that without the existing TSC framework, the council will struggle to ensure that every supervisor is cleared to handle sensitive materials. She questions who will take responsibility when things go wrong, noting that a hired casual laborer will not have the same career investment as a teacher who faces a disciplinary committee for lapses.

Meanwhile, representatives from student advocacy groups are worried about the classroom environment. They argue that the presence of unfamiliar faces in the exam room will create unnecessary anxiety for candidates who are already struggling with the pressures of the modern curriculum. For them, the shift feels less like an administrative efficiency and more like a disruption to the familiar support system they rely on during final exams.

### The Financial Fallout

Beyond the immediate administrative nightmare, the loss of exam invigilation pay represents a significant economic blow to the teaching workforce. For many, these payments act as an annual buffer that helps cover end-of-year expenses. By decoupling this income stream from the teaching profession, the council risks further demotivating a workforce that is already stretched thin. This struggle for fair compensation mirrors the broader financial anxieties seen in other sectors, such as when public figures navigate the complexities of reality television earnings or face allegations of illicit wealth accumulation. If the government proceeds with this gig-economy model, it may find that the cost of professionalizing casual staff far outweighs the savings they hope to achieve by sidelining current teachers. As the standoff continues, the mood in the staffrooms is one of quiet defiance, waiting to see if the proposed payment of 1.5 billion shillings will be enough to stop the exodus from the examination centers.
