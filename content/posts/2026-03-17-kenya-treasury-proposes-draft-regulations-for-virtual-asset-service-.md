The National Treasury on Tuesday, March 17, published the draft Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASP) Regulations 2026, introducing stringent measures to regulate the country's burgeoning crypto industry. The regulations aim to enhance consumer protection, combat financial crimes such as money laundering, and provide regulatory clarity for businesses operating within this sector.

New Regulatory Framework for Digital Assets The proposed VASP Regulations 2026, issued pursuant to the Virtual Asset Service Providers Act, 2025 (Act No. 20 of 2025), seek to establish a legal framework for licensing and supervising virtual asset service providers in and from the country. This move by the Treasury underscores a commitment to fostering a secure and transparent digital finance ecosystem.

Licensing and Operational Mandates Under the draft regulations, only locally incorporated companies are eligible for VASP licensing. Foreign companies must first obtain a compliance certificate before proceeding with a license application. All providers are mandated to maintain a physical office within the country, and directors alongside senior officers will undergo a comprehensive background and competence assessment by regulatory bodies. These requirements are intended to ensure robust oversight and accountability within the sector.

Financial Safeguards and Stablecoin Provisions The draft rules impose strict reserve requirements for issuers of virtual assets. Reserves must be held in highly liquid, low-risk instruments, including cash, central bank deposits, short-term government securities with maturities not exceeding 90 days, and repurchase agreements with maturities of no more than seven days. Stablecoin issuers, specifically, are required to hold at least 30 percent of customer funds in segregated accounts within Kenyan commercial banks, with the remainder invested in secure, low-risk assets classified as high-quality liquid assets, minimizing exposure to market, credit, and concentration risks.

Transaction Fees and Prohibited Activities New fees are proposed for digital asset platforms. A 0.05 percent transaction fee is suggested for token issuance platforms, to be borne by each party involved in a trade. Additionally, entities seeking approval for virtual asset offerings will be required to pay a fee equivalent to 0.5 percent of the value of a successful offering. The regulations also explicitly prohibit certain high-risk activities, such as facilitating transactions designed to conceal the identities of participants. These measures aim to enhance financial integrity and prevent illicit activities within the digital asset space.

Public Consultation and Industry Impact The legislation provides crucial safety rails for an estimated USD 1.2 trillion (Ksh155 trillion) in virtual assets held within the country, assuring investors and companies of a safe environment for new opportunities. The National Treasury has initiated public participation forums in major towns, including Nairobi, Mombasa, Kisumu, and Eldoret, to gather feedback on the proposed regulations. Public comments are invited through April, after which the Treasury will review submissions before finalizing the rules. This engagement process aligns with broader government efforts to ensure comprehensive development across various sectors, similar to the establishment of frameworks like the Railway Development Levy Fund for infrastructure advancement. The anticipated adoption of these rules is expected to significantly reshape operations for crypto businesses, particularly those dealing with stablecoins and tokenized assets, aligning the country with evolving global standards in digital finance.
