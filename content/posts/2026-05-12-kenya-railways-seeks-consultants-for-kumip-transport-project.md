## Kenya Railways and the Ksh65 Billion Urban Mobility Illusion

Kenya Railways is calling for consultants to spend 15 months planning how to spend Ksh65 billion on urban mobility. While the project promises to bridge the transit gap across five counties, it ignores the chaotic, indispensable matatu network that already keeps Nairobi moving.

The notice issued today, May 12, 2026, invites bidders to sharpen their pens for the Kenya Urban Mobility Improvement Project. On the surface, it is a grand vision of integrated rail and bus networks. Underneath, it is a familiar script: hire expensive consultants, wait for a report, and watch as the actual commuters in Nairobi, Kiambu, Machakos, Kajiado, and Murang-a continue to fight for space on rusted minibuses.

### The Integration Policy Gap

The core of the issue is not engineering. Kenya Railways has plenty of tracks and a growing fleet, much like the logistical focus seen with the MV Uhuru I and II projects on Lake Victoria. Yet, these assets remain isolated islands in a sea of logistical dysfunction. The current plan assumes that a formal, top-down rail system can simply overlay the existing, informal matatu sector. This is a fantasy that has haunted every Nairobi transport study for the last two decades.

History is littered with similar white papers that promised seamless connectivity but delivered nothing but paper trails. By focusing on a 15-month consultancy period starting in October, the state is effectively kicking the can down the road while the streets remain gridlocked. The matatu industry is not just a nuisance to be integrated; it is a complex, responsive, and private-sector-led ecosystem. Official plans rarely account for the fact that these informal operators provide the exact flexibility that rigid, government-run rail schedules cannot replicate. Unless the Ksh65 billion project moves beyond mere bureaucratic expansion and acknowledges the survival-based economics of the matatu sector, it will likely become another stranded asset, much like public infrastructure projects that get left behind when funding priorities shift, as seen with the recent exclusion of stadiums from national funding.

### The Window for Elite Capture

A 15-month timeline for consultancy is a lifetime in Kenyan politics. It is a golden period for bureaucratic maneuvering, where the primary objective becomes the justification of the consultant fees rather than the actual mobility of the mwananchi. When the government invites firms to study mobility, it often creates a buffer zone where accountability is deferred. The World Bank funding adds a layer of global prestige, but it also creates a feedback loop where success is measured by the completion of reports, not the reduction of travel times for a worker commuting from Ruiru to the city center.

Why does this matter now? Because the commuter nightmare is hitting a breaking point. Every shilling poured into a plan that ignores the reality of the street is a shilling stolen from a citizen who spends four hours a day stuck in traffic. If the consultancy does not prioritize physical infrastructure that serves the informal transit hubs, the Ksh65 billion will vanish into administrative costs and high-level meetings, leaving the commuter exactly where they are today.

### What to watch next

The real test for this project is not the selection of consultants, but the terms of reference. Watch for whether these firms are tasked with working alongside matatu saccos or if they are mandated to force a top-down structure that the market will inevitably reject. Any plan that views the current informal transit system as a problem to be erased, rather than a partner to be upgraded, is already destined to fail. 

If the government can secure billions for rail integration but cannot guarantee a seat for a commuter on a bus in the periphery, whose mobility is this project actually designed to improve?
