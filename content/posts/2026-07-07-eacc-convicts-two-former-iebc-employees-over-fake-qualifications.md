## Degrees of Deception: The Public Anger Over Fake Credentials EACC Convicts Two Former IEBC Employees Over Fake Qualifications is the central subject of the update.

Kenyan graduates are venting raw frustration as the Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission cleans house, revealing how years of meritless imposters have climbed the public sector ladder while qualified professionals remain sidelined by institutional fraud.

The Milimani Anti-Corruption Court recently finalised cases against two former IEBC employees who built careers on foundations of sand. One individual was caught having used a forged KCSE certificate to secure a clerical role dating back to 2012, while another falsified a degree from Moi University to climb the promotion ladder. The financial cost is staggering: one was ordered to pay fines totalling Ksh 1.5 million after pocketing over Ksh 2.6 million in salary, while the other faces a Ksh 110,000 fine for their Ksh 1.6 million illegal earnings. These convictions are part of an aggressive EACC campaign targeting the rot of academic dishonesty that has plagued the civil service for years.

### The Human Cost of the Paper Chase

Walking through the streets of Nairobi, the reaction is not surprise, but exhaustion. For the thousands of university graduates who spent sleepless nights and millions of shillings in fees, these revelations feel like a slap in the face. Many point to the systemic inequality where a fake document carries more weight than genuine effort.

One unemployed graduate from the University of Nairobi mentioned that seeing these imposters thrive while youth unemployment hits record highs confirms a deep-seated suspicion that the playing field was never level. Another job seeker noted that the sheer ease with which these individuals bypassed vetting processes suggests a culture where connections outweigh capability. A third perspective from a private sector employee highlighted that this is not just about the IEBC; it is about the thousands of public sector roles filled by people who have never set foot in a lecture hall, effectively devaluing the hard-earned degrees held by legitimate job seekers.

### Institutional Trust and the Ripple Effect

Beyond the social resentment, the EACC’s crackdown is beginning to shake the foundation of public sector confidence. Experts observe that academic fraud acts as a silent poison to institutional integrity. When public servants achieve their positions through deception, the entire quality of service delivery suffers. There is a palpable fear that these two convictions are merely the tip of an iceberg.

If the EACC continues to audit the educational backgrounds of long-serving government employees, the ripple effect could be massive. Some analysts suggest that this campaign might eventually force a total overhaul of how credentials are verified at the point of hiring. For those holding genuine qualifications, there is a glimmer of hope that the era of the shortcut may be reaching a bitter end, though many remain skeptical about how deep the rot actually goes.

### Paying the Price for Shortcuts

The recent fines imposed by the court-ranging from Ksh 50,000 to over a million-serve as a sharp reminder that the law is finally catching up with the paper chase. However, critics argue that these financial penalties barely scratch the surface of the total economic damage caused. While the EACC continues to chase down these individuals, the focus is shifting toward how such blatant forgeries went unnoticed during initial recruitment phases.

Much like recent warnings from [HELB regarding fake loan application notices](https://zandani.co.ke/article/helb-warns-students-over-fake-kmtc-loan-application-notice), the prevalence of fraudulent documents is forcing a harder look at the digital gatekeepers of our institutions. The system is finally being forced to reckon with the reality that an academic degree, once the gold standard for advancement, has been weaponised by those looking to skip the queue. For the thousands currently sending out applications, the promise of a merit-based system feels distant, overshadowed by the reality of those who chose to forge their way to the top.

The public mood remains heavy with a mix of vindication and lingering bitterness, as citizens wait to see if this is a genuine cleansing or just a temporary optics exercise in a sea of systemic deception.
