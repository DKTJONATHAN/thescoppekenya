## Casemiro's Farewell: A High Price for Fleeting Glory
Manchester United's recent win over Brentford saw Casemiro lauded by teammates and staff. Yet, the effusive praise for a player about to depart, combined with the substantial investment in his brief tenure, exposes a concerning reliance on short-term fixes and deeper tactical deficiencies at Old Trafford.

### The £70 Million Farewell
It's rather quaint, isn't it, to hear such effusive praise for a player on his way out the door? Casemiro, the "massive boost" and "clinical" finisher, according to Harry Maguire, is reportedly set to depart Old Trafford at season's end. This is the same Casemiro for whom Manchester United shelled out a staggering £70 million in 2022. While his nine Premier League goals this season are commendable for a defensive midfielder, one must question the long-term strategic wisdom of such a substantial investment in a player who, at 34, was always destined for a brief stay. Was it truly a shrewd piece of business, or merely a stop-gap measure that provided isolated moments of brilliance without fundamentally transforming the team's trajectory?

### Set-Piece Scramble, Not Systemic Strength
Maguire's revelation that United's league position would be "nowhere near" its current standing without Casemiro's goals is a damning indictment, not of Casemiro, but of United's broader attacking strategy. It suggests a team heavily dependent on set-piece heroics from a defensive midfielder and a central defender - Maguire himself - rather than consistent, fluid offensive play from their designated attackers. The idea that having "two big targets" in the box "frees us both up" sounds less like sophisticated tactical innovation and more like a hopeful punt. While effective against Brentford on a Monday evening, relying on such a rudimentary approach for sustained success against top-tier opposition is merely a testament to tactical limitations, not strengths.

### Convenient Compliments, Lingering Questions
The chorus of praise from Maguire and interim manager Michael Carrick following a 2-1 victory cannot mask the underlying narrative: Casemiro is leaving. These glowing reviews, coming just as his tenure nears its end, feel less like genuine revelations and more like a convenient narrative constructed around a departing asset. It’s easy to laud a player for isolated contributions when the larger financial commitment and his imminent departure present a clear, if uncomfortable, truth about United's transfer policy and squad planning. The question isn't whether Casemiro delivered moments of quality - he clearly did - but whether the significant outlay was justified for a fleeting impact and if the club has truly progressed beyond relying on individual flashes rather than a coherent, long-term vision.
