## Solving Kenya's Cold Case Crisis Through Regional Forensic Training

The Ethiopian Federal Police recently opened the Dr. Workneh Gebeyehu Scholarship Program, inviting Kenyan law enforcement to master forensic science at the Crime Investigation Academy in Sandafa. This initiative aims to address deep seated technical shortcomings in local criminal investigations.

The reality for many families seeking justice in Kenya is a frustrating cycle of stalled investigations and dismissed charges. Often, the barrier is not a lack of effort, but a fundamental failure in how crime scenes are processed. When evidence is contaminated or mishandled by officers who lack specialized technical training, the prosecution cannot build a case that stands up in court. The discrepancy between local evidence handling and international forensic standards remains a primary reason why suspects walk free. By sending officers to Ethiopia for rigorous, standardized training, Kenya gains a pathway to fix these procedural gaps.

### How does this address the local police performance gap?

Current investigations in many Kenyan precincts suffer from a reliance on traditional, non-scientific methods of evidence collection. Officers are frequently tasked with securing scenes without the necessary knowledge of modern DNA preservation, digital data extraction, or toxicology standards. When crime scenes are compromised at the initial stage, no amount of later effort can rectify the loss of admissible evidence. This scholarship program acts as a targeted intervention for these specific failures. It introduces officers to the exact methodologies utilized by the Ethiopian Federal Police, which prioritize the scientific integrity of a case from the very first minute an officer steps onto the scene.

### Can forensic education lead to higher conviction rates?

Conviction rates are directly tied to the quality of the investigative file presented to the Office of the Director of Public Prosecutions. When officers lack the forensic literacy to gather solid, irrefutable data, prosecutors are forced to build cases on shaky ground. By adopting the Ethiopian approach, which emphasizes meticulous evidence documentation and laboratory readiness, Kenyan police can begin to bridge this divide. Increased competence in these areas means that prosecutors receive higher quality files that require less backtracking and fewer expert witness interventions, ultimately clearing the backlog of cases that currently haunt local courts.

This shift toward evidence-based policing is part of a broader push for professionalization across the IGAD region. Just as advancements in medical infrastructure are required to fix [why Kenya urgently needs more radiologists for better healthcare](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenya-radiology-shortage-diagnostic-healthcare), the justice sector requires a similar technical upgrade. If local law enforcement continues to rely on legacy investigative techniques, the gap between crime and punishment will only widen. While this program represents a diplomatic collaboration between IGAD member states, its true utility lies in the technical skills that individual officers will bring back to their stations.

These skills represent a move away from the procedural carelessness that has defined too many cold cases in the country. If the program succeeds in producing officers who can effectively manage digital footprints or preserve microscopic evidence, the impact on public trust in the police force will be significant. The transition from reactive, intuition-based policing to a proactive, science-led model is the only way to ensure that criminals are held accountable by the weight of forensic truth rather than the uncertainty of witness testimony alone. As the region coordinates its security efforts, the emphasis on academic and technical rigor at the Sandafa academy provides a measurable benchmark for how Kenyan law enforcement might eventually modernize its approach to solving crime.

Better science in the courtroom starts with better habits at the crime scene.
