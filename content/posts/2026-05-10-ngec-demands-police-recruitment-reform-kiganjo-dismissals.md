## Police Recruitment Reform in Kenya

The dismissal of recruits from Kiganjo Training College has sparked a national conversation about fairness and administrative oversight. Many Kenyans are left wondering how individuals pass initial screening stages only to be disqualified months into their expensive, taxpayer-funded training.

The National Gender and Equality Commission (NGEC) has now intervened, demanding a complete overhaul of how the National Police Service (NPS) handles its vetting pipeline. This situation highlights a recurring gap between the criteria advertised during recruitment drives and the reality of how these standards are enforced once training begins.

### Is the current vetting process actually working?

The short answer is no. The recent events at Kiganjo reveal a reactive system rather than a proactive one. When thousands of applicants show up at recruitment centers across the country, the NPS relies on initial documentation checks and physical fitness tests. However, the discovery of 18 recruits with forged academic certificates, 10 recruits with previous criminal records, and two individuals using falsified national identification cards proves that this early vetting is failing to catch significant discrepancies.

When the system waits until an individual is already inside a high-level training facility to discover that their documents are fake or their history is compromised, the state loses significant resources. This suggests that the internal vetting standards lack the depth required to protect the integrity of the institution before candidates are admitted to the college. Instead of a robust pre-entry verification process, the NPS appears to be utilizing the training period as a secondary, albeit messy, screening phase.

### Why are so many recruits being dismissed mid-training?

Beyond the criminal and academic fraud cases, the dismissal of 18 pregnant recruits has drawn sharp criticism from the NGEC regarding human rights and constitutional compliance. While the NPS cites health and safety protocols as the primary reason for these dismissals, the commission argues that the current policies are discriminatory and outdated.

The recruitment advertisement typically outlines strict physical and medical requirements, yet these criteria often fail to account for the evolving legal interpretations of workplace rights. When a recruit is dismissed halfway through their tenure for a medical condition or pregnancy, it raises questions about the transparency of the medical evaluation performed at the start. If the screening process at the county level were as thorough as the public is led to believe, these issues would ideally be identified during the initial application phase. The fact that they are surfacing now points to a broken pipeline that penalizes recruits for failures in administrative oversight.

### Why it matters now

For ordinary Kenyans, this is about more than just police policy. It is about how the state manages public funds and talent. Every recruit who is dismissed for a preventable reason, such as a forged certificate, represents a lost opportunity for a qualified candidate who was turned away. Taxpayers are essentially paying to train individuals who should have been flagged weeks or months prior. As the global shift toward advanced technology requires better systems, similar to the efficiency seen in the [Samsung mass production of HBM4 memory](https://zandani.co.ke/article/samsung-begins-mass-production-of-hbm4-memory-for-nvidia-ai-systems), the NPS must adopt more rigorous, data-driven vetting to prevent such wastage.

### What to watch next

The NGEC is pushing for a formal policy revision to ensure that recruitment standards are both constitutional and consistently applied. Watch for how the National Police Service responds to these recommendations. If they agree to modernize their vetting protocols, it could mean a shift toward a more transparent, digitized, and fair recruitment cycle. Failure to reform will likely lead to continued legal challenges and a loss of public trust in the professional standards of the security services.

A broken vetting pipeline is a costly expense that the country can no longer afford to ignore.
