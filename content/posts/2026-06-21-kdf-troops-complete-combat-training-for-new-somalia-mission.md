## The Mirage of Security at the Frontier KDF troops complete combat training for new Somalia mission is the central subject of the update.

KDF soldiers are emerging from the School of Infantry in Isiolo, battle-hardened and prepped for the transition from ATMIS to AUSSOM. Yet, while the military prepares to project power into Somalia, the government persists in keeping the Mandera and Garissa border points locked, strangling the local economy under the guise of an indefinite security lockdown that has outlived its tactical relevance.

### From Linda Nchi to a Perpetual Standoff

Operation Linda Nchi began in 2011 with the promise of creating a buffer zone to insulate Kenya from Al-Shabaab. Fifteen years later, the mission has morphed into a permanent fixture of regional foreign policy, yet the domestic reality remains frozen in time. The military is evolving, shifting its mandate toward the African Union Support and Stabilisation Mission in Somalia (AUSSOM), which ostensibly focuses on long-term institutional stability and training Somali forces.

However, the disconnect between these high-level international objectives and the ground truth in Northern Kenya is stark. While KDF commanders in Isiolo are drilled on the nuances of counter-insurgency and regional stabilization, the residents of border towns are still governed by the same restrictive policies established in the immediate aftermath of the 2011 incursion. This is a strategic schizophrenia: the state spends billions to project stability abroad while simultaneously treating its own border regions as captive, economically irrelevant zones.

### The Economic Cost of the Border Silence

The indefinite closure of the Kenya-Somalia border is not a temporary security measure anymore; it is an administrative policy that has hollowed out the regional middle class. Merchants in Mandera and Garissa, once the hubs of a thriving cross-border trade, now exist in a state of suspended animation. Official reports tout the success of border sealing in preventing illicit movement, but the reality for the average trader is a permanent shuttering of their livelihoods.

The security apparatus argues that the frontier must remain a ghost town to protect the interior. Yet, the persistent presence of militant activity in the very areas the government claims to be shielding suggests that the border closure is less a security triumph and more a failure of intelligence and local integration. Much like the noise surrounding [Ruth Odinga Hits Back at John Mbadi Over ODM Ownership Dispute](https://zandani.co.ke/article/ruth-odinga-clashes-with-john-mbadi-over-odm-party-ownership), the political bickering in Nairobi distracts from the tangible collapse of governance in the periphery. While the elite trade barbs over party registers, the border counties remain trapped in a 2011 mindset that refuses to acknowledge the changed nature of the threat.

### Training for a Reality That Never Arrives

The specialized drills in Isiolo suggest a military that is highly capable and ready for the shift to AUSSOM. The combat coordination exercises are precise, reflecting a force that has learned the hard lessons of the last decade. But the effectiveness of these soldiers is being undermined by a state that lacks a coherent end game. A mission in Somalia is only as successful as the policy framework that supports it back home. If the military continues to focus on offensive stabilization while the civilian administration doubles down on isolationist policies, the cycle of insurgency will never be broken.

The transition to AUSSOM is being sold as a modernization of Kenya's commitment to regional peace. But if the strategy does not evolve to include the economic rehabilitation of the North Eastern border, it is merely repeating the errors of the past under a different acronym. The government is preparing its troops for a war abroad, yet it seems to have forgotten how to govern the citizens at home who live at the frontline of that very war.

If the border is secure enough to host trade and commerce in every other part of the country, why are the residents of Mandera and Garissa still being asked to subsidize the national security policy with the total destruction of their livelihoods?
