## Eze: Arsenal's Accidental Messiah or Another False Dawn?

Rio Ferdinand's latest prophecy crowns Eberechi Eze as Arsenal's designated league winner, a bold claim that conveniently sidesteps the team's ongoing inconsistencies. Relying on one player, however talented, to "drag" a side over the finish line after an unconvincing win speaks more to desperation than genuine title-winning quality. Manufactured momentum based on fixture scheduling is hardly the stuff of champions.

### The Lone Star Delusion

Ferdinand's insistence that Eberechi Eze, a "summer signing," can single-handedly "win" Arsenal the league if "let free" feels like classic punditry-by-numbers. If Arsenal truly possesses a player capable of such decisive influence, why has he not been consistently unleashed earlier? The suggestion that Mikel Arteta is keeping the "handbrake half on" is a tacit criticism of the manager himself, implying a strategic failure to utilise a key asset. Pointing to Eze as the sole "shining light" in a pedestrian victory over Newcastle only underlines the collective attacking struggles that have plagued Arsenal, hardly a foundation for a commanding title charge.

### The Mirage of Momentum

The notion of a "shift of momentum" predicated on Arsenal potentially being six points clear after playing two more games than Manchester City is a statistical sleight of hand, not a psychological advantage. Any seasoned observer understands that games in hand offer no guarantees, and a nominal lead built on a greater volume of matches is easily eroded. To assume "easier games" guarantees three points is naive, and City's formidable experience in these high-stakes run-ins consistently exposes such wishful thinking. History shows that genuine momentum comes from relentless, dominant performances, not from merely capitalising on a scheduling quirk.

### Experience Trumps Optimism

Ferdinand's hesitant prediction that "it's Arsenal's time" is immediately undercut by his own admission that he "would not be surprised if City win it" due to their experience, Pep Guardiola, and understanding of the title race demands. This contradictory stance highlights the fundamental insecurity surrounding Arsenal's challenge. Until the Gunners demonstrate the unshakeable composure and ruthless efficiency that Manchester City routinely exhibit in the crunch moments, any talk of their "time" feels more like hopeful fan sentiment than a cold, hard assessment of a team truly ready to claim the Premier League crown.
