## Halting the Laikipia Ebola Facility: Why Your Voice Matters

The High Court has issued conservatory orders stopping the construction of a U.S.-backed Ebola isolation center at Laikipia Air Base. This judicial intervention forces a pause on a project intended to bolster regional health, effectively grounding the development until constitutional public participation requirements are properly addressed.

The Katiba Institute moved to court this week, arguing that the government bypassed the mandatory legal processes for involving local communities in decisions that directly impact their physical safety and environmental health. While the government maintains that the project is a vital component of a Ksh 1.74 billion preparedness package, the court has prioritized the principle that residents in areas hosting hazardous facilities must be consulted. This legal maneuver highlights a recurring tension: when does national security or international diplomatic partnership supersede the local right to know what is being built in the neighborhood?

### Why was this project paused by the courts?

At the core of the dispute is the lack of transparency surrounding the 50-bed isolation unit. Residents of Laikipia raised alarms, questioning how a facility designed to handle highly infectious pathogens would affect the surrounding civilian population. Under the Constitution, any project with significant environmental or public health implications requires rigorous public engagement. By failing to hold these consultations, the authorities left the project vulnerable to a legal challenge. The court agreed with the petitioners that the government cannot simply impose health infrastructure on a community under the guise of security protocols without first explaining the risks, waste management plans, and infection control measures.

### How does this affect the Ksh 1.74 billion funding?

The funding, discussed as recently as May 28 during high-level talks between President William Ruto and U.S. officials, is now technically in limbo. The injunction specifically bars the U.S. government from operating isolation facilities or transferring infected individuals into the country until the case is fully heard. This funding is intended to strengthen national preparedness against potential outbreaks, yet the court order signals that monetary support from international partners does not grant the state a waiver from constitutional accountability. If the state wants to access these funds for the specific purpose of building at the Laikipia Air Base, it must now backtrack and initiate the public participation process it previously ignored. This delay complicates the timeline for the preparedness initiative, proving that even well-funded medical infrastructure cannot bypass the rule of law.

### What is the difference between security and health mandates?

The government often frames projects at sites like the Laikipia Air Base as matters of national security, which sometimes allows for expedited, secretive decision-making. However, the High Court has drawn a clear line: the establishment of an Ebola quarantine center is a public health matter, not purely a military one. While the air base is a secure, controlled environment, the nature of the facility-which deals with biological risks-brings it under the purview of public health regulations that mandate transparency.

The distinction is critical. If it were a purely military installation, the government would have more leeway in managing the site without public oversight. By attempting to place a health quarantine facility within that same framework, the state blurred the lines between defense and civil health. The court’s intervention serves as a reminder that the health rights of Kenyans are not secondary to international diplomatic agreements. As the country continues to modernize its medical response-much like the recent rollout of [new rapid diagnostic tools for HIV and STIs](https://zandani.co.ke/article/new-rapid-diagnostic-tool-for-hiv-and-stis-launched-in-kenya)-the methods used to implement these systems remain as vital as the technology itself. True progress relies on a partnership between the state and its people, ensuring that even in the pursuit of national security, the local community maintains its seat at the table.

Public consultation is a non-negotiable constitutional requirement for any health facility that poses risks to the local environment.
