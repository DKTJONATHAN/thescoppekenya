## Structural Integrity Concerns Follow Ngong School Tragedy DCI investigates death of Grade 9 student at CITAM Schools Ngong is the central subject of the update.

The Directorate of Criminal Investigations has launched a probe into the death of a Grade 9 student at CITAM Schools Ngong after the teenager suffered a fatal fall from a school building on June 13, forcing an urgent review of private facility safety standards.

This incident has ignited a national debate regarding the oversight of multi-story infrastructure in Kenya's rapidly expanding private education sector. As urban schools continue to build upward to maximize land use in areas like Ngong, architectural compliance with student-centric safety regulations faces renewed scrutiny from parents and educational stakeholders alike.

Current Ministry of Education guidelines require all private schools to conduct periodic structural audits to ensure building designs mitigate hazards for adolescent students. However, questions persist regarding the frequency of these inspections and whether existing regulatory frameworks sufficiently account for the behavioral risks associated with high-rise school environments.

The tragedy in Ngong follows a pattern of structural concerns observed in other institutions, such as Lakewood Private School in Utawala, where facility safety has recently dominated boardroom discussions. Critics argue that the Ministry must move beyond paper-based compliance to enforce physical retrofitting of older buildings that may lack the specialized safety barriers required for modern Grade 9 configurations.

Architects specializing in institutional design warn that many private learning centers were converted from residential structures not originally intended for high-density student populations. This adaptation often results in narrow corridors, inadequate stairwell railings, and accessible rooftops that pose significant risks to students if proper structural supervision is not strictly maintained.

Education advocates and child safety experts are now calling for a mandatory national safety audit of all multi-story private schools. They advocate for a policy shift where architectural certification must be renewed annually by independent engineers rather than relying solely on initial building permits. Such a transition would prioritize active risk mitigation over the current reactive approach to facility management.

The focus remains on whether the government will tighten the mandates surrounding building height restrictions and the installation of safety netting or reinforced perimeters. As the DCI continues its investigation, the Ministry of Education is expected to face mounting pressure to release a revised framework for structural safety that addresses the unique challenges of vertical school designs.

The Ministry of Education is expected to announce a multi-agency task force to evaluate the structural integrity of private school facilities across the Nairobi metropolitan area by the end of this month.
