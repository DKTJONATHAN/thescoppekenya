The Teachers Service Commission (TSC) today, Tuesday, March 31, 2026, issued a public caution regarding a fraudulent Facebook account posing as its acting CEO, Evaleen Mitei. The commission urged stakeholders to disregard all information originating from the unverified page, emphasizing reliance on official communication channels only.

Public Caution on Impersonation

Identifying Fraudulent Activity The fake Facebook account, named ‘Mrs Evaleen Mitei,’ operates with 6,800 followers and 62 posts, following 73 other accounts. It deceptively uses an image of the Acting CEO and displays a fraudulent verification badge. "The Teachers Service Commission (TSC) wishes to inform the public and all stakeholders that this page purports to belong to the Acting TSC CEO. Evaleen Mitei is FAKE and fraudulent," a notice from TSC stated.

Verified Platforms for Information TSC clarified that all official communications are exclusively shared through its verified social media platforms. These include its official Facebook page, "TSC Kenya," and its X handle, "@TSC_KE," both distinctly marked with a blue verification badge. "Please note: Official communication by the Commission is only shared through the verified social media pages, i.e., TSC Kenya for Facebook and @TSC_KE on X with a blue verification badge," the commission added. This alert comes as institutions increasingly combat digital identity theft and the manipulation of online presence, a challenge that underscores broader concerns about digital consent and ownership, as recently highlighted by the [High Court's ruling against telcos reassigning phone numbers without owner consent](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenyan-high-court-blocks-telcos-from-reassigning-phone-numbers-witho).

Broader Impersonation Trends The TSC's warning aligns with its recent instructions to all registered teachers not in active service to update their professional information via its online portal, a move aimed at streamlining educational sector records. This initiative, announced in MyGov on Tuesday, March 31, 2026, coincides with a surge in public official and institutional impersonation cases, primarily through social media, targeting individuals for financial exploitation. For example, the Tana Water Works Development Agency cautioned the public on Wednesday, March 25, against fraudsters posing as its officials to offer fake procurement opportunities. These perpetrators have been making fraudulent phone calls, inviting individuals to meetings under the guise of offering contracts while demanding advance payments. The agency confirmed that all its legitimate tenders and procurement opportunities are advertised solely through its official website and local newspapers.
