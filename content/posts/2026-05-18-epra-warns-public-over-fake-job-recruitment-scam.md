## Avoiding Employment Scams in Kenya

Job seekers are increasingly targeted by sophisticated fraud rings promising government positions for a fee. As the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority issues a warning against a con artist named David, it is critical to understand how these scams exploit hope and how to protect yourself.

The promise of a stable, well-paying government job is a powerful lure in today's economy. When someone named David calls with an offer that seems tailored specifically to your profile, the urge to believe it is human nature. However, the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA) released a public alert today, May 18, confirming that they are not hiring through private agents and do not demand payments to secure employment.

### How do scammers build false authority?

Scammers like David succeed because they rely on psychological triggers rather than technical skill. They often mimic the formal language used by government agencies to create a sense of legitimacy. By using official-sounding titles, referencing recent government initiatives-such as the ambitious [Ksh45 Billion Solar Project to Power 3,000 Kenyan Schools](https://zandani.co.ke/article/ruto-launches-ksh45-billion-solar-project-power-3000-kenyan-schools)-they manufacture a context where a bribe feels like a standard, albeit unofficial, processing fee.

These actors often create a sense of urgency. They tell the victim that the position is filling up quickly or that a high-ranking official is personally overseeing the hiring. This pressure is designed to shut down critical thinking. When a victim feels the clock is ticking, they are less likely to pause and verify the identity of the recruiter.

### What are the red flags of recruitment fraud?

Recognizing a scam early is the only way to safeguard your savings. If you are ever contacted about a job opportunity, look for these specific warning signs:

*   **Financial Demands:** Public institutions in Kenya have transparent, legal processes for recruitment. They will never ask you to send money via mobile money, bank transfer, or cash to "facilitate" an interview or guarantee a job.
*   **Unofficial Communication:** Legitimate government agencies use official email domains ending in .go.ke. If the recruiter is using a personal Gmail or WhatsApp account to conduct "official" business, walk away.
*   **Lack of Public Record:** Every government vacancy must be advertised through the Public Service Commission or the official website of the hiring agency. If you cannot find the job listing on an official government portal, the offer is fake.
*   **The "David" Dynamic:** Scammers often claim to have an "insider" connection. If someone implies they have a secret path to employment that bypasses standard merit-based assessments, they are misleading you for their own gain.

### Why it matters now

The rise in these scams coincides with a period of high economic anxiety. Vulnerable Kenyans are being drained of their savings, sometimes borrowing money to pay these "fees." This is not just a loss of cash; it is a violation of trust that makes honest job seekers cynical about legitimate processes. Understanding that your data, like your [digital footprint and personal information](https://zandani.co.ke/article/how-digital-platforms-collect-and-use-data), can be used to tailor these scams makes vigilance even more important.

### What to watch next

Moving forward, expect state agencies to tighten their digital communication protocols to prevent further impersonation. The authorities are likely to ramp up public awareness campaigns. Until then, check the official website of any agency you are applying to. If a recruiter asks for a shilling to secure your future, report the number to the police and the relevant authority immediately. 

Government jobs are earned through merit, not through payments to mysterious callers.
