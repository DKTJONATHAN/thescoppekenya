## Roadblocks and Ruined Wages: Nairobi Locked Down Police defend Nairobi roadblocks amid protest anniversary is the central subject of the update.

The morning commute in Nairobi today felt less like a city waking up and more like a massive, stationary prison as security cordons strangled the capital. Thousands of workers are counting their losses as police checkpoints turned a forty-minute journey into a five-hour nightmare.

Today marks the one-year anniversary of the 2024 Gen Z demonstrations, a date the government approached with visible apprehension. Across major arteries including Mombasa Road, Thika Road, and Lang-ata Road, security forces mounted heavy roadblocks, effectively throttling the flow of traffic into the central business district. While Deputy Inspector General of Police Gilbert Masengeli defended the operation as a vital security measure designed to intercept potential threats and weapons before they could reach protest sites, the view from the ground paints a different picture. For the average Kenyan, this was not a triumph of intelligence, but an economic wall that stripped them of their morning hours and their daily wages.

### The Commuter Toll

At Mlolongo, the atmosphere was thick with frustration as hundreds of passengers abandoned stationary matatus to trek toward the city center on foot. One casual laborer, who earns his keep by the hour, shared that he had already missed two job calls because the police had turned the bypass into a parking lot. His sentiment was echoed by a receptionist stuck near Allsops, who watched her salary tick away in lost minutes while officers performed meticulous, slow-motion vehicle inspections. The economic productivity lost during these morning hours is incalculable, as small-scale traders who rely on early deliveries found their goods wilting in the heat of traffic jams.

The contrast between this morning and a standard Thursday is stark. Commute times that usually peak at an hour exploded into four or five-hour ordeals, with public transport operators in Roysambu and Athi River reporting that many drivers simply gave up and returned to the depots. While the [fashion designer Nimrod Nick recently found himself in a heated confrontation with police](https://zandani.co.ke/article/fashion-designer-nimrod-nick-in-public-confrontation-with-police), today’s standoff was less about individual celebrity and more about the collective exhaustion of a workforce held hostage by state caution. For those trying to balance the demands of work with the lingering anxiety of a national protest day, the checkpoints felt like an intentional exercise in intimidation.

### Voices from the Gridlock

Three distinct perspectives highlight the depth of the public mood as the day progressed. A long-distance driver parked near the Haile Selassie Avenue intersection noted that security should not come at the cost of the very people the police are meant to serve, arguing that the disruption caused more anger than the protests themselves. Meanwhile, a student traveling from the outskirts toward the city center remarked that the heavy presence of armed officers made the environment feel hostile rather than secure, turning a routine commute into an exercise in psychological fatigue. Finally, a small-business owner at the entrance to the industrial area lamented that while the police spoke of intercepting weapons, they mostly intercepted the hopes of low-income earners who cannot afford to miss a single day of work.

This ongoing tension reflects broader struggles, much like the recent frustrations felt by [Kenyan football fans watching the latest VAR controversies](https://zandani.co.ke/article/manchester-city-victory-sparks-controversy-as-kenyan-fans-protest-va). Both situations leave the public feeling as though the rules of the game are being dictated by authorities who remain insulated from the consequences.

As the sun climbs higher and the gridlock slowly begins to shift, the path forward remains uncertain. The security apparatus has clearly prioritized containment, yet the resentment building among commuters suggests that the cost of these roadblocks will be paid in public trust long after the traffic clears. Whether this strategy effectively deterred violence or simply fueled further agitation among the working class is a question that will likely be debated in the city's informal settlements for weeks to come. For now, the city is left to reconcile the official narrative of security with the reality of a paralyzed workforce.
