Mumias East Member of Parliament Peter Salasya, on Saturday, April 11, initiated a legislative proposal to amend the Social Health Insurance Act, 2023. The core of his proposition seeks to introduce a uniform KSh 500 monthly contribution for all registered members of the Social Health Authority (SHA), aiming to rectify identified operational and financial deficiencies.

Legislative Push for SHA Reforms Salasya formally addressed National Assembly Speaker Moses Wetang'ula, outlining his concerns regarding the SHA's framework. He specifically cited what he described as deep operational and financial failures within the authority, prompting the proposed overhaul. The amendments aim to foster a more sustainable and accessible healthcare financing model.

Shift to Flat Rate Contributions Central to the legislator's proposal is a move away from the current income-based annual contribution system. Salasya advocates for a standard KSh 500 monthly contribution for all registered individuals, a structure reminiscent of the defunct National Hospital Insurance Fund (NHIF). He asserts that this flat rate would be both affordable and simpler for the populace to contribute consistently, thereby enhancing SHA's self-sustainability and reducing reliance on exchequer funding.

Addressing Low Compliance and Financial Sustainability Salasya's proposal highlights critical issues within the existing system, particularly low compliance rates. While approximately 29 million individuals are registered under SHA, only around 5 million are reportedly active contributors. This disparity raises significant concerns about the fund's long-term viability. The MP argues that a streamlined, affordable contribution model will significantly boost active participation, ensuring adequate funds to support a wider demographic.

Reinstatement of Linda Mama and Other Amendments Beyond the contribution structure, Salasya is pushing for the reinstatement of the Linda Mama programme, arguing its suspension has hindered access to maternal healthcare and increased financial burdens for expectant mothers. Additionally, he seeks to introduce provisions for voluntary contributions on behalf of other registered beneficiaries and the creation of a national solidarity pool to support indigent and vulnerable persons. The proposed legislation also aims to consolidate the three existing SHA funds-the Primary Healthcare Fund, the Social Health Insurance Fund (SHIF), and the Emergency, Chronic, and Critical Illness Fund-into a single unified pool to eliminate duplication and enhance efficiency. Such legislative discussions often underscore broader parliamentary efforts, as seen with discussions on national security and preparedness.

Claims Settlement and Current SHA Status A key amendment within the proposed bill mandates SHA to settle all verified claims from hospitals within 60 days. This aims to improve service delivery and accountability within the healthcare system. According to current health ministry records, SHA has over 30 million registered members since its introduction in 2024. Salaried individuals presently contribute 2.75% of their gross monthly salary, while those in the informal sector or without income contribute a minimum of KSh 300. The MP's proposal arrives amid ongoing scrutiny regarding alleged financial irregularities within SHA, although the Ministry of Health has dismissed these claims.
