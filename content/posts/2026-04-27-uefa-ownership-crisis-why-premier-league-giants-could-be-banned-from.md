The current European football season has intensified the scramble among Premier League clubs to meet UEFA's stringent multi-club ownership (MCO) rules. With the 1 March 2026 deadline for compliance having passed, several prominent clubs face potential bans or demotions from European competitions for the upcoming 2026/2027 season, highlighting UEFA's commitment to sporting integrity.

## The Multi-Club Ownership Conundrum

UEFA's MCO regulations are designed to prevent conflicts of interest and maintain sporting integrity, stipulating that two clubs with closely linked ownership should not compete in the same European competition. This includes significant shareholding, voting rights, and decisive influence in key decisions. The consequences for non-compliance can range from demotion to a lower-ranked competition, as Crystal Palace experienced in the 2025 season from the Europa League to the Conference League, to outright exclusion from Europe.

The 1 March deadline for compliance, reinforced by rulings from the Court of Arbitration for Sport (Cas) in December 2025, has triggered a flurry of activity among clubs with multi-club arrangements. These rulings stemmed from cases involving Crystal Palace, Irish club Drogheda United, and Slovakian outfit FC DAC 1904 in the 2025/2026 season, all of whom lost their appeals at Cas.

## Premier League Giants Under Scrutiny

Half of the Premier League clubs are currently part of some form of multi-club arrangement, with several facing direct challenges under UEFA's strict MCO rules for the 2026/2027 season.

### Everton and Roma's Friedkin Link

The ownership structure linking Everton and Italian club AS Roma, both controlled by the Friedkin Group through subsidiary entities, poses a significant concern. Dan Friedkin serves as chairman for Everton and president of Roma, alongside other high-ranking officials from the Friedkin Group on Everton's board who were previously associated with Roma. If both clubs qualify for the same European competition, one would face exclusion or demotion. As of late March 2026, Roma was sixth in Serie A, in a Europa League spot, while Everton was eighth in the Premier League, in a Conference League spot but with ambitions for the Europa League. Everton insists they have taken necessary steps to ensure both clubs can play in the same European competition.

### Chelsea and BlueCo's European Ambitions

Chelsea and Strasbourg, both operating under the BlueCo umbrella, present another prominent case. In February 2026, four members of BlueCo Alsace's board, Strasbourg's holding company, stepped down, including Chelsea's joint-sporting directors Paul Winstanley and Laurence Stewart. Todd Boehly and Behdad Eghbali also resigned as directors of BlueCo Data Limited on February 28, 2026, though they remain on Chelsea's board. Despite player movements between the two clubs this 2025/2026 season, BlueCo has reportedly established a blind trust for their Strasbourg shares and removed key Chelsea personnel from the Strasbourg board, leading to clearance for both clubs to potentially compete in the same European competition for the 2026/2027 season. However, some Chelsea and Strasbourg fans have protested the BlueCo ownership.

### Forest's Blind Trust Strategy

Nottingham Forest owner Evangelos Marinakis has placed the club into a blind trust to comply with UEFA rules, particularly given the possibility of Forest qualifying for the Champions League alongside his other club, Olympiakos. This action, completed before the 1 March 2026 deadline, involved Marinakis ceasing to be a "person with significant control" of NF Football Investments, which owns Forest. Independent trustees now control Pittville Four Limited, the company with significant control over Forest. UEFA previously accepted a blind trust for Manchester City and Girona, and Manchester United and Nice, in the 2024 Champions League and Europa League, respectively.

### Brighton's Expanding Network

Brighton's owner Tony Bloom has expanded his football interests, holding shares in Scottish Premiership club Heart of Midlothian and Belgian club Union Saint-Gilloise. While Bloom reduced his controlling stake in Union Saint-Gilloise below 30% in 2023 to comply with UEFA rules when both clubs qualified for the Europa League, his 29% stake in Hearts, acquired in June 2025, remains a point of interest for compliance if all three clubs were to qualify for the same European competition.

Leeds had also taken steps to resolve a potential conflict with Rangers if they had qualified for the Europa League, as 49ers Enterprises owns 100% of Leeds and a consortium including 49ers Enterprises owns 51% of Rangers.
