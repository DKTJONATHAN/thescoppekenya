Ringtone blames church leadership for decline of gospel music is central to this update for Kenyan readers.

## The Pulpit Versus The Podium

Alex Apoko, better known as Ringtone, spent more than a week in police custody recently, yet he insists his most significant battle remains with the clergy. While the artist maintains his commitment to the gospel genre, he argues that the local industry is not dying because of secular encroachment, but because the pulpit has turned its back on the very artists it claims to shepherd.

### A Pattern of Public Reprimand

The friction between Kenyan gospel artists and church leadership is not a recent phenomenon. It is a documented history of public shaming. For years, established ministries have utilized their platforms to castigate performers, often prioritizing moral policing over structural mentorship. When a secular artist faces a setback, industry peers often rally with support mechanisms. Conversely, when a gospel artist falters, the default reaction from the church is public distancing. 

This environment fosters a culture of fear rather than growth. By focusing on public rebukes rather than private guidance, church leaders have effectively severed the link between the institutional church and the creative sector. This lack of a support structure means that when an artist needs a foundation, they find only a courtroom. Just as we saw with the scrutiny following the [John Matara case](https://zandani.co.ke/article/john-matara-charged-with-murder-of-ann-starlet-wahu-in-south-b-airbn), the public eye often demands accountability, but within the gospel industry, that demand is weaponized to exile artists instead of rehabilitating them.

### The Neema Pivot

Ringtone claims his latest track, Neema, is a direct result of his recent legal entanglement. He characterizes the song as a pivot, shifting his brand from a combative public figure to a reflective artist who has found clarity in isolation. By framing his arrest as a spiritual awakening, he challenges the church leadership to define their role. If they are truly concerned with the state of gospel music, he argues, then they must stop using their pulpits to broadcast condemnations and start using their resources to foster talent.

This is a stark departure from how other sectors operate. In professional environments, even when things go wrong, there is a protocol for consultation and regulation. Much like the [Treasury's recent public consultations on eCitizen fee rules](https://zandani.co.ke/article/treasury-opens-public-consultations-on-ecitizen-fee-regulations), there is an expectation of procedural fairness. In the gospel music industry, however, there is no such framework. There are only gatekeepers who decide who remains in the fold and who is cast out for public consumption.

### The Institutional Disconnect

The decline of gospel music in Kenya is often blamed on a lack of spirituality among the youth or the influence of secular trends. Ringtone flips this narrative. He posits that the industry is failing because it has been stripped of its institutional protection. When the church treats artists as liability risks rather than creative assets, the quality and integrity of the output inevitably suffer. 

The secular music industry thrives because it builds ecosystems. It creates networks of managers, investors, and supporters who understand that an artist is a business. The gospel industry, by contrast, relies on a precarious, morality-based support system that can evaporate at the first sign of scandal. If the church leadership wants to reclaim its influence over the musical output of this nation, it needs to stop functioning as a high court of public opinion and start functioning as an institutional partner. 

Until the church decides whether it wants to be a judge or a mentor, the industry will continue to fracture. Ringtone is not just complaining about his treatment; he is identifying a structural failure. The gap between the pulpit and the stage has become a chasm, and for as long as leadership prefers the former over the latter, the music will continue to lose its rhythm. The question remains whether those holding the microphones in the church are willing to listen to the feedback, or if they are too committed to the sound of their own condemnation.
