## Celebrity Accountability and Child Rights in African Entertainment

Nollywood actress and evangelist Tonto Dikeh faces a significant legal challenge in Abuja, Nigeria, following a fundamental rights enforcement suit. This development brings into sharp focus the responsibilities of public figures and the protection of minors within religious practices across the continent, prompting conversations about celebrity influence and ethical conduct in African cultural spaces.

### Legal Action Against Tonto Dikeh

Human rights lawyer Ikechukwu Obasi has initiated a lawsuit against Tonto Dikeh, alleging violations of a female schoolgirl's fundamental rights during a "vicious religious deliverance ritual." The suit, filed on behalf of a Junior Secondary School 1 student from JSS Durumi II, Abuja, seeks ₦200 million in damages. The incident, which allegedly occurred on March 6, 2026, became widely publicized through a video and photographs posted on Dikeh's official Facebook page. The footage reportedly shows the actress speaking to the student before placing her hands on the girl and conducting a deliverance session, an action Dikeh stated was spiritually guided due to a perceived "spirit of death hovering around the child." The video depicts the child lying on bare ground and pressed against a stony surface during the ritual.

### Allegations of Rights Violations and Damages Sought

Obasi's affidavit contends that Dikeh's actions constituted degrading treatment, exposing the child to public humiliation, stigma, and potential psychological trauma. He argues that publishing the video and images without consent violated the child's right to privacy under Section 37 of the 1999 Constitution and the Child Rights Act 2003. The lawyer further asserts that portraying the child as demonically possessed could lead to ridicule and discrimination among her peers. Beyond the ₦200 million in damages for dignity and privacy violations, the suit seeks several court orders. These include a declaration that the alleged ritual infringed upon the child's constitutional rights, an order compelling Dikeh to remove the content from her social media, a public apology in three national newspapers, and a perpetual injunction preventing her from performing similar religious rites on any Nigerian child. The suit also calls for a broad court declaration that any child exorcism or harmful religious rite disguised as spiritual deliverance violates fundamental rights in Nigeria.
