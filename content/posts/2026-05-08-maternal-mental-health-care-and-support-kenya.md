## The Happy Motherhood Myth: Why We Must Prioritize Maternal Mental Health

The prevailing narrative surrounding motherhood in Kenya often paints it as an effortless, blissful transition. This romanticized version of the maternal experience suggests that biological instinct should naturally result in total fulfillment. However, this perspective is not only inaccurate; it is a harmful facade that forces many women to suffer in silence, isolated from the support they deserve.

The societal demand for the perfect, resilient mother creates a barrier between women and necessary clinical intervention. By framing matrescence as a purely spiritual or natural rite of passage, we dismiss the reality of complex biological and psychological transitions. This cultural narrative effectively gaslights women who face legitimate medical complications, equating silence with strength when the opposite is true.

### Moving Beyond the Facade of Bliss

In reality, one in ten Kenyan mothers faces peripartum or postpartum mental health challenges. This is not a deficiency of character or a lack of natural instinct. It is a biological event that warrants the same objective, evidence-based care we typically reserve for physical health issues.

Take the experience of Bianca, a new mother in Nairobi who struggled with profound depression while being pressured by her community to simply feel grateful. The persistent expectation to display outward perfection prevented her from accessing care until her situation reached a crisis point. When public discourse emphasizes surface-level gestures, such as viral displays of luxury gifts, it reinforces the notion that material status can offset psychological distress. We often prioritize the external image of motherhood, ignoring the silent, internal decline that can occur behind closed doors.

### Standardizing Clinical Recognition

We must shift from vague observations about a mother's mood toward standardized clinical protocols. The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale should not be treated as a casual suggestion or an optional screening. It is a critical diagnostic tool. This ten-question assessment should be as mandatory and routine as checking blood pressure at facilities like Aga Khan University Hospital.

The reluctance to adopt these screenings is revealing. Perhaps acknowledging that one in ten women requires clinical intervention challenges the foundations of our social structure. If we accept this data, the myth of the inherently blissful, unflappable mother begins to dissolve. A struggling mother does not require platitudes regarding patience. She requires a scored clinical assessment that provides a clear trajectory toward professional care.

### Rethinking the Support Framework

Meaningful support is not found in toxic positivity or whispered advice. It is found in accessible, professional intervention. If you are experiencing these challenges, it is vital to understand that this is a manageable health condition. Reach out to services like 1199 for guidance or connect with networks that prioritize clinical reality over outdated cultural expectations.

We must discard the dangerous notion that mothers can manage significant neurochemical changes through sheer force of will. When a woman experiences postpartum anxiety or depression, the most effective community response is to facilitate a connection with a psychiatrist or a qualified therapist. We need to dismantle the pride that stops our sisters and daughters from seeking help. The myth of the supermom is destructive, whereas the thriving mother is one who receives the medical attention she is entitled to.

### The Urgency of Systemic Reform

While mental health awareness often feels performative, maternal health remains deeply stigmatized. As we mark Mental Health Month this May, it is time to insist that primary healthcare providers integrate mental health screenings into every postnatal visit. If our medical systems continue to prioritize the physical body while neglecting the mind, we are failing the very bedrock of the Kenyan family.

Moving forward, we must monitor the development of community-based maternal mental health initiatives. Without them, the burden of care remains sequestered within private institutions, leaving low-income mothers without a viable path to treatment. Furthermore, we must watch for formal commitments from the Ministry of Health to train community health volunteers, effectively bridging the critical gap between rural homes and urban medical clinics. Our commitment to maternal health must transition from polite conversation to measurable, systemic change.
