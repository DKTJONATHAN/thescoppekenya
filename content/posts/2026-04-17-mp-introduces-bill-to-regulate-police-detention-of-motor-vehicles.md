Kathiani Member of Parliament Robert Mbui has introduced the Traffic (Amendment) Bill of 2026, poised to redefine police procedures for vehicle and motorcycle detention across Kenya. The Bill aims to mitigate concerns from vehicle owners regarding prolonged impoundments by establishing clear guidelines and time limits for law enforcement.

## New Framework for Vehicle Impoundment

### Establishing Grounds and Timelines

The Traffic (Amendment) Bill of 2026 stipulates that a vehicle or motorcycle can only be detained if it is directly linked to an offense, serving either as evidence in a trial or for investigative purposes. This provision seeks to prevent arbitrary detentions. For vehicles held as evidence, where a photograph or an assessment report is deemed sufficient, the proposed law limits detention to a maximum of 48 hours. For investigative purposes, the Bill caps the maximum detention period at 14 days, a measure designed to curtail prolonged impoundments.

### Ensuring Vehicle Preservation and Maintaining Judicial Authority

A crucial aspect of the Bill is the requirement for any officer in charge of a police station to ensure the proper preservation of a detained vehicle. This addresses frequent reports of damage or vandalism to vehicles while in police custody, offering vehicle owners enhanced protection. It is important to note that the proposed legislation explicitly states it does not diminish the power of the courts, ensuring that judicial authority to issue orders concerning detained vehicles, as per section 121 of the Criminal Procedure Code, remains intact. The Bill acknowledges that it limits the right to property under Article 40 of the Constitution to the extent that a police officer may detain a vehicle or motorcycle for purposes of investigation. This legislative action reflects broader efforts to enhance road user experiences and safety within the transport sector, complementing initiatives such as recent defensive driving and safety workshops for women drivers.
