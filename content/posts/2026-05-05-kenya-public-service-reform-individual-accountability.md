## Shifting the Mandate: Why Individual Responsibility Defines the Future of Public Service

Standing in a government office yesterday, I witnessed a moment that felt like a quiet revolution. A junior clerk faced a request from a superior to jump the queue for an acquaintance. Instead of offering the expected deference, the clerk simply pointed to the digital tracking screen and went back to work. In that brief, unremarkable interaction, I saw the fading echo of a system that has long held Kenya back. We are finally moving away from the era of the Bwana Afande-that distant, untouchable figure of authority-toward a culture where public service is measured by individual responsibility rather than administrative power.

### Moving Beyond the Colonial Hangover

For generations, our public offices operated under a shadow left by colonial-era structures. The office was often viewed as a personal fiefdom, where power was a badge of rank rather than an instrument of utility. Under this model, silence and subservience were the primary requirements for advancement. You waited for the boss to nod, and you certainly did not question the hierarchy.

However, the current push for individual accountability, championed by figures like Felix Kosgey, is dismantling this rigid architecture. This transition is less about new policy memos and more about a fundamental change in mindset. We are shifting from being subjects governed by a regime to becoming stewards who hold public trust. When an individual officer is personally accountable for the speed and accuracy of their work, the old practice of hiding behind the anonymity of a department becomes impossible. The goal is no longer to please a superior but to satisfy the citizen. This is a difficult pivot for those who built careers on seniority and influence, yet it remains the essential foundation for a modern, functional state.

### Dignity Through Professional Ownership

Accountability does more than improve service delivery. It redefines the workplace. When responsibility is pushed downward, the office shifts from a site of patronage to a place of professional merit. The recent emphasis on staff well-being is a critical component of this change. It is unreasonable to expect a public servant to treat citizens with grace if they are constantly being diminished by a bullying, top-down hierarchy.

When we foster inclusive leadership, we empower the official at the desk. There is a profound difference between a worker waiting for instructions and an official who takes personal pride in solving a problem independently. By treating employees as valued contributors rather than mere cogs in a machine, the state cultivates a workforce that is far more efficient and invested in the outcome of their duties.

### The New Standard of Service

The old habits-the intentional delays, the circular filing, and the constant deferral to an invisible authority-are becoming social liabilities. Just as we have observed in other sectors, such as the DCI’s shift toward sophisticated, technology-driven policing, the government is realizing that heavy-handed, manual processes have no place in a modern digital economy. Citizens are becoming more vocal about their right to quality service, and the institutions are beginning to respond to this pressure.

For too long, the Kenyan state was synonymous with unnecessary friction. When public officials embrace personal responsibility, that friction begins to evaporate. This cultural shift is the bedrock of future economic growth. If our small, daily interactions with the state are transparent and efficient, the national mood shifts from frustration to confidence in our shared systems.

### Monitoring the Change

Over the next year, keep a close watch on how internal performance appraisals are conducted. If the new directives remain just words on paper, we will see the status quo continue. However, if performance metrics begin to dictate career progression and promotions, we will know the era of the Bwana Afande has finally concluded. We should look for the rise of clear, data-driven feedback loops that allow citizens to bypass the traditional gatekeepers of influence.

The old guard will not disappear in a day. Change at this scale is always gradual. Yet, the clerk who refused to compromise his process for a superior suggests that the tide has turned. We are collectively learning a simple but powerful lesson: the authority of a public office is not something to be hoarded or wielded for status. It is a responsibility to be practiced for the common good.
