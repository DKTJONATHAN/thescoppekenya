## The NTSA Digitisation Paradox

The National Transport and Safety Authority is heralding its new instant fine system as a milestone in administrative efficiency, yet the platform omits a crucial safeguard: a mechanism to distinguish between the vehicle owner and the driver actually behind the wheel.

While the government celebrates the automation of 37 traffic penalty categories through eCitizen, thousands of Kenyans find themselves trapped in a bureaucratic oversight. The digital infrastructure treats the logbook holder as the sole point of liability, effectively ignoring the reality of the secondary transport market and the notoriously sluggish pace of official ownership transfers.

### The Liability Gap

The NTSA system hinges on a singular premise: if the vehicle is flagged for a violation, the fine hits the registered owner. This ignores the legal and practical discrepancy between the person whose name sits on the digital portal and the person operating the vehicle at the moment of the offence. For those who have sold their vehicles but are still trapped in the slow churn of the transfer process, the risk is not merely academic.

When a logbook transfer is initiated, the window of time before the system updates is often weeks, if not months. During this period, the seller remains the legal face of the vehicle in the eyes of the digital fine system. An automated penalty triggered by a speeding camera-even one exceeding the limit by a mere 1 km/h-falls squarely on the previous owner. There is no automated prompt to query the driver before the fine is registered. Instead, the system operates on a presumptive guilt model that places the burden of proof on the victim of the state’s own administrative lag.

### A System Built for Convenience, Not Justice

The promise of instant payments ranging from Ksh500 to Ksh10,000 obscures the absence of a viable dispute resolution framework. Within the current portal, there is no immediate "opt-out" or "not my vehicle/not my driver" button that halts the ticking clock of a penalty. The NTSA has pushed a system that prioritizes revenue collection over the constitutional requirement for fair administrative action.

Critics point out that this resembles the operational rigidity seen elsewhere in the financial sector, where institutional systems often disregard the individual's lack of control over processes, much like how [Access Bank Kenya losses narrow as merger with National Bank looms](https://zandani.co.ke/article/access-bank-kenya-losses-narrow-as-merger-with-national-bank-looms) shifts the burden of structural consolidation onto shareholders. In the transport sector, however, the burden is being shifted onto motorists who are effectively being held hostage for traffic infractions they did not commit. If an owner sells a car today, they remain legally exposed to the driver’s recklessness for as long as the NTSA database remains tethered to outdated registration records.

### The Paper Trail Trap

The reality is that NTSA infrastructure is lagging behind the digital speed it demands from citizens. Despite the focus on modernising transport, the authority has failed to link its transfer records with its fine-processing engine in real time. This creates a dangerous liability vacuum. Owners who have submitted their transfer documents find themselves receiving automated notifications for fines incurred by new drivers, yet they are told that they must pay the fine to avoid further penalties or court summons.

The state expects citizens to embrace a digital system that cannot identify its own errors. When the law links liability to a logbook entry rather than the actual driver, the NTSA essentially turns the ownership document into a debt trap. If the system is capable of instantly detecting a 1 km/h transgression, why is it incapable of hosting a transparent verification process that allows owners to disclaim liability before the fine becomes a permanent mark on their record?

As the authority mandates compliance with these 37 new categories, one must wonder: at what point does the pursuit of digital revenue collection cease to be an exercise in traffic safety and become a systematic extraction of funds from citizens based on the failures of the state’s own internal records?
