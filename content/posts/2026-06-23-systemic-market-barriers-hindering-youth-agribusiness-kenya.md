## Beyond the Hoe: Why Young Kenyans Are Not Returning to the Farm

We often romanticize the image of the young Kenyan farmer, hands stained with earth and eyes fixed on the horizon. Yet, when I speak with young producers, the reality is far removed from this pastoral idealism. The struggle is not rooted in a lack of effort or an aversion to manual labor. Instead, it is a calculation born of hard, financial experience. We are asking a generation to commit their futures to a field that offers little in the way of security or fair compensation.

The Alliance for a Green Revolution in Africa and the Council of Governors recently published findings that quantify what many already suspect. While the youth make up 75 percent of our population, only 28.5 percent are engaged in the agri-food sector. This disconnect is frequently misinterpreted as a lack of interest, but the data from counties like Bungoma, Kakamega, Kirinyaga, Meru, and Nakuru tells a story of economic caution. With 58 percent of these young people identifying price volatility as their greatest barrier, it is evident that they are acting as rational entrepreneurs who recognize a losing game when they see one.

### The Myth of Apathy in Agriculture

Politicians frequently suggest that youth are too enamored with urban life or digital distractions to work the land. This narrative is as convenient as it is inaccurate, serving only to shift the burden of failure from a broken system onto the shoulders of the individual. When the market is structured to prioritize intermediaries, the producer is left to absorb the highest risks while securing the slimmest profit margins.

When market information is treated like a guarded secret rather than a public utility, young farmers are essentially placing bets against a house that is rigged to win. To expect them to continue under these conditions is not an encouragement of diligence; it is a request for them to participate in their own impoverishment. Agriculture, like any other professional industry, requires institutional stability. Without the predictability needed to plan a business cycle, we cannot reasonably expect the brightest minds to commit their lives to farming.

### From Training to Market Integration

Our current policy obsession involves endless training workshops, the distribution of seed packs, and sermons on the potential of vertical farming. While these efforts are well-intentioned, they fail to address the core problem: what happens after the harvest. We cannot continue to treat young people as agricultural students when they are attempting to act as serious business owners. 

A meaningful shift requires us to change how we interact with the harvest. If county governments used their procurement power to source food for schools, hospitals, and public offices directly from youth-led agribusinesses, it would establish a foundation of predictable demand. Such a commitment would transition agriculture from a subsistence-based struggle into a professional endeavor. With guaranteed cash flow, these entrepreneurs could finally afford to invest in modern equipment and value-addition technology, ultimately strengthening the entire sector.

### Rebuilding the Value Chain

Even with a guaranteed market, true advancement remains tethered to the issue of capital. Young farmers are currently labeled as high-risk by financial institutions, despite being the demographic most inclined to adopt the climate-smart practices necessary for long-term productivity. We need to implement blended finance models that dismantle these barriers, ensuring that a producer in Kakamega has the same opportunity for growth as an established trader operating out of Nairobi.

If we refuse to pursue a structural overhaul, we will inevitably continue to lose our most capable minds to foreign economies that reward their ambition and efficiency. Agriculture remains the backbone of our GDP, yet it currently rests on the shoulders of an aging demographic that is increasingly unable to sustain the weight. We must stop weaving romanticized tales about the virtues of rural life. Instead, we should prioritize fair pricing and reliable market access. It is time to stop blaming the youth for failing to succeed in a system that was never built to support them. The soil is undeniably fertile, but until we mend the broken market surrounding it, the harvest will never reach its potential.
