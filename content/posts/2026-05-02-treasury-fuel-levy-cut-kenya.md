## Treasury Proposes 50 Percent Cut to Kenya Fuel Levy

The National Treasury has proposed a 50 percent reduction in the Kenya fuel levy directed to the Road Annuity Fund through the Road Maintenance Levy Fund (Amendment) Bill, 2026. This move aims to save Kenyans approximately 80 cents for every Ksh100 spent on fuel, easing the financial burden on motorists and public transport operators across the country. The Bill is currently before Parliament.

### Proposed Amendments and Consumer Savings

The Road Maintenance Levy Fund Act (Cap. 427) currently channels Ksh3 per litre of the Kenya fuel levy into the Road Annuity Fund. The proposed Bill seeks to halve this allocation to Ksh1.50 per litre. Calculations indicate that this change could save Kenyans about Ksh75 to Ksh80 when filling a 50-litre tank. Public service vehicles like a 14-seater matatu making eight daily round trips on the Rongai-Nairobi CBD route, consuming around 46 litres of diesel, could save approximately Ksh75 per day. A larger 33-seater Isuzu NQR on the same route, using about 74 litres, stands to save nearly Ksh120 daily.

### Road Annuity Programme and Securitization Concerns

The Road Annuity Programme is a government initiative enabling road construction without upfront payments, where contractors are repaid over time from the Ksh3 per litre fuel levy. This proposed cut comes as the Treasury has already securitized a substantial portion of the Ksh25-per-litre Road Maintenance Levy. President William Ruto's administration has pledged Ksh12 of the levy to raise Ksh300 billion, committing nearly half of every collected shilling to bondholders for the next decade. The first tranche, executed in February 2025, involved securitizing Ksh7 per litre to raise Ksh175 billion for clearing road sector pending bills. In November, Cabinet approved a second tranche, securitizing an additional Ksh5 per litre to raise Ksh125 billion for future contractor payments.

### Standoff with the International Monetary Fund

This securitization arrangement is a point of contention with the International Monetary Fund (IMF). Bloomberg reported in April that the IMF insists Kenya classify this securitized revenue as public debt. Such a reclassification would push the country's debt stock above Ksh13 trillion, potentially impacting whether the Ruto administration secures a new IMF lending program to replace the one that expired earlier this year. The transparent valuation of public assets and liabilities remains crucial, much like the [DCI explains the process for valuing seized narcotic drugs](https://zandani.co.ke/article/dci-explains-the-process-for-valuing-seized-narcotic-drugs) in ensuring accountability across government operations. Treasury Cabinet Secretary Mbadi has not yet clarified how cutting Ksh1.50 from the annuity allocation will affect the already securitized levy or from where the displaced funding for annuity-financed projects will be sourced.
