## The Malabo Mirage: Why Policy-First Farming Leaves Kenya Behind

A persistent narrative suggests that African agriculture sits on the precipice of a total transformation, assuming nations simply hit the milestones laid out in frameworks like the Comprehensive Africa Agriculture Development Programme (CAADP). This perspective offers a comfortable, institutional sedative, yet it remains untethered from the harsh realities of the field.

The 2025 deadline for the Malabo Declaration has passed, yet the promised outcomes are nowhere to be found. For years, leaders have championed the target of allocating 10 percent of national budgets to agriculture to achieve 6 percent annual growth. These metrics have evolved from mere goals into the primary yardstick for success. While officials toast to these commitments in air-conditioned conference rooms, the average Kenyan farmer continues to grapple with the same infrastructure decay and logistical bottlenecks that defined the sector decades ago. We remain obsessed with the aesthetic of policy while the soil itself demands solutions that operate independently of state ledgers.

### The Fiscal Fetish

Policy architects frequently treat the 10 percent budget allocation as a panacea. The logic follows that if the fiscal targets are met, the continent will automatically be secured. This is a form of fiscal fetishism. Injecting capital into existing, sclerotic state structures does not inherently generate food. In Kenya, the state-led model functions as a bloated mechanism that prioritizes administrative overhead at the expense of last-mile efficiency. 

By fixating on macro-level spending, we ignore the informal networks that provide the actual backbone of national food security. From local roadside traders to independent smallholder cooperatives, the informal sector possesses a structural resilience that government databases often fail to capture. Policymakers attempt to formalize these sectors to satisfy international compliance, yet in doing so, they risk fracturing the very systems that function most effectively.

### Innovating Around the Gridlock

Real advancement in Kenyan agriculture is rarely incubated in government ministries or during the pomp of regional summits. Instead, progress is being driven by young entrepreneurs developing digital marketplaces and climate-resilient logistics platforms. These startups act as a digital bypass to systemic bureaucratic failure. They do not wait for the state to realize its 6 percent growth targets; they tackle post-harvest waste through data-driven logistics and direct-to-consumer models. 

Rather than compelling these innovators to align with the rigid, lethargic mandates of CAADP, the government should prioritize the removal of regulatory friction. If the state intends to be useful, it should cease its attempts to micromanage harvests and start building the foundational infrastructure that facilitates trade for those who actually understand the land.

### Beyond the Paper Summit

It is time to pivot away from the maps drawn by bureaucrats and toward the practicalities of production. Policy should serve as a support system rather than a ceiling. Currently, our agricultural framework acts as a barrier, favoring large, formal entities that possess the capacity to navigate government tenders, while small-scale producers are left to fend for themselves. 

Our collective fascination with international standards creates a dangerous disconnect. Governments report triumphant success on official documents, yet the cost of basic staples continues to skyrocket in urban markets. This creates a feedback loop of institutional self-congratulation that blinds us to the erosion of genuine food security.

### Why It Matters Now

The failure to meet the 2025 Malabo targets is not merely a logistical lapse; it is proof that our reliance on top-down, state-directed development has reached a dead end. If we persist in chasing these hollow metrics, we face the prospect of losing another generation of farmers to economic stagnation, while the rest of the world evolves without us.

### What to Watch Next

Monitor the rise of private, tech-centric agricultural firms that operate in defiance of traditional market boards. These startups provide the true litmus test for the industry. If the state chooses to smother these firms with regulation to protect a stagnant status quo, the responsibility for future food price shocks will rest squarely on the shoulders of the regulators. Do not look for progress in the next official policy statement. Instead, look for it in the digital, unpoliced corners of the market where the government has yet to intervene.
