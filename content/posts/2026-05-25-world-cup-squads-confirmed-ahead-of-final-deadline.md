## World Cup Squad Farce: FIFA's Grip Tightens

With the 2026 World Cup on the horizon, national federations are grudgingly confirming their 26-man squads. However, FIFA's rigid deadlines and opaque rules reveal a bureaucracy more concerned with control than genuine sporting clarity, leaving fans to sift through provisional lists and delayed announcements. Expect more calculated manoeuvring than raw footballing conviction.

### The Illusion of National Autonomy

As the June 2 deadline for final squad submission approaches, it’s increasingly clear that national federations are merely going through the motions until FIFA’s ultimate confirmation. Countries are "free to name their final 26-man squads any time before FIFA’s June 2 deadline," yet these are only "considered formal and final when FIFA confirm all 48 of them." This isn't freedom; it's a dress rehearsal for FIFA's grand announcement. The requirement for a provisional squad, kept secret by many, only adds to the farcical lack of transparency. What purpose does a non-public "up to 55 names" serve, beyond giving FIFA an exclusive peek and national managers a security blanket? It certainly doesn't benefit the fans or foster any genuine connection.

### Lingering Indecision and Questionable Picks

The protracted nature of squad announcements highlights either a severe lack of conviction from management or a cynical attempt to keep opponents guessing. While South Korea and Switzerland have already locked in their rosters, countries like Mexico, South Africa, and the Czech Republic are dragging their feet, pushing announcements close to the deadline. Mexico, for instance, still lists a staggering provisional roster heavy on Liga MX talent, with their final decision set for June 1. South Africa waits until May 27, and the Czech Republic until May 31. This isn't strategic genius; it often smacks of indecision.

Furthermore, a closer look at some of the provisional and confirmed squads raises eyebrows. The presence of players like Guillermo Ochoa (AEL Limassol) for Mexico, Edin Dzeko (Schalke 04) for Bosnia, and Neymar (Santos) for Brazil, suggests that reputation or name recognition still trumps current form, especially given the influx of teams in the expanded format. This focus on past glories over present output makes one wonder if the expanded 48-team World Cup format simply dilutes the overall quality. For more details on these convoluted squad regulations, one can refer to the official rules.

### Injury Rules: A Bureaucratic Nightmare

Even after the final squads are locked in, FIFA's iron grip on player changes remains absolute. Teams can replace players up to 24 hours before their first game, but "only in the case of illness or injury, with FIFA’s blessing, and only with a player from the provisional squad previously submitted." This restrictive clause means that if a star player picks up a last-minute knock, managers can't scout for a genuinely in-form replacement outside their pre-selected bubble. The only real exception is for goalkeepers, who can be replaced "at any time during the tournament" with FIFA's "okay." It's a system designed to protect FIFA's procedural integrity, not to ensure teams field their strongest possible side. The entire structure feels less about football and more about paperwork.
