The nation's passport has significantly improved its standing in the 2026 global rankings, moving up five places to 64th worldwide, as reported by Henley & Partners. This advancement occurs despite ongoing accusations regarding its issuance to the Rapid Support Forces.

Global Passport Ranking Advancement The latest report from Henley & Partners indicates the passport moved from position 69 to 64 globally. The Henley Passport Index meticulously compares 199 passports across 227 destinations, leveraging exclusive data from the International Air Transport Association (IATA), the world’s most extensive travel database.

Regional and Global Standing Within Africa, the nation holds the 10th position, trailing countries such as South Africa, Morocco, Seychelles, Botswana, Namibia, Lesotho, Malawi, Eswatini, and Mauritius. Passport holders can access approximately 69 to 74 destinations, which include visa-free and visa-on-arrival options. This positions it among the top 10 most influential passports on the continent, with access frequently exceeding 60 countries across Africa, Asia, and the Americas. In the East African Community, Tanzania is ranked 65th, while Uganda is at 71st globally.

Factors Influencing the Rise The observed rise in rankings is primarily attributed to global mobility realignments, where declines in the relative standing of other nations inadvertently boosted the country’s position, rather than a substantial increase in direct visa-free access. Key contributing factors also include enhanced bilateral diplomatic ties and the widespread adoption of Electronic Travel Authorizations (eTA), which are categorized as visa-free access.

Challenges and Reforms Despite the positive ranking shift, the government has faced scrutiny over allegations of issuing passports to the Rapid Support Forces (RSF), a Sudanese paramilitary faction implicated in atrocities in Sudan. The Immigration Department, appearing before the National Assembly Committee on Monday, March 16, dismissed these claims, with Immigration Principal Secretary Belio Kipsang affirming that documents are exclusively granted to verified citizens. Concurrently, concerns have arisen regarding the nation's visa-free policy, specifically granting Nigeria visa-free access while citizens are reportedly required to pay over KSh10,000 to enter Nigeria. In response to these challenges and to strengthen travel and diplomatic standing, the government has implemented critical reforms. These include abolishing the Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA) for most African visitors and modernizing immigration systems to foster regional integration. Such measures underscore efforts to streamline processes and uphold national integrity. Recent judicial actions, such as the High Court ordering the forfeiture of a vehicle linked to a money laundering case, further highlight the ongoing commitment to robust legal and administrative frameworks across various sectors of governance.
