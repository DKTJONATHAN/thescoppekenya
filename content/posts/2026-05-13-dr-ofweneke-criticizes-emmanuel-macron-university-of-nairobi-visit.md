## The Decorum Test: Why Kenyans are Policing Global Leaders' Tone

"If you come to our house, take off your boots before you step on the carpet," declared one X user, perfectly capturing the blistering mood of Kenyans today. Emmanuel Macron's recent visit to the University of Nairobi has sparked a fiery national conversation about respect.

The visit, which was intended to showcase diplomatic warmth, has instead triggered a massive backlash. Comedian Dr Ofweneke led the charge, publicly slamming the French President for what he described as a condescending tone. While the political class often navigates these visits with quiet diplomacy, the public is now drawing a line in the sand, demanding that international figures treat Kenyan intellectual spaces with the same gravitas they would afford European halls of power. This is no longer just about trade agreements or summits; it is about the "dignity tax" that Kenyans now expect foreign leaders to pay.

### Voices from the Digital Frontline

The reaction on X has been swift and unforgiving. The narrative has shifted from awe at the stature of a global leader to a critical audit of his manners. The shift signifies a departure from the historical norms where international guests were granted an unquestioned platform.

One user wrote, "Ofweneke is right. We are past the era where we just clap for whoever flies in from Paris. The tone matters as much as the content." Another observer noted, "The colonial-era attitude died years ago, but some visitors haven't received the memo. Macron walked into the University of Nairobi like he was doing us a favor." Meanwhile, a third voice cut straight to the point: "It is about time we stopped acting like tourists in our own country. If you can't be respectful, don't come at all."

This digital consensus suggests that the "dignity tax" is a growing force. Much like the way citizens are currently monitoring local politics, such as when [Millie Odhiambo warns government against use of criminal gangs and hired goons](https://zandani.co.ke/article/millie-odhiambo-warns-government-against-use-of-criminal-gangs-and-h), there is a parallel demand for accountability in the public square. The public is essentially acting as a watchdog for national self-respect, refusing to let even the highest-ranking visitors slide when they exhibit what locals perceive as arrogance.

### Why it Matters Now

This pushback represents a pivot in Kenyan cultural identity. For decades, the presence of Western leaders was met with a passive, deferential reception. Today, that has been replaced by a desire for reciprocal respect. The criticism leveled by Dr Ofweneke is not just a personal opinion; it is a manifestation of a broader sentiment that Kenya is an equal partner on the global stage. 

The incident highlights a disconnect between traditional diplomatic protocols and the modern Kenyan public's expectations. While state officials might focus on the optics of the Africa Forward Summit, the average citizen is watching the micro-expressions and the cadence of the speech. This active monitoring means that any perceived slight is instantly magnified across social media, turning a university lecture into a test of character that many believe the French President failed. It is a reminder that in 2026, the global stage is also a local stage, and every guest is under the microscope.
