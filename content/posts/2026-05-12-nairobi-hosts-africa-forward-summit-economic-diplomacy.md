## Nairobi and the Africa Forward Summit: A Question of Dividends

Mr. President, as Nairobi prepares to host the Africa Forward 2026 summit, the city is adorned with the symbols of economic diplomacy. While your administration positions our capital as the preeminent diplomatic hub of the Global South, many citizens are left questioning whether this international prestige will ever offer relief from the rising cost of living or provide pathways to meaningful employment.

### The Mirage of Diplomatic Prestige

Your government has invested significantly in the belief that grand international events and the expansion of the United Nations complex will act as a catalyst for our local economy. We observe the heavy machinery currently breaking ground on a new conference center, which serves as a visible anchor for your administration’s trade-focused foreign policy. However, we must address the growing gap between these high-level maneuvers and the daily lives of Kenyans. 

Once the summit concludes and the security cordons are removed, the residents of areas like Kayole or Mukuru continue to face the pressures of persistent inflation and stagnant wages. Diplomacy is intended to be a strategic instrument for national development rather than an end in itself. If these summits do not translate into tangible outcomes-such as reduced energy costs, expanded access to credit for young innovators, or a streamlined regulatory environment-they risk being perceived as costly theater. We are rapidly becoming proficient at hosting global delegations while the foundational needs of our own population remain unmet.

### Silicon Savannah and the Reality of Local Enterprise

You frequently highlight the moniker of the Silicon Savannah when addressing foreign dignitaries, yet local tech entrepreneurs continue to navigate a regulatory landscape that appears to obstruct rather than nurture growth. While you pursue industrialization and financial reforms in partnership with nations like France, the average trader at Gikomba still contends with a tax regime that many find predatory. 

Furthermore, recent infrastructure failures, such as the flooding that incapacitated Mombasa Road and restricted access to the airport, underscore the inherent fragility of our domestic systems. It is challenging to project the image of a functional, modern economic hub when our infrastructure falters under minimal stress. If Nairobi is to serve as the diplomatic heartbeat of Africa, it must first be a resilient and functioning city for those who live and work within its borders every day.

### Integrity and the Foundation of Governance

There is a palpable sense that your administration prioritizes the optics of international approval over addressing internal governance concerns. While your government pursues shifts in visa services and engages in bold regional leadership maneuvers, public trust continues to be eroded by domestic scandals. Allegations involving senior officials, such as those concerning the Principal Secretary for Internal Security, suggest a level of institutional decay that no amount of diplomatic success can conceal. 

A nation cannot build its future solely upon the foundation of international towers when its own internal house lacks necessary integrity. Our youth do not require more conference facilities; they require a domestic environment where diligence and ingenuity are rewarded, rather than stifled by bureaucratic obstacles and corruption.

### The Path Toward Measurable Impact

The Africa Forward 2026 summit serves as a definitive litmus test for your trade-focused foreign policy. If this strategy remains a top-down branding effort, the disconnect between your diplomatic objectives and the struggles of the average citizen will only widen, potentially fueling further social disillusionment. 

Moving forward, we must closely observe the implementation of the investments promised during this summit. It is vital to monitor whether construction contracts for new international facilities prioritize local labor and supply chains, or if these economic opportunities are primarily captured by foreign entities, leaving Kenyans to bear the burden of construction while reaping few of the rewards. 

Mr. President, the burden of proof rests with your office to demonstrate that this summit delivers value beyond the diplomatic corps. We urge you to commit to a transparent and measurable timeline that outlines exactly how this diplomatic outreach will serve to lower the retail price of basic commodities and improve the ease of doing business for local entrepreneurs over the next twelve months. The measure of our success should not be the warmth of our welcome to guests, but the tangible improvement in the lives of our own people.
