## The Broken Promise Audit: Tracking the Ksh 30,000 PAYE Threshold Ghost

The National Treasury promised a lifeline for the struggling Kenyan workforce, but the Finance Bill 2026 tells a different story. While officials talked of tax relief, the fine print reveals an aggressive squeeze on the low-income bracket instead.

Treasury Cabinet Secretary John Mbadi spent weeks building a narrative of empathy. He signaled a policy shift intended to exempt earners making Ksh 30,000 or less from Pay-As-You-Earn (PAYE) taxes. It was a calculated move to offer breathing room to a population currently watching their purchasing power vanish as high cost of living exhausts salaries within days of payment. The public listened. They planned their budgets around this relief. Yet, as the Finance Bill 2026 landed in the public domain, the promised clause was nowhere to be found. 

### The Illusion of Relief vs. The Reality of Revenue

Former Law Society of Kenya President Faith Odhiambo has labeled this omission a definitive breach of institutional trust. The disconnect between verbal policy signaling and legislative drafting is not merely an oversight; it is a strategic maneuver that keeps the tax net tight around the most vulnerable. While the Ksh 30,000 threshold remains a phantom, other measures have gained traction with startling efficiency.

The Bill proposes a hike in residential rental income tax to 10 percent, up from the previous levels that provided some semblance of stability for small-scale landlords and their tenants. Simultaneously, the push for a 25 percent excise duty on mobile devices functions as a tax on connectivity in an economy increasingly dependent on digital transactions. When financial services lose their VAT exemptions, the cost of basic banking and digital money transfers will inevitably climb. For the ordinary Kenyan, the math is cruel. The government is effectively clawing back more than it ever intended to concede through the missing PAYE reform.

### Legal and Fiscal Accountability

The absence of the promised tax relief is not just a policy failure. It represents a cynical disregard for the social contract. Odhiambo’s critique highlights a growing pattern where public expectations are managed with optimistic rhetoric while the legislative apparatus is primed for maximum extraction. 

The strategy creates a dual burden. Low-income earners who were banking on a reprieve find their take-home pay stagnant, while their essential expenses-housing and mobile connectivity-are targeted for higher taxation. This is not fiscal prudence; it is an erosion of the bottom-tier taxpayer's ability to survive. By prioritizing revenue targets over the promised alleviation of financial pressure, the Treasury has made it clear whose interests are being guarded. The state has chosen the path of least resistance: taxing the people who have the least, rather than addressing the deeper structural failures of revenue collection.

### Why it matters now

The cost of living crisis is at a breaking point. With salaries disappearing almost as soon as they hit bank accounts, the promise of a Ksh 30,000 PAYE exemption was a significant point of hope. Its disappearance from the Finance Bill is a signal that the government views the low-income population as a primary source of tax liquidity, rather than a group requiring support. 

### What to watch next

Parliamentary debates on the Finance Bill 2026 will serve as the final test of accountability. Will the legislature demand the insertion of the promised relief, or will the Treasury continue to push for the 10 percent rental tax and the 25 percent excise duty on mobile devices unchallenged? Public attention must now shift from what officials say in briefings to the specific amendments proposed on the floor of the House.

If the promised threshold remains absent when the bill moves toward its final vote, what does that confirm about the integrity of the current fiscal policy?
