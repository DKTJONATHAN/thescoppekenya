## Why Your Wallet Feels Lighter Despite the NSE Record

The Nairobi Securities Exchange reached a historic valuation of KSh 3.812 trillion this July. Despite this milestone, most workers are discovering that their monthly budgets are more restricted than in previous years. This gap between large corporate valuations and individual purchasing power highlights a challenging reality within the current national economy.

### Is Corporate Wealth Actually Improving Your Daily Life?

Financial data from the Nairobi Securities Exchange shows a notable rise in market value, with capitalization increasing by KSh 817.2 billion over a six-month period. This upward trend is primarily driven by the banking sector and high-profile share movements, such as the significant block trade involving Safaricom that wrapped up in late June. 

It is important to understand that rising numbers on a trading board do not equate to more money in a typical Kenyan bank account. When a major firm sees its share price improve or a bank records strong earnings, this reflects value for shareholders. It is not an accurate indicator of general prosperity for the workforce. While investors see their portfolios grow, employees are often managing a different situation. The cost of essential items is rising, with inflation currently sitting at 6.4 percent. This inflation continuously erodes wages that have largely stayed the same. Much of the wealth generated on the exchange remains concentrated among institutional investors rather than the individuals whose labor drives company output.

### Why Banking Gains Do Not Lead to Cheaper Loans

A large part of the growth seen at the exchange originates from banks that performed well during the first half of 2026. While this proves that these institutions are financially stable, it does not mean that personal loans or business credit lines have become more affordable. High profitability in the banking sector often moves in lockstep with higher interest rates. 

When banks prioritize maximizing returns for their shareholders, the expense of borrowing money remains a burden for the average citizen. This leads to a situation where the banking sector benefits from high interest margins, while the customers who pay those interest rates struggle to manage their finances. Even as matatu operators increase fares and discuss potential nationwide strikes due to rising fuel prices, the primary concern for most Kenyans is the accessibility of credit and the cost of basic food items. The massive liquidity circulating through the exchange via major trades does not provide relief for the price of diesel at the pump or the cost of staple goods like maize flour.

### The Myth of the Wealth Effect

Economists often discuss the "wealth effect," a theory suggesting that as asset prices rise, people feel wealthier and subsequently increase their spending. This effect is missing for the majority of the working class in Kenya today. Most households lack the level of stock market exposure required to see their personal net worth climb alongside the NSE index. 

Instead of seeing an increase in disposable income, the economy appears to be segmented. Large corporations are successfully capitalizing on regional developments, such as the recent partnership between the DRC and the IFC to create a new national stock exchange, which aims to boost cross-border investment. However, for a professional earning a salary in Nairobi or Kisumu, these large-scale economic maneuvers provide no immediate relief from the rising cost of living. 

The record-breaking performance of the exchange is a clear sign of market development, but it should not be viewed as a gauge for individual financial stability. If you are hoping to see a change in your personal finances, it is more practical to ignore the market ticker. Instead, monitor your existing debt obligations and the inflationary trends that impact the price of your daily commute and your monthly grocery list. Ultimately, the exchange tracks the value of what investors own. Your personal budget, however, is shaped by what you can afford to pay for your daily needs.
