## Truecaller Pivots to eSIMs as Ad Revenue Slumps

Truecaller is betting its future on cross-border travel data, launching an eSIM service across 29 countries to counteract a 27 percent decline in sales. As advertising income falters, the platform is evolving from a simple caller ID utility into a travel companion, marking a significant strategic departure for the Swedish tech giant.

For those of us in Lagos, Nairobi, and Cairo, Truecaller has long been the primary shield against the persistent annoyance of spam calls. Yet, the company's recent financial disclosures reveal a 44 percent drop in ad revenue, a reality that forced the recent layoff of 70 employees. It is becoming increasingly evident that the reliance on ad-based models has reached its limits. Consequently, the firm is aggressively pivoting toward subscription services. By leveraging its massive 500 million user base, the company aims to disrupt the expensive roaming landscape that continues to stifle African travelers moving between major regional business hubs.

### Transitioning from Spam Defense to Connectivity

The newly introduced eSIM functionality offers data bundles between 1 GB and 20 GB, with durations ranging from seven to 30 days. For the frequent African business traveler, this feature provides a potential path to bypass the exorbitant roaming fees often levied by domestic telcos when traversing markets such as Nigeria or South Africa. By integrating this service directly into an app already present on millions of handsets, Truecaller is attempting to convert its passive utility into a consistent revenue stream.

The financial pressure on the firm is undeniably palpable. With Q1 2026 sales reporting at $39 million, the company is under immense strain to diversify. Although regulatory hurdles have stalled the service in India, the company is aggressively expanding elsewhere. Converting casual users into paid travel-data subscribers is a bold maneuver to stabilize the balance sheet. However, they face stiff competition. Specialized travel-tech startups have spent years cultivating deep relationships with local tower providers to secure competitive pricing that an app-based overlay may find difficult to replicate.

### Navigating the Future of Digital Utility

This shift represents a fundamental evolution in how digital utilities endure in a volatile market. When advertising sectors cool, tech companies invariably look toward the hardware and infrastructure stack. By facilitating global connectivity, Truecaller is trying to transform from an app users open to silence callers into one they rely on to stay connected while traveling. This is a high-stakes transition. If they succeed, they cement themselves as a permanent fixture in the travel-tech ecosystem. If they fail, they risk alienating a user base that has grown accustomed to free services. This pivot is more than a feature update; it is a search for sustainability as the era of easy, ad-supported revenue fades into the past.

### The Regional Roadblock

The primary challenge lies in cost parity with local African telcos. While roaming remains notoriously expensive, regional operators are becoming increasingly savvy, offering competitive intra-African roaming packages that cater to budget-conscious travelers. Truecaller must ensure its pricing is significantly more attractive than these established incumbents, or it will be ignored by those carefully managing their expenses. Furthermore, investors should monitor how the company handles its leaner workforce following the recent layoffs, as maintaining a global eSIM network requires high-level operational support and constant optimization.
