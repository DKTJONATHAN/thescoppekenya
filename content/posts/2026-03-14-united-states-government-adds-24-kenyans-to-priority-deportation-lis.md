## United States Expands Priority Deportation List for East African Nationals The United States government has augmented its list of 'worst of the worst' illegal immigrants, increasing the number of individuals from the East African nation targeted for priority deportation from 15 to 28. Compiled by the U.S. immigration department, this expanded list focuses on individuals living in America illegally who have been convicted of serious criminal offenses, marking them as priority cases for detention and removal.

Expanded Priority List Details The U.S. Department of Homeland Security (DHS) has officially added 13 more individuals to its priority deportation list within the last two months, bringing the total to 28. This initiative is part of an intensified immigration enforcement drive, with those included considered priority cases for detention and deportation due to their severe criminal offenses. The database, which tracks undocumented immigrants arrested and convicted across all 50 U.S. states, was initially launched by DHS in December 2025. U.S. authorities emphasize that this system is particularly crucial in preparation for the 2026 FIFA World Cup, which the United States will co-host starting in July.

Nature of Offences The additional individuals placed on the list have been convicted of a range of serious crimes. These include drug trafficking, armed robbery, manslaughter, sexual assault and child molestation, domestic violence, cruelty to children, and aggravated assault. Other offenses cited in similar cases involve fighting to evade prosecution, simple assault, terror-related threats, money laundering, cheque forgery, driving under the influence, fraud, kidnapping a minor, violation of a court order, and receiving stolen property. DHS officials describe these individuals as posing "significant public safety risks."

Voluntary Departure Incentives and Cost Savings To streamline the removal of illegal immigrants, President Donald Trump's administration has continued to promote a self-deportation application, now known as the CBP Home app. This mobile application is designed to allow undocumented immigrants in the U.S. to voluntarily leave the country, circumventing arrest and formal deportation proceedings. To encourage participation, the U.S. government has introduced financial incentives, including a $2,600 stipend, along with free travel assistance and the forgiveness of any civil fines for failing to depart the country. This program is presented as a financially prudent strategy. The current cost of a single enforced deportation is significantly higher than a self-deportation through the app, which saves American taxpayers over $13,000 per individual. Such fiscal discipline in government initiatives highlights broader discussions on efficient resource allocation, resonating with the principles outlined in "Fiscal discipline needed to guard against costly polls: The Unseen Costs of Our Democracy." Since President Trump's return to office in January 2025, nearly 3 million illegal aliens have departed the U.S. due to the administration's intensified efforts, with an estimated 2.2 million self-deportations and over 675,000 enforced deportations. President Trump has described this move as integral to what he promised would be the largest deportation campaign in U.S. history.
