## The Surveillance Tax on Kenya’s School Infrastructure

The fire at Sameta Boys Senior School has forced a sudden shift in education policy, prioritizing high-tech surveillance over basic fire mitigation. While the Ministry of Education pushes for mandatory CCTV, the financial viability of this directive for rural public boarding schools remains critically unexamined.

### A Familiar Reaction to Policy Pressure

Following the incident in Kisii County on June 1, Education Cabinet Secretary Julius Ogamba joined Interior officials in calling for immediate, nationwide boarding school audits. The ministry is pivoting toward a standard that requires every dormitory block to be monitored via digital surveillance. This reaction mirrors the frantic policy pivots seen after the May 28 emergency at Utumishi Girls Academy in Gilgil, where infrastructure safety was once again questioned under the glare of public scrutiny. The pattern is cyclical: a dormitory burns, the ministry mandates a technological upgrade, and local schools are left to scramble for the capital.

The shift toward electronic surveillance represents a significant departure from the traditional emphasis on physical fire safety measures like smoke detectors, fire extinguishers, and emergency exits. While digital eyes provide accountability, they offer zero protection against the electrical faults that frequently plague aging school grids. By mandating an infrastructure-heavy solution, the government is inadvertently imposing an unfunded mandate on rural institutions already struggling with basic maintenance costs.

### The Fiscal Burden of Mandated Tech

Public boarding schools in Kenya operate on razor-thin margins. Integrating a comprehensive CCTV network requires not only the capital expenditure for cameras and storage servers but also a reliable power supply and consistent internet connectivity. Many rural schools currently lack the stable electricity required to maintain these systems. When [Kenya Secures Ksh227 Million for Water and Energy Infrastructure](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenya-secures-ksh227-million-for-water-and-energy-infrastructure), those funds are targeted at broad utility projects, leaving individual school boards to bear the brunt of security procurement.

This creates a high risk of localized procurement corruption. Without centralized oversight or a dedicated state fund, schools may be forced to contract private, predatory vendors to meet the ministry's compliance deadline. These contracts often carry long-term service and maintenance agreements that bleed school budgets dry. This financial strain mirrors the broader economic environment where revenue collection is prioritized over service delivery, similar to the tension seen in the recent [2026-05-04-kra-whistleblower-tips-recover-kes-6-8-billion-in-taxes](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-05-04-kra-whistleblower-tips-recover-kes-6-8-billion-in-taxes), where the focus remains on top-down enforcement rather than supporting the operational stability of the grassroots sectors.

### Procurement Risks and Maintenance Realities

The history of educational policy in the country shows a recurring trend where mandates are issued without accompanying budget allocations. Historically, when a requirement is issued for school safety, it is often treated as a project to be funded by parents through increased school levies. Forcing parents to subsidize the cost of state-mandated security cameras at a time of rising commodity prices and stagnant income is a recipe for further social friction.

Moreover, a system of cameras is only as effective as the personnel monitoring them. In many cases, these surveillance feeds end up unmonitored, rendering the initial investment futile. A focus on hardware ignores the deeper, systemic issues of building safety, electrical certification, and fire-hazard compliance. By prioritizing the appearance of safety through CCTV, the administration risks sidelining the more expensive but necessary work of upgrading the actual structures where students sleep. The state continues to buy expensive digital window dressing while the foundations remain as brittle as ever.
