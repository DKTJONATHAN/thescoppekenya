## Kenya's Automated Traffic Fines System Faces Judicial Halt Kenya's National Transport and Safety Authority (NTSA) recently launched an automated instant fines system, which has now been temporarily suspended by the High Court. This innovative system, designed to detect traffic violations via cameras and issue SMS penalties, faces constitutional challenges from petitioners arguing it bypasses fundamental due process rights without human intervention.

NTSA's Automated System Rollout The NTSA introduced its Instant Fines Management System, described as "fully automated and operating without human intervention," to streamline traffic enforcement. The system employed road cameras to identify traffic violations, read number plates, and subsequently send penalty notices directly to motorists via SMS. Fines imposed through this system ranged from KES 500 to KES 10,000, with a seven-day window for drivers to remit payments via KCB Bank channels.

Constitutional Concerns and Due Process The system's implementation was challenged by petitioners Sheria Mtaani and advocate Shadrack Wambui. They argue that issuing fines through text messages, generated by a camera and an algorithm without an officer, a hearing, or an opportunity for explanation, strips Kenyans of basic constitutional protections. Their core contention is that individuals are required to pay a penalty before having a chance to exercise their right to be heard.

High Court's Intervention and Future Outlook Milimani High Court Justice Bahati Mwamuye has issued a conservatory order that temporarily restrains the NTSA, the State Law Office, KCB Bank Kenya, and all related parties from enforcing the automated traffic penalties. The order specifically prohibits the issuance, generation, demand, or enforcement of instant or automated traffic penalties produced through algorithmic or other automated decision-making systems. This judicial intervention highlights the critical balance between leveraging technological innovation for efficiency and safeguarding citizens' fundamental constitutional rights. The case is poised to determine the legality of automated, contactless enforcement within Kenya's legal framework, specifically addressing whether motorists' rights necessitate human involvement in the loop. The next court mention is scheduled for April 9, 2026, where further directions on the full hearing are expected. This case underscores a critical need for transparent and rights-respecting mechanisms in all state actions, a concern that also arises in other contexts such as the KeNHA demolitions affecting traders.
