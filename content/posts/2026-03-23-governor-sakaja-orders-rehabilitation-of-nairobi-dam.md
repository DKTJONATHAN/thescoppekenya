Nairobi County Governor Johnson Sakaja on Monday, March 23, 2026, initiated a full-scale rehabilitation of Nairobi Dam in the Kibera area. This directive follows a multi-agency assessment conducted after a weekend evacuation order, addressing critical structural and safety concerns posed by rising water levels and historical degradation.

Governor Outlines Rehabilitation Strategy

Sacrifices for Long-Term Benefit Governor Sakaja emphasized a resolute approach to the Nairobi Dam issue, stating that a select few must endure temporary inconvenience for the enduring benefit of all Nairobi residents. "We have made the decision that, for all Nairobians to enjoy, a few have to suffer. Therefore, we have made a concise decision to restore Nairobi Dam, so that people living around the facility live a life of dignity," Sakaja declared. This stance reflects a broader commitment to decisive action on challenging issues, aligning with firm directives on governance and public safety like those highlighted in Za Ndani's coverage on [PS Raymond Omollo Declares Zero Tolerance: Unpacking the Hypocrisy of Political Violence in Kenya](https://zandani.co.ke/article/ps-raymond-omollo-zero-tolerance-political-violence).

Comprehensive Three-Pronged Approach Sakaja presented a clear three-pronged strategy to address both immediate and long-term concerns. The first immediate priority involves clearing debris and blockages downstream of the spillway to restore natural water flow and alleviate pressure on the dam walls. Subsequently, geotechnical surveys will be conducted to assess the embankment's structural integrity, providing essential data for long-term restoration. Finally, a 30-metre protective barrier will be erected around the dam's perimeter to prevent further encroachment, ensure public safety, and protect the infrastructure from ongoing degradation.

Decades of Degradation During the site visit, Governor Sakaja acknowledged the dam's extensive history, dating back to 1953. Originally constructed for recreation and flood management along the Ngong and Mitumi rivers, the dam has progressively deteriorated. Sakaja identified pollution, sewage, encroachment, and uncontrolled settlements as the primary challenges posing serious risks to communities residing nearby. The county boss stressed that these interventions aim for both flood prevention and crucial environmental protection, alongside restoring security to long-affected communities.

Urgent Evacuation Mandate The Nairobi Dam, situated in the Kibera area approximately five kilometres southwest of Nairobi's CBD, places thousands of residents at risk of potential disaster if it were to breach. Before Sakaja's rehabilitation order, the Water Resources Authority (WRA) had issued an urgent evacuation notice on Friday, March 20, 2026, directing residents of six nearby estates to vacate immediately due to ongoing heavy rains. The WRA's notice unequivocally stated the dam was on the verge of breaching its embankments. Residents in informal settlements in Kibera, Nyayo Highrise, Dam Estate, Lang'ata, Nairobi West, and Madaraka were instructed to relocate to higher ground without delay. Authorities continue to urge compliance with evacuation orders as rehabilitation work commences.
