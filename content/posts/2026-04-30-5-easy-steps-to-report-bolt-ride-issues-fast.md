## Kenya's Governance and Economic Headwinds

Kenya's political landscape in April 2026 is marked by persistent public dissatisfaction with service delivery, despite government efforts at digitalization. While economic growth is projected, high debt and living costs remain significant challenges. Internal political tensions and shifts in power dynamics underscore a complex governance environment.

### Public Service and Accountability Concerns

The efficacy of public service delivery remains a critical concern across Kenya, with recent findings highlighting widespread dissatisfaction. A January 2026 Afrobarometer survey indicated that many Kenyans struggle to access vital public services, expressing particular difficulty in obtaining police assistance, identity documents, and healthcare. Reports suggest that a majority of citizens seeking police assistance (69%) or identity documents (67%) faced difficulties, with a significant number resorting to bribery for these services. This discontent is particularly pronounced in rural areas, where essential infrastructure like piped water systems and police stations are less available compared to urban centers.

Government initiatives aimed at improving service access through digitalization, such as Huduma Centres, have seen mixed results. While some administrative functions, like tax collection and certain document processing, have improved, systemic inefficiencies persist. The National Transport and Safety Authority (NTSA) has recently announced new timelines for smart driving licences, with processing expected to take between 4 and 8 weeks, a significant deviation from earlier promises of a five-day to three-week period. This situation exacerbates existing delays, impacting citizens' employment and travel. The NTSA has responded by entering a Public-Private Partnership (PPP) to reduce production times to within 48 hours and plans to establish 102 new enrollment centres across the country. ### Economic Trajectory and Fiscal Stance

Economically, Kenya projects a robust outlook for 2026, with the Treasury forecasting a 5.3 percent growth rate, building on a 5.0 percent expansion in 2025. This growth is anticipated to be driven by enhanced agricultural productivity, sustained expansion in the services sector, and increased diaspora remittances. The government emphasizes a commitment to fiscal consolidation, including reforms in domestic revenue collection, controlled spending, and digitalization of public finance systems.

Despite these positive projections, the nation grapples with considerable economic challenges. High public debt, accumulated during the previous administration and exacerbated by heavy reliance on borrowing, continues to exert pressure on fiscal policy. Reports indicate Kenya's debt service to revenue ratio stood at 67.1 percent as of May 2025, significantly above the IMF's recommended 30 percent threshold. Rising living costs and the operational hurdles of flagship initiatives, such as the Hustler Fund, which has faced high default rates, contribute to public discontent. President William Ruto, however, maintains that the economy is on a path to transformation, citing improvements in various sectors and a goal for Kenya to achieve first-world economy status within a generation.

### Shifting Political Dynamics

The Kenyan political arena demonstrates signs of fluidity and internal strain within the ruling coalition. Recent accusations from the Orange Democratic Movement (ODM) allege that top officials in President William Ruto's administration are attempting to induce its Members of Parliament to defect, offering significant campaign funds. This adds to a narrative of deepening political instability, following events such as the impeachment of Deputy President Rigathi Gachagua and the subsequent appointment of Kithure Kindiki, alongside the co-optation of opposition figures into government.

The contentious Finance Bill 2024, which proposed new tax measures, sparked widespread public protests that culminated in the storming of Parliament buildings. In response, President Ruto dissolved his cabinet and announced measures to address the concerns of primarily young protestors. Debates also persist regarding the efficiency and number of devolved county governments, with proposals to reduce the current 47 units for economic reasons, counterbalanced by arguments for preserving Kenya's plurinational identity.
