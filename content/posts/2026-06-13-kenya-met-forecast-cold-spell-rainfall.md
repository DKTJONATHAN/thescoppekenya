## Kenya Faces A Sharp Climate Divide

The Kenya Meteorological Service has issued an alert for a five-day weather shift beginning today, June 13, that will see central highlands endure bone-chilling 4-degree temperatures while northern corridors grapple with searing 38-degree heat.

This extreme 34-degree temperature delta marks a stark bifurcation in the country’s economic and environmental landscape. As the Central Rift and high-altitude regions prepare for a damp, cold spell lasting through June 17, the arid north remains locked in an intense heat cycle. These climatic extremes are forcing disparate regional economies to adjust their survival strategies in real time.

In high-altitude zones like Nyandarua, Nyeri, and Kericho, the mercury is projected to plunge toward 4 degrees Celsius. Residents in these areas are advised to brace for increased cloud cover and persistent drizzle. Meteorological officials emphasize that this specific environmental combination significantly elevates the risk of respiratory infections, particularly among the elderly and children, necessitating immediate health precautions.

Conversely, counties such as Mandera, Wajir, and Turkana face the opposite challenge. Temperatures are set to peak at 38 degrees, continuing a streak of extreme heat that demands rigorous water management and livestock cooling protocols. While these northern regions often endure harsh conditions, the current intensity tests the limits of their drought-resilient infrastructure.

### Economic Costs of the Deep Freeze

The cold snap poses a direct threat to the agricultural productivity of the Central and Western regions. Dairy farmers are already reporting concerns regarding declining milk yields, as livestock metabolic rates drop under the stress of sustained low temperatures. Similarly, tea plantations across the highlands expect a temporary slowdown in leaf maturation due to the lack of sufficient sunlight and persistent damp conditions.

This disruption adds pressure to an agricultural sector already struggling with the broader macroeconomic environment. While recent adjustments in national energy costs, such as the [2026-04-30-kenya-electricity-prices-drop](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-30-kenya-electricity-prices-drop), provided some relief to agro-processing firms, this sudden weather shift threatens to offset those gains by increasing heating and maintenance overheads for factories located in the cooler zones.

### Strategic Heat Management in the North

In the Northeastern corridors, the focus remains on the economic necessity of livestock protection. Pastoralist communities are deploying mobile water trucking strategies to mitigate the impact of the 38-degree highs. These heat levels are particularly unforgiving for herds, forcing a rapid adjustment in grazing hours to avoid peak solar intensity.

The divide highlights the vulnerability of regional value chains to sudden climate shifts. While the North adapts to heat, the South faces cold-induced output volatility. This structural variance confirms that national economic resilience is inextricably linked to localized climate management. As businesses prepare to report under [Kenyan Public Firms to Adopt New IFRS Sustainability Standards](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenyan-public-firms-to-adopt-new-ifrs-sustainability-standards), the costs of these weather-related interruptions are expected to feature prominently in future fiscal disclosures.

The meteorological department will continue to monitor the progression of these high and low-pressure systems through mid-week. Observers should look for updated advisories on Tuesday regarding whether this cold front will linger beyond the current five-day window or if a warming trend will reclaim the central highlands.
