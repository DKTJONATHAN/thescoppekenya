The net groaned, a deep, protesting sound that echoed across the steel-grey waters of the Baltic. Jan Karlsson, his hands raw from the salt and cold, leaned into the winch, his eyes fixed on the straining ropes. It was 1978, off the coast of Bornholm, and a good haul was a blessing. He imagined cod, perhaps some flatfish - the bounty the sea promised. Instead, a heavy, encrusted lump, the size of a child's head, tumbled onto the deck. It was not fish. It was dark, waxy, and smelled faintly of garlic, a smell he had learned to dread. Jan knew this shape, this peculiar weight. Another ghost of the war. He cursed, a low, guttural sound, as the familiar burning sensation bloomed on his skin where the dark substance had touched him. His father had suffered worse. Many had. The sea, which gave life, now regularly offered death, a silent, submerged legacy from a conflict supposedly ended decades before.

## From Bornholm's Depths: Homa Bay Fishermen's Explosives Discovery

For years, the tales of the Baltic's poisoned depths were a grim litany among those who plied its waters. After the cannons had fallen silent in 1945, the victorious powers, in their infinite wisdom, had decided the surest way to rid the continent of its chemical nightmares was to entrust them to the deep. Tonnes upon tonnes of mustard gas, arsenic compounds, and other unspeakable agents, once intended to choke and blister, found new resting places in the Bornholm Basin, off the shores of Gotland, and within the Skagerrak. The sea, an unwitting accomplice, swallowed the evidence. Yet, the ocean held no secrets forever. Jan’s grandfather had seen the ships, the purposeful dumping, the dark crates sinking beneath the waves. The official pronouncement was peace, but for the fishermen, peace was a slow, seeping poison, rising from the seabed in their nets, decades later.

### The Legacy of Yperite and Arsenic

The injuries were legendary in the fishing villages. A Danish fisherman, in 1988, mistook a lump of solidified yperite for amber and felt his skin erupt in angry blisters, a chemical burn that would mark him for life. Children, playing on beaches near Darłowo in 1955, found corroded barrels and suffered agonizing burns when they touched the leaking contents. The chemical agents, though submerged, did not simply disappear. They corroded their containers, transforming into viscous, sometimes wax-like, forms that clung to nets and caused severe harm. The sea, patiently, yielded these hidden truths, reminding each generation that the decisions of the powerful had consequences that rippled far beyond the immediate moment of conflict.

### Litare Beach: When the Waters Yielded Iron

A generation later, a continent away, the sun beat down on the shimmering expanse of Lake Victoria. The air was thick with the scent of freshwater and anticipation. On Sunday, March 1, 2026, the fishermen of Litare Beach in Mbita cast their nets, hoping for a catch to feed their families and sustain their humble lives. They felt the familiar resistance, a heavy drag, and their hearts quickened. *A big one,* they thought, pulling with practiced rhythm, their muscles straining against the weight. But the haul that emerged from the depths was no silver-scaled Nile Perch, no bountiful harvest of tilapia. Instead, their nets brought forth a box, ancient and rusted, its contents an ominous collection of metal objects - live bombs.

The joyous cries of a good catch died in their throats, replaced by a chilling silence, then a rising tide of fear. Just twenty-four hours later, the scene repeated itself at Koguna Beach, where another group of fishermen made a similar alarming discovery, their nets yielding fourteen additional explosives. The devices, described by authorities as propellant-type, some even containing bullets, lay on the shore, stark reminders of a past that refused to stay buried. These were not the spoils of a peaceful harvest, but the insidious remnants of forgotten conflicts, rising like spectres from the very waters that nurtured life.

### A Call to the Kisumu Bomb Disposal Unit

The fear intensified among residents. The lake, which had always been a source of sustenance and solace, now held hidden dangers. Police were quickly alerted, securing the perilous cargo. The recovered devices were transported to the Mbita Sub-County Police Headquarters in Homa Bay County, awaiting safe disposal by the specialized Bomb Disposal and Hazardous Materials Unit based in Kisumu. It was not the first time. In 2019, explosives believed to date back to the colonial era were found, hinting at a long-buried history of violence resurfacing. The irony, sharp as a fisherman's hook, lay in the unchanging nature of the danger. From the cold, grey expanse of the Baltic, where the victors of one war carelessly discarded their poisons, to the warm, life-giving waters of Lake Victoria, where the echoes of colonial power and past skirmishes refused to dissipate, ordinary people found themselves grappling with the deadly debris of other men's conflicts.
