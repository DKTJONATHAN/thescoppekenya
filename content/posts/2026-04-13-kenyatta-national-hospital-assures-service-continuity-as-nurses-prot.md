Kenyatta National Hospital (KNH) is assuring the public of continuous specialized healthcare services despite a looming industrial action by nurses represented by the Kenya National Union of Nurses (KNUN). The union's grievances, centered on delayed statutory remittances and staff welfare, threaten a significant strike scheduled for today, Monday, April 13, 2026, prompting KNH to engage in active dialogue to resolve the issues.

Addressing Staff Welfare and Service Continuity

Nurses Detail Grievances Amidst Funding Concerns The Kenya National Union of Nurses and Midwives (KNUNM) Secretary General Seth Panyako has voiced serious concerns regarding the treatment of nurses at KNH, citing inadequate funding as a core issue. Panyako highlighted that numerous nurses have been on casual contracts for four to six years, receiving salaries that banks often retain due to unremitted statutory deductions. This situation leaves many staff unable to access their earnings. Furthermore, Panyako criticized what he termed unfair hiring practices, where newly appointed personnel reportedly earn more than long-serving casual employees. Such long-standing employment issues, particularly regarding casualisation and remuneration discrepancies, echo broader systemic challenges, similar to concerns highlighted in the TSC ruling involving Fred Apima Obita [cite: https://zandani.co.ke/article/tsc-cruel-blunder-fred-apima-obita-tsc-ruling-exposes-rot]. Nurses are demanding immediate answers, declaring "enough is enough" as they consider downing tools today.

KNH Responds to Union Demands In a statement issued on Sunday, April 12, KNH acknowledged the concerns raised by KNUN and the possibility of industrial action. The hospital clarified that the dispute primarily involves delays in remitting statutory and third-party deductions, not unpaid salaries, confirming that all staff salaries have been processed and paid up to March 2026. KNH attributed the timing of remittances to cash-flow cycles and reimbursement processes linked to national health financing arrangements, noting that a significant portion of its operational funding is derived from these frameworks. On pension contributions, the hospital affirmed its operation within the existing pension administration framework to ensure compliance. KNH also addressed concerns about nurses on contract terms, stating that many healthcare workers have transitioned to permanent and pensionable employment over the past years, with ongoing discussions among relevant stakeholders to resolve outstanding disputes through established consultation mechanisms. Despite KNH's reassurances, it remains uncertain whether the nurses will proceed with their planned strike, a move that could severely impact the country's largest referral hospital.
