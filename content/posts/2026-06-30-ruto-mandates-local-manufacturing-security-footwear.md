## The Cobblers Left in the Cold

President Ruto has decreed that Kenyan security forces will now wear exclusively locally produced footwear, promising 20,000 jobs. Yet, in the cramped stalls of Kariokor, the artisans who have powered the nation's leather trade for decades are not celebrating; they are counting the prohibitive costs of the tender process.

### The Barrier to Entry

The directive sounds noble on the podium. By forcing the hand of the state to purchase boots and shoes from local manufacturers, the government claims it will spark an industrial revolution in the leather sector. However, the procurement rules for security agencies tell a different story. To secure a contract for military or police footwear, a firm must demonstrate massive scale, sophisticated machinery, and significant cash reserves to survive the notoriously slow government payment cycles.

Kariokor’s master cobblers operate on lean margins with manual tools. They lack the capital to warehouse thousands of units while waiting for a treasury release that often lags by months. Past import-substitution policies failed these exact workers because the eligibility criteria acted as a filter, favoring established industrial giants over the informal sector. When a tender demands ISO certification, a verified factory footprint, and tax compliance documentation that requires a full-time accountant, the street-level artisan is disqualified before the first sample is even stitched.

### Centralized Hubs and Political Capture

The government is betting on the 47 County Aggregation and Industrial Parks to bridge this gap. The vision is to provide centralized infrastructure where small producers can allegedly plug into the supply chain. But geography is not the only hurdle. History suggests that when the state centralizes manufacturing power, the contracts gravitate toward politically connected procurement firms that outsource the actual labor to the lowest bidder.

While the rhetoric flows freely about supporting domestic manufacturing, the reality is a system where the "industrial park" becomes a gated warehouse for intermediaries. These middlemen win the tenders through lobbying and connections, then squeeze the actual leather workers on pricing to ensure their own margins. If the state continues to ignore the financial insolvency of the informal sector, these parks will serve as empty monuments to political branding rather than engines for the 20,000 jobs promised.

The struggle is not about the quality of the leather. Kenyan artisans are already supplying the commercial market with durable products. The friction lies in the disconnect between the government's procurement standards and the reality of how business is conducted at the grassroots level. While the country reels from the economic strain that led to recent transport chaos and strikes, as seen when [Matatu operators hiked fares and announced a strike over fuel prices](https://zandani.co.ke/article/matatu-operators-increase-fares-and-plan-nationwide-strike), the average worker needs direct access to the state budget, not more layers of bureaucratic middlemen.

### The Paper Trail of Exclusion

The procurement guidelines for the police and military have remained rigid, emphasizing historical supply capacity over potential growth. Without a specific carve-out or a tiered bidding process that allows cooperatives of Kariokor artisans to bid for smaller, decentralized tranches of the contract, the mandate will remain a closed loop.

Evidence from previous manufacturing drives shows that capital tends to follow existing influence. When contracts for state supplies are announced, firms with the legal and financial muscle to engage in government lobbying consistently beat out the informal sector. Unless the government mandates that a specific percentage of these security contracts must go to small-scale, non-corporate entities, the "local manufacturing" policy is simply a rebranding of old patronage networks.

If the government genuinely intends to empower the 20,000 workers it mentions in its projections, why are the tender requirements being designed to suit large-scale factories rather than the vibrant, existing artisan clusters that are currently the backbone of the local leather trade?
