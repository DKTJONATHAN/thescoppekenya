## Manchester United's Midfield Misery: Fernandes Saga Exposes Ineptitude Again

Tottenham's reported surge for Mateus Fernandes, coupled with Real Madrid's swift exit, paints a damning picture for Manchester United's transfer strategy. What was once "nailed on" for the Red Devils now appears to be another expensive miss, confirming their status as a consistently outmaneuvered Goliath in the market.

### The Art of Being Outmaneuvered

Here we are again, watching Manchester United fall short in a transfer pursuit they once supposedly led. The mainstream narrative suggests Mateus Fernandes simply "favours a move to Tottenham," a convenient platitude designed to mask the underlying truth: United were outmaneuvered. Schira's latest update on Tuesday claiming Spurs have "overtaken" United, even securing Fernandes's availability for a long-term contract, merely confirms what many already suspected. While Real Madrid, ever decisive, simply pulled the plug and pivoted to Enzo Fernandez to appease Jose Mourinho, United are left scrambling, seemingly always playing catch-up in a race they thought they were winning.

### The Agent's Puppet Show and West Ham's Windfall

Let's not pretend this is simply about player preference. When Jorge Mendes is involved, the script is usually written by cold, hard cash and strategic posturing. West Ham, fresh from relegation, are undoubtedly relishing this bidding war, with figures like £70m being thrown around for a player from a Championship club. Even if Fernandes performed well, is that figure genuinely reflective of his market value, or the premium applied when two wealthy Premier League clubs and an agent of Mendes's calibre are playing a high-stakes game? This isn't about footballing fit; it's about maximised profit, and Mendes is a master conductor of such symphonies.

### United's Familiar Fumbling

The Fernandes saga is just another chapter in United's long-running manual on how *not* to conduct transfer business. They were "nailed on," then "working very, very hard," and now, seemingly, second-best. Gary Neville's pragmatic suggestion of Felix Nmecha as a cheaper alternative highlights the club's perceived lack of a coherent strategy. Why chase a £70m-£100m player when cheaper, perhaps equally effective, options are available if you conduct thorough scouting and decisive negotiations? Until United shed their reputation for indecision and inflated bids, they will continue to be targets for every opportunistic agent and selling club looking to extract maximum value from their desperation.
