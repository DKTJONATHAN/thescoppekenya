## Junior Secondary School Autonomy Demands

Permanent Secretary Julius Bitok is scheduled to meet with Junior Secondary School representatives in Naivasha today to negotiate a structural decoupling of JSS programs from primary school management, a move intended to resolve systemic financial and administrative friction points.

The meeting addresses a growing crisis of institutional identity within the Competency-Based Curriculum framework. Currently, JSS instructors operate under the direct oversight of primary school headteachers, a hybrid arrangement that many educators argue is fundamentally incompatible with the specialized demands of secondary education. This administrative limbo has left teachers trapped in a management structure designed for foundational schooling rather than the more rigorous academic requirements of the junior secondary level.

Data from the Kenya Junior Secondary Teachers Association (KEJUSTA) suggests the current model creates significant bottlenecks. Financial resources allocated for JSS programs frequently become entangled in primary school ledgers, leading to delays in procurement for essential learning materials and laboratory equipment. These logistical hurdles have stifled the implementation of the CBC, forcing teachers to navigate primary-level bureaucracy that lacks the technical capacity to support advanced pedagogical needs.

The professional friction between JSS staff and primary school administrators has reached a boiling point. Many headteachers, accustomed to the traditional management of primary learners, reportedly struggle to adapt to the specialized staff requirements and instructional autonomy demanded by JSS professionals. This misalignment has triggered a push for a distinct management tier, specifically designed to oversee the unique financial and operational mandates of the JSS curriculum.

### Structural Friction and Operational Bottlenecks

The primary frustration stems from the lack of a defined budgetary pathway for Junior Secondary School. Under the current arrangement, headteachers exercise control over funds that are earmarked for JSS activities. KEJUSTA representatives claim this creates a persistent power struggle that prevents teachers from accessing the resources necessary to meet curriculum standards. This creates a disconnect similar to other sectors facing institutional overreach, much like [Thee Pluto's Automated Trading: A High-Risk Hustle](https://zandani.co.ke/article/thee-plutos-automated-trading-high-risk-hustle) where a lack of clear operational oversight often leads to volatile outcomes for participants.

Administrative independence is framed as a mandatory requirement for educational progress. Educators argue that without a separate budget line and a dedicated management structure, the JSS program will remain a secondary thought in the broader school agenda. The demand is not for a shift in base salary, but for the creation of a professional environment where JSS leadership can account directly to the Ministry of Education, removing the layers of primary school administration that act as a buffer between the ministry and the classroom.

### Stakeholder Reaction and Institutional Reform

Teacher unions, including KUPPET, have rallied behind the push for autonomy, arguing that the status quo undermines the professionalism of JSS staff. They maintain that the current placement of these instructors is an administrative relic that refuses to recognize the distinct nature of secondary-level instruction. By demanding a formal separation, these groups hope to force the Ministry of Education to acknowledge that JSS programs require specialized administrative support that current primary school management is ill-equipped to provide.

Government officials maintain that the goal of today's meeting is to streamline operations. While the Ministry has been cautious about committing to a full separation of assets and management, the pressure from KEJUSTA and other stakeholders has made the status quo increasingly untenable. Officials are now under scrutiny to provide a roadmap that satisfies the need for operational efficiency while ensuring that the transition does not disrupt ongoing learning cycles across the 47 counties.

### Why It Matters Now

The transition to the CBC model was marketed as a foundational shift in how students interact with knowledge, but the administrative implementation has lagged behind the pedagogical vision. The current bottleneck is not merely an internal dispute between staff members; it is a structural failure that threatens the credibility of the entire curriculum. Without resolving these administrative hurdles, the government risks failing to deliver the specialized environment necessary to support the advanced learning modules promised under the national education mandate.

### What to Watch Next

The outcome of the Naivasha meeting will dictate the future of JSS administrative governance. Observers are awaiting a formal statement on whether the Ministry will approve a phased transition to independent budgeting for JSS units or suggest a reformed oversight model that keeps them tethered to primary schools. Failure to provide a clear path forward will likely lead to escalated industrial action by teacher associations currently demanding systemic relief.
