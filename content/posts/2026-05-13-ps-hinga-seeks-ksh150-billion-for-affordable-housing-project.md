## State Department for Housing Requests Ksh150 Billion Budget Injection

State Department for Housing Principal Secretary Charles Hinga has requested an additional Ksh150 billion from Parliament to stabilize the national affordable housing programme amid concerns that current funding levels could derail project completion timelines and housing delivery targets.

The budget request presented to the National Assembly Committee on Housing, Urban Planning and Public Works seeks to address a critical fiscal gap identified for the 2026/27 financial year. While the government continues to rely on the housing levy, the requested Ksh150 billion represents a significant escalation in resource allocation. Treasury records indicate the state requires a total of Ksh228 billion to meet its obligations, yet current projections show a shortfall that threatens the continuity of ongoing construction sites across the country.

Internal assessments submitted to the committee break down the funding requirements into specific pillars. These include Ksh25 billion for critical infrastructure support, Ksh100 billion for direct project capitalization, and additional operational costs. The PS emphasized that without this capital infusion, the state faces a heightened risk of stalling projects that are currently at various stages of completion.

Central to this financial strategy is a new revenue target assigned to the Affordable Housing Board. CEO Sheila Waweru has been tasked with generating Ksh50 billion through the direct sale of units. This shift signals a transition from pure state-funded development toward a model that incorporates market-driven returns. Whether this target is realistic depends heavily on the absorption rate of units in urban centers where demand is high but buyer purchasing power remains constrained by broader economic conditions.

### The Fiscal Math of Housing Delivery

The reliance on housing sales as a pillar of the financial strategy introduces a variable of market risk. To reach the Ksh50 billion target, the state must effectively convert inventory into liquid cash. If the sales do not materialize as planned, the programme may revert to a dependency on the national budget, placing further strain on the consolidated fund. Project timelines currently hang in the balance. With several developments housing roughly 2,000 units each, the pace of construction is dictated by the velocity of payments to contractors. If the Ksh150 billion request is trimmed or delayed, contractors may pause work, leading to idle sites and rising costs. Economists observing the sector suggest that the government must balance its social mission of providing affordable shelter with the fiscal discipline required to maintain such a massive capital expenditure.

### Why it Matters Now

The viability of this project is a litmus test for the government's long-term economic planning. While the focus often shifts toward debates on [Wakadinali: 2026 Dominance or Fading Echo?](https://zandani.co.ke/article/wakadinali-2026-dominance) in cultural spheres, the housing sector remains the primary driver of domestic construction activity. Taxpayers are essentially backing a massive debt and equity play. If the state succeeds in moving toward a sales-based revenue model, it reduces the burden on public coffers. However, if the market fails to consume the units at the projected price points, the fiscal deficit will widen, potentially necessitating higher taxes or increased public debt to sustain the housing pipeline.

### What to Watch Next

Legislative debate in the coming weeks will reveal if the National Assembly supports the full Ksh150 billion demand or mandates a leaner fiscal approach. Close attention should be paid to the upcoming quarterly reports on unit sales, as these figures will determine if the Affordable Housing Board can achieve its Ksh50 billion self-funding goal or if the project will require further legislative interventions before the next fiscal cycle.
