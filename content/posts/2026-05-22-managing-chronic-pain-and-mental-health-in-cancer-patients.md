## Chronic Pain and the Myth of Psychiatric Failure in Cancer Care

**Suicidal ideation in terminal cancer patients is not a symptom of mental illness; it is a brutal, logical response to systemic failures in pain management that our hospitals are choosing to ignore.**

We have spent decades sidelining the agonizing reality of the cancer ward by mislabeling the cries of the suffering as psychiatric crises. When a patient reaches a breaking point, the default reaction within the Kenyan healthcare system is too often a referral to a psychiatrist or a sedation protocol that masks the problem rather than treating the source. This is a policy trap. By framing unbearable physical agony as a mental health condition, we absolve the institution of its primary duty: to alleviate pain before it destroys the human spirit.

### The Anatomy of Agony

Consider the case of a patient recently admitted with Stage 4 soft tissue sarcoma. She arrived at the facility presenting with extreme agitation, aggressive outbursts, and documented suicidal ideation. Under standard hospital protocol, this profile triggers an immediate psychiatric evaluation, effectively treating her spirit as the site of the pathology. 

However, a closer look at her clinical metrics told a different story. Her pain score sat at an intolerable 14. This was not a woman losing her mind; this was a woman trapped in a body functioning as a torture chamber. When her pain management regimen was adjusted and her score dropped to a 7, the aggression vanished and the suicidal intent ceased. The psychiatric symptoms were not the primary issue. They were the biological byproducts of unmanaged physical trauma. If our doctors cannot distinguish between a patient wanting to die and a patient wanting the pain to stop, we are not practicing medicine-we are facilitating negligence.

### Redefining the Protocol

The disconnect between physical pain management and emotional stability in our oncology wards is a systemic failure that requires an urgent policy shift. We need to stop treating psycho-oncology as an elective luxury or a soft science. It must become a core component of the treatment cycle.

The Ministry of Health must mandate comprehensive psycho-oncology training for every oncologist practicing in this country. Oncology is not just about the chemistry of tumor suppression; it is about the stewardship of the patient’s capacity to endure. When we fail to integrate pain control with psychological support, we force patients to live in a state of high-alert panic. This mirrors the same institutional failures seen in local administrative governance, where, much like the recent oversight disputes seen in Siaya County, a lack of standardized procedural accountability leads to systemic breakdown and human suffering. 

### Why it matters now

Our healthcare infrastructure is currently built to prioritize curative success while ignoring the reality of the palliative journey. As cancer rates rise across the country, we are facing a surge of patients whose physical agony is being mismanaged, leading to preventable psychological distress. Families are currently left to navigate this alone, often misinterpreting a loved one’s plea for relief as a sudden onset of depression. 

### What to watch next

Families must begin demanding transparency regarding pain management protocols. If a patient is exhibiting distress, ask the oncology team for the specific pain score and the pharmaceutical justification for the current dosage. We should watch for upcoming policy reviews from the Ministry of Health regarding mandatory palliative care certifications for oncologists. Advocacy must shift from demanding better psychiatric referrals to demanding better chemical and physical pain relief.

The healthcare system remains a place where we invest heavily in education and potential, much like the commitment shown by organizations supporting scholarships for the youth, but we fail to protect those at the end of their struggle. If we continue to pathologize the reaction to pain rather than treating the pain itself, we are not healing patients. We are simply ignoring their agony until they no longer have the strength to demand justice. Death is an inevitability for the terminal patient; however, dying in agony because of a bureaucratic failure to measure pain is a choice the system makes. It is time we made a different one.
