## The 8.75 Percent Stagnation Trap Central Bank of Kenya Maintains Benchmark Interest Rate at 8.75% is the central subject of the update.

The Monetary Policy Committee has opted to keep the benchmark interest rate at 8.75 percent, prioritizing inflation control over aggressive stimulus. This decision effectively cements a high-cost environment, forcing local businesses to weigh stagnant expansion plans against the relative safety of government yields.

### The Divergence of Credit and Reality

While the Central Bank of Kenya reports that private sector credit growth reached 9.3 percent in May 2026, this headline figure masks a deepening divide in the domestic economy. The reality for most small and medium enterprises is far bleaker than the credit metrics suggest. Banks, facing a gross non-performing loan ratio of 15.3 percent, have turned hyper-cautious, restricting new capital to only the most established corporate entities. 

This environment creates a clear bifurcation: institutional investors are finding refuge in government paper, which offers consistent, risk-adjusted returns, while the real economy suffers from a severe liquidity drought. With commercial lending rates averaging 14.5 percent, the cost of servicing debt has become prohibitive for businesses attempting to scale or pivot. For many, the math no longer supports growth. Why risk capital on business expansion when the interest burden threatens to erode margins entirely? This cautious stance from lenders is a direct consequence of the volatility in the Middle East, which continues to drive energy costs higher and suppress local consumer spending power.

### Managing a Downgraded Outlook

The decision to hold rates firm arrives alongside a sobering downward revision of the 2026 GDP growth forecast to 4.9 percent. This adjustment reflects a broader cooling of economic momentum, driven by supply chain fragility and diminished domestic demand. The current account deficit sits at a manageable 2.6 percent, supported by foreign reserves of 13,203 million dollars, yet these macro stabilizers do little to mitigate the day-to-day pressure on entrepreneurs. 

For the average Kenyan consumer, the high-rate environment is a double-edged sword. While it keeps the currency relatively stable, it limits the flow of capital into sectors that drive employment. Investors looking for growth are finding that traditional expansion is currently sidelined in favor of cash preservation.

### Capital Allocation in a High-Rate Era

Portfolio managers are increasingly shifting away from private sector debt, viewing it as a secondary priority compared to sovereign debt instruments. This flight to quality is not just a trend but a survival strategy for institutional capital. As businesses grapple with these constraints, the focus has shifted toward efficiency rather than aggressive market share capture. 

This shift in strategy highlights a disconnect between official growth figures and the operational hurdles faced by the private sector. While larger conglomerates may absorb the 14.5 percent average lending cost, the majority of the market is currently in a defensive holding pattern.
