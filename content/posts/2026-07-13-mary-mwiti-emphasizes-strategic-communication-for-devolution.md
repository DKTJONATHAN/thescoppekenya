## The Communication Trap: Why Better PR Won't Save Devolution

Council of Governors CEO Mary Mwiti recently doubled down on the idea that strategic communication is the missing link to fixing Kenya's devolved governance. This view posits that if counties simply polished their messaging and adopted citizen-centered engagement, the public would regain faith in local leadership. It is a comforting fantasy, but it ignores the brutal reality that no amount of slick PR can mask the stench of poor service delivery.

### Data Versus Delivery

The 2025 County Budget Transparency Survey provides a jarring reality check. It paints a picture of fiscal compliance and sophisticated online dashboards, yet the average Kenyan citizen on the ground experiences an entirely different truth. While these reports boast of high transparency scores, citizens are struggling with crumbling rural access roads, understocked public clinics, and water shortages. 

When the state treats transparency as a marketing exercise, it creates a dangerous disconnect. The survey measures the quality of documentation, not the quality of life. If a governor publishes a perfect, audit-compliant budget but fails to fix a single pothole in three years, the citizen does not need a better communication strategy; they need a functioning public works department. Public cynicism toward devolution is not a result of poor information flow. It is a logical, rational response to the consistent misuse of tax revenue.

### The Cult of Personality

Modern county communication is frequently hijacked by the ego of the incumbent. We see endless social media streams of governors cutting ribbons or commissioning projects that rarely survive the first heavy rains. This personality-driven approach is designed to build a brand rather than an institution. When the primary goal of a county communication office is to generate viral clips for a governor's political future, the actual mechanics of citizen feedback loops wither away.

True engagement requires vulnerability and the ability to accept negative feedback. Instead, current strategies are built on a bedrock of defensive posture. Institutionalizing feedback would mean admitting to errors or delays, but counties prefer the sterile safety of staged photo opportunities. This is not governance; it is a long-form campaign advertisement funded by the taxpayer. Even when sports headlines, like [those tracking international player transfers](https://zandani.co.ke/article/agents-push-inter-milan-s-dumfries-for-20m-liverpool-transfer), capture more public attention than county public participation forums, it is because the former offers authenticity while the latter feels like a scripted performance.

### Beyond the Bandage

The reliance on strategic communication as a governance tool is a symptom of a larger, systemic malaise. We have reached a point where the performance of duty is secondary to the performance of perception. Whether the topic is a [high-stakes tech partnership](https://zandani.co.ke/article/xledger-bristol-bears-women-tech-tackles-partnership) or the fundamental provision of health services, the priority seems to be the announcement rather than the impact.

We must stop treating every policy failure as a misunderstanding that can be cleared up with a press release. The solution to the devolution crisis is not better spin doctors or more refined talk tracks. It is the hard, unglamorous work of resource allocation and bureaucratic accountability. If a county government spent half the money it currently burns on PR firms and social media influencers on actual infrastructure maintenance, the public trust gap would close naturally. Accountability is not something you broadcast; it is something you demonstrate. Until leadership accepts that their work speaks louder than their press statements, the skepticism of the Kenyan voter will remain entirely justified.
