## Kerosene Price Hikes Ignite Kitchen-Table Chaos

"How are we supposed to boil tea or cook githeri when the fuel price jumps by nearly forty shillings overnight?" This is the loud, frustrated roar echoing through every WhatsApp group and market stall in Nairobi today as citizens wake up to a massive surge in kerosene costs.

The Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA) announced its price cycle for May 19 to June 14, 2026, dropping diesel by KSh 10.06 but slapping a heavy KSh 38.60 increase on kerosene. While EPRA officials insist this sharp climb is a necessary tactic to stop the black-market trend of mixing cheap kerosene with diesel to cheat the taxman, the people in our informal settlements are not looking at policy charts. They are looking at empty cooking pots. 

### Voices from the Trenches

In the heart of Mukuru, the sentiment is pure panic. Mama Njuguna, who runs a small food stall, feels the squeeze immediately. "I use kerosene for my pressure lamp and for heating water during the early morning rush," she says. "They tell us it is to stop bad business practices, but my business is the one suffering now. This is a tax on the poor, plain and simple."

Across the city in Mathare, the mood is equally grim. John, a boda-boda operator who often finds himself caught between the politics of fuel and the reality of his thin margins, thinks the logic is flawed. "They lower diesel for the big trucks and increase kerosene for the small homes. Do they think we do not see the math? They are fixing a fuel fraud problem by emptying the pockets of the people who can least afford it."

Some observers are even comparing this administrative headache to other recent government frustrations. While sports fans might be venting about Arsenal's title drought at [nine points clear](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-12-nine-points-clear-and-still-no-title-arsenal-face-unprecede), the drama here is far more tangible. It is not about a trophy; it is about the price of a hot meal. As one user posted on social media, "We are struggling with [system failures in public tech](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-05-06-system-failures-and-transparency-concerns-hit-sh104-billion) and now we cannot even light a stove. Life has become a series of unending shocks."

### The Regulatory Logic vs. The Kadogo Economy

EPRA claims that by narrowing the price gap between diesel and kerosene, they can effectively discourage fuel adulteration. The regulator maintains that the price difference has historically incentivized rogue dealers to mix the two products, leading to sub-standard fuel hitting the market and damaging engines across the country. 

However, this macroeconomic strategy overlooks the 'kadogo' economy. For thousands of households, kerosene is not just fuel for lamps; it is a primary energy source for cooking. When the price of this essential commodity surges by such a significant margin in a single month, the household budget for food is the first thing to collapse. The state sees a strategy to clean up the petroleum sector, but the average citizen sees a sudden, sharp reduction in their daily purchasing power.

### Why it Matters Now

The timing of this hike places immense pressure on low-income families already grappling with high food inflation. By targeting kerosene, the government has moved the fight against fuel fraud from the fuel pump into the heart of the home. If the price of cooking energy remains at this height, we should expect a shift in consumption patterns, potentially forcing more families to rely on even more dangerous or expensive alternatives.

### What to Watch Next

Keep an eye on how small-scale traders adjust their prices for cooked food and charcoal. If the cost of lighting and cooking becomes unsustainable, market vendors will likely pass those expenses directly to the consumer, triggering a secondary wave of inflation in the informal sector. Furthermore, observe if the government faces pushback from parliamentary representatives who fear this price jump will become a lightning rod for broader anti-government sentiment as we creep closer to the 2027 election cycle.

The air in the city is thick with resentment, and for now, the kitchen fire is costing much more than anyone planned.
