## Kenya's Digital Governance Stagnates Amidst Automation Failures Kenya's Public Service Commission (PSC) reports significant failures in public sector digital transformation. Only 15.9% of institutions met the June 2025 automation deadline, with payroll non-compliance at 14.1%, perpetuating "ghost worker" fraud and costing taxpayers billions. This stagnation undermines service delivery and digital security, despite repeated national targets.

Automation Lag and Compliance Gaps The Public Service Commission's 2024/2025 compliance evaluation report, encompassing 511 public institutions, revealed that a mere 81 institutions had automated and migrated their business processes to e-platforms by the June 2025 deadline. This means 84.1% of the evaluated public service remains unautomated. An additional 166 institutions that claimed compliance failed to provide valid supporting documentation, often submitting undated, unsigned, or irrelevant materials. This recurring issue signals a broader challenge, as the June 2025 deadline itself was set in a previous evaluation cycle, anchored in the National Digital Master Plan 2022-2027.

The Cost of Decentralized Payrolls A critical area of non-compliance lies in payroll management, with 14.1% of institutions, specifically 72, not utilizing the centralized Human Resource Information System (HRIS). This bypassing of official systems undermines efforts to standardize salary payments and eliminate "ghost workers," who continue to siphon public funds. A 2025 audit found that 22 counties collectively spent Ksh 6.5 billion in nine months paying non-existent employees. The issue of ghost workers has contributed to Kenya's burgeoning public wage bill, which audits indicate has reached Ksh 1.25 trillion annually. Public universities lead in off-platform payroll practices at 3.3%, followed by state corporations and semi-autonomous government agencies (SAGAs) at 2.6%, and statutory commissions and authorities at 2.5%.

Impact on Service Delivery and Security The neglect of digital infrastructure has tangible consequences for service delivery. ICT system downtimes are frequently cited as barriers, contributing to a notable decline in the overall service delivery performance index from 53.6% in 2023/2024 to 32% in 2024/2025. A stark example is the National Social Security Fund (NSSF), where system outages following cyberattacks directly led to a Ksh 2.23 billion shortfall in pension payouts during FY 2024/2025. NSSF had initially denied any breach, but its own audited report later confirmed the attacks caused backlogs extending into the next financial year. This highlights severe vulnerabilities when digital transformation lacks robust security and disaster recovery mechanisms.

Future Prospects for Digital Governance Despite ongoing challenges and missed targets in June 2024 and June 2025, a new deadline of June 2026 has been set for non-compliant institutions. The repeated setting of targets underscores the persistent struggle to achieve comprehensive digital transformation within Kenya's public sector, a critical component for enhancing efficiency, transparency, and public trust.
