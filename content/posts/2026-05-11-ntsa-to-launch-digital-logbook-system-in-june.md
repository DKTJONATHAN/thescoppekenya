## NTSA to Launch Digital Vehicle Logbook System in June

The National Transport and Safety Authority will roll out a mandatory digital logbook system on June 10, effectively phasing out physical ownership documents to eliminate widespread forgery and curb the influence of illicit intermediaries in the vehicle registration market.

The transition shifts the burden of proof for vehicle ownership from vulnerable paper certificates to a secure, cloud-based ledger integrated within the Transport Integrated Management System (TIMS) and the e-Citizen portal. By digitizing the title deed for every vehicle in Kenya, the state aims to neutralize the market for forged documents that has historically allowed criminals to manipulate ownership records.

This policy marks a definitive end to the risks associated with the traditional Ksh2,600 physical document, which has frequently been the target of document brokers and corrupt insiders. Owners will no longer need to store fragile paper certificates, as verification will be handled instantly through secure digital channels.

For stakeholders such as lenders and insurance firms, the June 10 rollout provides an immediate mechanism to verify the status of vehicle collateral. This integration allows financial institutions to confirm ownership legitimacy in real-time, reducing the risk of fraud that previously haunted the asset-backed lending sector. Similar to recent administrative shifts where authorities have prioritized [tax transparency](https://zandani.co.ke/article/tax-appeals-tribunal-cuts-nairobi-county-tax-bill), this move centralizes control to strip power away from unregulated third parties.

### Disrupting the Market for Intermediaries

The shift to an e-logbook environment functions as a disruptive force against the brokerage industry. For years, unauthorized agents have leveraged the complexity of the paper-based system to extract fees from motorists under the guise of expediting services. By mandating that all ownership records reside on the e-Citizen platform, the authority effectively bypasses these intermediaries.

Motorists are now empowered to conduct transfers and verification requests independently. While this reduces the costs associated with hiring fixers, it requires owners to maintain active and accurate profiles on the national digital portals. Those who have previously relied on third parties to manage their paperwork face a period of forced adjustment as they take direct control of their ownership data.

### Why It Matters Now

The move toward digital documentation is a calculated response to the persistent vulnerability of the Kenyan transport sector to document fraud. Criminal networks have long exploited the physical logbook system to alter vehicle identities, often leading to significant financial losses for both private owners and credit providers. The transition forces a new level of accountability, as every transaction will be logged with a digital timestamp, leaving a permanent trail that is impossible for local forgers to erase.

The impact on credit security cannot be overstated. With lenders gaining the ability to lock or verify assets digitally, the institutional risk of financing vehicles drops significantly. This creates a more stable lending environment, though it necessitates that current vehicle owners ensure their existing records are clean and synchronized with the TIMS database before the June deadline. Much like the public debates surrounding [personal disputes and family accountability](https://zandani.co.ke/article/relative-disputes-kamene-goro-s-claims-that-husband-dj-bonez-abandon), this policy update forces individuals to reconcile their administrative records or face the consequences of non-compliance.

### What to Watch Next

Motorists must observe how the transition period handles existing loan collateral, as lenders will need to migrate physical securities to the digital ledger without causing liquidity freezes. NTSA will be expected to release guidelines on the handling of legacy documents for vehicles currently held as security. Future observers should track the rate of reported forgery cases in the secondary vehicle market to determine if the digital shift effectively achieves its goal of market sanitization.
