## EPRA Fuel Price Adjustments and SME Logistics

For many small business owners, the recent transport strikes felt like a direct hit to the bottom line, but the aftermath of these protests brings a complicated reality. As EPRA recalibrates fuel costs, your monthly budget faces a split-impact scenario that requires more than just a quick look at the pump price.

The energy regulator has announced a KSh 10.06 diesel reduction, bringing the price to KSh 232.86, while simultaneously hiking the cost of kerosene by a staggering KSh 38.60. For the average Kenyan trader, this shift is not just a price change; it is a fundamental reconfiguration of how your logistical and operational overheads interact.

### The Logistics Trade-off

If your business relies heavily on transport to move goods from wholesale markets to your shop floor, the diesel reduction is a welcome breather. Since diesel is the primary fuel for the trucks and matatus that keep supply chains moving along Nairobi roads like Thika and Mombasa, a reduction in the fuel cost should theoretically lower your transport costs. However, this is rarely a one-to-one translation. Many logistics providers operate on fixed contracts that do not immediately reflect pump price drops. You should reach out to your transporters this week to renegotiate delivery rates. If they are paying less at the pump, those savings should start showing up in your invoice totals by next month.

### The Hidden Energy Tax

The real sting for many small-scale enterprises is hidden in the KSh 38.60 increase for kerosene. While passenger transport grabs the headlines, many SMEs in the informal sector still rely on kerosene for power generators, water pumps, or small-scale heating equipment. If your business depends on machinery that runs on kerosene, your operational margins are effectively being tightened. 

This creates a cost-shuffling scenario. You might save a few hundred shillings on weekly product deliveries, only to see that same amount eaten up by the increased cost of running your business equipment. For those struggling to keep costs down, the recent government announcement regarding the KSh 12 billion stabilization fund offers a thin cushion, but it is unlikely to offset the sharp rise in kerosene costs for micro-enterprises.

### Managing Your Cash Flow

To survive this volatility, you must treat your energy costs as a fluid variable rather than a fixed expense. First, audit your reliance on different fuel sources. If you have equipment that runs on kerosene, look for alternative energy efficiency measures or maintenance checks to ensure you are not burning more fuel than necessary. Second, track your logistics providers closely. Do not assume that the KSh 10.06 diesel reduction will automatically result in lower delivery fees; you must be proactive in requesting price adjustments from your vendors. 

This move by the regulator is an attempt to discourage fuel adulteration, but for the SME owner, it remains a balancing act. Keep a close eye on your profit margins over the next thirty days. If your energy costs are rising faster than your delivery savings, you may need to adjust your retail prices to maintain the same level of business liquidity. 

### Why it matters now

The volatility in energy prices highlights the fragility of SME budgets that are tethered to the national transport network. With the state injecting KSh 12 billion into the stabilization fund, the government is signaling that it is attempting to manage price shocks, but these funds are finite. Businesses that fail to monitor these shifts in real-time risk absorbing costs that should be passed on or mitigated through supply chain adjustments.

### What to watch next

Keep an eye on whether the cost of goods in retail markets starts to stabilize following the diesel price reduction. If logistics costs do not fall, it suggests that market inefficiencies are eating the savings. Also, monitor any further policy shifts from the Treasury regarding the stabilization fund, as a depletion of these reserves could lead to another round of sudden, steep price hikes in the coming quarter. Small business owners should also keep an eye on how international market trends continue to influence domestic pricing.
