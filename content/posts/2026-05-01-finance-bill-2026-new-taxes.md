## Finance Bill 2026 Proposes New Taxes on Phones, Mitumba, and Betting

Treasury Cabinet Secretary John Mbadi has submitted the much-anticipated Finance Bill 2026 to Parliament, introducing new taxes on imported mobile phones, second-hand clothing, and increased levies on betting activities across Kenya. The comprehensive bill aims to significantly broaden the government's revenue streams and adjust to evolving economic landscapes.

### Mobile Phones and Communication Devices Face Higher Excise Duty

Under the new Finance Bill 2026, imported smartphones and related communication devices will attract a 25 per cent excise duty. This excise duty will be charged when a phone is activated, rather than at the point of import or purchase, a change expected to increase the overall cost of imported mobile phones for consumers.

### New Tax Framework for Second-hand Clothing

The bill proposes significant changes for the second-hand clothing, footwear, and other worn imports, commonly known as mitumba. A deemed profit approach will be implemented, where 5 per cent of the customs value of these imported goods will be treated as taxable income. This tax will be payable at the point of importation, shifting the tax burden from traditional import duties and VAT to a direct income-based tax on traders.

### Betting Winnings and Deposits Targeted

Gamblers in Kenya will face tougher taxation measures. The Finance Bill 2026 introduces a 20 per cent withholding tax on all winnings. Additionally, the definition of taxable deposits in betting accounts has been expanded to encompass all funds intended for gambling purposes. This broadens the tax base by treating all money deposited for gambling as taxable, moving beyond specific account credits or defined transactions.

### Expanded Taxation for Digital Services and Environmental Adjustments

Companies operating digital payments, financial technology payment systems, card networks, and digital platforms will now fall under expanded definitions of royalties, leading to more transactions and service fees being taxed. Virtual asset service providers will be required to submit detailed annual reports on user transactions. The government also plans international agreements for the automatic sharing of crypto-related tax information.

In an effort to address environmental concerns and climate change, the Treasury has proposed a 10 per cent tax adjustment on plastic products and 5 per cent on coal. New adjustments are also outlined for alcohol, tobacco, and sugar-related products such as soft drinks. Fruit juices are set to attract a per-litre excise duty ranging between Ksh14 and Ksh20, depending on their sugar content, with vegetable juices and similar beverages brought under a comparable framework.
