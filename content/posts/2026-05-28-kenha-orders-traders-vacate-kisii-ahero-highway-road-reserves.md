## Kisii-Ahero Highway Traders Face Total Economic Displacement

The 30-day notice issued by the Kenya National Highways Authority to clear all roadside stalls along the Kisii-Ahero corridor is being met with disbelief and quiet fury by rural entrepreneurs who view their workstations as the lifeblood of the regional economy. This directive, requiring full compliance by June 27, effectively signals the end of a vast, informal supply chain that feeds commuters and connects local farmers to immediate markets.

KeNHA officials are pushing ahead with a nationwide enforcement campaign, targeting road reserves across the country including the Chebarbar-Lessos-Nabkoi and Mayoni-Bungoma stretches. While the agency frames the move as a critical intervention for traffic safety and road expansion, the reality on the ground is a scramble to save inventory and capital. This comes as the divide between the country's rising elite, where [wealth for the top bracket grows by 14 percent](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenya-records-16-centi-millionaires-as-private-wealth-grows-by-14-pe), and the struggling informal sector becomes increasingly sharp. For the roadside vendor, the road reserve is not merely a legal boundary to be cleared; it is a primary point of sale where commuters stop for quick supplies and farmers offload produce before it perishes in the sun.

### Voices from the Roadside Edge

Eunice, a produce seller near the Ahero junction, argues that her stall provides more than just goods. She explains that she functions as a critical link for smallholder farmers who cannot afford the logistical costs of reaching urban wholesale markets. If she is forced to pack up her wooden kiosk, she says, the farmers will lose their quickest buyer and she will lose her only source of daily capital. To her, the eviction is a direct attack on a self-sustaining ecosystem that keeps money circulating within the community.

Peter, a motorcycle spare parts dealer operating along the route, echoes this concern. He points out that his shop serves as a vital service station for the thousands of riders who dominate transport in the region. He questions why there is no plan for integration or designated zones for these small businesses. According to him, the authorities treat their livelihoods as disposable obstructions rather than essential service providers that have organically grown to meet the needs of the passing public.

The sentiment is similar among transporters who rely on these roadside stops. A long-distance driver mentioned that these informal markets offer the only accessible, affordable nutrition for transit workers on the route. He notes that without these small hubs, the cost of his daily operations would inevitably rise, adding a hidden tax on the goods transported into the interior. He sees the eviction as a short-sighted move that ignores the reliance the transport sector has on these local hubs.

### The Cost of Disruption

The economic ripple effect of clearing these reserves goes beyond the loss of individual kiosks. By dismantling these nodes, the state is effectively severing the last-mile connectivity that keeps regional food security stable. When vendors are uprooted without a transition strategy, the immediate consequence is the waste of perishable goods and a sudden drop in household income for thousands of families. This situation stands in stark contrast to efforts elsewhere in the country, such as the [use of community scorecards to hold local officials accountable](https://zandani.co.ke/article/enhancing-county-accountability-through-community-score-cards), where citizens are demanding more participation in the policies that affect their daily survival.

There is a palpable frustration that enforcement takes precedence over economic design. Many traders are asking why the agency cannot adopt a spatial planning approach that organizes these vendors rather than erasing them. The current directive leaves the local economy in a state of paralysis, as business owners are currently too anxious to reinvest in stock that might be demolished in less than a month. As June 27 approaches, the atmosphere along the highway remains tense, with many families wondering how they will replace the income generated at these stalls, leaving a heavy cloud of uncertainty over one of the most active trade arteries in western Kenya.
