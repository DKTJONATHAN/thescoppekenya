## Finance Bill 2026 Targets Mobile Activation and Mitumba Imports

Treasury Cabinet Secretary John Mbadi has unveiled the Finance Bill 2026, introducing a shift toward activation-based excise taxation for mobile devices and a new presumptive income tax framework for second-hand clothing importers to bolster domestic revenue collection efforts.

### The Shift to Activation-Based Excise Duties

The proposed legislation marks a departure from traditional customs-based levies on electronic goods. Under the new framework, the government plans to enforce a 25 percent excise duty on mobile phones and wireless devices at the moment they are registered on a mobile network. By moving the tax point from the port of entry to the point of network activation, the Treasury aims to capture revenue from devices that previously entered the country through unregulated channels or under-reported imports.

This technical integration requires a direct link between the Kenya Revenue Authority and local telecommunications providers. The state intends to utilize network-level data to ensure that any mobile device connecting to a Kenyan cellular tower triggers the mandatory excise payment. This system effectively automates tax compliance, removing the reliance on physical declarations at border points. For consumers, this shift suggests that mobile service providers will likely incorporate these tax costs into their network registration or SIM activation fees.

### Formalizing the Mitumba Sector

Alongside digital taxation, the Treasury is targeting the second-hand clothing trade with a structural shift in income assessment. The bill proposes a presumptive tax for mitumba importers, calculated on an assumed 5 percent profit margin subjected to a 30 percent income tax rate. This measure is designed to curb the historical under-declaration of profits within the informal sector.

Officials at the Treasury contend that the current reliance on weight-based import tariffs has allowed large-scale importers to minimize their tax obligations. By transitioning to an income-based model, the government seeks to align the tax contributions of mitumba dealers with those of established formal retail entities. This policy represents a broader push to bring the informal economy under a singular, predictable fiscal umbrella.

### Why It Matters Now

The transition to activation-based taxation is a response to the rapid expansion of the digital economy, which has outpaced current revenue collection mechanisms. With millions of handsets entering the market annually, the state views the digital interface as a more reliable monitoring tool than the physical borders. This pivot follows recent legal scrutiny regarding the oversight of telecommunications operations, particularly after the [Kenyan High Court blocked telcos from reassigning phone numbers without owner consent](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenyan-high-court-blocks-telcos-from-reassigning-phone-numbers-witho), highlighting the state's growing reliance on telco-held data for both regulatory and administrative purposes. By tying excise duties to device activation, the government is transforming mobile operators into frontline tax collection agents.

### Industry and Public Response

Market analysts suggest that the activation-based model may inadvertently create barriers for low-income households that rely on affordable, second-hand handsets. While the Treasury views this as an efficiency gain, hardware retailers have raised concerns regarding the potential for decreased consumer demand. They argue that the cumulative effect of the 25 percent excise duty, when combined with existing taxes, will significantly inflate the cost of basic connectivity.

Mitumba traders have expressed apprehension regarding the new presumptive tax structure, noting that a flat 30 percent income tax on a presumed profit margin ignores the volatile nature of the second-hand garment business. Business associations representing these importers are seeking clarity on how the KRA will verify profit margins, warning that the added fiscal burden could force smaller vendors to exit the market. These stakeholders remain wary of how the government intends to balance revenue targets with the necessity of maintaining the affordability of essential goods.

### What to Watch Next

The legislative process will now shift to parliamentary debates where the feasibility of the technical integration between telecommunications infrastructure and the KRA’s digital systems will likely be scrutinized. Parliament must determine if the mechanisms for activation-based levies provide adequate safeguards for consumer data privacy. All eyes will be on the National Assembly Finance Committee to see if they propose amendments to lower the excise percentage or provide exemptions for low-cost, entry-level smartphones.
