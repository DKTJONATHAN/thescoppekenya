Pressure is mounting on the Nairobi County Assembly to launch investigations into the structural integrity of Central Business District buildings. This urgent call follows the appearance of crumbling walls and visible cracks in several city structures, raising significant safety concerns amidst ongoing heavy rainfall and a series of recent devastating collapses across the capital.

CBD Building Safety Under Scrutiny

Calls for Immediate Investigation Kiamaiko Ward MCA Joseph Ndung’u has amplified calls for the Nairobi County Assembly's Sectoral Committee on Lands, Planning, and Housing to initiate thorough investigations. Ndung'u highlighted specific concerns regarding a building located at the corner of Mfangano Street and Sheikh Karume Street, noting visible cracks that "raise serious concerns about the structural integrity of the building and the safety of people who operate inside and around the building." He seeks clarity on whether the county is aware of such defects, the validity of building approvals, and the status of crucial documents like Certificates of Occupation and compliance records for the affected structure. Additionally, Ndung'u demands a comprehensive explanation of how authorities plan to mitigate similar risks across Nairobi's aging CBD infrastructure, including stronger enforcement measures and tightened oversight of developers.

Recent Fatalities and Expert Warnings The heightened scrutiny follows a tragic series of structural failures across Nairobi in 2026. On March 18, a worker died when a 22-storey building under construction in Westlands partially collapsed during a concrete pour. This incident occurred just two days after four individuals were crushed to death on March 16 while salvaging materials from a condemned five-storey building in Shauri Moyo. These recent tragedies add to the toll of a February 11 collapse on Kirinyaga Road in the Central Business District that injured six workers. In January 2026, experts from the Institution of Engineers of Kenya (IEK), the Architects Alliance (TAA), and the Kenya Institute of Planners (KIP) issued a stark warning, asserting that 85 percent of buildings in Nairobi are unsafe for occupation. This alarm was prompted by several fatal collapses, notably a 16-storey building in South C on January 2 and another in Karen on January 10. Experts cautioned that even a slight tremor lasting 20-30 seconds could compromise the vast majority of Nairobi's structures. Since January, these structural failures have claimed at least seven lives and left dozens injured across the capital. As concerns over public safety dominate discussions within the Nairobi County Assembly, the attention of the populace is also drawn to various other events, including recent public disturbances at sporting venues, as witnessed during the AFC Leopards vs Shabana FC clash at Nyayo Stadium.
