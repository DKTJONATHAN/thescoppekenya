## The Quiet Expansion of the Legislative Retirement Buffet Ruto signs law granting senators access to parliamentary pension  is the central subject of the update.

President William Ruto has signed the Parliamentary Pensions Amendment Bill 2023 into law at State House, quietly folding senators into a lucrative retirement scheme. While the state calls this a procedural alignment with the 2010 Constitution, the timing could not be more insensitive.

### The Fiscal Weight of an Expanded Entitlement

The administrative rhetoric surrounding this bill centers on correcting a historical oversight regarding the bicameral structure of the legislature. However, the move forces taxpayers to foot the bill for a growing cadre of political retirees while simultaneously debating new tax rules for everyday essentials like mitumba clothes and mobile devices.

Economic analysts estimate that extending the pension net to cover the Senate will exert additional pressure on the national treasury, which is already struggling to manage a ballooning public wage bill. While the government insists on austerity for the average Kenyan, this legislation guarantees that the legislative branch secures its own financial future. The disparity is stark. A typical civil servant must navigate a grueling, decades-long journey toward a modest pension that barely accounts for inflation. In contrast, senators stand to benefit from a parliamentary scheme that is significantly more generous, often requiring much shorter periods of service to trigger substantial lifetime payouts.

This legislative maneuvering is particularly jarring given the current fiscal climate. Just as the Treasury explores ways to squeeze more revenue from the informal economy to service mounting debt, the political elite have found a way to institutionalize their own comfort. There is no public outcry from the chambers when it comes to self-preservation, unlike the rapid and often chaotic shifts in tax policy that leave small business owners in limbo.

### A Legislative Glass House

The amendment does more than just add senators to the list of beneficiaries. It updates the definition of dependents to include children under 18, a technical adjustment that formalizes the extension of state-funded support. By refreshing the language of the 1983 Act, the government has essentially retrofitted the law to ensure the Senate is treated with the same financial reverence as the National Assembly.

The political class operates in a different reality. While citizens deal with the aftermath of systemic failures, such as the dangerous road conditions that recently endangered public figures like Reuben Kigame, parliamentarians prioritize the security of their post-office years. This focus on legislative perks occurs alongside the government's aggressive pursuit of digital tax compliance, targeting everyone from social media creators to the average mobile phone user. The state has been busy regulating the virtual space, including [TikTok bans on AI-generated personas used for illicit content], yet it shows no similar speed or enthusiasm for regulating its own appetite for public funds.

The silence from the legislative benches regarding the impact on the taxpayer is deafening. There has been no debate on whether the country can afford to expand this liability. The process was handled with the mechanical efficiency that only appears when legislators vote on their own welfare.

When the treasury is hollowed out by debt and citizens are told to tighten their belts, why is the priority always the expansion of the golden handshake for those already at the top of the economic hierarchy?
