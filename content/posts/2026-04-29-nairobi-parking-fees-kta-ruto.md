## KTA Challenges High Nairobi Parking Fees in Ruto Plea

The Kenya Transporters Association (KTA) has formally appealed to President William Ruto, Nairobi Governor Johnson Sakaja, Senator Edwin Sifuna, and County Assembly Speaker Kennedy Okeyo, seeking intervention over the prohibitive Ksh4,000 daily Nairobi parking fees for trucks. This action stems from their deep dissatisfaction with the economic strain on local delivery businesses.

### Economic Burden on Transporters

KTA highlights that the current Ksh4,000 daily charge for Nairobi parking fees consumes a significant 16 percent of a truck's gross revenue from a typical Ksh25,000 local delivery trip within Nairobi. This cost is incurred before accounting for essential operating expenses such as fuel, driver wages, maintenance, insurance, and loan repayments, rendering many transport businesses financially unviable. The association argues that such steep levies make compliance punitive for transporters already navigating high operational costs, including rising fuel prices.

### Corruption Incentives and Lost Revenue

The association asserts that the high Nairobi parking fees are counterproductive, fostering corruption rather than increasing county revenue. Transporters, unable to absorb the Ksh4,000 daily fee, are often compelled to resort to informal payments, typically around Ksh1,000, to enforcement officers. These payments bypass official county coffers, leading to a significant loss of potential revenue for Nairobi County. KTA states that this situation forces transporters to choose between maintaining business viability and engaging in illicit practices. The industry also suspects the presence of entrenched parking fee cartels operating within Nairobi, further diverting revenue and hindering legitimate businesses.

### Proposed Solutions and Comparative Analysis

To address these challenges, KTA has proposed a revised daily rate of Ksh800 per truck. They contend that this adjusted fee would substantially increase compliance among transporters, significantly reduce incentives for corruption, and ensure the continued viability of transport businesses within the city. This move, KTA believes, would also bolster Nairobi's competitiveness as a commercial hub. The association drew comparisons with other counties, noting that Kiambu charges Ksh400, Mombasa Ksh700, Eldoret and Nakuru Ksh500 each, and Bungoma just Ksh300 for similar services. This disparity underscores KTA's argument that Nairobi County's current rates are unfairly high. Supporting businesses through reasonable regulation is crucial for their growth and can be observed in various sectors, including the adaptive strategies seen in retail, as highlighted by discussions on new business models.

KTA has expressed its readiness to engage constructively with Nairobi County authorities to develop reforms that benefit both the government and private enterprises. The formal petition underscores the urgency for intervention from the targeted leaders to resolve the ongoing dispute over parking charges.
