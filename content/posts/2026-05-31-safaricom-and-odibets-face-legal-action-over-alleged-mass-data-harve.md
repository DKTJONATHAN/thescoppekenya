A major class-action lawsuit is currently underway in Kenya, where 11.5 million Safaricom subscribers allege their personal data was harvested and sold to betting firms including Odibets between 2018 and 2019. The case addresses severe privacy breaches and corporate data misappropriation.

## The Legal Reckoning for Betting Industry Giants

### The Erosion of Corporate Immunity
The legal landscape surrounding the betting industry has shifted significantly, challenging the perception that these firms operate under a shield of total invincibility. Recent events, including the arrest of Andrew Aligula and heightened regulatory scrutiny directed at Odibets Chairman Jimmy Kibaki, indicate that the era of complete immunity for the sector has effectively ended. As national financial pressures mount, the judiciary has taken a more aggressive stance, leaving previously protected corporate figures vulnerable to the full weight of legal accountability.

### The Collapse of Political Protection
Public attention has focused on the visible anxiety of high-level figures who once commanded absolute influence. There is a marked shift in the posture of those who formerly felt untouchable, as they now face a reality where previous political guarantees no longer apply. The interventions once provided by high-level political figures, such as President William Ruto and legislator Oscar Sudi, have failed to materialize in this instance. Their lack of involvement highlights a departure from the political patronage that historically shielded these betting giants, signaling that the machinery of protection has been dismantled.

### Digital Rights and Data Commodification
This lawsuit represents a critical turning point for digital rights and consumer privacy. The core of the complaint involves the unauthorized harvesting and sale of private user information, which was treated as a commodity for corporate profit. While the industry previously operated with little regard for the fundamental privacy rights of its subscribers, this litigation seeks to establish a new standard of accountability. The case underscores a growing demand for control over personal digital assets, marking a decisive shift toward holding large corporations responsible for their data management practices.
