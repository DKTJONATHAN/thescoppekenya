## A Strike in Name Only

The Law Society of Kenya launched its nationwide court boycott today, yet the picket lines remain curiously thin. While President Charles Kanjama frames this as a bold stand for judicial reform, the fine print creates a convenient backdoor that threatens to turn a militant protest into a gentlemanly inconvenience for the state.

### The Elasticity of Urgency

The directive issued by LSK leadership on July 17 promised an indefinite disruption of court operations. However, the operational reality revealed this morning shows a strategy built on porous boundaries. By carving out exceptions for the 24-hour rule regarding criminal suspects and the extension of interim orders, the society has effectively offloaded the burden of the strike onto the individual practitioner. In practice, this allows any attorney with a billable hour to protect to categorize their presence in court as a matter of urgent constitutional necessity.

The term urgent is a legal chameleon. It is a broad umbrella under which routine procedural motions now hide, shielding the legal fraternity from the very friction their boycott intends to create. If every remand case and every routine injunction can be laundered through this loophole, the boycott loses its teeth. The state does not need to concede to demands if the machinery of the judiciary continues to churn, even at a reduced speed. This is not a cessation of service. It is merely a shift to a more selective, and potentially more profitable, schedule of operations.

### Erosion via Exception

The risk of fatigue is already evident in the corridors of Milimani and beyond. When a protest is defined by its loopholes rather than its rigidity, commitment wavers. Junior lawyers, often pressured by the demands of senior partners to keep files moving, are left to navigate a grey zone where they must decide between professional solidarity and the demands of a high-value client. This internal inconsistency creates an uneven playing field. Those willing to exploit the vague language of the LSK notice will continue to maintain their lucrative practices, while the optics of a united front slowly crumble under the weight of commercial reality.

The contrast between this performative activism and actual systemic change is becoming impossible to ignore. Much like the [Northern Kenya infrastructure developments](https://zandani.co.ke/article/northern-kenya-infrastructure-development-strategy) that are often touted as progress while failing to address the fundamental isolation of those regions, the LSK boycott risks becoming a bureaucratic exercise that changes nothing for the common litigant. While the [self-improvement movement trends](https://zandani.co.ke/article/how-the-self-improvement-movement-is-reshaping-online-trends-in-keny) currently dominate the cultural discourse, the legal sector remains stuck in a cycle of circular protest, where the rules of engagement are designed to ensure that the disruption is never truly felt by those holding the levers of power.

### The Cost of a Half-Measure

Litigants wandering into courthouses today find themselves in a guessing game. They must discern which lawyers are genuinely fighting for justice and which are merely taking advantage of the ambiguity provided by their own professional body. This confusion serves no one but the architects of the status quo. If the LSK intended to bring the judiciary to a standstill to force reform, this half-hearted methodology suggests a lack of appetite for a genuine confrontation.

A boycott that offers an exit route for every inconvenient case is not a weapon of reform. It is a safety valve. By prioritizing the convenience of the bar over the necessity of a total, undeniable disruption, the leadership has prioritized the preservation of their own professional ecosystem.

When the dust settles on this indefinite action, and the court dockets look exactly as they did before the picket signs were raised, who will actually be held accountable for the failure of this movement?
