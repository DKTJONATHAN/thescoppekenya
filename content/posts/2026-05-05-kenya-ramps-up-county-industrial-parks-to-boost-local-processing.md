## The Hard Math Behind County Aggregation and Industrial Parks

**County Aggregation and Industrial Parks are currently transition vehicles moving from expensive construction sites into either engines of local wealth or hollow monuments to bureaucratic overreach.**

The reality of Kenya's devolution project is shifting. We are moving past the celebratory phase of breaking ground on County Aggregation and Industrial Parks (CAIPs). Across the 47 counties, the rhetoric of industrialization is meeting the unforgiving arithmetic of balance sheets. For taxpayers, the question is no longer about which governor cut the ribbon, but whether these parks can generate a genuine internal rate of return or if they are destined to become idle industrial shells that drain local coffers.

### Chasing the Private Sector Ghost

The financial sustainability of these parks hinges entirely on private sector uptake. Public money provides the shell-the land, the sheds, and the basic utilities-but the engine must be private investment. Meru County offers a cautionary and instructional case study. As one of the first to transition from construction to operational status, Meru is revealing the friction of moving from theory to trade. The parks are designed around a public-private partnership model where counties lease space to processors. If the lease rates are too high, they stifle the small-scale agro-processors they intend to serve. If they are too low, the county government loses the ability to recoup the initial capital expenditure, shifting the burden of maintenance back onto an already strained tax base.

Evidence from the first 14 counties suggests a uneven uptake. Investors are not looking for vanity infrastructure; they are looking for reliable logistics and low-cost input access. Just as residents in the Rift Valley demand tangible solutions for infrastructure like the Eldoret-Shamahoho highway to ensure goods reach markets, manufacturers in CAIPs demand that these parks be more than just fenced-off plots. Without reliable electricity, water, and proximity to regional transport nodes, the private sector will simply bypass these facilities in favor of more established industrial zones.

### The Tax Incentive Trap

Counties are rushing to dangle tax incentives to lure anchor tenants, but this strategy carries a hidden cost. When a county government offers a multi-year waiver on land rates or business permits to an industrial operator, it directly reduces the immediate revenue stream available to maintain the park. This creates a circular dilemma. The park needs revenue to stay operational, but it needs to surrender that revenue to attract the very companies that would keep it alive.

This is not a purely Kenyan phenomenon; even global giants like Manchester City have learned that ignoring the math behind long-term financial health leads to systemic instability. Counties must move beyond generic incentives and toward value-based partnerships where the government earns a cut of the processing success. If the county is to recoup the millions spent on construction, the CAIPs must operate as business units, not as social welfare programs for businesses.

### Why It Matters Now

These parks represent a massive deployment of public capital during a period of extreme fiscal tightening. If the CAIPs fail to become self-sustaining within the next 24 months, the debt servicing associated with their construction will limit county development budgets for a decade. The transition from building structures to managing industrial hubs is the most dangerous phase of this initiative.

### What to Watch Next

Watch the occupancy rates in the first 14 counties over the next two quarters. If the space remains empty, look for moves by county assemblies to subsidize operational costs. Any attempt to hide these expenses within general county dockets is a red flag that the park is failing to stand on its own feet. Real success will be measured by the number of private firms signing long-term leases, not by the number of ground-breaking ceremonies reported in the press.

The transition from concrete to cash flow is rarely smooth. If counties treat these parks as real businesses rather than political trophies, there is a path to prosperity. If they ignore the balance sheet, they will be left with the modern equivalent of ghost towns, built at the expense of the very farmers they were supposed to empower.
