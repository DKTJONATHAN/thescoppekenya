## Balancing the Books on June 25

Government Spokesperson Isaac Mwaura confirmed this morning that June 25 will remain a standard business day despite calls for a memorial holiday. This decision highlights the government's push to prioritize national productivity as the country approaches a critical fiscal milestone.

### Why is the government insisting on a working day?

The decision to maintain a standard schedule is driven by the immediate need to stabilize the national economy. With the 2026 Finance Bill set for presidential assent on June 23, the administration is emphasizing the necessity of keeping the wheels of commerce turning. Every lost day of production carries a heavy price tag for the national exchequer. Data from the Kenya Revenue Authority indicates that previous civil disturbances resulted in losses amounting to Ksh 6 billion in tax revenue and infrastructure damage. For a government currently adjusting its fiscal trajectory, these daily contributions are vital to funding public services and maintaining debt obligations. The state views the preservation of a regular work cycle as a mechanism to protect the fragile economic recovery that experts have monitored since the [challenges faced by medical consultants earlier this year](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-03-16-senior-medical-consultants-seek-presidential-intervention-o).

### How does this impact the informal sector and small-scale traders?

For the vast majority of Kenyans working in the informal economy, the difference between a working day and a public holiday is often the difference between profit and loss. Small-scale traders, street vendors, and those operating in urban markets rely on consistent daily foot traffic to survive. When a day is marked as a public holiday, supply chains are often disrupted, and commuter transport services frequently scale back, effectively isolating these workers from their customers. A forced or voluntary pause in operations due to instability creates a ripple effect, reducing the daily cash flow that many households rely on for food and basic necessities. While some may support the idea of a memorial, the economic reality for those living hand-to-mouth is that every hour not spent on trade is an hour of lost income that the household budget cannot easily absorb.

### What is the broader economic logic behind this decision?

The state is signaling a shift toward maintaining institutional continuity. President William Ruto and cabinet secretaries have repeatedly emphasized that political expressions should not come at the expense of national development goals. By refusing to declare a holiday, the government is reinforcing the message that the country's economic indicators-specifically those tied to tax collection and investment confidence-are too precarious to sustain further voluntary shutdowns. The Ksh 6 billion figure associated with past unrest serves as a cautionary benchmark for policymakers. They are weighing the potential for civil disruption against the reality of a shrinking fiscal space. This approach is intended to signal to international markets and local stakeholders that despite the ongoing political discourse, Kenya remains open for business. The focus is now squarely on the implementation of the new Finance Bill, which the government argues is essential to correct the course of the national budget. Maintaining a regular calendar is a strategic move to ensure that the revenue projections underpinning the 2026 budget remain within reach rather than being further eroded by consecutive days of inactivity.

The primary objective for the administration right now is to safeguard the daily revenue intake that sustains essential public functions, ensuring the country avoids the deeper financial strain seen in previous cycles.

A working nation is the only way to fund the services that every citizen requires.
