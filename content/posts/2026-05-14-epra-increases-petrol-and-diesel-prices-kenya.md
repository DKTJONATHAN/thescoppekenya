## EPRA Fuel Prices: The Ksh46 Diesel Shock

The streets are murmuring with a singular anxiety as the latest fuel price review confirms a massive Ksh46 jump in diesel costs. For commuters and transporters, this is not just a regulatory update but a direct threat to the daily survival of the Kenyan economy.

The Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority released its latest price schedule on Thursday, May 14, 2026, catching many off guard. Effective midnight, the cost of diesel will surge by Ksh46.29, pushing the pump price to Ksh242.92 per litre. Super Petrol is also seeing a sharp climb of Ksh16.65, landing at Ksh214.25 per litre. While kerosene prices remain steady, the massive diesel hike is set to trigger a domino effect across the logistics chain. This follows a period of volatile international landing costs between March and April that regulators say forced their hand.

### Voices from the Road

At the main bus terminal in downtown Nairobi, the mood is one of quiet desperation. Samuel, a matatu conductor, says the math no longer adds up. He explains that if the stage increases the fare by even ten shillings, passengers simply choose to walk. For him, the Ksh46 jump means they are essentially working for the fuel companies, leaving almost nothing for vehicle maintenance or the owner of the matatu. He notes that unless the saccos agree on a unified fare hike, many operators might simply park their vehicles tomorrow rather than run them at a loss.

Further along the highway, long-haul truck drivers are feeling the pressure of a different kind. Jackson, who transports fresh produce from the Rift Valley to the coast, says this is the most aggressive price move he has seen in years. He points out that current freight rates were negotiated months ago based on cheaper diesel. He fears that small businesses will be the first to cut back on deliveries. For a driver like Jackson, the sustainability of the industry is hanging by a thread, as he cannot simply pass these costs onto farmers who are already struggling with razor-thin margins.

Small-scale distributors are also bracing for the hit. Sarah, who runs a grocery supply business, explains that everything from cabbage to potatoes relies on diesel-powered trucks. She anticipates a sharp rise in the price of food items by the end of the week. In her view, the cost of fuel acts as a silent tax on every household item. She is already warning her regular customers that the prices written on her price list today will be invalid by Monday morning, as transport costs begin to manifest in the cost of basic commodities.

### Why it matters now

This price spike threatens to disrupt the tenuous balance of the national supply chain. Since diesel powers the heavy machinery and fleets that move goods from rural farms to urban markets, the multiplier effect on inflation is immediate. Businesses are currently operating in a climate already stressed by regulatory shifts, such as the recent discourse around the High Court suspension of the NTSA instant traffic fines system. With transport costs spiking, the pressure on the bottom line for small and medium enterprises has reached a tipping point, potentially slowing down commercial movement across the country.

### What to watch next

The focus now shifts to how matatu saccos and cargo associations respond. Many are expected to meet over the weekend to decide whether to implement universal fare hikes. Commuters should prepare for potential disputes at bus stops and likely increases in the cost of short-haul travel. Additionally, look for how government agencies respond to the outcry from the transport sector, particularly regarding any potential subsidies or tax adjustments that could soften the blow of the diesel surge. Market analysts will also be watching to see if food prices show an immediate uptick or if retailers attempt to absorb the initial costs to prevent a total collapse in consumer demand.

The public mood remains grim as Kenyans realize that their wallets are about to get significantly lighter.
