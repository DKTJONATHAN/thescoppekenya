Why political campaigns at Kenyan funerals violate human dignity is central to this update for Kenyan readers.

## The Price of a Eulogy: How Funeral Monetization Erodes Constitutional Dignity

The hijacking of Kenyan funerals by political actors is not merely a breach of etiquette. It is a systematic violation of the constitutional right to human dignity that transforms profound grief into a transactional arena for political leverage.

For decades, the Kenyan funeral has mutated from a space of communal mourning into a high-stakes campaign podium. Politicians arrive in noisy convoys, disrupting sacred rites of passage to secure airtime. Often, they use their financial contributions as a tactical weapon, effectively purchasing the right to dominate the microphone. While this performance art is normalized as part of our cultural fabric, it directly undermines Article 28 of the Constitution, which guarantees that every person has the inherent right to have their dignity respected and protected. When a bereaved family is coerced into hosting political grandstanding simply to ensure their burial expenses are covered, our society has reached a moral nadir.

### The Transactional Trap of Funeral Giving

The relationship between funeral funding and political speech has fostered a perverted economy. Because families often face crushing burial costs, they become hostages to the benevolence of political elites. This financial reliance is a calculated trap. The donation is rarely a selfless gift; it is a down payment on a campaign speech. The moment a politician hands over an envelope, they feel entitled to hijack the proceedings. 

This predatory behavior turns the deceased into political props. It strips away the sanctity of the moment, forcing survivors to endure partisan rhetoric when they should be focused on closure. If we accept that dignity is a constitutional entitlement, we must recognize that serving as a captive audience for a political rally during a burial is a profound indignity. The current system forces families to trade their personal peace for social and financial survival, a bargain no citizen should ever be forced to make. 

### A Path Toward Restoration

We have proof that this cycle can be broken. The funeral of the late mother of Johnstone Muthama and Agnes Kavindu serves as a vital precedent. By setting firm boundaries and excluding political speeches entirely, the family reclaimed the event for its true purpose: mourning and remembrance. Their courage demonstrated that funerals can function with grace, free from the interference of the political class. 

The defense frequently offered by politicians is that they are merely attending community functions. This is a disingenuous mask for raw opportunism. A funeral is a private ceremony, not a public meeting. Just as we prioritize precision and integrity in other sectors of national life, we must apply that same standard to our social rites. We must prioritize the health of our cultural institutions by cleansing them of political toxicity.

### Why it Matters Now

The political calendar is accelerating, and the intensity of campaigns will only grow. If families do not draw firm lines immediately, funeral grounds will become primary campaign battlefields, further eroding the social cohesion that mourning is intended to foster. We must detach financial support from the right to speak. If a contribution is made, it should be done anonymously and without expectation of a microphone.

### What to Watch Next

Keep an eye on regional administrative guidelines being drafted by community elders and local governments. A growing movement seeks to formalize funeral codes of conduct that restrict political speech at private ceremonies. If these initiatives gain traction, we may finally witness the return of the quiet, dignified send-off. 

The ultimate measure of our respect for the deceased is not the size of the crowd or the number of politicians in attendance. It is the ability to maintain a space where dignity remains non-negotiable. Until we stop selling the microphone to the highest bidder, we remain complicit in the theft of our own grief.
