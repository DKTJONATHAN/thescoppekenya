## Finance Bill 2026: The Liquidity Squeeze

KCB Group posted a 15.3 percent increase in pre-tax profits to KSh 24.43 billion for the first quarter of 2026. This performance coincides with government efforts to accelerate tax filing timelines, creating a divergent economic landscape where Tier 1 financial institutions maintain high liquidity while smaller enterprises encounter mounting cash flow pressures.

The Kenyan financial sector is currently defined by a sharp divide. Major lenders are securing record-breaking margins even as proposed fiscal policies threaten to constrict the operational runway for small and medium-sized enterprises. As the state seeks to maximize revenue collection through abbreviated tax cycles, the private sector must navigate the compounding effects of elevated borrowing costs and shrinking working capital reserves.

### KCB Group Earnings and Bank Liquidity

For the period ending March 31, 2026, KCB Group reported pre-tax earnings of KSh 24.43 billion, marking a significant rise from the KSh 21.18 billion recorded during the same interval in 2025. This 15.3 percent growth trajectory is attributed to sustained interest income and strengthened loan book quality. With total assets reaching KSh 2.25 trillion, the institution exemplifies the resilience of Tier 1 lenders within a high-interest rate environment.

Yet, this institutional liquidity does not translate into broader market accessibility. Banks continue to prioritize low-risk lending and high-yield government debt, leaving SMEs with limited avenues for affordable capital. The banking sector’s profitability is currently insulated by high interest rates; however, these same rates impose a premium on capital that restricts the growth potential of average entrepreneurs who lack the diversified revenue streams of larger financial entities.

### Tax Policy and SME Cash Flow Strain

The Finance Bill 2026 introduces a structural challenge by proposing to reduce the annual tax filing deadline from six months to four. Historically, the six-month window has acted as a vital buffer for SME cash flow management. Requiring tax compliance within a 120-day timeframe effectively extracts liquidity from the private sector at a pace that outstrips the replenishment of operational reserves for many smaller firms.

This policy adjustment compounds the existing burdens of inflationary pressure and volatile energy costs. While larger corporations possess the financial depth to absorb these administrative shifts, smaller enterprises face an acute working capital crunch. These businesses are now forced to choose between aggressive accounting cycle optimization or the risk of penalties that would further diminish their narrow margins.

### Why It Matters Now

A critical disconnect is emerging between institutional profitability and the stability of the real economy. Because small-scale businesses are hypersensitive to the velocity of money, a condensed tax deadline prioritizes immediate state revenue over long-term private sector investment. This shift risks stifling expansion across the SME landscape. Investors are advised to monitor whether lenders adjust their risk appetite toward smaller borrowers as the government intensifies its reliance on the private sector to cover fiscal deficits.

### Market Context and Corporate Moves

Broader market activity reflects a trend toward consolidation and institutional restructuring. Cable Experts has moved to acquire East African Cables from receivership, a development that could reshape the regional infrastructure supply chain. Simultaneously, the judiciary has ordered the contract dispute between Digital Mara Media Limited and Kenya Airways to move toward arbitration. These events underscore a period of regulatory rigor as the market undergoes significant recalibration.

### What to Watch Next

Market participants are currently focused on the public participation phase of the Finance Bill 2026. Amendments to the four-month filing proposal remain a possibility that could alleviate sector-wide pressure. Furthermore, KCB Group is expected to announce interim dividend details alongside its upcoming half-year audit reports in July, which will serve as a bellwether for the sustainability of Q1 gains. Regional observers are also assessing whether the new cooperation agreement between the Nairobi International Financial Centre Authority and the Astana International Financial Centre will create alternative channels for foreign capital to mitigate domestic liquidity constraints.
