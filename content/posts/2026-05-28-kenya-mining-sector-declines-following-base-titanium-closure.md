## Beyond the Titanium Sunset

The conclusion of large-scale titanium mining in Kwale County serves as a definitive structural turning point for the Kenyan economy. After a decade defined by heavy reliance on beach sand extraction, the state is reorienting its strategy toward more consistent, industrial-grade mineral exports such as soda ash. This transition marks a departure from the volatile boom-and-bust cycles characteristic of coastal mineral sands, signaling a broader movement toward sectors with longer extraction lifespans and more stable market demand.

The shuttering of the Base Titanium operation in late 2025 has necessitated a major reassessment of the national mineral ledger. As the project reached the exhaustion of its viable resources, the sector confronted an immediate, significant void. Official fiscal records underscore the depth of this impact, with the total value of mineral production retreating from KSh 25.5 billion to KSh 20.3 billion during the most recent annual cycle. This downturn was largely attributed to a 37.2 percent decline in royalties linked to titanium. In its final full year, the facility produced 63,000 tonnes of ilmenite and approximately 30,731 tonnes of rutile, leaving a gap that policymakers are now working to fill through diversification.

### From Extraction to Industrial Anchor

While public attention has focused on the decommissioning of the Kwale site, the national mineral portfolio has quietly evolved. Soda ash has solidified its position as the primary stabilizer within the export basket. During 2025, production reached 289,610 tonnes, effectively providing a buffer against the revenue shortfall caused by the exit of the titanium giant. Unlike the depletion-prone nature of mineral sands, the domestic soda ash industry leverages integrated regional supply chains and consistent industrial applications, offering a more predictable fiscal trajectory.

This strategic pivot is more than a simple substitution of one commodity for another. It represents a fundamental shift in investment philosophy, moving away from high-risk, high-reward extraction projects toward the steady, long-term refinement of non-metallic industrial minerals. Although the gold sector maintains a presence in the national data with a contribution of 329.1 kilogrammes, it is the soda ash value chain that is currently providing the necessary volume to prevent state mineral royalties from sliding into a deeper deficit. For stakeholders, this recalibration suggests that the future of Kenyan mining lies in sustainability and capacity building rather than the rapid depletion of isolated sites.

### Managing the Fiscal Shock

The period between 2024 and 2025 has highlighted the inherent vulnerabilities of a mono-commodity mining strategy. In response, the government is intensifying efforts to attract new exploration ventures while prioritizing the expansion of existing soda ash and cement production lines. The primary objective is to replace the KSh 7.8 billion revenue deficit left by the titanium sector with a collection of smaller, more resilient industrial contributors.

This transition is occurring alongside a broader revaluation of economic infrastructure. Much like the rapid evolution seen in the technology sector, the mining industry is recognizing that dependence on a single asset represents a significant liability. To mitigate this risk, policymakers are focusing on the formalization of artisanal mining and the development of local processing facilities. By prioritizing domestic value addition over the raw export of materials, the government aims to create a more robust fiscal foundation. This approach is intended to offset the loss of raw material export taxes while fostering long-term industrial growth.

### A New Mineral Horizon

Looking toward the coming years, the national mining basket will likely remain in a state of flux as the state reviews pending exploration licenses and evaluates new resource claims. The path forward requires a delicate balance between managing the environmental legacy of coastal mining and maintaining the consistent royalty flows essential for public infrastructure. 

The era of the titanium giant has concluded, but the industrial base is already recalibrating to compensate for the loss. Emerging trends in commodity production illustrate the cyclical nature of the extractive industries, where the exhaustion of a major resource often acts as a catalyst for discovery and modernization elsewhere. As Kenya moves forward, the focus is shifting from simple extraction to the sustained management of a diverse portfolio of industrial minerals. This evolution is expected to strengthen the national economy against future external shocks and provide a more stable framework for long-term mineral resource development.
