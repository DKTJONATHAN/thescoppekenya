President William Ruto today, Thursday, March 26, 2026, issued a stern warning at Statehouse to oil marketers against creating artificial fuel shortages. The President also detailed government measures to mitigate anticipated supply disruptions stemming from the Middle East conflict, emphasizing consumer protection amidst rising regional airline ticket prices.

Government Responds to Fuel Shortage Warnings

Geopolitical Pressures on Supply Chains President Ruto acknowledged that disruptions at the Strait of Hormuz, a crucial route for global energy trade, are the primary factor behind the anticipated fuel shortage. He highlighted the ongoing Middle East conflict's impact on logistics and transport across this vital waterway. Reports indicate that independent fuel retailers have experienced shortages at approximately 20% of stations due to the Iran war's disruption of oil and liquefied natural gas shipments through the Strait of Hormuz. This critical chokepoint has seen a significant decline in maritime traffic, affecting around 20% of the world's daily oil supply and leading to surging oil prices.

Stern Warning Against Artificial Scarcity The President delivered a firm message to oil marketers, stating that the government would not tolerate the creation of artificial fuel shortages designed to inflate profits at the expense of consumers. He reiterated that his administration is closely monitoring every licensed oil marketer, promising consequences for any company operating outside its license conditions. These warnings come amidst accusations that some major oil marketing companies are hoarding and rationing fuel to push for upward pump price revisions. On March 26, 2026, Shell Kenya also reported temporary stock-outs at some of its service stations due to a sharp increase in demand. While the Ministry of Energy confirms sufficient national fuel reserves, current shortages are attributed to distribution challenges, with warnings issued against any hoarding or delayed distribution.

Strategic Measures and Economic Vulnerabilities To alleviate pressure, President Ruto has tasked the Ministry of Petroleum and Energy with engaging existing fuel suppliers and identifying alternative oil sources, aiming to reduce the nation's reliance on Middle East supply chains. He noted progress in securing alternative supply options without specifying the sources. Beyond fuel, the country's exposure to the Middle East crisis impacts critical exports such as flowers, coffee, tea, and sulphur blue, all now facing deep uncertainty. Za Ndani previously reported the nation remains poised for major deals to supply flowers, coffee, and macadamia to the U.S. market [cite: https://zandani.co.ke/article/agri-news-b06094ab]. The administration pledged stringent enforcement of licenses to shield consumers from exploitation.
