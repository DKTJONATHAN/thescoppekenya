The Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA) has flagged 23 petrol stations for selling adulterated fuel in its Biannual Statistics Report 2025-2026, despite ongoing crackdowns. This follows tests on 10,598 samples from 2,305 outlets, revealing a persistent 1% non-compliance rate across several counties.

EPRA Uncovers Persistent Fuel Adulteration

Report Findings and Enforcement Actions EPRA's Biannual Statistics Report 2025-2026 details that out of 10,598 fuel samples collected from 2,305 retail outlets nationwide, 99 per cent (2,282 stations) adhered to national standards. However, 23 stations were found non-compliant, leading to statutory enforcement actions and penalties. These affected stations are located in Nakuru, Uasin Gishu, Kisumu, Machakos, Makueni, Bungoma, Vihiga, Nyandarua, Kwale, Kilifi, Meru, and Mombasa counties.

Advanced Fuel Monitoring Techniques To combat fuel adulteration, EPRA employs a stringent fuel marking process where biochemical markers are added to petroleum products. This system enables the regulator to detect instances of adulteration or the diversion of fuel intended for export. Additionally, mobile laboratories equipped with X-ray fluorescence (XRF) analysers facilitate rapid, on-site forensic-level testing within five minutes, significantly enhancing detection capabilities against fraudulent operators.

Market Dynamics Amid Quality Concerns Fuel adulteration, often involving the mixing of kerosene or redirection of export-bound products, has long plagued the petroleum sector, causing damage to vehicle engines and reducing efficiency. The ongoing concern around fuel quality coincides with a period of increased domestic demand. During the review period, the country imported over 5.6 million cubic metres of petroleum, with 48 per cent allocated for local consumption. Domestic demand saw an 8.38 per cent increase, reaching over 3.1 million cubic metres, driven by heightened travel and greater diesel consumption, particularly during festive seasons. Such challenges in fuel quality underscore the importance of vehicle maintenance, an area also addressed by initiatives like the Transport Ministry's July 1 rollout for decentralised NTSA mandatory vehicle inspection. Between July and December 2025, EPRA issued nearly 9,500 licenses for various petroleum activities, including retailing and transport operations.
