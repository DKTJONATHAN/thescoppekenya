## Presidential Security Breach and the Public Disconnect

Kenyans are reacting with a mixture of cynicism and exhaustion to the latest drama surrounding presidential security. While political elites trade accusations of incompetence, the public is questioning whether these security concerns are genuine or simply another high-stakes distraction.

The digital corridors of X and Facebook have been buzzing since Rigathi Gachagua took to the podium yesterday to call out the government on its handling of the Kilifi security lapse. His fiery demand for the resignation of Kipchumba Murkomen and police chief Douglas Kanja has effectively split the internet, but not along the lines you might expect. Instead of rallying behind calls for accountability, many citizens are rolling their eyes, viewing the entire spectacle as a performance where the real issues of governance take a backseat to tribal politics.

### The Cynicism of the "Elite Unit" Narrative

When Gachagua alleged that the Presidential Escort Unit is riddled with graft and ethnic favoritism, he likely hoped to stir a firestorm of righteous indignation. Instead, he met a wall of skepticism. Many social media users are asking a simple, piercing question: if security is so broken, why is it only being weaponized during political spats?

"We have seen real threats, like the recent [security officers who foiled a terror attack on the Garissa-Dadaab road](https://zandani.co.ke/article/security-officers-foil-terror-attack-on-garissa-dadaab-road), where the focus is on protecting lives rather than scoring points," remarked one user. The consensus among the youth demographic is that the "elite unit" label has become a buzzword for whoever holds power at the time. When the former Deputy President talks about ethnic bias in the ranks, the average Kenyan is not seeing a champion of reform, but a politician trying to frame the security apparatus as an extension of his own political rivalry with the current administration.

### Voices from the Digital Frontline

The reaction across the country shows a population that has moved past taking these allegations at face value.

- Njoroge, a resident of Kiambu, posted: "Gachagua wants resignations, but he was in that office yesterday. Did he just wake up to the reality of the Presidential Escort Unit, or is he just mad he is no longer the one pulling the strings?"
- A youth advocate from Mombasa tweeted: "When leaders start accusing each other of ethnic bias in the police, they are just exposing how they all think. They do not care about a professional service; they care about who controls the guns."
- Sarah, a digital creator in Nairobi, noted: "Hassan Omar tried to apologize on behalf of the UDA, and Gachagua slammed it as scripted. Honestly, the public sees the whole thing as scripted. It is a win for the politicians who get three days of headlines, and a loss for us who have to live with the actual security gaps."

### Why it Matters Now

The narrative of "insecurity" is being transformed into a political weapon. By focusing on the Presidential Escort Unit, the debate shifts away from the systemic reforms the police force actually needs. When high-level officials argue over who gets to command the guards, the average citizen is left wondering who is left to guard the streets. The government is struggling to maintain its image as a stable institution, while Gachagua’s strategy relies on convincing a weary public that he is the victim of a biased system.

### What to Watch Next

The coming days will show if this pressure actually leads to any internal changes within the security ministries or if it is just noise. Watch for how the Presidency responds to the specific corruption allegations leveled against the escorts. If they ignore Gachagua, it looks like arrogance; if they launch an investigation, it looks like a reaction to political pressure. Meanwhile, the public will be waiting to see if these security concerns ever extend to the safety of ordinary citizens, or if we are stuck watching the same script repeat itself until the next election cycle.

The air remains thick with distrust as the political class continues to play chess with the security of the state.
