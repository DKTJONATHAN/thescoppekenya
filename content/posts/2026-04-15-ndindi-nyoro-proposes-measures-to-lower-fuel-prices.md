Kiharu MP Ndindi Nyoro sent ripples through the nation on Wednesday, April 15, 2026, unveiling a daring strategy to significantly cut fuel prices. His vocal challenge to the government’s approach, promising an immediate Ksh 27 per litre reduction, instantly captured public attention and ignited a spirited political discourse.

## Nyoro's Fuel Price Showdown

### The MP's Bold Blueprint for Relief
Ndindi Nyoro did not hold back, presenting a comprehensive plan that he believes could dramatically ease the burden on Kenyans. His proposals include a swift reversal of VAT on fuel back to 8 percent-with a vision for complete exemption during volatile periods-and the outright scrapping of the Ksh 7 fuel levy introduced in 2024. Furthermore, he advocated for a robust utilization of the Fuel Stabilization Fund, suggesting a Ksh 10 billion injection from its estimated Ksh 20 billion reserve to cushion prices until mid-May. Nyoro dismissed current government efforts as “inhumane,” emphasizing the urgent need for decisive action.

### Presidential Counter-Moves from Kisii
In a swift rejoinder during his Kisii County tour, President William Ruto defended his administration’s record, attributing the recent Ksh 28 to Ksh 40 price hikes to the Iran War and global instability. The President asserted that without government intervention, prices would be considerably higher than the current Ksh 206 average. He highlighted a Ksh 6.2 billion deployment from state coffers to moderate costs and praised the Government-to-Government (G-to-G) import arrangement for ensuring supply security, a stark contrast to fuel shortages observed in neighboring countries.

### Unanswered Questions and Public Whispers
Nyoro’s challenge extended beyond mere proposals, raising pointed questions about the opaque nature of the G-to-G import deals. He voiced concerns that such arrangements could be ripe for exploitation and demanded full transparency, drawing a comparison to 2022 when global oil prices were higher but local pump prices lower. This lack of clarity, he warned, might foster “artificial shortages” as dealers grapple with uncertainty, potentially further destabilizing the supply chain and making it harder for Kenyan households to recover from financial shocks. The MP's call for “unprecedented and fast” leadership underlines the growing urgency felt by many.
