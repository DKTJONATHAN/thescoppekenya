## Saturday Night Live and the Repackaging of Political Memory

The penultimate episode of Season 51 proved that Hollywood is no longer interested in challenging the American political establishment. Instead, the industry is comfortable turning historical political tension into a recurring performance piece, treating public controversy as a static, safe punchline.

By revisiting the 2018 Supreme Court confirmation hearings, the show demonstrates how quickly intense political battles are sanitized for mass consumption. When Damon plays Kavanaugh, he is not offering a biting critique of power. He is participating in a ritual that forces the audience to view the character as a familiar, albeit bumbling, television trope.

### The Normalization of Political Performance

In Season 51, the tone of political satire on Saturday Night Live has shifted from urgency to apathy. In previous years, the show functioned as a mirror, reflecting the immediate anxieties of the nation. Now, it operates more like a museum. It is a brilliant, albeit cynical, way to keep the show relevant while ensuring it never pushes the boundaries of its corporate ecosystem.

### Why It Matters Now

The American political establishment and Hollywood have entered a symbiotic relationship where the former provides the script and the latter provides the stage. By turning figures like Brett Kavanaugh and Tucker Carlson into sketch comedy staples, the television industry effectively neutralizes the friction of political disagreement. This is no longer about holding power to account; it is about proving that any politician, no matter how controversial, can be folded into the bland, high-budget comedy landscape of late-night television. It makes the impossible feel mundane.

### What to Watch Next
