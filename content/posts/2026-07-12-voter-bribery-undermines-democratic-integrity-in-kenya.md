## The Cost of a Shilling: Choosing Integrity Over Handouts Why voter bribery undermines democratic integrity in Kenya is the central subject of the update.

As voters prepare for the upcoming by-elections, the rising tide of campaign handouts threatens to dismantle the foundation of local governance. This shift toward transactional politics demands a critical look at how immediate cash trades away our collective future.

To the constituents of Ol Kalou and the people of Mbeere North, this is a plain message about the choice you face. Recent days have seen a worrying return to the open distribution of cash and commodities in these regions. When a campaign stops being a debate over policy and infrastructure and becomes a bidding war for your ballot, the primary loser is always the taxpayer. You are being offered a temporary relief in exchange for five years of silence from a leader who feels they have already settled their debt to you with a single note.

### The Bankruptcy of Transactional Voting

The logic of accepting a handout is rooted in a false sense of gain. When you accept money from a candidate, you are effectively selling your right to hold them accountable. If a politician buys your vote, they are under no illusion that they owe you service or development projects. Instead, they view that cash as an investment they must recoup through diverted public funds. We are already seeing the local service delivery models in regions like Mbeere North fray under the weight of such spending. When funds that should be earmarked for roads, clinics, or clean water are redirected into campaign war chests, the community pays the price ten times over.

This is a form of the sunk cost fallacy. You might think that taking the money does not change your intent to vote for the best candidate, but the systemic outcome is that only those with the largest pockets survive the primaries. We have already seen the [Kenya Economic Survey](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kenya-economic-survey-shows-slowed-growth-and-rising-debt) report that our national growth is sluggish and debt levels are high. Our local economies cannot afford to elect leaders who enter office needing to recover their campaign spending. Every shilling handed out today is a shilling stolen from the school desks, village clinics, and market stalls of tomorrow.

### Accountability as a Shared Duty

The power dynamics of our democracy shift the moment a voter accepts a bribe. You lose your leverage. A politician who owes their seat to cash bribes rather than public trust has no incentive to attend town halls or listen to petitions once they are sworn in. In Ol Kalou, the conversation must move away from who is offering the most to who offers the most credible plan for the next half-decade. If we continue to reward the highest bidder, we must stop complaining when the budget for your local road is redirected to cover personal debts or private investments.

While the noise of the election cycle often mimics the distraction of [I'm A Celebrity South Africa 2026](https://zandani.co.ke/article/i-m-a-celebrity-south-africa-2026), the stakes here are not about entertainment or points on a leaderboard. This is about the survival of the village and the prosperity of the constituency. The degradation of local service delivery is a direct symptom of treating elections as a marketplace. When you treat the ballot as a commodity, you invite a representative who views the public office as a profit center.

### A Call for Change

The responsibility to break this cycle does not lie with the police or the election officials, but with the person standing in the polling booth. It is time to treat these handouts as evidence of a candidate who has nothing of value to offer the community. A candidate who pays for your vote expects to ignore your needs. Refuse the handout, demand a manifesto, and look for a track record of service. 

Will you continue to accept a fleeting incentive that guarantees a future of underdevelopment? Demand real commitments to infrastructure and health, and make it clear that your vote is not for sale.
