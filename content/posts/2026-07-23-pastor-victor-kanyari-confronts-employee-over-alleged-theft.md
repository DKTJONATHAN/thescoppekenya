## From Asset Liquidation to Scrap Metal Pennies

A rusted bus skeleton resting on the Salvation Healing Ministry grounds stands as a somber monument to a deepening crisis. While a viral video captured the raw emotion of a recent confrontation, the situation points to a far more clinical issue: the systematic draining of charitable resources. Behind the footage lies a harsh reality where essential project assets are being dismantled and funneled into the shadows of Kenya’s scrap metal trade.

### The Math of Internal Betrayal

The July 22, 2026, confrontation involving Pastor Victor Kanyari offers a stark window into the mechanics of asset depletion. When Kanyari faced an employee accused of stripping vehicle parts and construction supplies, he was addressing more than a single theft; he was confronting a vulnerability that plagues many ministry sites and construction zones. 

The economics of this pilferage are as bleak as they are predictable. Consider the conversion of a bus into scrap. An employee might harvest copper wiring or structural steel, liquidating an asset that holds a replacement value of KSh50,000 for a meager KSh500. This disparity defines the illicit trade. For the perpetrator, the transaction offers immediate, albeit tiny, liquidity. For the ministry, the loss extends far beyond the material itself, encompassing the heavy costs of downtime and the expense of replacing what was destroyed. Meanwhile, the scrap yard middlemen operate with near-total impunity, rarely scrutinizing the origins of the metal that passes through their gates.

### The Weakness in Institutional Infrastructure

Security discussions in Kenya often center on dramatic, high-stakes crimes, such as the alarming rise of drug-related threats in nightlife districts. However, the gradual erosion of construction and ministry assets remains a silent, under-reported plague. The fundamental flaw for organizations like the Salvation Healing Ministry is a reliance on informal trust rather than hardened security protocols. When oversight is lax, inventory is treated as communal property, and large assets are easily converted into petty cash by workers under financial duress.

This pattern ripples through the broader construction sector, driving up project costs as contractors scramble to replace supplies that vanish by the kilogram. The illicit market acts as a relentless vacuum. Once heavy machinery or building supplies are reduced to scrap, they enter the smelting cycle, making them untraceable. Because these markets provide a ready outlet for stolen goods, the incentive for internal theft remains exceptionally high.

### Tightening the Perimeter

The nature of internal property threats is evolving. It is no longer just about petty pilferage; we are seeing the calculated, methodical dismantling of heavy equipment. The Kanyari incident underscores a critical turning point: the urgent need for organizations to shift away from casual site management and toward robust, tech-driven inventory systems. Without precise asset tagging and strictly monitored disposal channels, charity-based projects remain soft targets for those who understand the gap between an item's functional value and its scrap weight.

The path forward for ministry leaders and project managers requires a total re-evaluation of site security. As public awareness of the black market grows, the days of staff treating company property as a personal safety net should be coming to a close. The true scope of this breach will likely surface during the upcoming facility audit, which will finally quantify the cost of these internal leaks. For those watching from the outside, the hope is that these events force a transition toward accountability. Projects must be protected with rigorous systems, or they risk being sold off in fragments long before they can fulfill their original mission. Without a shift in how these sites govern their hardware, the cycle of depletion will continue to consume resources intended for the public good.
