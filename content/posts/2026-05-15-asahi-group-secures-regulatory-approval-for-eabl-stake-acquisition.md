## Navigating corporate shifts: Lessons from the Asahi-EABL acquisition

If you operate a distribution business in Kenya, the US$2.3 billion acquisition of Diageo’s stake in EABL by Japan’s Asahi Group is more than just corporate news. It serves as a stark reminder that your long-term contracts remain vulnerable whenever major multinationals reorganize their ownership structures. While this deal has moved forward following a High Court decision to dismiss an application intended to block the transaction, the legal tension surrounding it offers a practical lesson for local entrepreneurs on contract security and regulatory oversight.

### The leverage of the local distributor

The dispute involving Bia Tosha, a local distributor, highlighted a significant friction point in regional business. By challenging the deal and naming the Capital Markets Authority and the Competition Authority of Kenya in their legal filings, the distributor sought to hold regulators accountable for the impact of global mergers on local firms. Even though the court ultimately cleared the path for the acquisition to proceed, the move was a strategic play. It underscored a growing frustration among SMEs regarding whether their interests are prioritized during the high-level approval of multinational buyouts.

For other business owners, this creates a clear takeaway. If a major deal impacts your operations, your legal strategy may need to shift from merely challenging the multinational entity to scrutinizing the regulatory process itself. It raises the question of how much influence SMEs have when the government evaluates the competition and economic impacts of international transactions.

### Collateral damage in the consolidation wave

Large-scale acquisitions are typically driven by global strategies, but the fallout is often felt most acutely at the local distribution level. Many SMEs base their operations on long-term stability, investing heavily in infrastructure like warehousing, logistics, and market development. When a giant like Asahi enters the market, the primary goal is often operational efficiency, which can lead to the consolidation of distribution networks.

Smaller partners are frequently the first to face pressure when these new owners look for ways to cut costs. While the court has allowed the Asahi deal to move ahead, the case has already served as a warning. It signals that local firms must prepare for a landscape where their tenure is not guaranteed by historical performance alone. Much like the fluctuations seen in the banking sector, where recent data shows shifts in institutional profitability, the local business climate for independent distributors is becoming increasingly unpredictable.

### Practical steps for SME owners

If your business model depends on a long-term agreement with a multinational firm, now is the time to audit your legal protections. Begin by thoroughly reviewing your existing contracts for specific clauses regarding a "change of control" or the "assignment of rights." If these documents are silent on what happens if the manufacturer is sold, your company is essentially exposed to sudden contract terminations or unfavorable unilateral changes.

Furthermore, maintain rigorous documentation of your capital investments. Whether you have spent money on specialized storage facilities or regional market research, keeping detailed records of these contributions provides you with tangible evidence of your value to the brand. In the event of a corporate restructuring, having this data ready is your strongest defense for negotiating continued participation or securing a fair exit package.

### Why this matters for the future

The legal battle over the Asahi-EABL deal functioned as a high-stakes litmus test for Kenyan antitrust law. Even though the application to block the deal was dismissed, the process has brought the rights of local distributors into the public discourse. The judiciary's decision to allow the acquisition to proceed establishes that for now, the path remains open for such large-scale transactions.

Moving forward, SME owners should anticipate that multinational consolidation will continue to be a feature of the Kenyan economy. Just as industries across the country are adjusting to new labor standards and evolving wage expectations, local firms must modernize their legal and operational strategies. The era of assuming that a long-term partnership with a global brand is inherently permanent has passed. To stay competitive, you must treat your contracts as living documents, constantly assessing the risk of ownership shifts and ensuring your legal footing is as solid as your operational performance.
