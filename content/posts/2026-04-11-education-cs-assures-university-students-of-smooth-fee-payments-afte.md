Education Cabinet Secretary Julius Ogamba on Saturday assured 2026 university hopefuls that the government's revamped higher education funding model will ensure smooth fee payments. Speaking in Kisii County, Ogamba confirmed that reforms address past challenges with the means-testing mechanism, aiming for enhanced affordability and fairness for all students this year.

Revamped University Funding Model The government's commitment to addressing long-standing concerns over affordability and fairness in university education was reiterated by Education Cabinet Secretary Julius Ogamba. He assured Form Four leavers who sat the 2025 KCSE examinations that the revamped higher education funding model has been significantly improved. The CS noted that earlier complaints regarding the means-testing mechanism disadvantaging students from poor backgrounds have been resolved, preventing misclassification into unaffordable funding bands.

Addressing Past Disparities Ogamba highlighted that the 2024 cohort faced initial challenges with the funding model, prompting President William Ruto to establish a review team. This team meticulously examined the means-testing issues, leading to significant improvements. Consequently, the 2025 candidates, admitted in September 2025, did not encounter the same difficulties, as the Means Testing Instrument (MTI) has been revamped. The government has also indicated a commitment to continually review the system, learning from past reforms, and acknowledging the need for transparent and effective public systems. Such adjustments reflect an ongoing commitment to refine public service delivery and accountability, a principle also seen in recent judicial interventions concerning government systems, such as the High Court's suspension of the NTSA automated traffic fine system.

Equity Through Means-Testing The new university funding system is anchored on a sophisticated means-testing tool designed to evaluate a student's household financial background. This comprehensive assessment considers income levels, the number of dependents, and various socio-economic indicators to precisely determine a family's ability to contribute to education costs. Based on this evaluation, students are assigned to different funding bands, with those from lower-income households receiving higher support categories, translating into greater government funding for tuition and related expenses. This structured approach aims to ensure equity by aligning student need with government support, effectively reducing instances of misclassification that previously led to needy students being placed in unaffordable bands.

KUCCPS Portal Open for Applications The Kenya Universities and Colleges Central Placement Service (KUCCPS) officially opened its application portal on April 7, 2026, following extensive deliberations and a prolonged wait by candidates. To assist applicants, KUCCPS has announced the deployment of officers to universities and TVET institutions across all counties, providing guidance for course selections. This year, 251 public TVET colleges, including 33 university-affiliated TVET institutes, are available for placement. Secondary teacher training colleges collectively offer a capacity for 2,480 students. Universities, on the other hand, have been allocated 322,396 degree programme spaces, all approved by the Commission for University Education (CUE). Additionally, middle-level colleges boast over 1.1 million spaces for diploma, certificate, artisan, and other TVET programmes. Students admitted to degree programmes are expected to join universities in September 2026, while those placed in TVET institutions will begin reporting in May 2026 as placement continues on a rolling basis.
