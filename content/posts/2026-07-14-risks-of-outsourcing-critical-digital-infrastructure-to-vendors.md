## The Cost of Our Digital Keys

Last Tuesday, I stood at a local Huduma Centre watching a woman struggle to navigate a fee payment on her phone, eventually paying an extra Sh50 convenience charge just to access a government form. That small surcharge is the heartbeat of a growing problem where public infrastructure functions like a subscription service rather than a right.

We are drifting into a digital tenant trap. By outsourcing our primary portal for governance to private vendors, we have traded national autonomy for short-term convenience. When a state hands over its digital storefront to a third party, it does not just lose control of software updates; it effectively signs a lease on its own administrative capacity.

### The Profit Logic of Public Portals

The evolution of our eCitizen platform since 2017 highlights a dangerous fiscal U-turn. After the government initially brought the system under the direct control of the Treasury to ensure public oversight, the 2023 shift back to private management reintroduced the vendor-led model. This is not merely an administrative choice. The Auditor General’s 2025 report laid bare the reality of this arrangement: a significant portion of service fees now flows into private hands rather than the national exchequer.

When we view these Sh50 transaction fees through a wider lens, they represent a hidden tax on digital inclusion. Every time a citizen pays to access a government service, they are essentially subsidizing the private maintenance of a platform that should belong to the people. This financial loop prioritizes vendor profit margins over the stability of the system, turning basic bureaucratic transactions into a revenue stream for a corporate intermediary. Just as we watch the [KUCCPS Extends University and TVET Application Deadlines](https://zandani.co.ke/article/kuccps-extends-university-tvet-application-deadlines) process create anxiety for students when systems lag, we see the fragility inherent in platforms where the government is not the final decision-maker on infrastructure health.

### The Cautionary Tale of Dependency

Rwanda once faced a similar crossroads with its Babyl digital health service. The system promised a revolution in public health delivery but collapsed when the parent company faced international financial instability. The lesson there was brutal: when your healthcare or essential services are built on a foundation owned by a foreign or private entity, you are at the mercy of their corporate survival. If that vendor pivots, goes bankrupt, or decides the contract is no longer lucrative, the public is left with a void that takes years to rebuild.

The risk is not just a temporary outage. It is the loss of sovereign data hubs and the loss of institutional memory. By treating our digital infrastructure as something to be procured rather than something to be built, we allow proprietary black boxes to dictate how citizens interact with the law and state resources. This is a far more pressing concern than the outcomes of local sports, even when passions run high elsewhere, such as when [FKFPL: Leopards Face The Lion, Seeking to Pile Pressure on Leaders Gor Mahia](https://zandani.co.ke/article/fkfpl-leopards-face-the-lion-pile-pressure-on-leaders-gor-mahia). While one is a game, the other is the machinery of our national existence.

### Sovereignty Beyond the Code

True digital sovereignty requires more than just owning a domain name. It demands that the government maintains the ability to audit, modify, and sustain its own services without seeking permission from a vendor. When we allow private entities to hold the keys to our data and our revenue streams, we turn the state into a perpetual tenant.

We must pivot toward a model where the government acts as the architect and primary operator of its digital sphere. The convenience of a private vendor is a temporary bandage over a structural wound. If we do not reclaim the ownership of our digital infrastructure, we risk a future where accessing the state becomes a luxury, mediated by the shifting priorities of the private sector. The goal should be a system that serves the public interest, not one that requires a toll gate at every digital door.
