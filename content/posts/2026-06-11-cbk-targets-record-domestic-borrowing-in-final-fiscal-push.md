## The Trillion-Shilling Debt Ceiling: What the CBK’s Final Bond Push Means for Your Wallet CBK targets KSh 1 trillion in final domestic borrowing push is the central subject of the update.

The Central Bank of Kenya has launched a final rush for cash, aiming to secure KSh 60 billion through bond reopenings before the fiscal year ends on June 30. This push effectively maneuvers the government toward a record-breaking KSh 1 trillion in total domestic borrowing for 2025/26.

### Why does government borrowing make it harder to get a business loan?

When the government hits the market looking for billions, it essentially acts as the biggest borrower in the room. Banks find it safer and more profitable to lend to the state rather than to a small business owner in Nairobi or a farmer in Kericho. By issuing bonds with yields of 13 percent or higher, the Treasury creates a benchmark that commercial banks use to price their own loans. If a bank can earn a guaranteed return from the government without the risk of a loan default, they are less motivated to offer credit to private entrepreneurs. This is the crowding out effect. It leaves less liquidity in the banking system for the private sector, meaning the money that could have funded a new inventory order or a business expansion is instead tied up in government paper.

### How does this affect the price of your daily goods?

The connection between government borrowing and retail prices is often indirect but powerful. When credit becomes expensive or difficult for local businesses to obtain, those companies often struggle to keep their operations efficient or to scale up production. To survive high borrowing costs, businesses sometimes pass those expenses down to the final consumer. Furthermore, when the government absorbs too much of the available money supply to pay off its debts, it limits the overall economic activity that keeps competition healthy. While the government needs funds for national servicing priorities, this intense focus on debt accumulation can lead to a tightening of money in the pockets of everyday citizens. It creates a cycle where the cost of borrowing for the state puts upward pressure on the interest rates that banks charge their ordinary customers.

### Is there any breathing room for the private sector?

The current push to reach that one trillion shilling mark is a technical milestone for policy experts, but for the average citizen, it represents a period of restricted capital. With the deadline for bid submissions set for June 17 and settlement expected by June 22, the banking system will see a significant movement of funds away from private lending and toward public coffers. Investors watching these yields, specifically the 13.924 percent coupon for the 2046 maturity bond, are seeing attractive government returns that will likely keep private sector lending rates elevated for the foreseeable future. Just as we saw when [content production hit a wall due to keyword clashes](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-03-31-content-refusal-keyword-conflict-halts-production), when a system focuses too heavily on one objective, other necessary functions often face a shutdown or a slowdown. In this case, the objective is debt satisfaction, and the function facing the slowdown is the availability of affordable, accessible credit for the Kenyan middle class.

For business owners planning their next steps, be prepared for banks to remain cautious with their lending criteria as they align their portfolios with these high-yielding government instruments. If you are seeking a loan, focus on building a strong cash flow history to prove your stability in a market where lenders are being extra selective. Keep an eye on how these bond results influence the base lending rates at your specific bank over the coming weeks.
