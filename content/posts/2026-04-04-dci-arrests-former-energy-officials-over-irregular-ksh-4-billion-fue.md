The Directorate of Criminal Investigations (DCI) has arrested former Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA) Director General Daniel Kiptoo, former Kenya Pipeline Company (KPC) Managing Director Joe Sang, and former Petroleum Principal Secretary Mohamed Liban in connection with an irregular Ksh4 billion fuel deal. The DCI confirmed Saturday, April 4, 2026, that investigations are focused on two private companies implicated in the emergency cargo purchase.

Allegations of Fuel Stock Manipulation and Irregular Procurement The DCI is investigating two private companies central to a Ksh4 billion emergency fuel cargo purchase made outside the established Government-to-Government (G-2-G) oil supply framework with Gulf nations. One company, registered in 2010, is described as a prominent Kenyan oil marketing firm and a key infrastructure player in East and Central Africa, owned by a Kenyan-Indian tycoon of Indian origin and a fifth-generation Kenyan. This company operates the region's largest independent terminus at Mombasa Port for heavy fuel oil and bulk bitumen. The second company is a Swiss-owned dealer in petroleum products, operating approximately 20 petrol stations across the country.

Resignations and Ongoing Investigations Daniel Kiptoo, Joe Sang, and Mohamed Liban were arrested on Thursday, April 2, 2026, on suspicion of manipulating in-country fuel stock data to trigger the emergency purchase. Following their arrests, Kiptoo, Sang, and Liban tendered their resignations, which President William Ruto accepted on April 4, 2026, as announced by Head of Public Service Felix Koskei. While Liban was released on medical grounds, Kiptoo and Sang remain in police custody. Petroleum Deputy Director Joseph Wafula, also arrested, has not resigned and is still detained. The DCI emphasized that resignation from office does not absolve suspects of criminal culpability and urged full cooperation with investigators.

Substandard Quality and Exploitation Claims According to the Office of the President, the arrested officials manipulated data on in-country fuel stocks to create a false impression of an impending supply shortage. This allegedly pressured the Ministry of Energy and Petroleum into procuring emergency cargo from One Petroleum Limited at prices significantly above contracted G-2-G rates and in breach of established emergency procurement procedures. "This appears to have been done to exploit rising global prices and public anxiety, thereby creating a false impression of an impending supply shortfall," Koskei stated. The Head of Public Service further revealed that the Ksh4 billion shipment was of substandard quality, escalating the potential financial detriment to the public. The DCI's robust action underscores the government's commitment to tackling corruption, aligning with broader efforts to ensure accountability within public service.
