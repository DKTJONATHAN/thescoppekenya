## Kenyan High Court Upholds Digital Identity Rights

Kenya's High Court has ruled against telco reassignments of inactive phone numbers without owner consent, deeming them digital identifiers under constitutional privacy protections. This landmark decision safeguards personal data, affecting mobile money and digital accounts, and mandates the Attorney General to implement safeguards.

### Protecting Digital Identity and Privacy
The High Court at Milimani in Nairobi has declared that a registered mobile telephone number qualifies as a digital identifier, directly linking to an individual’s personal and private affairs. This ruling mandates protection under Article 31(c) and (d) of the Constitution of Kenya, which guarantees every person’s right to privacy and prohibits the unnecessary requirement or disclosure of private life information and infringement of communications.

### The Challenge to Telco Practices
The petition, initiated by Erastus Ngura Odhiambo and another, challenged the widespread telecommunications practice of deactivating and reassigning inactive phone numbers without the original owner’s knowledge or consent. This practice carries severe real-life consequences, as new recipients of recycled numbers can potentially receive M-Pesa transfers, one-time passwords for bank accounts, gain access to WhatsApp groups, and obtain verification messages for various digital services. The court emphasized that automatic deactivation of SIM cards is arbitrary because it does not account for valid reasons for inactivity, such as incarceration, students in restrictive schools, or Kenyans living abroad without roaming services. ### Implications for Telecommunications and Future Safeguards
Justice Mugambi's judgment underscores that constitutional rights, including digital identity, are not surrendered at the prison gate under Article 51. The ruling places mobile numbers in the same protective category as other private personal information under the law. The Office of the Attorney General has been given six months to implement all necessary measures to safeguard the digital identity associated with registered mobile numbers against arbitrary deactivation and reassignment. This ruling serves as a definitive warning to telecommunication firms, including Safaricom, Kenya’s largest mobile network operator, which typically deactivates lines after 120 days of inactivity. The High Court has clearly prioritized subscriber privacy over operational convenience, signaling a significant shift in the handling of digital assets within Kenya's rapidly evolving tech landscape.
