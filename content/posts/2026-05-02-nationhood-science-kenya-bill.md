## Nationhood Science Kenya to Be Mandatory in Schools

Suba South MP Caroli Omondi has sponsored the Education Laws (Amendment) Bill, 2025, proposing Nationhood Science as a new mandatory subject across all Kenyan education levels. The Bill, now before the National Assembly, invites public input by May 18, aiming to cultivate national values and citizenship responsibilities among learners.

### Defining Nationhood Science

The proposed legislation defines Nationhood Science as a program designed to instill national values and principles, foster a shared national ethos, and promote the responsibilities of citizenship among students. This new subject will align with Article 10 of the 2010 Constitution, which outlines core national values and principles of governance such as patriotism, national unity, rule of law, democracy, public participation, human dignity, social justice, and sustainable development. Omondi’s Bill specifies that the subject will incorporate elements like patriotism, national unity, integrity, accountability, inclusiveness, and encourage innovation and entrepreneurship skills.

### Integrating Nationhood Science Across Education Levels

If passed, the law will mandate that all educational institutions embed the teaching and learning of Nationhood Science within their curricula, representing a significant shift in values-based education delivery. The amendments target key education laws, including those governing universities, technical and vocational education and training (TVET), basic education, and curriculum development. Universities will be required to actively promote Nationhood Science, while TVET institutions will integrate it into their training programs. Basic education institutions, including early childhood development centers, are also expected to incorporate the subject, ensuring early exposure to its concepts. The Kenya Institute of Curriculum Development (KICD) will be central to designing and implementing the curriculum content to align with these proposed changes.

### Public Engagement in the Legislative Process

The Education Laws (Amendment) Bill, 2025, has been published in the Kenya Gazette Supplement and is currently under consideration by the National Assembly. The public, stakeholders, and institutions have been urged to submit their written memoranda on the proposed law by the May 18 deadline as Parliament prepares to deliberate on these far-reaching education reforms. This call for public participation is a crucial step in the legislative process, echoing similar opportunities for citizens to shape national policies, such as when the Independent Electoral and Boundaries Commission (IEBC) schedules by-elections for vacant seats.
