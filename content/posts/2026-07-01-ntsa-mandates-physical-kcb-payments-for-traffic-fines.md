## NTSA Shifts Traffic Enforcement to Physical Banking Channels

The National Transport and Safety Authority has mandated that all traffic fine payments must now be processed at KCB branches, effectively deactivating online payment portals to neutralize a growing wave of SMS-based financial syndicates targeting Kenyan motorists.

This policy reversal marks a departure from the government-wide digitization agenda as officials prioritize financial security over transactional speed. The authority identified systemic vulnerabilities within SMS-based payment gateways where malicious actors intercepted transaction requests, rerouting funds to private accounts while leaving motorists with unpaid citations and escalating penalties. By forcing payments through physical banking halls, the regulator aims to re-establish a verifiable audit trail that digital platforms currently fail to guarantee under existing cybersecurity threats.

### Surveillance and the Enforcement Gap

The payment directive coincides with the full activation of an extensive monitoring network consisting of 1,000 cameras. This deployment includes 700 fixed units strategically positioned along major national highways and 300 mobile cameras designed for dynamic urban deployment. The system tracks 37 distinct traffic violations, ranging from minor lane discipline errors to critical speeding offenses. When an infraction is recorded, the system generates an automated notice directed to the vehicle owner.

Motorists are now subject to a rigid 7-day payment window upon receipt of an official notification. Failure to settle the fine within this timeframe triggers an automatic blacklisting process, which restricts access to essential NTSA digital services, including vehicle inspection bookings and license renewals. Fines under this new framework are tiered according to severity, starting at KES 500 for minor infractions, climbing to KES 3,000 for standard violations, and reaching KES 10,000 for high-risk maneuvers.

### Operational Strain on Kenyan Motorists

The requirement for physical banking visits introduces significant operational friction for daily commuters. While the government previously touted the digitization of transport services as a way to reduce time spent in queues, this mandate forces motorists to trade their digital convenience for financial safety. Business owners who rely on fleet operations are particularly concerned about the logistical burden of dispatching drivers to banking halls during work hours to satisfy these requirements.

This friction follows a period of economic tension regarding transport-related costs, as seen in recent debates over fuel levies during industry-wide transport strikes. While the government maintains that the physical payment mandate is a temporary necessity to purge syndicates from the revenue collection stream, the move places a sudden, manual administrative weight on the public. It serves as a stark acknowledgment that despite the promise of automated enforcement, the technical infrastructure required to secure digital transactions against sophisticated domestic fraud has not kept pace with the rollout of surveillance technology.

### Market Reaction and Forward Outlook

Market analysts are closely monitoring the impact of this move on service delivery. Many observers argue that while the shift secures funds against theft, it creates a bottleneck that may hinder the overall efficiency of the national transport system. The focus for regulators is now on the integrity of the KCB payment gateway and the speed with which the system updates the status of a vehicle from blacklisted to compliant.

As the authority balances the transition, the next phase of the program is expected to involve a review of the fine collection software. Officials are scheduled to evaluate the effectiveness of the physical payment requirement in October 2026. Should the incidence of fraud successfully decline, the agency may consider a phased reintroduction of secured digital payment methods that utilize advanced biometric verification to bypass the vulnerabilities inherent in the previous SMS-based portals. Future updates to the surveillance network will likely emphasize tighter integration between camera telemetry and automated, secure clearinghouse protocols.
