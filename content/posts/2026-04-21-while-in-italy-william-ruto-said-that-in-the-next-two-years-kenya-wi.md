President William Ruto’s recent declaration from Italy, promising Kenya would export sugar within two years, has struck a discordant note across the nation. This April 2026 pronouncement starkly contrasts the grim reality faced by sugar farmers in Western Kenya, who are currently reeling from significant losses due to a flood of imported sugar that has crippled local production.

## Sugar Scandal: A Bitter Pill for Kenyan Farmers

While the President painted a rosy picture of future exports abroad, the situation on the ground for thousands of sugarcane farmers is anything but sweet. Companies like Kibos Sugar and Allied Industries Limited and Mumias Sugar Company are reportedly refusing to purchase locally grown sugarcane, citing an oversupply. This leaves farmers literally counting losses, with their hard-earned produce rotting in the fields, a stark reminder of economic struggles often overshadowed by grand political statements.

### The Importation Imbroglio

The root of this crisis, according to industry observers, lies squarely in the massive importation of sugar. Thousands of tonnes of the sweetener have reportedly entered the Kenyan market, creating an unfair playing field for local producers. What’s particularly galling for many Kenyans is the allegation that some of these imported consignments are being repackaged by local companies and sold off as genuine Kenyan sugar. This practice not only undercuts local farmers but also deceives consumers who believe they are supporting domestic industries.

### Greed and Hypocrisy at Play

The sheer audacity of importing sugar at the expense of local farmers, only for some entities to then profit by repackaging it as local, has left many Kenyans questioning the true beneficiaries of such policies. It highlights a painful hypocrisy where grand visions are articulated overseas, while back home, fundamental agricultural sectors struggle. This situation sharply contrasts with the potential success stories in other sectors, such as Kenya’s internationally acclaimed coffee industry, which saw its finest coffees shine at the Nairobi Taste of Harvest 2026 Africa event, showcasing what careful cultivation and market support can achieve.

The narrative of economic growth, therefore, feels hollow to those whose livelihoods are being systematically dismantled. As the debate continues, questions are being raised about the perceived political maneuvering and inconsistencies that often plague governance, reminiscent of the critical commentary surrounding matters like the [Ruto's Shauri Moyo Gambit: Johana Ng’eno Estate Hypocrisy](https://zandani.co.ke/article/rutos-shauri-moyo-gambit-johana-ngeno-estate-hypocrisy). For many, this isn't just about sugar; it’s about a system that seemingly prioritises quick profits for a few over the sustained well-being of the many.

In the streets, the sentiment is clear: one cannot export what they are busy importing, especially when it costs local farmers their daily bread.
