## The Hidden Price of Security Screenings

Nairobi residents faced a morning of gridlock today as police cordoned off key arteries into the city center under the guise of security verification. While authorities frame these maneuvers as necessary safety protocols, the ripple effect on the city's informal economy is becoming increasingly difficult for the average citizen to absorb.

### Why do police blockades hit the hustler hardest?

For a roadside vegetable vendor in Gikomba or a casual laborer waiting for a job site in the CBD, time is not just money; it is the only currency they have. When major routes like Thika Road and Waiyaki Way grind to a halt due to police checkpoints, the impact moves beyond simple annoyance. Public transport operators, unable to predict arrival times, often hike fares or refuse to head toward the city center entirely. This forced logistical standstill creates a massive productivity gap. Informal traders who rely on early morning stock deliveries find their goods delayed, leading to spoilage and lost sales. Every hour spent idling in a matatu at a security blockade represents a direct deduction from a daily wage that barely covers the cost of living. As the city continues to address governance issues, such as the [PS Bitok to Meet JSS Leaders Over Institutional Autonomy Demands](https://zandani.co.ke/article/bitok-to-meet-jss-leaders-over-institutional-autonomy), these daily operational frictions remind us that policy decisions have immediate, tangible consequences on the ground.

### How much does a city-wide slowdown actually cost?

While exact figures are notoriously difficult to track in an informal market, the scale of disruption is clear. Thousands of commuters spent their morning hours trapped in traffic on Mombasa, Langata, and Jogoo roads. If one calculates the average daily earnings for a Nairobi worker-often ranging between five hundred and one thousand shillings-the collective loss from today's delays runs into millions of shillings across the capital. Passengers have reported missing shifts, losing out on guaranteed pay for the day, and facing penalties from employers for late arrivals caused by circumstances beyond their control. This is the silent tax of instability; when movement is restricted, the economy of the streets suffers more than any other sector. Unlike formal businesses that might pivot to remote work, the survival of the Nairobi street economy depends entirely on the physical presence of people and the free flow of goods.

### Are security measures truly balanced against economic health?

Nairobi Regional Commander Issa Mohamud maintained that the police presence was strictly for security management, noting that the protests concluded without casualties. However, the disconnect between this security-centric objective and the reality of the stalled economy remains sharp. By focusing on physical access control, the authorities inadvertently created an environment where the cost of doing business became prohibitive for the working class. Ten individuals were arrested in connection with the demonstrations, but for the thousands who remained on the road, the detention was not legal but temporal. These citizens were held captive by traffic, unable to reach their places of work or conduct their business, effectively mirroring the disruption the security measures were meant to prevent. Balancing public order with the right to earn a living remains the most pressing challenge for urban management. When security becomes the primary filter through which all city movement is processed, the resulting bottleneck disproportionately drains the resources of those who can afford it the least. The pursuit of order should not come at the expense of the basic ability to participate in the local economy.

A city that cannot move is a city that cannot earn.
