## Dismantling the Imperial Presidency for Constitutional Reform

The transition from the 1963 Westminster model was never intended to produce an absolute monarch. A review of the historical record reveals that our current executive structure is not a timeless Kenyan tradition but rather a six-decade-old administrative mistake.

### The 1964 Departure from Consensus

Kenya’s initial constitutional framework, established at independence in 1963, functioned as a deliberate equilibrium of power. It utilized a parliamentary system that clearly separated the head of state from the head of government. However, this design faced rapid erosion beginning with the constitutional amendments of 1964. Within a five-year span, the state passed ten distinct amendments. These legislative acts funneled legislative, executive, and security authorities into a single, centralized office. 

This was not a process of natural political evolution. It was the calculated dismantling of established checks and balances. By 1969, the framework for the imperial presidency was solidified. This centralization elevated the executive branch above the law, fostering a culture where personal authority mattered more than institutional integrity. For decades, the presidency functioned as the sole arbiter of national destiny. This immense concentration of power now stifles ministerial performance. Much like the suffocating hierarchies documented in Bolu Essien's observations on secondary school power dynamics, the modern executive structure creates a bottleneck that prevents effective governance. When the presidency holds a monopoly over military control and the national budget, constitutional sovereignty remains a mere suggestion rather than a lived reality.

### Echoes from the Archives

The movement to rectify this structural imbalance is not a recent phenomenon. Public testimony archived by the CKRC between 2001 and 2002 reveals a consistent, weary refrain from the citizenry. Thousands of Kenyans articulated a profound fatigue regarding the concentration of power in a single individual. Their proposals consistently called for a decoupling of the roles of head of state, head of government, and commander-in-chief. These extensive consultations, paired with the 2005 Naivasha Accord, aimed to draft a blueprint for a genuinely republican executive branch.

The 2010 Constitution represented a significant attempt to constrain executive reach, yet it remains a compromised document. While it mitigated some of the worst excesses, the imperial presidency survived in its essential, core functionality. Today, we inhabit the shadow of these incomplete negotiations. Recent debates concerning fuel pricing policy serve as a case study for this dysfunction. These fiscal tensions highlight the absurdity of expecting a single office to resolve modern complexities that demand a decentralized, consultative approach to governance.

### Why it matters now

Our current governance architecture is inherently brittle because it is top-heavy. As the nation grapples with the mounting pressure to provide transparent public services and fiscal accountability, the presidency acts as a persistent barrier to progress. The existing bottleneck prevents adaptive policy-making. If we hope to restore the parliamentary oversight originally envisioned in 1963, we must separate the responsibilities of the head of state from those of the head of government. This shift would provide the necessary space for legitimate ministerial accountability.

### What to watch next

Future reform efforts will likely concentrate on the legislative mechanisms needed to redistribute executive authority. Observers should monitor upcoming constitutional petitions that challenge the current delegation of administrative and military control. These legal maneuvers will determine whether the 2010 framework serves as the definitive end of our constitutional journey or merely a transient stage in a much longer process of institutional maturation.

History provides a grim lesson for those in power. Systems that refuse to distribute authority eventually create conditions where that power consumes itself. Unless we fundamentally recalibrate these structures, we risk repeating the errors of the last sixty years, sacrificing the stability of the state for the convenience of the executive office.
