## Addressing the 2015 Companies Act Oversight

The Kenyan government is initiating a regulatory overhaul to bring non-deposit-taking digital lenders under stricter supervision. This move seeks to close a ten-year compliance loophole that has exposed consumers to predatory pricing and coercive debt collection methods. By refining the legislative framework, authorities aim to curb practices that have thrived in an environment lacking traditional banking oversight.

### The Legislative Gap and Its Consequences

The current regulatory friction stems from revisions made to the 2015 Companies Act. When these amendments were enacted, they inadvertently carved out a sector of financial entities exempt from the prudential standards governed by the Central Bank of Kenya. Because these digital firms do not hold public deposits, they bypassed the mandatory capital requirements and stringent consumer protection protocols required of conventional banks. This legal exclusion served as a foundation for the rapid, unchecked expansion of digital lending platforms. Consequently, these companies scaled their operations without the oversight mechanisms typically utilized to prevent unethical financial conduct.

### The Escalation of Financial Hardship

The economic consequences of this regulatory void are evident in the rapid transformation of the digital credit market since 2018. During that period, the sector operated with relatively moderate interest rates and clearer fee structures. Currently, the industry has transitioned to an aggressive model, with many platforms applying monthly interest rates of 10 percent, which equates to an annual rate of 120 percent. 

Borrowers seeking liquidity between KES 200,000 and KES 500,000 are frequently subjected to these exorbitant charges, often compounded by opaque administrative fees. This structure has ensnared a broad demographic in a persistent cycle of high-interest debt that was largely absent under previous fiscal regimes.

The Competition Authority of Kenya has become a focal point for recording the fallout of this policy deficit. In the fiscal year concluding in June 2023, the agency processed 180 formal complaints regarding digital platforms. Common grievances include the unauthorized exploitation of personal data, the use of intimidating communication directed at a borrower’s family, and asset retrieval strategies that violate standard collection ethics. These patterns illustrate a broader failure of policy, where the prioritization of rapid capital collection has superseded the necessity for ethical business standards. Much like the demand for improved digital infrastructure oversight, regulators now emphasize that fintech enterprises must face rigorous scrutiny to prevent widespread algorithmic exploitation of the public.

### Balancing Innovation and Consumer Rights

A collaborative reform strategy is now underway, involving the Central Bank of Kenya, the Competition Authority, and the Office of the Data Protection Commissioner. These entities are designing a mandate that requires digital lenders to provide comprehensive, plain-language disclosure of all credit costs-including incidental fees-prior to loan disbursement. By eliminating the current exemptions for non-deposit-taking entities, the government intends to compel these firms to align with standardized risk-management and liquidity ratios.

This transition marks a deliberate effort to shift the sector from an unregulated periphery into the formal financial ecosystem. However, this shift has generated pushback from some fintech operators. These firms contend that the resulting compliance costs may diminish credit accessibility for high-risk individuals who remain excluded from traditional banking institutions. 

As the government moves toward these enhanced requirements, the overall impact on market stability remains to be seen. Platforms built exclusively on high-margin, low-oversight strategies may face industry consolidation or total closure if they fail to adapt to mandated pricing transparency. Stakeholders and investors are currently anticipating the finalized regulatory draft, which will stipulate the specific licensing requirements for the 2026 fiscal year. Furthermore, additional policies governing debt collection practices are expected in the final quarter of the year. These measures signal a definitive conclusion to the permissive regulatory environment that characterized the era following the 2015 legislative updates.
