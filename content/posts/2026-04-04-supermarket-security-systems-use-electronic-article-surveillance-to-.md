Supermarkets nationwide are deploying Electronic Article Surveillance (EAS) systems in 2026 to combat rising shoplifting incidents, safeguarding inventory and revenue. These sophisticated electronic deterrents, operational at store exits, instantly alert staff when unpaid merchandise is removed, representing a critical defense strategy for retailers.

## Electronic Article Surveillance Fundamentals

### How EAS Systems Function
Electronic Article Surveillance (EAS) technology, invented in 1966 by American inventor Arthur Minasy, functions through two primary components: antennas and tags. Antennas, often referred to as pedestals, are strategically installed at store entrances and exits, emitting and receiving signals across a detection zone typically six to eight feet wide. Merchandise within the store is fitted with specialized tags or labels. Should an item with an active tag pass through the detection zone without being properly cleared at the checkout, the system immediately triggers an alarm, alerting store staff to a potential shoplifting attempt.

### Dual Technologies: AM and RF
Today's retail environment predominantly utilizes two main EAS types: Acousto-Magnetic (AM) and Radio Frequency (RF). Both are designed to prevent theft but operate on distinct signal frequencies. AM systems function at 58 kilohertz and are frequently adopted by clothing stores due to their straightforward installation, reliability, and resilience against magnetic interference. However, their inability to support paper-thin labels makes them less suitable for food or cosmetic packaging. RF systems, conversely, operate at 8.2 megahertz and are the preferred choice for grocery and cosmetics retailers because they accommodate very thin, sticker-like labels. Optimal performance for RF systems requires skilled installation and careful calibration to minimize disruptive false alarms at store exits. A crucial operational principle for all EAS technology is that tags must correspond precisely to the system in use; an AM tag will not activate an RF system, leaving the merchandise vulnerable.

### Tag Deactivation and Evolution
EAS labels are never physically removed at the till. Instead, cashiers deactivate them by passing items over a deactivator pad, which is frequently integrated directly into barcode scanners for efficiency. Hard plastic EAS tags, distinct from labels, are reusable and require a detacher device to be removed by cashiers at the register. Non-alarming hard tags can last for many years, while battery-powered self-alarming versions typically have a lifespan of two to three years. Beyond simple alarming, some tags incorporate advanced features. Self-alarming tags detect tampering and sound their own alerts. Others contain hidden ink capsules that burst upon forceful removal, permanently damaging the garment and deterring theft. This continuous evolution of EAS tag technology aims to counter new strategies developed by shoplifters, thereby enhancing security for retailers.
