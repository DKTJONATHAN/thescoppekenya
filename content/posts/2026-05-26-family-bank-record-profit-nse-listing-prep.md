## Family Bank's Profit Paradox: What It Means for Your Loans

Family Bank has announced record profits, a headline that might make you think the financial waters are calm. But beneath the surface, a surge in the bank's own borrowing and a rise in unpaid customer loans could directly affect how easy it is for you to get credit and at what cost.

### How Can a Bank Make Record Profits While Loans Go Bad?

Family Bank reported a net profit of KSh 1.60 billion for the first three months of 2026, marking a significant 52.6% jump from the previous year. This impressive profit comes as the bank prepares to list on the Nairobi Securities Exchange, having recently secured KSh 8.00 billion through a private placement. You might wonder how a bank can show such strong earnings while, at the same time, many of its customers are struggling to repay their debts.

The answer lies in how banks make money. A large part of a bank's profit comes from the interest earned on loans. Even if some loans are not performing well, the bank can still generate substantial income from the majority of its loan book. Family Bank’s total assets have grown to KSh 230.30 billion, with customer deposits reaching KSh 168.18 billion. This growth allows the bank to lend more, which in turn can lead to higher profits. However, the flip side is also visible: the bank's gross non-performing loans (NPLs) now stand at KSh 17.19 billion. This means a substantial amount of money lent out is not being repaid on time, which is a concern for the bank's long-term health and its ability to lend new money.

### What Do 'Borrowed Funds' Mean for Your Interest Rates?

For the first time, Family Bank has seen its "borrowed funds" expand to a record KSh 14.13 billion. This figure represents money the bank itself has borrowed from other institutions to fund its operations and lending activities. When a bank relies more heavily on borrowed funds, it often faces higher costs, which can then be passed on to you, the customer.

Think of it this way: if a bank has to pay more interest on the money it borrows, it will likely charge higher interest on the loans it gives out to maintain its profit margins. This could translate into more expensive personal loans, business credit, or mortgages for ordinary Kenyans and small businesses. Increased borrowing by banks can also signal tight liquidity in the broader financial system, potentially affecting overall credit availability in the market. As Nairobi continues to grow, initiatives like the push to modernize [street lighting systems through public-private partnerships](https://zandani.co.ke/article/nairobi-to-modernize-street-lighting-system-through-public-private-p) will need a healthy and stable financial sector to secure funding.

### Will Your Business Loan Get Approved?

The bank also saw the sharpest single-year deterioration in its net NPL exposure, which now sits at KSh 1.14 billion. Net NPL exposure is the amount of bad loans that the bank expects not to recover, even after accounting for provisions it has set aside. This figure is particularly important because it directly impacts a bank's risk appetite.

When a bank's bad loans increase, it usually becomes more cautious about who it lends to. This might mean tighter credit filters for loan applications, especially for small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) that are often seen as higher risk. If you run a small business or are planning to apply for a personal loan, the bank's rising NPLs could mean you face stricter eligibility criteria, require more collateral, or get offered loans at higher interest rates. While Family Bank's shareholders' funds remain strong at KSh 34.77 billion, providing a buffer, the trend of rising NPLs suggests caution in lending decisions moving forward.

### Why it matters now

Understanding these dynamics is crucial for working Kenyans. Family Bank's strong profits are good news for its shareholders, but the underlying trends of increased borrowed funds and rising non-performing loans could make accessing affordable credit more challenging for you. This impacts job creation, as businesses find it harder to get capital to expand, and it can also affect your personal purchasing power if borrowing costs go up.

### What to watch next

Keep an eye on interest rates offered by Family Bank and other lenders. If the trend of increased borrowed funds and NPLs continues across the banking sector, it could signal a general tightening of credit conditions. For businesses, monitoring how banks adjust their lending policies will be key to planning future growth. It's always wise to maintain a strong credit history and explore different financing options to ensure you can access funds when you need them.
