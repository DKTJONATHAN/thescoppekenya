## The Sweetness That Turned Sour for Kenyan Wallets MPs probe KRA over Ksh2.98 billion sugar import tax exemptions is the central subject of the update.

Nairobi residents are expressing sharp skepticism after reports emerged that billions in tax waivers meant to ease the cost of living failed to reflect on retail shelves. The public sentiment is clear: while officials argue over customs papers, the sugar prices in local kiosks remain stubbornly high.

The National Assembly Departmental Committee on Trade is currently dissecting a massive Ksh2.98 billion tax exemption granted to sugar imports, questioning the Kenya Revenue Authority on why documentation appears incomplete. During hearings this week, Commissioner Lilian Nyawanda insisted that the process followed existing customs laws, even as MPs pointed to glaring discrepancies in the origin of the shipments. With fingers pointed at Singapore and South Africa as potential transit hubs for this sugar, the committee is now digging into whether these waivers were a genuine intervention to lower food costs or a calculated move to pad corporate profits.

### Voices from the Sugar Aisles

At a busy retail outlet in downtown Nairobi, skepticism is the order of the day. John Kamau, a trader who has managed a shop for over a decade, says that wholesale prices rarely drop just because a waiver is announced. He points out that if the government loses nearly three billion shillings in taxes, the benefit should reach the person buying a two-kilogram bag. Instead, he notes that they still buy stock at high prices from distributors, leaving them with no choice but to keep the retail price where it is. He believes the tax savings are likely being swallowed by middlemen before the sugar even reaches the neighborhood shops.

In the residential suburbs, households are equally vocal about the lack of relief. Mary Achieng, a regular shopper, mentions that she hears about these multi-billion shilling deals on the news, but her monthly budget for essential commodities like sugar and cooking oil never seems to shrink. She suggests that if these waivers were truly designed for the common person, the government would monitor the final price tags in the market rather than just auditing paper trails in Parliament. To her, the news of the probe feels like another cycle of bureaucratic noise that changes nothing at the dinner table.

Public accountability watchdogs are also weighing in, expressing frustration over the KRA internal auditing standards. A representative from a civil society group monitoring fiscal policy argues that these exemptions are often shrouded in complex customs codes that mask the real economic winners. They emphasize that the focus on whether the paperwork was signed off correctly misses the bigger picture: the distortion of the market. According to the watchdogs, if a tax waiver is granted on the promise of consumer relief, there must be a mechanism to enforce a price reduction, otherwise, the KRA is essentially subsidizing corporate margins at the expense of the national budget.

### Looking Beyond the Paperwork

The parliamentary committee chaired by Bernard Shinali is expected to continue its probe into the missing documentation and the origin of the imports. As the investigation deepens, the focus is shifting from simple compliance to the actual economic impact of these exemptions. There is growing pressure for the tax authority to explain why imports tied to such large tax breaks do not correlate with lower landed costs for the average consumer. While other national issues like the [LSK push to hold officials accountable for defying court orders](https://zandani.co.ke/article/lsk-to-pursue-officials-defying-court-orders) dominate the headlines, the sugar question remains a sore point for taxpayers who feel they are paying for a subsidy they never receive.

The next steps for the committee involve reconciling the conflicting reports on where the sugar originated and how much it actually cost to land in Mombasa. If the audit confirms that the savings did not translate to market relief, the push for stricter controls on tax waivers will likely gain momentum. For now, the public waits to see if this parliamentary scrutiny will lead to policy changes that prioritize the consumer or if it will end like so many other inquiries into government revenue losses.

Across the country, the mood remains one of weary resignation, with citizens watching closely to see if accountability will ever actually translate into lower daily costs.
