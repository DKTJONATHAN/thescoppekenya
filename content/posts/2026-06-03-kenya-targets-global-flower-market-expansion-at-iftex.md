## The Export Expansion Gap at IFTEX 2026

The Kenyan floriculture industry, a cornerstone of the national economy, currently generates KES 110 billion in annual export revenue. Despite this robust performance, smallholder producers are encountering severe liquidity challenges as the government shifts its strategic focus toward North American and Asian markets. These competing priorities were a central theme at the 13th International Flower Trade Exhibition held at the Visa Oshwal Centre.

During the event, 210 exhibitors highlighted the sector’s resilience, noting that Kenya currently supplies 40 percent of all flowers imported by the European Union. Cabinet Secretary Lee Kinyanjui expressed a clear government objective to decrease dependence on the established EU pipeline, which accounts for €500 million in yearly shipments. However, for the average small-scale grower, this push for diversification introduces a significant logistical paradox. Successfully penetrating non-EU markets necessitates advanced cold-chain infrastructure and specialized air-freight logistics. These requirements often exceed the cost structure of current domestic operations, creating a barrier that only the largest corporate estates can effectively navigate.

While the industry sustains 200,000 direct jobs, the primary obstacle to reaching these new international frontiers is the persistent delay in government VAT refunds. These capital-intensive backlogs deplete the working budgets of mid-sized farms. Without the benefit of substantial financial buffers, these producers struggle to meet the rigorous quality standards and extended transit requirements demanded by buyers in North America and Southeast Asia. Unless tax policy is synchronized with these ambitious export goals, global market expansion will remain restricted to a handful of large-scale enterprises.

### The Logistics of New Market Access

Exporting to the United States or emerging Asian economies requires a level of consistency and shelf-life precision that the current EU-centric freight model is not designed to support. Smallholder farmers operate on narrow profit margins, and the rising costs of air freight to distant, non-traditional hubs frequently erase the price premiums offered by new international buyers. When transportation costs surge, they are deducted directly from the farm-gate price, forcing the producer to absorb the volatility of global fuel prices while waiting months for pending tax offsets.

The requirements for these new markets extend well beyond securing a buyer. Each destination mandates specific phytosanitary protocols and pre-cooling standards that necessitate localized investments in sophisticated pack-house technology. Although industry leaders advocate for market diversification, the existing infrastructure leaves the average farmer disconnected from these high-value opportunities. The current reliance on the European trade route is a matter of survival rather than preference. It remains the only viable path because it utilizes established, predictable logistical chains that do not yet exist for Pacific or North American trade corridors.

### Bridging the Liquidity Hurdle

Clement Tulezi, CEO of the Kenya Flower Council, has frequently noted that a KES 110 billion industry cannot sustain growth while essential capital remains locked in state coffers. The ongoing delays in VAT refunds force many farmers to secure high-interest credit simply to cover basic operational inputs. For a producer aiming to upgrade facilities to meet the stringent quality demands of a broader global market, this cycle acts as a functional barrier to entry. Stakeholders are now calling for greater transparency and speed in state-managed financial obligations to prevent the stagnation of the sector.

The success of the mandates discussed at IFTEX 2026 will ultimately depend on the government’s capacity to lower the entry barriers for smaller participants. If the state fails to provide a direct mechanism to expedite VAT repayments and mitigate the freight costs linked to exploring new global corridors, the transition toward North America and Asia will likely remain confined to the exhibition halls. To ensure that smallholders can participate in this expansion, the coming season must feature a pivot from setting ambitious export targets toward a fundamental overhaul of the domestic financial environment.
