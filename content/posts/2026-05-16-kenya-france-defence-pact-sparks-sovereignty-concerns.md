## The Ghost of 1964: Why the France-Kenya Pact Feels Like Déjà Vu

The ink on the Kenya-France defence agreement is barely dry, yet it carries the heavy scent of an older era. As we examine the immunity clauses granted to French personnel, we find ourselves retracing the same legal vulnerabilities that have defined our post-colonial trajectory since the mid-sixties.

### A Legacy of Jurisdictional Erosion

When Kenya transitioned to independence, the 1964 defence agreements with the British were framed as temporary safety nets. These arrangements introduced the concept of the Status of Forces Agreement (SOFA), effectively carving out zones where local law was secondary to the internal disciplinary codes of the visiting military. It was a concession of sovereignty sold to us as a necessity for regional stability. Sixty years later, the framework has barely shifted. 

The current pact with France, solidified in late 2025, mirrors these colonial-era structures with unsettling precision. While modern diplomats speak of strategic partnerships and regional security in the Sahel or the Red Sea, the fine print remains a relic. We are still negotiating the same surrender of judicial authority that we were warned against in the dawn of our republic. The evolution of this policy shows a steady drift from non-alignment toward a permanent dependency on Western military presence, yet the price for this protection remains paid in our own courtroom limitations.

### The Long Shadow of Nanyuki

We cannot discuss current agreements without acknowledging the unresolved tragedy of Agnes Wanjiru. The 2012 death of a young woman in Nanyuki served as a brutal longitudinal reference point for what happens when judicial sovereignty is outsourced. Despite the public outcry and the subsequent legal back-and-forth, the BATUK framework proved a fortress against local prosecution. 

The French agreement arrives in a climate where citizens are increasingly sensitive to these immunity gaps. When Soipan Tuya and the diplomatic envoys like Arnaud Suquet engage in these high-level summits, the rhetoric often centers on the 23 billion euros in investment or security cooperation. However, for the average Kenyan, the question is not about the grand geopolitical chessboard. It is about whether our legal system maintains the teeth to hold any individual accountable for harm caused on our soil. We have seen the same script play out for decades; the promise of modern security often masks a regression into old patterns of extraterritorial impunity. 

### Why It Matters Now

The timing of this agreement, coming on the heels of the Africa Forward Summit in Nairobi, signals a fundamental shift in Kenya's strategic posture. As Western nations retreat from instability in Mali, Niger, and Burkina Faso, Kenya has positioned itself as the new anchor for their regional interests. This pivot is significant because it trade-offs our domestic judicial independence for a seat at the high table of Western military logistics. The debate is no longer just about a single treaty but about the long-term cost of hosting foreign armies in a post-sovereign, multipolar world.

### What to Watch Next

As the implementation of the pact begins, the focus must shift to the operational details of the SOFA. Watch for how the government addresses the inevitable friction between local investigative agencies and French military police. If the precedent of the 2012 case remains the ceiling for our expectations, we should anticipate growing tension between the state's security ambitions and the public's demand for equal justice under the law. 

Public discourse is shifting away from the glamour of international summits toward the grittier realities of administrative sovereignty. We are witnessing a cycle that began in 1964, and the lessons we failed to learn then are now demanding our full attention. History rarely repeats itself without keeping the receipts.
