## The Eurasia Pivot: Is Kenya Trading Urban Development for Debt-Laden Infrastructure?

President William Ruto is currently in Baku, Azerbaijan, marketing Kenya’s urban renewal ambitions at the 13th Session of the World Urban Forum. While State House paints these diplomatic circuits as a triumph of global advocacy, the move signals a calculated pivot toward non-traditional, opaque lending markets.

The script is familiar: the President arrives in a foreign capital, poses for handshakes with leaders of regimes known for their own human rights and transparency struggles, and promises that "partnerships" will solve Kenya’s internal crises. This time, the destination is the Eurasia bloc-specifically Azerbaijan and Kazakhstan. But when the dust settles on these trade agreements, the question remains whether these deals are about genuine trade or simply finding new, less scrupulous creditors to replace the drying wells of the IMF and the World Bank.

### The Debt Shadow Behind the Architecture

In Nairobi, the Affordable Housing Programme is currently being funded by aggressive taxation-stripping the paychecks of workers who are already reeling from [rising operational costs](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-15-online-cab-fares-set-to-rise-by-50-per-cent-as-drivers-anno) and a shrinking middle class. The rhetoric in Baku suggests that international investment will supplement this effort. However, historical precedent warns that whenever the state reaches for foreign infrastructure loans to bridge budget gaps, the repayment terms are often buried in classified annexes. 

Azerbaijan and Kazakhstan are not typically known for their interest in Kenya’s social welfare. They are, however, major energy and infrastructure players with deep pockets and a preference for bilateral arrangements that bypass the stringent oversight of Western financial institutions. By pivoting East, the administration avoids the fiscal discipline demanded by traditional lenders, but it likely commits the country to higher interest rates and collateralized assets-potentially the very ports or energy corridors that are supposed to serve the Kenyan public.

### Transparency in the Blind Spot

The shift from Western institutional partners to Eurasian state-led entities is a fundamental change in foreign policy. These regimes do not operate under the same transparency standards as the multilateral institutions Kenya has leaned on for decades. When the government signs these memoranda in Astana, the public will likely be fed soundbites about job creation and technology transfer. What will remain invisible is the nature of the sovereign guarantees attached to these loans.

If the government’s energy sector is any indicator of how these deals end-look no further than the ongoing [calls for leadership resignation](https://zandani.co.ke/article/2026-04-04-pressure-mounts-for-energy-cs-opiyo-wandayi-to-resign-follo) following recent administrative failures-the risk of entrapment in high-interest, non-transparent contracts is high. The administration’s refusal to disclose the terms of recent deals suggests that this Eurasia pivot is not about securing better trade. It is about finding a lender that does not ask too many questions about how the money is spent or how the debt will be paid back.

### Why it matters now
The national debt remains the single greatest threat to domestic stability. By diversifying the pool of creditors, the government is not reducing its liability; it is merely shifting the risk to a new set of lenders whose terms are less visible to the public. If these agreements are tied to future infrastructure projects, taxpayers will be footing the bill for decades, regardless of whether the projects actually deliver affordable housing.

### What to watch next
Watch for the formalization of these trade agreements in the coming weeks. Specifically, track whether these deals include "take-or-pay" clauses for energy infrastructure or if future housing projects are linked to the importation of materials and labor from the lending countries. If the details remain shielded under "confidentiality clauses," citizens can safely assume that the cost of these projects far outweighs the purported benefits to the local economy.

If the state is truly committed to the success of its housing agenda, why must it go thousands of miles away to secure funding that it refuses to account for at home?
