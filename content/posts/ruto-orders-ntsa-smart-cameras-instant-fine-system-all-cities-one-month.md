## Ruto Orders NTSA: The Ambitious Smart Cameras Instant Fine System Rollout

President William Ruto has directed the National Transport and Safety Authority (NTSA) and the Ministry of Transport to implement a smart traffic camera and instant fine system across all major cities within one month. This mandate, issued on Monday, March 1, 2026, aims to curb road carnage and systemic corruption within the traffic department.

### The One Month Deadline: A Race for Smart Cameras in All Cities

The presidential directive for a nationwide rollout of the smart camera instant fine system within a mere thirty days presents a formidable logistical and technical challenge. While the NTSA has already commenced a broader Integrated Transport Management System (ITMS), involving the deployment of 1,000 smart cameras (700 fixed and 300 mobile units) on major highways such as Thika Superhighway, Mombasa Road, and the Southern Bypass, extending this to "all major cities" comprehensively within such a short timeframe is highly ambitious. The system relies on automatically capturing violations, sending SMS notifications to registered vehicle owners, and posting fines to NTSA accounts, with payments facilitated via mobile money, USSD, or banking channels. However, the rapid expansion risks overlooking critical infrastructure gaps in various urban centres, potentially leading to an uneven or incomplete implementation. Questions arise about adequate testing, public sensitisation across diverse urban populations, and the readiness of backend systems to handle a surge in instant fines in areas beyond the initial pilot zones.

### Beyond Fines: Unpacking the Smart Cameras Instant Fine System's True Impact

The government’s primary justification for this expedited rollout is to enhance road safety and eradicate pervasive corruption within the traffic police department. Indeed, President Ruto expressed frustration over "years of inaction" and explicitly linked the initiative to revenue collection, stating it "should be a priority for the government." This dual emphasis raises critical questions about whether the system prioritises revenue generation over genuine behavioural change and accident reduction. The project, structured as a 21-year Public Private Partnership (PPP) with KCB Bank Kenya and Pesa Print, involves an initial investment of Sh42 billion. While designed to limit human interaction and thus reduce bribery, such large-scale, technology-driven procurements inherently carry their own set of integrity risks if oversight is not rigorous. The effectiveness of digital enforcement also hinges on the widespread adoption of Smart Driving Licenses, yet only about 1.3 million of an estimated 5 million drivers possess these essential e-DLs, signalling a significant digital readiness gap that could impede the system's full potential and fairness to all motorists.

### Systemic Integrity and Digital Integration: More Than Just Smart Cameras

The directive to significantly reduce the number of traffic officers on the roads, coinciding with the operationalisation of smart cameras, directly addresses a long-standing public grievance regarding corruption. The Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission (EACC) has consistently highlighted deep-rooted systemic corruption within the traffic department, with a November 2025 report revealing officers were assigned daily bribe collection targets. The EACC even recommended the disbandment of the traffic unit and regular rotation of officers to curb this institutionalised bribery. While technology offers a potent antidote to such direct human-interface corruption, it is not a panacea. The success of the "Smart Cameras Instant Fine System" relies not only on robust technology but also on transparent administrative processes for appeals, equitable application of fines, and continuous public education. The government's broader digital agenda, as seen in initiatives like the deployment of [Mawingu Starlink Internet to Deploy to 450 Rural Hubs in Kenya, Enhancing Digital Access](https://zandani.co.ke/article/mawingu-starlink-internet-450-rural-hubs-kenya), underscores a national push towards technological solutions. However, for the NTSA's ambitious project, the focus must extend beyond mere installation to fostering a culture of accountability and ensuring that the digital transition genuinely serves justice and safety for all Kenyans, rather than merely automating revenue collection.
