## Unpacking the Budgetary Tug of War in Nairobi Nairobi protests highlight health worker demands and memorial pla is the central subject of the update.

Public funds are being pulled in two directions today as health workers and advocacy groups descend on the capital, highlighting a growing tension between settling long-overdue labor debts and accounting for the high costs of civil unrest.

### Why are Universal Health Coverage workers protesting for permanent contracts?

Health staff recruited under the Universal Health Coverage program have hit a wall after six years of temporary service. These workers, who form the backbone of local dispensaries, are demanding that their positions be converted into permanent and pensionable terms. The central issue is financial: the Ministry of Health has consistently struggled to balance the national budget, often leaving gratuity payments for these workers in arrears.

For the average Kenyan, this is not just a labor dispute. When these contracts remain in flux, service delivery at the grassroots level suffers. Local clinics face staffing shortages as experienced personnel leave for more stable roles in the private sector or abroad. The government currently faces a fiscal choice: either absorb these workers into the permanent payroll, which increases the long-term recurrent expenditure, or continue managing the high turnover costs associated with short-term contracting. The latter often ends up being more expensive due to constant training and recruitment needs.

### How does memorial planning intersect with state expenditure?

Alongside the health workers, political leaders like James Orengo and Martha Karua were at the Nairobi Central Police Station today. They are seeking authorization for a memorial procession scheduled for June 25, 2026, to honor those lost during the protests of June 2024. While the march itself is a matter of social justice and memory, it brings the government face-to-face with the hidden price tag of civil unrest.

State-sanctioned events of this nature require significant administrative and security resources. Beyond the immediate deployment of police officers, there is the lingering issue of reparations. If the state continues to ignore the grievances stemming from previous demonstrations, it risks facing prolonged legal battles. These litigation costs, paired with the potential for future civil liability, represent a significant drain on public coffers. It is a harsh reality that the money spent on managing protests and potential litigation could otherwise be redirected into the health sector or infrastructure projects similar to the [massive regional oil refinery proposed for Mombasa](https://zandani.co.ke/article/aliko-dangote-favors-mombasa-for-oil-refinery-project).

### What are the fiscal implications for the ordinary taxpayer?

The Kenyan exchequer is currently performing a delicate balancing act. The government is essentially funding two types of crises. On one side, there is the operational deficit in the health sector caused by unresolved employment status. On the other, there is the mounting social and legal pressure from the families of victims from past protests.

Every shilling spent on protest management or settling court-ordered compensation is a shilling diverted from the essential services that keep our clinics functional. As Muchiri Nyagah noted during the discussions with authorities, the goal is to ensure that the events of two years ago are not forgotten, yet the administrative hurdles put in place by the state only add to the mounting costs of civil tension. This cycle of conflict, much like [the fading applause that follows short-lived political promises](https://zandani.co.ke/article/the-echo-of-applause-a-familiar-fading), serves only to exhaust public resources. Kenyans must realize that whether through unpaid gratuities or the high price of maintaining order during memorials, the budget is paying a premium for unresolved administrative and social grievances.

True fiscal stability in Kenya depends on the state settling its labor debts to avoid service collapse and addressing its civil liabilities to move past the trauma of the 2024 unrest.
