## The Hidden Cost of VAT on Repossessed Collateral

Kenyans are bracing for yet another potential hike in loan interest rates as banks warn that the new tax on repossessed assets will be offloaded onto ordinary borrowers. This battle over fiscal policy is hitting the pockets of everyday citizens.

The Kenya Bankers Association recently locked horns with the Kenya Revenue Authority before the tax appeals tribunal regarding the Finance Bill 2026. The legislation mandates a 16 per cent VAT on the sale of property or goods seized from borrowers who default on their loans. While the tribunal ruled in favor of the KRA, allowing the tax to persist, bankers argue that these sales are merely recovery efforts to recoup losses rather than commercial profit-making ventures. The association is now pivoting to lobby for a legislative amendment to protect lending margins, as the current environment reflects broader challenges in Kenya economic shifts, infrastructure projects and regional ties.

### The trickle-down effect on your pocket

For the average Kenyan seeking a personal loan or a mortgage, this tax is not just a line item in a government ledger. Financial analysts point out that banks operate on risk-pricing models. When the cost of recovering a defaulted asset increases, that 16 per cent tax essentially becomes a sunk cost for the lender. To maintain their bottom lines, banks are expected to bake this risk into their interest rates for all customers. If a bank knows that selling a repossessed vehicle or piece of land will now cost them significantly more in tax, they will simply increase the cost of credit for everyone else to offset the potential future impact. It is a classic case of the taxpayer and the borrower ending up as the losers while the state revenue coffers swell.

### Three perspectives from the street

Business owners and consumer rights advocates are already voicing their frustration over what they see as a double-edged sword for the struggling economy. 

A Nairobi-based credit consultant suggests that the tax effectively punishes the borrower twice. Once for failing to pay, and a second time by shrinking the pool of affordable credit available to the public. He argues that this move will force banks to tighten their underwriting criteria, making it even harder for small-scale entrepreneurs to secure start-up capital.

A consumer rights representative notes that the burden will inevitably move from the boardroom to the shop floor. She claims that whenever a bank faces an extra cost, it does not eat that loss; it passes the bill to the customer in the form of higher monthly repayments or increased processing fees. To her, this is a clear sign that fiscal policy is becoming increasingly detached from the reality of the average citizen who is already struggling with the cost of living.

A local property auctioneer echoes these concerns from the front lines of the repossession cycle. He explains that auction prices for seized goods are already depressed because of low liquidity in the market. Adding 16 per cent VAT to these already thin margins makes it much harder for these assets to find buyers, which could lead to a backlog of stagnant assets sitting on bank balance sheets and further restricting credit flow.

### Why it matters now

This policy creates a barrier to entry for many Kenyans. By taxing the recovery of assets, the government is inadvertently making it more expensive for banks to manage risk. This directly translates into higher interest rates for loans, credit cards, and mortgages. In an economy where professional commitment is a daily practice not a talent, the last thing people need is a higher financial hurdle when trying to expand their businesses or buy homes.

### What to watch next

The Kenya Bankers Association is expected to take their fight to the National Assembly, hoping to secure an exemption for collateral disposal in the final version of the Finance Bill 2026. If they fail, borrowers should prepare for banks to adjust their risk premiums. The next few weeks will be crucial as legislators weigh the government's need for revenue against the banking sector's warning about the health of the credit market. Expect to hear more noise from lobby groups as the debate shifts from the courtroom to the floor of Parliament.

The public mood is one of wary exhaustion, with many Kenyans feeling that the tax net is tightening just as their financial flexibility continues to fade.
