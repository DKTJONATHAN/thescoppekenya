## The Ghost of 2013: Why County Pending Bills are a Repeat of the Municipal Council Collapse

The Sh183 billion debt crisis currently strangling Kenya’s counties is not a novel administrative mishap. It is an echo. A weary glance at our fiscal history reveals that this decay mirrors the terminal decline of the defunct municipal councils. Unless the government performs deep, structural surgery, the devolution experiment risks a total collapse, retracing the path of its failed predecessors.

### Lessons from the Ledger of Lost Authorities

In the years before 2013, Kenyan local governance was synonymous with insolvency. Municipal councils operated in a fever dream where procurement existed in a vacuum, entirely detached from available cash. Contractors were frequently engaged for projects that lacked any budgetary backing, resulting in debt mountains that eventually rendered service delivery impossible. When the county government system debuted in 2013, the constitutional framework was sold as a firewall against such chaotic financial management.

We were wrong to assume the transition offered a clean slate. The current Sh183 billion in pending bills mirrors the reckless habits of those old councils. Modern counties persist in a model where the ledger remains permanently tilted toward insolvency. Nairobi County alone accounts for a staggering KSh86.8 billion, a burden that obscures the operational capacity of smaller, less visible units. When procurement cycles sprint ahead of actual revenue collection, service stagnation is the inevitable outcome. 

Much like the recent government strategy of shifting burdens onto consumers through new electricity levies, counties are effectively downloading the costs of their own poor planning onto local suppliers. The structural architecture of this debt is identical to the rot that claimed the municipal councils of the early 2000s. We have simply changed the names on the stationery.

### Procurement as a Tool for Paralysis

The crisis is rooted in a fundamental misalignment of priorities. Recurrent expenditure consumes Sh130.8 billion of the total debt, while development projects languish at Sh52.2 billion. This is not a temporary liquidity dip. It is a profound failure of governance. When an administration authorizes a contract without verifying the funds, it operates under the dangerous assumption that the public treasury is an infinite credit line.

Years ago, this exact practice destroyed all trust in local authorities. Reliable contractors fled the market, and those who dared to stay inflated their bids to account for the persistent risk of non-payment. We are watching this inflationary cycle repeat itself in counties like Kilifi, Machakos, and Kiambu. The Senate Committee on County Public Accounts and Investments is currently attempting to manage the fallout through hearings, but these efforts remain reactive measures applied to a chronic disease. Even as leadership pushes for high-profile infrastructure projects, the central question of affordability remains unanswered. Until accounting officers are legally mandated to tether their procurement calendars to the actual release of equitable share funds, the debt will continue to compound.

### Why it Matters Now

The financial health of our counties acts as the bedrock for the entire devolution project. When suppliers go unpaid, the downstream effects are predictable: small businesses shutter, credit markets seize up, and the private sector loses all faith in local governance. If this trajectory remains unchecked, we risk a total erosion of local service delivery. Such a catastrophe would eventually force the national government to reclaim county functions, effectively dismantling the progress achieved over the last decade.

### What to Watch Next

Legislators are currently considering a total freeze on new projects for the 2026/2027 financial year to stem the bleeding. While this policy may be a painful necessity, it serves only as a temporary salve. The true test of our resilience will be whether the government can finally implement rigid, automated budgetary controls that link every single procurement request to a verified digital audit trail. 

History provides a brutal, repetitive lesson: fiscal mismanagement does not resolve itself through patience or oversight committee reports. Without a fundamental shift in how we handle public money, we are simply waiting for the inevitable collapse. We are once again treading the path of our ghosts.
