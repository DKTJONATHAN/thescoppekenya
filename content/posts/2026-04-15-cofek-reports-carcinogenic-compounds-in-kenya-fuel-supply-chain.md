The Consumers Federation of Kenya (COFEK) today, April 15, 2026, issued an urgent alert regarding hazardous carcinogenic compounds identified in the nation's fuel supply chain, following independent laboratory analysis. The findings indicate potential widespread public exposure to these dangerous substances.

Fuel Supply Contamination Confirmed COFEK commissioned Conti Testing Laboratories in Bethel Park, Pennsylvania, an internationally accredited facility, to analyze fuel samples drawn from the country’s supply chain. The results confirmed a significant presence of carcinogenic halogenated bromides in transit fuel and fuel destined for regional markets. Alarmingly, the same compounds were detected in fuel diverted to the local market, suggesting consumers may already be exposed through daily fuel use.

Marking System Under Scrutiny Unmarked domestic fuel samples reportedly tested clean, a finding that points to a direct link between the contamination and the fuel marking process itself. This process is managed by SICPA SA, a Swiss-based firm contracted by the government to supply chemical markers designed to detect adulteration and track petroleum products. SICPA SA's contract was initially awarded in June 2022 and extended in June 2025. COFEK plans to initiate civil action seeking a permanent ban on SICPA SA and Société Générale de Surveillance (SGS) from operating in the nation's fuel sector, citing unfair trade practices and alleged anti-competitive conduct. The federation is also preparing private criminal prosecution against five individuals, alleging abuse of office and reckless endangerment of consumer health.

Serious Health Implications Brominated organic compounds are associated with various cancer risks, including liver, skin, thyroid, and testis malignancies, with some identified as multisite carcinogens in laboratory studies. They can also cause endocrine disruption, neurotoxicity, liver damage, metabolic disorders, and immune suppression. Exposure can occur through inhaling fuel vapors at petrol stations or vehicle exhaust emissions as compounds are released into the air when fuel is burned. COFEK has called on the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA) to immediately suspend the SICPA SA fuel marking program, recall fuel currently in circulation, test for contaminants, and publish the full chemical composition of the marker system used.

Broader Fuel Crisis Context These revelations emerge amidst an ongoing national fuel crisis. Recent scrutiny has focused on Cabinet Secretary for Energy Opiyo Wandayi concerning the alleged importation of Ksh4.8 billion worth of substandard fuel, believed to contain high amounts of sulphur. Such challenges in the fuel supply chain are further complicating the energy landscape, with implications that could potentially lead to increased costs for sectors like aviation, as airlines consider fare increases amidst broader disruptions to fuel supply.
