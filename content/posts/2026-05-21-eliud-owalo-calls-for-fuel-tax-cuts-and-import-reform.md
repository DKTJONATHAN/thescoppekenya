## The Sh80 Burden: Why Owalo’s Math Challenges the State’s Energy Monopoly

**The relentless rise in fuel prices is not a global fate we are forced to accept, but a local policy choice built upon layers of state-sanctioned inefficiency.**

For too long, the Kenyan taxpayer has been fed a convenient narrative: that our fuel pump prices are a direct hostage of volatile global markets and the lingering shocks of geopolitical conflicts. This justification has become the default shield for a government strategy that prefers taxation over efficiency. However, economist and presidential contender Eliud Owalo is stripping away the varnish, revealing that the misery at the pump is a manufactured outcome of systemic domestic friction.

### The Anatomy of an Overpriced Litre

At the heart of the current crisis is a fiscal architecture that treats the fuel pump as an inexhaustible revenue stream. When you pull up to a petrol station today, roughly Sh80 of every litre paid represents a cocktail of taxes, levies, and miscellaneous charges. This is not just a rounding error. It is a massive, structural weight that sits on the shoulders of every commuter, transporter, and small business owner in the country.

Owalo’s critique highlights a disturbing mathematical reality: Kenya’s petrol and diesel prices currently hover 19 percent and 14.1 percent above global averages, respectively. These figures are not the product of the international market. They are the product of an 8 percent VAT on fuel combined with a web of levies that seem designed to punish consumption rather than foster growth. When a government opts to tax an essential production input as if it were a luxury good, it inevitably stunts the entire economy.

### The G2G Illusion

The Government-to-Government (G2G) fuel import deal, once heralded as the panacea for our foreign exchange woes, has revealed itself to be a closed loop of non-transparency. Designed to bypass the immediate need for hard currency, the deal has instead locked the country into a rigid procurement model that lacks the competitive bidding necessary to drive down premiums. 

By removing the competitive tension that usually governs oil imports, the state has effectively created a captive market. When procurement lacks transparency, the costs are almost always hidden in the fine print and passed directly to the consumer. Similar to how the state recently fumbled the oversight of the Kenya Airways advertising contract, the G2G framework lacks the accountability required to protect public interest, leading to the inflated premiums we see today. We are paying more to sustain a broken, centralized system than we would if we embraced a transparent, market-driven procurement strategy.

### Structural Failures and the Path Forward

The path to relief is not found in more subsidies, which only defer the pain, but in fundamental structural reform. Owalo’s proposal hinges on two pillars: dismantling the tax-heavy model and building actual energy security. 

Currently, Kenya lacks a meaningful strategic petroleum reserve, leaving us perpetually vulnerable to short-term supply chain disruptions. True energy security requires the capacity to buffer against price spikes through localized reserves, not through the whims of a single government-managed contract. If the state were to pivot toward diversifying our supply chains and trimming the bloated tax component, the immediate relief to the cost of living would be seismic.

### Why it matters now
Economic stability has been a flashpoint for public unrest, particularly following the protests observed in late June 2026. As the cost of basic commodities remains tethered to the price of fuel, the government's refusal to audit its energy policy is no longer just a fiscal issue; it is a direct threat to social order.

### What to watch next
Watch for whether the Treasury makes any attempt to adjust the 8 percent VAT in the upcoming budget readings. If there is no movement on the tax component or a move to replace the G2G deal with an open, competitive tender system, the disconnect between state rhetoric and the reality on the ground will only widen.

The state acts as if the high price of fuel is a force of nature, but the math proves it is a human construct. Until we confront the Sh80 tax burden and the failures of our procurement monopoly, we will continue to pay a premium for our own stagnation. True energy independence starts when the government stops using the fuel pump as its private ATM.
