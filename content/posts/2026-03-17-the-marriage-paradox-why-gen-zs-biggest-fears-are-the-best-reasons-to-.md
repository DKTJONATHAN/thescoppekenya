Sitting in a Westlands cafe this Tuesday morning, I’m watching Gen Z professionals navigate a world of endless options and zero commitment. Marriage has become the ultimate fear, yet the reasons they avoid it are exactly why they need it.

<p>I’ve spent more than a decade documenting the highs and lows of the Kenyan entertainment scene. I have seen celebrities throw million-shilling weddings in Karen only to delete every trace of their spouse from Instagram six months later. I understand why the younger generation—the 20-somethings I see every day—looks at marriage and sees a trap. They see it as a loss of identity, a financial drain, or just a lot of "hard work" that doesn't seem to pay off. But after ten years of observing the chaos of the "situationship" era, I’ve realized that the very things Gen Z is running away from are the things that could actually save them.</p>

<h2>The Fallacy of the Perpetual Soft Life</h2>

<p>The biggest hurdle for Gen Z is the idea that marriage is "hard work." We’ve commodified the "soft life" so much in Nairobi that anything requiring effort feels like a failure. I hear it all the time: "Why would I sign up for a project that never ends?" They see marriage as a job they can't quit. But here is the reality I’ve observed: everything worth having is a project. You work hard on your personal brand, you work hard on your fitness, and you work hard on your career. Why is it that when it comes to the person who will hold your hand when you’re seventy, effort is suddenly a red flag?</p>

<p>When people say marriage is work, they aren't saying it's a chore. They mean it’s an investment. The "work" is what creates the "rich and lovely" life that many people crave but are too scared to build. It’s about finding a live-in best friend. In a city like Nairobi, where everyone is constantly "networking" and "vibe-checking," having one person who knows your soul, your messy morning face, and your deepest anxieties is a luxury that money cannot buy.</p>

<h2>Why Accountability is Better Than Absolute Freedom</h2>

<p>There is a fear that getting married means losing yourself. Gen Z is the generation of "main character energy," and they worry a spouse will dim their light. I’ve found the opposite to be true for the couples who actually make it. A good marriage doesn't erase you; it refines you. I’m a better person because I have someone who gently holds me accountable. In my years of writing, I’ve seen talented people spiral because they had no one to tell them "no" or to point out when they were being inconsiderate or snarky.</p>

<p>Having a partner who graciously overlooks your grumpy responses but also pushes you to be better is the ultimate hack for personal growth. You can "put your best face on" for the world, but your spouse knows the real you. That kind of love—the kind that sees the flaws and stays to help you fix them—is far more empowering than the shallow "yes-men" culture we see on social media.</p>

<h2>Crisis Management in an Uncertain World</h2>

<p>Another reason I see young people avoiding the altar is the belief that they can do it all alone. They are independent, high-earning, and self-sufficient. But life in 2026 is unpredictable. Whether it’s a medical emergency, a sudden career shift, or emotional turmoil, being a "strong independent person" is exhausting when the world is falling apart. I’ve watched friends go through crises alone, and I’ve watched them go through them with a spouse.</p>

<p>There is a massive advantage to having someone who is strong where you are weak. My husband is a champion in a crisis; he stays clear-headed when I’m spiraling. That balance is what keeps a household standing. Marriage provides a safety net that a group of friends—no matter how loyal—simply cannot replicate. There is a specific kind of reassurance in knowing that another human being is committed to your well-being for the long haul.</p>

<h2>The Exhaustion of the "What Are We?" Phase</h2>

<p>Let’s talk about the nights. Gen Z spends so much time on dating apps, "talking," and wondering where they stand. It is mentally draining. One of the most beautiful parts of marriage that no one talks about anymore is the simple peace of "sharing our nights." There is something incredibly grounding about reaching over in the middle of the night and knowing someone is there. No more wondering if they’ll text back, no more wondering if they are seeing someone else, and no more performative dating.</p>

<p>We’ve replaced the security of commitment with the "excitement" of the hunt, but the hunt eventually gets old. The pleasure of married life isn't just about the physical; it's about the emotional rest. It’s about being "the chosen one" and having that honor every single day. We enjoy going for walks, watching old movies, and the simple thrill when your person walks into the room. That doesn’t have to die just because you signed a marriage certificate.</p>

<h2>Building a Legacy Beyond the Gram</h2>

<p>Finally, there’s the parenting aspect. I see many young Kenyans saying they want kids but don't want the "hassle" of a spouse. As someone who has watched the dynamics of different family setups in the limelight, I can tell you that doing this together is a game-changer. Relying on someone else’s wisdom and direction when you are feeling down as a parent is invaluable. It’s about more than just splitting bills; it’s about a shared vision for the future.</p>

<p>Marriage, when done right, is a light to the world. People are naturally drawn to a loving relationship. When you see a couple holding hands, laughing, and actually talking at a restaurant in Sarit Centre, it gives people hope. It shows that in a world of temporary things, something can actually last. So, to the Gen Z crowd looking at marriage like it’s a death sentence: maybe stop looking at what you’re giving up and start looking at the thousand or more reasons to look forward to it. It’s not just about the "sherehe" of the wedding day; it’s about the life you build when the music stops.</p>
