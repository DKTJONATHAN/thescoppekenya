## The Hidden Tax of Justice: Why Personal Injury Claimants Are Being Pushed Out of the Small Claims Court

The Judiciary frames its latest shift as an operational cleanup, but the numbers reveal a different reality. By moving road accident injury claims out of the Small Claims Court and into the Magistrates Court, the state has quietly made justice a luxury for the wealthy.

The Chief Justice wants the public to believe this is about efficiency. Behind the sanitized language of the gazette notice lies a cold calculation: road accident victims, often already struggling with medical bills and loss of income, are being moved to a venue where the price of entry is significantly higher. The Small Claims Court was designed to bypass the expensive, labyrinthine corridors of formal litigation. By stripping it of its mandate to handle personal injury claims, the Judiciary is effectively closing a door that was meant to be the last refuge for the common citizen.

### The Cost of Admission

In the Small Claims Court, the filing fee is capped and the process is simplified. It is a system built for the person who cannot afford to keep an advocate on a permanent retainer. Move that same case to a Magistrates Court, and the financial landscape shifts violently. Filing fees in the Magistrates Court are pegged to the value of the claim. As these claims grow in monetary demand to account for medical inflation, the cost of initiating a case creates an immediate barrier. 

Beyond the court fees, the procedural complexity of a Magistrate's Court almost necessitates legal representation. While the Small Claims Court operates under a user-friendly framework that encourages self-representation, the Magistrate's Court is a different beast. Here, the rules of evidence and civil procedure demand a level of technical expertise that the average claimant lacks. For many Kenyans, the cost of hiring a lawyer to navigate these proceedings will consume a significant portion, if not the entirety, of any eventual compensation. Just as commuters find themselves paying more for a seat on an [Easy Coach bus](https://zandani.co.ke/article/easy-coach-increases-bus-fares-across-major-routes-following-fuel-pr) due to rising fuel costs, victims of road accidents are now facing a surge in the cost of seeking redress for their own bodies.

### Legal Attrition by Design

Legal aid advocates are already sounding the alarm. When the barrier to entry rises, cases do not simply find a new home; they vanish. This is known as legal attrition. A victim who cannot afford the higher filing fees or the mandatory legal representation will be forced to abandon their claim entirely. This is not justice reform. It is an exercise in case-load reduction through the silent exclusion of the most vulnerable.

Low-income earners, who rely on public transport and are disproportionately affected by the hazards of the road, are being told that their grievances are too cumbersome for a court meant to be fast and accessible. The Judiciary may claim this protects the core mandate of the Small Claims Court, but it does so by sacrificing the very people that mandate was supposed to serve.

### Why it matters now

The cost of living crisis, recently highlighted by [sky-high personal expenses elsewhere](https://zandani.co.ke/article/sh49000-for-braids-kenyan-nurse-judy-california-salons-high-cost), has left little room for error in the average Kenyan household budget. By moving these claims, the Judiciary has added an invisible, heavy tax on injury victims precisely when they are least capable of paying it.

### What to watch next

Keep a close eye on the filing statistics in the Magistrates Courts over the coming months. If the number of personal injury claims drops significantly, it will be the clearest indicator that the policy has achieved exactly what critics fear: the total alienation of the low-income victim.

If a system is designed to make the pursuit of compensation more expensive than the injury itself, can it truly be called a court of law?
