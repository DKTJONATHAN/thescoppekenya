Recent corporate filings from the Registrar of Companies have confirmed that Joshua Kulei, the former aide to the late President Daniel arap Moi, holds a 32.99 percent stake in Liaison Capital, a firm centrally involved in major government infrastructure projects valued at Sh457 billion.

## The Financial Architects of State Infrastructure

### Legacy Figures in Modern Contracts
The Kenyan political landscape continues to reflect a pattern where figures from previous regimes maintain significant influence over current national developments. Corporate documents filed in May reveal that Kulei is a primary stakeholder in Liaison Capital, the firm serving as a key partner for the Sh42 billion Talanta Sports City and the Sh415 billion Kenya Defence Forces housing project. These disclosures illustrate that the established political elite remains deeply embedded in the administration of multibillion-shilling state initiatives.

### The Mechanism of Wealth Accumulation
Public-Private Partnership models have become the primary vehicle for current infrastructure development. Proponents of these partnerships suggest they foster economic growth, but observers argue the model effectively minimizes traditional procurement oversight. This structural shift provides a pathway for well-connected individuals to secure major contracts, ensuring that the financial rewards of national development projects are directed toward private interests rather than solely the public treasury.

### Evidence of Institutionalized Patronage
The involvement of Moi-era figures in modern state contracts suggests that the current administration is utilizing long-standing networks of patronage. By leveraging public-private frameworks, elites are able to secure massive government projects with limited transparency. This trend confirms that the infrastructure of crony capitalism remains a defining feature of the national economy. As the government continues to award these high-value contracts, the concentration of wealth among a select group of individuals highlights a persistent cycle of influence that survives political transitions.
