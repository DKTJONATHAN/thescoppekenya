President William Ruto confirmed daily load shedding is unavoidable across some parts of the country during peak hours, citing a severe energy deficit. Speaking at the Ambassadors and High Commissioners' Conference at the Kenyatta International Convention Center (KICC) on Saturday, March 28, 2026, the President reiterated that the measure is essential to manage the widening gap between power demand and available supply.

Energy Deficit Necessitates Load Management

Current Power Supply and Demand The national grid currently maintains a capacity of 3300 megawatts, yet the nation requires a minimum of an additional 10,000 megawatts to adequately meet its energy demands. According to data from the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA), electricity demand has surged to 2,316 megawatts, marking the highest level in five years. This growing disparity forces Kenya Power to implement intentional electricity cuts in rotating areas, typically between 5pm and 10pm daily during evening peak hours, to prevent a total collapse of the national grid. President Ruto stated that these power outages significantly undermine the economy.

Official Acknowledgment and Outage Statistics President Ruto first publicly acknowledged the practice of load shedding in November 2025 during a meeting with citizens living abroad in Doha, Qatar. His recent remarks follow a new EPRA report, covering the first half of the 2025/26 financial year, which revealed that customers experienced an average of 8.39 hours of power outages per month. This figure represents a slight decrease from the 9.15 hours per month recorded in the corresponding period of the previous year.

Government's Long-Term Energy Strategy To counter the persistent energy challenges, the government plans to utilize the newly established National Infrastructure Fund (NIF). The objective is to increase energy production to the national grid by at least 10,000 megawatts within the next five to seven years. This projected increase would be sufficient to meet the current electricity demand approximately four times over. Such significant infrastructure development is vital for national economic growth and progress. Parallel efforts are also underway, as the Ministry of Education finalises a competency-based curriculum framework for universities.
