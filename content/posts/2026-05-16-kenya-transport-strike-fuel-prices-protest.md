Kenyan Transport Stakeholders Call Nationwide Strike Over Fuel Pr is central to this update for Kenyan readers.

Transport stakeholders have confirmed a nationwide strike for May 18, 2026, to protest the latest fuel price hikes. The protest targets the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority, specifically challenging the inclusion of non-fuel levies that have pushed retail costs to Ksh242.92 per liter.

## The EPRA Margin: Unpacking the Hidden Levies Beyond Global Oil Trends

The official narrative from the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority on May 14 was a masterpiece of selective transparency. It cited global crude volatility to justify the jump in pump prices from Ksh214.25 to Ksh242.92. What remained unspoken, and what the transport sector is finally choosing to confront, is the deliberate engineering of these prices through domestic taxation rather than international market pressures.

When a motorist pays for a liter of fuel, they are not merely paying for the product. They are funding a sprawling architecture of government revenue collection. The current price includes a Ksh16.65 hike and a separate Ksh46.29 surge, neither of which corresponds to the barrel price on the global market. These figures represent the state's decision to treat the pump as a primary revenue stream to cover persistent overheads that have nothing to do with the actual energy supply chain.

The frustration bubbling in Nairobi and across the country is rooted in the realization that the tax components embedded in the Ksh242.92 retail price now account for nearly 50 percent of the total cost. While the government frames this as a necessary response to fiscal reality, transport stakeholders recognize it as an aggressive extraction strategy. The strike is not just about the cost of petrol or diesel. It is an industrial rejection of the mathematical gymnastics EPRA performs every month to justify keeping the treasury full at the expense of the logistics sector.

### The Paper Trail of Hidden Costs

Look closely at the receipts. The fuel prices are weighted down by levies that serve as silent taxes on every single movement of goods and people. By obfuscating these charges as part of an inevitable global surge, the regulator avoids a direct debate about the efficiency of state expenditure. When the costs of logistics rise, the impact ripples through every household, mirrored in the rising desperation seen in recent fiscal shifts, such as the trends identified in the [Fuliza User Base Hits 17 Million Amid Declining Loan Sizes](https://zandani.co.ke/article/fuliza-user-base-hits-17-million-amid-declining-loan-sizes) report.

Transport associations have made it clear that the current model is unsustainable. They are no longer accepting the convenient excuse of international trends. They have identified that a significant portion of the fuel price is fixed by domestic policy, meaning it could be lowered if there were political will to reform the tax structure. The insistence on maintaining these levies suggests that the state views the transport sector as a bottomless piggy bank, ignoring the fact that the sector is now teetering on the edge of total collapse.

### Why it matters now

The planned industrial action on May 18 is a collision between economic survival and state fiscal hunger. By inflating fuel costs with levies that bypass market fundamentals, the regulator has created an artificial floor for living costs that most Kenyans can no longer afford. The strike signals that the tolerance for being treated as a secondary revenue generator has finally expired.

### What to watch next

The focus will shift to whether the government attempts to suppress the strike through security mobilization or if it finally sits down to address the specific tax architecture of the pump price. If the authorities persist in blaming the global market while ignoring their own tax-heavy spreadsheets, the pressure from the transport sector will likely escalate into broader, more disruptive civil unrest. 

Does the state actually believe that the transport sector can continue to operate as a fiscal shock absorber for the national budget, or is it waiting for a complete halt of the economy to acknowledge that the math simply does not add up?
