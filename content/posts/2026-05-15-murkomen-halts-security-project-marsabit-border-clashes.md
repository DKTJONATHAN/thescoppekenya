## The Geography of Conflict: Why Border Security Infrastructure Often Stumbles in Northern Kenya

Cabinet Secretary Kipchumba Murkomen has halted a security facility project along the Moyale-Marsabit-Isiolo corridor following violent protests and boundary disputes. The suspension reflects a recurring failure in state planning that prioritizes centralized mapping over local realities in Northern Kenya.

The decision to pause construction arrives after tensions flared into clashes across Saku, Moyale, and Sololo on May 14. While the Ministry of Interior is now tasked with sourcing an alternative site, the core issue remains the persistent mismatch between national project blueprints and the intricate web of customary land tenure. Much like the broader challenges seen in [new logistics regulations for Kenyan truckers](https://zandani.co.ke/article/new-federal-cdl-rule-impacts-kenyan-truckers-and-job-seekers), the government frequently underestimates how colonial-era administrative lines collide with contemporary community interests.

### A History of Disputed Boundaries

The Marsabit-Wajir corridor is not merely a stretch of road but a zone where history remains an active participant in modern governance. Over the decades, efforts to install government infrastructure in this region have frequently ignited dormant disputes.

*   **1998:** Early attempts to formalize border markers triggered inter-community tensions that paralyzed infrastructure development for nearly five years.
*   **2014:** A failed survey project, intended to stabilize security, collapsed when local leaders challenged the legal status of the land parcels designated for state use.
*   **2022:** A series of administrative decrees meant to clarify county boundaries were met with court injunctions, preventing any physical construction of government installations on disputed stretches of the Moyale-Isiolo road.

These incidents highlight a pattern where administrative convenience at the ministry level ignores the ground-level reality of grazing rights and clan-based land usage. When the state treats these boundaries as simple lines on a digital map, it inadvertently re-opens grievances that were never properly resolved through traditional or formal legal channels.

### The Failure of Top-Down Planning

The 2010 Constitution mandates robust public participation for any project affecting land use, yet this requirement is often treated as a checkbox rather than a consultative process. In the case of the halted security facility, the disconnect is visible. National authorities view the facility as a necessary tool to curb insecurity, while local communities view the site selection as a territorial power play that favors one county over the other.

This systemic blind spot in the survey process ignores that land in the arid North is not just real estate; it is a vital resource tied to migration routes and water points. By bypassing deep engagement with elders and local representatives, the government ensures that any project-even one intended for public safety-is perceived as an encroachment. It is a recurring cycle: the project is announced, local resistance mounts, violence ensues, and the state retreats, leaving the region no more secure than before.

### Why it Matters Now

The suspension of the Moyale security project underscores a critical vulnerability in national security strategy. When the state cannot successfully occupy the space it intends to secure, it loses its ability to enforce the rule of law. If the government fails to reconcile its infrastructure goals with the requirements of the 2010 Constitution regarding community land rights, it will continue to lose both capital and public trust in the North.

### What to Watch Next

The immediate priority for the Ministry of Interior involves navigating the complex land claims before a new site is chosen. Observers should watch for whether the ministry invites the National Land Commission to conduct a fresh audit of the disputed corridors. Without a transparent process that accounts for customary land tenure, the government risks repeating this failure at the next site.

Infrastructure is only as permanent as the consensus that builds it.
