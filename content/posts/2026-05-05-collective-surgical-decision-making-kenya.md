The Surgical Soloist: Why Kenyan Hospitals Need a Tenth Man to Curb Preventable Deaths

Following the 1973 Yom Kippur War, the Israeli military underwent a somber period of institutional soul-searching. Their intelligence failures were traced back to a culture of consensus that effectively stifled dissent. In response, they institutionalized the Tenth Man doctrine: a mandate requiring at least one officer to actively oppose the prevailing group opinion to uncover overlooked hazards. In Kenya, our surgical environments operate with the opposite logic. Here, personal autonomy is frequently equated with professional mastery, while silence is misread as collective agreement.

### The Cost of Surgical Silence

During my tenure at the University of Dar es Salaam, I witnessed a clinical culture that valued deliberative practice. Senior consultants regularly encouraged junior registrars to scrutinize the surgical strategy, viewing dissent not as a challenge to their status, but as a necessary safeguard. This stands in stark contrast to the climate within the University of Nairobi system. In our local hierarchy, questioning the surgeon is often treated as a career liability. This systemic flaw is not a reflection of technical incompetence, but rather the danger inherent in a vacuum where one individual wields unchecked authority over a patient.

Aviation offers a pertinent, if underutilized, blueprint. A pilot does not rely solely on individual intuition to navigate a landing. They operate within rigid, multi-layered protocols that require validation from the entire crew and ground control. Conversely, our hospitals often leave the surgeon to operate in an environment devoid of institutional oversight. While investors are diversifying their portfolios away from singular, high-risk assets toward more balanced, fixed-income strategies, our medical sector persists in concentrating all of its operational risk upon a single point of failure. We have yet to implement the mandatory, multidisciplinary frameworks that would elevate surgery from a subjective, individual judgment to an objective, institutional responsibility.

### Institutionalizing the Review

Reforming this system requires a shift in both architecture and economics. Our current billing models prioritize volume over clinical deliberation. We must transition toward a model where multidisciplinary team meetings are recognized as billable, essential medical interventions. If a patient is billed for a diagnostic scan, the system should also recognize the hours required for a panel of oncologists, radiologists, and surgeons to debate the necessity of a procedure.

Furthermore, we need transparent, peer-reviewed cancer outcome registries. The existing system is largely built to shield the surgeon from scrutiny rather than protect the patient from poor outcomes. By adopting the Tenth Man doctrine, we would force a critical pause in the surgical pathway. When a procedure is proposed, a designated panel-a group of Tenth Men-would be required to review the case file. This is not an effort to diminish expertise; it is a mechanism to provide a safety net for those moments when even the most seasoned practitioner succumbs to human error.

### Why it Matters Now

Preventable surgical mistakes persist as a silent epidemic because we continue to view surgery as a private transaction rather than a public health obligation. Often, families are forced to navigate this terrain alone, unaware that the system itself is structurally predisposed to a single, unchallenged perspective. Whether one is tracking the volatility of financial markets or monitoring our national infrastructure, the conclusion remains constant: systems that refuse to stress-test their own decision-making processes are built to fail.

### What to Watch Next

Monitor the upcoming health policy white papers emerging from the Ministry of Health. Specifically, look for provisions regarding mandatory clinical audit committees and the public disclosure of patient outcomes. If these new directives do not establish multidisciplinary review meetings as a formal prerequisite for major surgeries, the status quo will remain unshaken. True progress will not be marked by the inauguration of new hospital wings or the acquisition of high-end equipment. Instead, it will be found in the quiet, necessary willingness of a surgeon to invite a dissenter into the operating room.

History serves as a weary reminder that the most significant risks often manifest when everyone in the room is in complete agreement. We must learn to welcome the shadow of the tenth man into our hospitals.
