## The Twilight of the Late Show Era

There was something profoundly poetic about Tom Hanks handing Stephen Colbert a vintage Underwood typewriter last night. As the studio lights dimmed, the exchange felt less like a birthday tribute and more like a final curtain call for a medium that once defined global pop culture.

The recent announcement that CBS will sunset *The Late Show* franchise by May 2026 feels like the closing of a massive, glowing door. For decades, the late-night monologue was the primary heartbeat of the entertainment world, a ritual that dictated what we talked about the next morning. Now, that heartbeat is fading into the static of a digital-first landscape. When CBS pulls the plug, it will not just be the end of a long-running show; it will be the official burial of the appointment-viewing ritual that taught generations of broadcasters how to hold an audience.

### The Old Guard and the Digital Shift

Tom Hanks appearing on stage, clutching a typewriter, paper, and a fresh pot of coffee, served as a living museum piece. He represents a Hollywood era built on prestige, longevity, and the shared experience of watching a master craftsman hold court in front of a live studio audience. His latest work, a documentary series on World War II, mirrors this legacy, grounded in historical reflection rather than the rapid-fire, algorithm-chasing content that defines 2026.

In Kenya, we have long looked at shows like *The Late Show* as the gold standard for broadcast production. For years, our own local media houses modeled their talk segments on the sleek, desk-and-mug format popularized by figures like David Letterman and carried forward by Colbert. We studied their timing, their musical accompaniment, and their ability to pivot from geopolitical commentary to celebrity banter. But as our own consumption patterns shift toward on-demand streaming and short-form social clips, the relevance of that hour-long, daily commitment has cratered. Just as global charts are being re-evaluated through the lens of longevity and impact-much like the metrics used to rank stars like Wizkid in [Wizkid Ranked Best: Unpacking ChartMasters' Metrics and Africa's Music Legacy](https://zandani.co.ke/article/wizkid-ranked-best-chartmasters-metrics-africa-music-legacy)-the late-night talk show is finding that its influence no longer translates to mass-market survival.

### Why it matters now

The decline of *The Late Show* is a metaphor for the broader death of linear television. When the screen demands a specific time of day for you to show up, it assumes a level of cultural monopoly that no longer exists. Today, we curate our own entertainment. If a segment is truly viral, it hits our phones in seconds; the rest of the hour is often relegated to background noise. CBS is making a cold, calculated move to stop funding a relic, choosing instead to reallocate resources toward platforms that meet the viewer where they are, rather than asking the viewer to wait for them. This transition is not just about a network changing its schedule, but about the total collapse of the cultural monolith.

### What to watch next

As the clock ticks toward May 2026, the question is not who will replace Stephen Colbert, but whether the format itself is viable in any form. We are seeing a shift where even established giants like Davido are opting for long-term project planning, such as his [Davido Confirms 2027 Release for Joint Album With Chris Brown](https://zandani.co.ke/article/davido-confirms-2027-release-for-joint-album-with-chris-brown), focusing on singular events rather than daily maintenance. Expect local broadcasters to follow suit, abandoning the daily talk-show grind in favor of high-production, seasonal content that can sustain interest across multiple platforms. The era of the global late-night host is sunsetting, and the void left behind will be filled by a fractured, decentralized future where no single show owns the spotlight.
