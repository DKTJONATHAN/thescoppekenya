## The Performance of Outrage: Why We Prefer Foreign Villains to Domestic Truths

The narrative surrounding Emmanuel Macron’s intervention at the Africa Forward Summit in Nairobi has been painted as a clear-cut case of neo-colonial arrogance. Critics argue he overstepped, effectively silencing African voices to assert Western dominance. This interpretation is not only lazy; it is a tactical distraction. By fixating on the perceived condescension of a foreign guest, we conveniently ignore the systemic failures occurring within our own borders.

### The Illusion of Resistance

When Macron commanded silence to hear the young African speakers, the audience’s visceral reaction was predictable. Digital platforms were immediately flooded with accusations of imperial overreach. However, if we peel back the layers of this performative indignation, a different reality emerges. The uproar was not a bold defense of African sovereignty. Instead, it was an indicator of an internal malaise. We have become accustomed to viewing the voices of our own peers as secondary, choosing instead to channel our collective energy into shouting at outsiders. This provides a comfortable shield, protecting us from the far more difficult task of confronting our domestic reality.

It is a bitter irony that we direct our ire toward a visiting head of state while remaining largely mute regarding the mounting death tolls on our own soil. We are quick to mobilize against foreign figures who fit the caricature of the oppressor, yet we exhibit a haunting apathy toward the violence sanctioned by our own leadership. Consider the ledger of our recent past: the 60 deaths and 74 disappearances during the 2024 Gen Z protests, the 38 casualties from the Saba Saba demonstrations, and the 48 lives lost in Cameroon after Paul Biya’s reelection. These are not merely statistics; they are the result of state-sponsored brutality that goes largely unpunished. We prioritize a shouting match with a foreign dignitary because it is safe, whereas challenging local governance requires courage we seem unwilling to muster.

### The Myth of Revolutionary Posturing

The spectacle at the summit was less a political movement and more an emotional outburst. True accountability is not found in heckling dignitaries; it is found in the grueling, often thankless labor of policing our own administrations. We have cultivated a brand of resistance that feels revolutionary while demanding zero personal risk.

While we broadcast our moral superiority online, the domestic social contract has disintegrated. Authorities openly ignore the 150,000 to 400,000 deaths in Sudan and the 11 million displaced people across the region. We look past these humanitarian catastrophes because they lack a Western villain to target. Domestically, Za Ndani of governance is equally abysmal. When petrol stations are caught peddling adulterated fuel with total impunity, it reflects a society that views the average citizen as a disposable asset. We demand respect from the global community, yet we fail to extend basic dignity to the very people fighting for transparency at home.

### The Cost of Our Selective Vision

This obsession with external targets creates a dangerous vacuum. By focusing our scrutiny on the optics of international summits, we grant the state a free pass. We allow them to evade responsibility for economic crises, human rights violations, and institutional decay. We are being systematically distracted by the theatre of grievances, while our own institutions are hollowed out by our collective silence.

### Defining the Path Forward

The true test of our political maturity will not be found in how we treat foreign leaders, but in how we address the architects of our own suffering. Watch the coming months carefully. Does the next wave of civil unrest demand specific, legally binding consequences for the police commanders who oversaw the killings during the 2024 protests? Or will we remain trapped in the cycle of symbolic warfare against external entities?

If we refuse to pivot from performative outrage to concrete, localized action, we are not participants in a democratic movement. We are merely the noise that obscures the truth. Real change requires us to listen to one another-not just when a microphone is being held by a foreigner, but when we are debating the victims of our own judicial and political failure. Until we hold our own leaders to the same standards we demand of the world, our anger is nothing more than a mask for our own hesitation to face the truth.
