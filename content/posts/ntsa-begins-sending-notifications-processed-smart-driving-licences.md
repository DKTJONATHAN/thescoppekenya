## NTSA Begins Sending Notifications, But Systemic Failures Persist The National Transport and Safety Authority (NTSA) has commenced sending notifications to motorists regarding the collection of their **processed Smart Driving Licences**. This move, which saw messages dispatched between February 25 and March 2, 2026, aims to alleviate a significant backlog that has left many Kenyans in limbo for weeks, and in some cases, months.

The Illusion of Progress in Smart Driving Licences While the recent wave of SMS notifications and portal updates from the NTSA may appear as a swift response to public outcry, a closer examination reveals a worrying pattern of reactive management rather than proactive efficiency. Many of the newly available **processed Smart Driving Licences** were applied for as far back as December and early January, highlighting a persistent bureaucratic inertia that has become synonymous with the authority's operations. The delays, which became particularly pronounced since January 2026, have inflicted considerable frustration upon motorists. Beyond mere inconvenience, the inability to acquire these crucial documents has had tangible economic consequences, hindering individuals from securing employment, facilitating essential travel, or ensuring compliance with traffic regulations. This persistent challenge underscores a systemic flaw that successive NTSA administrations have struggled to overcome, despite repeated assurances of improved service delivery.

New Leadership, Old Challenges for Processed Smart Driving Licences The current efforts to clear the backlog follow closely on the heels of new leadership at the NTSA. Nashon Kundiwa, appointed Director General effective February 17, 2026, had just a week prior, on February 23, assured Kenyans of prompt processing for driving licences and number plates. While this immediate action might be attributed to the new DG's directives, it also casts a critical light on why such backlogs were allowed to accumulate to a crisis point before intervention. Kundiwa’s predecessor, George Njao, also faced scrutiny during his tenure, with a history of NTSA leadership struggling to meet efficiency targets. Promises of processing critical identification documents within one day, made as early as July 2020, have largely remained unfulfilled. This history of unkept pledges cultivates a deep-seated public cynicism, questioning whether each new assurance represents genuine reform or simply another attempt to placate a frustrated populace.

The Costly Promise of a "Second-Generation" Smart Driving Licence In a seemingly ambitious bid to address these perennial issues, the NTSA recently unveiled a Sh42 billion Public-Private Partnership (PPP) with KCB Bank Kenya Limited and Pesa Print Limited. This extensive 21-year project aims to introduce a "second-generation" smart driving licence (e-DL) and promises production timelines of merely 24 to 48 hours, supported by 102 new enrolment centres and hundreds of enrolment kits across the country. However, the hefty investment and grand promises must be viewed through the lens of NTSA's past. The existing smart driving licence system, introduced in 2018, was itself meant to streamline processes and enhance security, with initial collection times often cited as three to five days. Yet, by June 2025, only 2.1 million smart licences had been issued against a target of 5 million, indicating a significant shortfall and operational struggles even before the latest delays. The shift of the project from National Bank of Kenya to KCB Bank, necessitated by NBK's acquisition by Access Bank Plc, further highlights the complexities and potential vulnerabilities within these long-term government contracts. This pattern of launching expensive, ostensibly transformative projects that eventually fall prey to delays and operational inefficiencies places an undue burden on ordinary citizens. While the nation often celebrates high-profile events, such as when [Omanyala, Samidoh, Azziad Flag Off Eric Omondi’s 466km Busia Charity Walk](https://zandani.co.ke/article/omanyala-samidoh-azziad-eric-omondi-busia-charity-walk), the daily struggles of citizens navigating a frequently inefficient public service sector receive far less sustained attention. The real losers in this cycle of promises and delays are the thousands of drivers whose livelihoods are directly impacted by the NTSA's inability to consistently deliver on its core mandate. Until concrete, sustained improvements in transparency, accountability, and consistent service delivery are demonstrated, the latest round of notifications for **processed Smart Driving Licences** will remain a temporary respite, rather than a definitive solution to a deeply entrenched problem.
